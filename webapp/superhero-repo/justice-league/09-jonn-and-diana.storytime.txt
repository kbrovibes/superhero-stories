Some of the most important conversations happen when the work is done and two people are sitting somewhere, watching the light change.

The mission in Kahndaq had ended the way most good missions end: the problem solved, the people safe, the equipment contained. The rest of the League had flown back to the Hall of Justice. J'onn J'onzz and Diana stayed a little longer to deactivate two more of Luthor's devices. When it was finished, they sat down on the stone steps outside the building.

The desert sun was setting. The sky went from blue to amber to the deep pink of an hour that doesn't last long.

They were quiet for a while. J'onn and Diana were both old enough to have learned that silence between two people who understand each other is not awkward — it is a kind of warmth.

J'onn J'onzz was the last survivor of Mars, a world that no longer existed except in his memory. He was a telepath, which meant he often knew what people were feeling even when they tried to hide it. He was a shapeshifter, which meant he could look like anyone but almost always chose to look like himself. He was ancient, and wise, and lonely in a very specific way that is hard to explain unless you have been the last of something.

Diana was Wonder Woman — an Amazon, trained on the island of Themyscira, shaped by a civilization older than most of the world's history. She had seen empires rise and fall, languages appear and vanish. She carried her history lightly, the way strong people carry heavy things — without showing the weight.

J'onn said that he sometimes walked the city at night, as a human in a coat and hat, along the streets of Middleton, the Colorado city where he had once lived quietly before the League. Walking, watching the windows glow, listening to people going about their evenings. Feeling something like belonging.

Diana said softly that she understood. She had spent time in Paris — just Diana, not Wonder Woman, walking along the river with bread and cheese, listening to a beautiful language, watching the light on the water.

Then J'onn said the harder thing.

He said that everyone else on the team had a people. Clark had Krypton in his heart and Earth in his daily life. Arthur had Atlantis. Barry had Central City and a family that loved him. Hal had Coast City and the Green Lantern Corps and friendships scattered across the galaxy. Even Bruce — closed-off, careful Bruce — had Gotham.

J'onn had one photograph in his room. A single framed image, the only object he had carried from Mars. He looked at it sometimes at night, when the Hall was quiet. And sometimes that weight was more than a room could hold.

Diana listened to all of this without interrupting — which is something different from being silent. When J'onn finished, she said: "You have a people. They are eight. They are loud and they argue about things that could be settled in one sentence and they eat all the coffee before you get to the kitchen. And they are yours."

J'onn considered this. The desert was cooling around them, the way deserts go quickly from warm to cool at sunset.

Then he asked, carefully: "Your island still exists. Themyscira. You could go back."

"I know," Diana said, without hesitation.

"Do you miss it?"

"Every day." She paused. "And every morning I choose this."

She chose the League. She chose this strange, imperfect, noisy family of eight, every morning, because it was worth choosing.

J'onn let his shape relax a little. He didn't transform — he just stopped holding the specific effort of perfect human form, the way you relax your shoulders when you've been tense. He looked a little more like himself: ancient and green and sad and strong.

Diana didn't move. She was simply there — steady, warm, like a room with a candle lit in it.

They sat until the stars came out over Kahndaq.

When they flew back to the Hall, J'onn took his photograph from his room and set it on the common-room shelf. Then he went to bed.

The next morning, Arthur set a spiral shell from the deep ocean beside it — pale and perfect, shimmering inside. A day later, Barry added a playing card in a cracked frame, worn at the edges, a queen of hearts he had carried for years. Then a small golden horse, carved from wood, appeared on the shelf.

No one asked who put it there. No one discussed the shelf. It just grew, slowly and quietly, one object at a time.

That is how belonging happens in the Hall of Justice. Not with a speech. With eight people, one at a time, placing something of themselves where the others can see it.

Close your eyes. You belong somewhere too.

Goodnight.
