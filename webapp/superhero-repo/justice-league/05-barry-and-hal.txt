Barry Allen and Hal Jordan did not seem like they would be friends. One was a careful forensic scientist who organized his coffee mugs alphabetically. The other was a test pilot who had once flown an experimental jet upside down just to see if the radio still worked. But somewhere between a mission gone sideways and a shared plate of pancakes, they became the best of friends.

The mission in question started in Coast City, Hal's home. Brainiac, the alien machine-intelligence who collected cities the way some people collect stamps, had deployed a shrink-ray device in the city's power grid. If it triggered, half of Coast City would be miniaturized and sealed in a bottle. Brainiac's ship had already done this to Kandor, a great Kryptonian city now tiny in a glass container aboard his vessel. He meant to add Coast City to his collection.

Batman had dispatched only two League members to the initial investigation, believing it was a reconnaissance mission. He was wrong. When Hal and Barry arrived at the power station, they found the device already active and three of Brainiac's combat probes circling it.

They had no time to call for backup. They went.

The Flash could not run near the device — the shrink field destabilized his speed energy. He redirected to the probes, leading two of them on a chase through the station's corridors while Hal fought the third. Green Lantern built a containment bubble around the device to slow its power-up, then switched to a pair of energy shears to dismantle its outer casing. The ring had to be precise here; one wrong cut and the field would invert.

Barry lured both probes into a turbine room and spun a tornado that jammed their sensors, leaving them colliding with walls. He raced back to Hal just as the containment bubble began to buckle. Together they improvised: Barry vibrated at a frequency that matched the device's power oscillation, and Hal used that vibration as a guide to find the exact seam in the casing where his energy shears could cut without triggering the field.

The device went dark. No explosion, no shrink-ray flash — just a quiet mechanical death.

They sat on a power station walkway afterward, shoulders against the railing, eating takeout pancakes that Barry had picked up on the way back through downtown. Hal said the syrup was wrong. Barry said the syrup was fine. Hal said Barry had no taste. Barry said Hal flew planes upside down recreationally.

By the end of the pancakes they were laughing.

"Next time," Hal said, "let's bring the rest of the team."

"Definitely," Barry agreed.

Neither of them meant it even slightly.
