The alert came from Cyborg's systems at three in the morning: a city-sized tidal wave was bearing down on the coast of Japan, triggered by an undersea fault, and it would strike Osaka in forty minutes. The Hall of Justice lit up like dawn.

Aquaman was already moving before anyone spoke. He dove into the Pacific and reached the fault zone in twelve minutes. He placed both hands on the ocean floor and pushed — not hard enough to break rock, but firm enough to redirect pressure sideways through a deep trench rather than up toward the surface. The wave slowed. It did not stop.

The Flash reached Osaka Harbor in the time it took most people to find their shoes. Barry Allen moved through the coastal district like warm wind, knocking on every door, shouting a warning in every language he had memorized on the run over. He did not wait to see if people listened — the certainty in his voice was enough. He had cleared twelve blocks of residents before the wave hit the breakwater.

Green Lantern Hal Jordan constructed a seawall of emerald light three miles offshore — the largest structure he had ever built. He held it steady with both fists clenched while the wave crashed against his construct and broke apart into foam.

Wonder Woman and Superman flew parallel sweeps over the harbor, pulling ships from anchorage and setting them in deeper water where the wave would pass beneath rather than overturn them. Cyborg coordinated from above, sending updated wave data to everyone's earpieces every ninety seconds. Batman monitored the seismic network, detected a second smaller rupture forming to the north, and redirected Green Lantern with a single transmission before it could build.

J'onn J'onzz drifted invisible over the coastal neighborhoods, locating anyone trapped with his Martian telepathy and guiding rescue teams to them by the dozen.

The wave arrived reduced, softened, a wall of white water that flooded the lowest streets and overturned market stalls but did not level buildings or take lives. Rescue teams found every stranded person within two hours.

On the shore at dawn, eight heroes watched the foam recede. Barry Allen wrung seawater from his sleeve. Arthur Curry sat on a piling and said nothing for a while, listening to the ocean settle.

"Good mission," said Victor Stone.

That was all. And it was exactly enough.
