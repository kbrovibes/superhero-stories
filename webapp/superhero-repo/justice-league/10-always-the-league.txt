One year after eight strangers stood on a Metropolis rooftop and looked at each other for the first time, the Justice League held a quiet gathering in the Hall of Justice.

It was Clark's idea, though he said it wasn't. Bruce organized it, though he said he only handled the logistics. The round table was set with eight mugs and eight glasses of water from Atlantis, carried in a sealed container by Arthur the week before. Barry arrived first, as always, and spent his extra time rearranging the chairs and putting them back exactly where they were.

The ceremony was brief. Superman talked about Osaka — the first real mission, three in the morning, forty minutes, the moment they discovered it was actually going to work. Aquaman talked about the rooftop — when the battle ended and nobody moved to leave. Wonder Woman said what she valued was this table: every chair equal, every voice counted the same. The Martian Manhunter said, quietly, that he had not expected to belong somewhere again.

Cyborg said that one year ago he had not been sure he was more human than machine. He had spent years working out how to be Victor Stone — not the technology, but the person. The League was the first place where the question had simply not come up. He was Victor. That was enough.

Green Lantern gave a toast with the wrong coffee mug and everyone laughed. Batman, who did not give speeches, said: "You kept your word. All of you."

Barry said: "Same time next year?"

Outside, the city went about its business: children walking to school, the river catching the morning light. All of it ordinary. All of it what they protected.

They had argued. They had misunderstood each other. They had each carried fears the others could not quite see. And they had also shown up, every time — every mission, every rescue, every crisis at three in the morning. They had shown up for each other until people who were never supposed to fit together, impossibly and completely, fit.

The Justice League was not a team because someone decided it should be. It was a team because these eight had chosen it, again and again, every morning.

Whatever came next, they would face it together.
