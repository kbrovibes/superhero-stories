The argument started over a map and almost ended the Justice League before it really began.

Brainiac had returned with a larger ship and a more specific target: Metropolis itself. Scans showed he intended to miniaturize the entire city within eighteen hours. Superman wanted to intercept Brainiac in orbit, meet the threat in space before it descended. Batman argued the opposite: draw Brainiac down, fight on familiar ground, use Earth's environment as a weapon.

For two hours, neither backed down. Clark's voice stayed calm, but his jaw set. Bruce's voice stayed controlled, which was its own kind of argument. J'onn J'onzz said nothing, observing. Diana finally said what everyone was thinking: "We are running out of time for this."

Arthur Curry, who had sat through enough Atlantean council debates to recognize when a disagreement was about strategy and when it was about something else, spoke quietly. "Clark. You are afraid Brainiac will hurt this city if he gets close enough." Clark said nothing, which meant yes. "Bruce. You are afraid your plan involves Clark going somewhere you cannot follow." Bruce said nothing, which meant also yes.

The argument, Arthur explained, was not really about strategy. It was about each of them protecting the other in the only way they knew how.

They took twenty minutes to build a combined plan. Hal and Clark would intercept Brainiac's ship in the upper atmosphere — not orbit, not Earth's surface, but the boundary between them, where Clark had the advantage and Hal's constructs would function at full power. Meanwhile, Batman's defensive perimeter around Metropolis would serve as the backup if any part of Brainiac's ship made it past them.

Hal Jordan went first, ring blazing, and built a containment frame around Brainiac's landing thrusters. Clark tore the primary scanner array from the ship's hull with both hands. Without his scans, Brainiac could not lock coordinates. Without locked coordinates, he could not shrink the city.

Brainiac retreated — not defeated, not captured, but thwarted. Metropolis remained its full size.

In the Hall that night, no one mentioned the argument. J'onn quietly made tea. Barry talked too much, the way he did when he was relieved. Diana and Arthur played chess near the window.

Clark and Bruce sat at the round table. Bruce turned a small chess piece over in his fingers.

"Good plan," Clark said.

"Yours was good too," Bruce replied. "We combined them."

"That's what the table is for," Clark said, nodding at the round surface between them.

Bruce looked at it for a long moment. "Yes," he said. "That is exactly what it is for."
