After a long mission in Kahndaq, the others had gone back to the Hall. J'onn J'onzz and Diana stayed behind to ensure the last of Lex Luthor's equipment was contained. The work was methodical and quiet, and when it was done they sat on a step outside, and the sun set over the desert.

They were two of the oldest members of the League. Diana had left the island of Themyscira before she came to the world of humans — she was an Amazon, shaped by a different time and a different kind of wisdom. J'onn had come from Mars, the last of his kind, carrying the memory of an entire world that no longer existed. Both of them understood what it meant to carry something no one else could fully share.

J'onn said he sometimes walked the city at night in a different form — as an ordinary person, not the Martian Manhunter, just someone in a coat and a hat, walking the streets of Middleton where he had once lived quietly before the League. Diana said she understood. She had spent time in Paris, in London, in cities where she was just Diana, not Wonder Woman — just a person watching the world go about its life.

J'onn admitted that he sometimes felt alone in a specific way: everyone else had a people. Clark had Krypton in his memory and Earth in his life. Arthur had Atlantis. Barry had his city and his family. Even Bruce, who closed himself off from almost everyone, had Gotham. J'onn had one framed photograph in his quarters — the only object he had brought from Mars — and sometimes that weight was more than a room could hold.

Diana listened without speaking, which is different from being silent. When J'onn finished, she said: "You have a people. They are eight. They are flawed and loud and sometimes they eat all the coffee, but they are yours now."

Then he said, carefully: "Your island is still there. You could return."

"I know," Diana said. "I choose this instead. Every morning, I choose this."

They sat until the first stars appeared over Kahndaq. J'onn shifted his shape slightly — not into anything else, just relaxing the careful human form he maintained for everyone's comfort — and Diana did not look away, only sat with companionable steadiness.

When they flew back to the Hall, J'onn moved his framed photograph to the shelf in the common area. No one commented. But over the next few days, others quietly added things: a seashell from Atlantis, a playing card in a cracked frame, a small figure of a golden horse.

A shelf of belonging, built without a word.
