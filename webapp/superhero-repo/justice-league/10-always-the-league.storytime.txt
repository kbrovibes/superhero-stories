One year is a small thing, measured against the age of the universe.

But one year in a team's life can be enormous.

One year ago: a Metropolis rooftop, eight strangers, a battle finished, the sky clearing. Nobody had planned to stand there. Nobody was quite sure, looking around at the others, how they felt.

One year later: the round table in the Hall of Justice, lit by morning light through the tall windows above the Potomac.

It was Clark's idea. He maintained it wasn't.

Bruce organized the details — eight chairs placed just so, the table polished. He said he had only handled the logistics. This was technically true and not the whole story.

Barry arrived early. He used the extra time to rearrange the chairs, study them for several minutes, and put them back exactly as they were.

Arthur had come four days earlier with a sealed container he carried under one arm — water from Atlantis, from a spring at the edge of the kelp forest where the light came down in long green columns. He poured it into each glass before anyone else was awake. Nobody mentioned that tap water would have worked. Because it wasn't the water that mattered. It was Atlantis in the glass.

Hal Jordan brought his coffee in the mug that said PROPERTY OF THE FLASH in bold letters. When Barry saw it, he laughed so suddenly that he spilled a little, cleaned it up before anyone reacted, and kept laughing.

They sat down, all eight of them.

Clark spoke first. He talked about Osaka — three in the morning, forty minutes, the moment he understood that they each trusted the others to handle their part without watching it happen. The first time he had known, not hoped, that this was going to work.

Arthur talked about the rooftop. Not the battle — the moment after. When there was no tactical reason anymore for eight strangers to stand together. He had waited to see who would leave first. Nobody had.

"That was the moment," he said simply.

Diana said what she had thought since the first day: she loved the table. This table, round and equal, where a forensic scientist from Central City had exactly the same seat as the last survivor of an ancient world. Every voice heard.

J'onn spoke quietly. He said he had not expected to belong somewhere again. He said the word again carefully. Nobody asked what it meant. Everyone understood.

Victor Stone stood up. He was tall, and when he stood the room paid attention. He said that for years after the accident that changed him, he had lived inside a question: which part of him was real? The human boy, or the machine? Here, that question had simply never come up. He was Victor. Both at once, exactly as he was.

And for the first time in a long time, that felt like more than enough.

Hal raised his mug — the wrong mug — and gave a toast. Everyone laughed: Aquaman's dignified expression cracking, Diana's smile, J'onn's warming eyes. Even Batman, by the window, made an expression with most of the characteristics of a smile in it.

Batman walked to the table and looked at each of them in turn.

"You kept your word," he said. "All of you."

Five words. From Bruce Wayne, who trusted almost no one completely and had spent his life surrounded by loss. Five words that meant a great deal more than five words.

Barry held up his mug. "Same time next year?"

There was no vote. No discussion. Eight people around a round table on a Tuesday morning, water from Atlantis in their glasses, wrong-mug jokes warm in the air. They all knew the answer at the same moment.

Of course. Always.

Outside the windows, the Potomac moved. In the city, the day was going about its business: delivery trucks crossing the bridge, a class of schoolchildren on the memorial plaza, a dog running loose and joyful across the grass.

All of it ordinary. Completely essential. What all of this had been for.

These eight had found each other the way puzzle pieces find each other: by the specific shape of their edges. What one lacked, another had. What one could not see, another could. What one could not reach, another could extend to. They had not been asked to become a family. They had become one anyway.

Whatever came next — and there was always something next — they would face it together.

That was the Justice League. Not the name on the building. These eight people, this round table, the equal chairs.

Sleep now. Dream of something good. The lights are on in the Hall of Justice, and the League is watching over everything.

They always will be.
