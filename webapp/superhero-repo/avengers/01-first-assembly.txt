Nick Fury had been carrying the idea for years — one team, the best of the best, assembled before the world needed them badly enough to beg. He walked into the briefing room at S.H.I.E.L.D. headquarters and looked at what he had: a soldier from another era sitting stiffly upright, a god from Asgard working on the coffee machine, a scientist doing breathing exercises in the corner, a spy whose expression had not changed once since she arrived, an archer who had chosen the chair closest to the exit, and a billionaire who arrived forty minutes late and immediately started an argument with the soldier about military strategy.

Natasha Romanoff had already read every file in the room — including files on people who didn't know files existed about them. She sat with her hands folded and watched. Thor called everyone "friend" in a way that made it plain he meant it with his whole heart, which flustered people who weren't used to being meant. Bruce Banner had chosen the farthest chair from the door and was cycling through a breathing pattern he'd developed in Calcutta. Steve Rogers had shaken every hand, looked Tony Stark in the eye, and known immediately that this was going to be complicated. Tony had reached the same conclusion in two seconds and responded the only way he knew: by talking loudly.

Then something went wrong. A circuit failure sent a flicker of cold blue light spilling from the Tesseract's secure case across the table. Everyone moved at the same instant. Steve was on his feet with his shield arm raised. Tony's repulsor gauntlet snapped from his watch. Thor stepped forward. Natasha crossed half the room. Clint Barton had an arrow nocked before anyone blinked. Bruce gripped the armrests and breathed, deliberately, and held. The light steadied. The case sealed. No one had planned it. No one had said a word. And it had worked — barely, imperfectly — and every person in the room felt the others notice it too.

The Tesseract was still out there, stolen by Loki, and the portal it could open was a danger none of them could answer alone. Fury let the silence settle. He looked at the brilliant, contrary, deeply unusual people around his table — the spy, the soldier, the scientist, the god, the billionaire, the archer with a second arrow certainly already nocked just in case.

He gave a single small nod. Not approval, exactly. Something older and more certain. Recognition. This improbable group was his team, and they had just proved it without meaning to.
