When the dust of the great battle settled, the world was safe — but it was also changed forever. Tony Stark was gone. Natasha Romanoff was gone. Steve Rogers had grown old and peaceful, living the quiet life he had always deserved. Three of the original six Avengers had stepped away, and the ones who remained had to ask a very hard question: what does it mean to be an Avenger now?

Peter Parker — the young Spider-Man — felt the weight of it every time someone compared him to Tony. He was clever and kind and brave, but he was also just a teenager from Queens trying to figure out his own way in a very big world. Carrying someone else's legacy is a heavy thing when you are still building your own.

Sam Wilson picked up the vibranium shield that Steve had passed to him. He didn't try to be exactly who Steve had been — he couldn't, and he wouldn't. He was Sam Wilson, with his own story, his own battles, his own reasons to stand up. And that, it turned out, was exactly what Captain America needed to be.

And then there was Wanda Maximoff. She had lost so much — her brother Pietro, then Vision, then five years to the Snap and back again. Her grief was enormous, and it was so powerful that sometimes it began to change the world around her in frightening ways. The new Avengers had to learn something the first team had struggled with too: heroes need help just like everyone else.

From that day on, the Avengers became something bigger than a team. They worked with sorcerers and kings and survivors from a hundred worlds. New faces joined the assembly — young, different, full of their own kind of courage. And in the city, at a quiet memorial made of stone, the names Stark, Romanoff, and Rogers were engraved for all to remember. The legacy was not a burden. It was a light to walk toward.
