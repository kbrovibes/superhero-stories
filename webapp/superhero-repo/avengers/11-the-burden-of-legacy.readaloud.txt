After the biggest battle ever fought on Earth or in space, after the dust settled and the portals closed and all of Thanos's army had gone to dust — the quiet was enormous. And in that quiet, a hard question appeared: now what?

Tony Stark was gone. Natasha Romanoff was gone. Steve Rogers had lived a full, peaceful life in the past and returned as a gentle old man, and he passed the vibranium shield to Sam Wilson before he sat down to rest. Three of the original six Avengers had stepped away. And the ones still standing had to figure out what the Avengers even meant without them.

Poor Peter Parker. He just wanted to go on a school trip to Europe and think about science. But everywhere he went, people called him "the next Iron Man" and looked at him with so much expectation that his shoulders felt like they were carrying something very heavy. Peter was clever, kind, brave — and fifteen. That is a lot to ask of anyone who is fifteen.

Sam Wilson was not fifteen. He picked up the vibranium shield and looked at it for a long time. He could have said no. He almost did. But eventually he understood: Captain America isn't a title you inherit. It's a choice you make every single morning. Sam Wilson made that choice, and he made it his own way, with his own story, his own voice.

And Wanda Maximoff had lost more than almost anyone — Pietro, then Vision, then five years to the Snap, and then everything again. Her grief was enormous and honest and sometimes it spilled out of her in ways she couldn't control, because she had powers that responded to feelings, and her feelings were very very large. The new Avengers were learning something the first team had always struggled with: heroes are not machines. They need help too.

New faces joined the assembly. Survivors of the Blip, young people from Wakanda and Queens and places in between, each with their own kind of courage. The New Asgardians settled into their fishing village in Norway, and Earth found itself sharing a planet with a small colony of gods, which took some getting used to.

In the city, a quiet stone memorial listed three names: Stark. Romanoff. Rogers. People brought flowers. Children came with their parents and looked at the names and felt something they couldn't quite name yet — a warm, serious feeling in the chest. The feeling of knowing someone kept going when it was hard.

That is the only kind of legacy worth carrying.
