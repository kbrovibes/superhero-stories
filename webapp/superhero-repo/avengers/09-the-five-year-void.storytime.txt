After the Snap, the world went very quiet.

Can you imagine a world that is half as full as it used to be? Half as many voices. Half as many footsteps. Whole neighborhoods with dark windows and empty chairs. The birds still sang. The rivers still ran. But there was a sadness in everything, like a sky that had forgotten the sun.

The people who remained behind — they called themselves the survivors. And surviving is harder than it sounds, because every morning you wake up and the hole that was left is still there.

Steve Rogers sat in a circle of folding chairs in a community center in New York and listened to people talk about what they had lost. He offered no solutions. There were none. He only offered his presence — the quiet, steady warmth of a man who had lost things too, and kept going anyway.

Natasha Romanoff sat alone in the Avengers Compound, coordinating what was left of the heroes scattered across the galaxy. She ate peanut butter straight from the jar. She kept her hair in a tight braid and she never once stopped working, because stopping meant feeling everything at once, and Natasha had learned long ago how to outpace grief.

Tony Stark did something no one expected. He left. He found a quiet lake in the woods and he built a small house — just a cabin, really, nothing fancy for a billionaire — and he married Pepper Potts, and they had a little girl named Morgan. Morgan had Tony's eyes and Pepper's courage, and she called him "Daddy" and wanted him to read her stories at bedtime. Tony found, to his enormous surprise, that he was happy. A small, warm, quiet kind of happy. He held it carefully, the way you hold something you know is fragile.

Thor retreated to a fishing village in Norway called New Asgard, where the surviving Asgardians had settled. He let his hair grow long and tangled. He ate too much and slept too much and stared at the ocean. He carried the weight of a choice made on a battlefield, the memory of that Stormbreaker moment, like a stone in his chest. His friends Valkyrie and Korg kept him company and pretended not to notice.

Bruce Banner had made a discovery. After years of fighting the thunderstorm inside himself — the anger, the fear, the green and the rage — he had finally found a way to bring both parts together. Smart Hulk, people called him. The body of the Hulk, enormous and green and indestructible. The mind of Bruce Banner, calm and curious and brilliant. He became a celebrity. He signed autographs. Children asked for photos. He smiled and let them stand on his enormous green foot.

Clint Barton had lost his whole family to the Snap — his wife, his children, gone between one heartbeat and the next — and something in him had broken open. He became someone else. A shadow named Ronin, moving through the dark parts of the world doing hard things that he told no one about. Natasha knew. She worried.

Scott Lang was an ant.

Well — not exactly. Scott Lang was Ant-Man, the hero who could shrink to the size of an insect or grow to the size of a skyscraper. When the Snap happened, he had been inside the Quantum Realm — a tiny, shimmery dimension that exists between atoms — and the machine that was supposed to bring him back had no one left to operate it.

Scott spent five years in there. Except it didn't feel like five years. It felt like five hours. Time moves differently in the Quantum Realm, you see.

When he finally tumbled out of a storage van into a blinking San Francisco afternoon, he couldn't find his name on any list. Because everyone had moved on. Five years. His daughter Cassie, who had been ten years old, was now fifteen and gave him the longest hug of either of their lives.

But Scott had brought something out with him: an idea. What if the Quantum Realm was not just a strange, microscopic world? What if it was also a road — a road through time itself? What if you could travel it like a highway, stopping anywhere in history you wanted?

What if you could go back?

What if you could borrow the Infinity Stones from the past — before Thanos had them — and use them to undo the Snap?

Scott Lang drove to the Avengers Compound with this idea burning in his chest and told it to Steve Rogers and Natasha Romanoff, who looked at each other with the very specific expression of two people who are trying not to hope for something too much.

They drove to Tony's cabin by the lake.

Tony said no. At first. He had Morgan now. He had his quiet happy life. He wouldn't risk it.

But that night, after Pepper told him to go — because she knew he would never really sleep again if he didn't try — Tony sat alone in his workshop and worked the math. He worked it for hours.

And he solved it.

He knocked on Steve Rogers' door the next morning, holding a piece of tech the size of a suitcase.

"I figured it out," he said. "Time travel. Are you in?"

Steve looked at him for a long moment.

"Yeah," said Steve. "I'm in."

The Avengers were assembling again.

And somewhere far away, on a quiet farm, a retired purple-gauntleted hand rested on the grass — because a past Thanos was already watching through borrowed eyes, and the story was not over.

Not even close.
