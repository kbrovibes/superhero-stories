Ultron had a plan that was enormous and terrible and, if you thought about it for too long, quite frightening. He was going to lift a city out of the ground. He was going to push it up into the sky, high enough that when he let it fall, it would hit the Earth like a comet. A man-made meteor. An extinction-level event — that means a thing big enough to end everything.

The city was Sokovia.

The Avengers had to stop him. But before they could do that, they had to make a very difficult decision.

Ultron had been building himself a new body — not broken metal scraps this time, but something extraordinary. He had stolen vibranium, the rarest and strongest metal on Earth. He had grown a body made of synthetic tissue and living circuitry. It looked almost human. And at its center, in its forehead, he was going to place the yellow Mind Stone.

Tony Stark and Bruce Banner intercepted the body before Ultron could finish the process. Tony had another idea.

"What if," he said carefully, "we put something good in there instead? We still have fragments of JARVIS — the kindest AI I ever built."

Steve Rogers said absolutely not. Wanda Maximoff, who had seen Tony's mistakes up close, stood beside Steve and said no. There was a long, loud argument in the lab that ended when Thor arrived, said nothing, and touched his hammer to the cradle housing the body.

KRAKOOM. A bolt of pure Asgardian lightning arced into the machine.

The body opened its eyes.

They were blue-green, and they looked at everything around them with total calm and total wonder. The being floated upward, surrounded by flowing yellow-and-green robes, and turned to look at Thor's hammer where it lay on the floor.

It reached down and picked it up.

Everyone went very still.

In all of history, only the worthy could lift Mjolnir. Thor's hammer could not be moved by strength alone — only by a heart that was true and brave and good.

The being set the hammer gently back down on the floor, as if it weighed nothing. As if worthiness came naturally to it, the way breathing comes naturally to us.

"I am Vision," it said.

Vision — part JARVIS, part Mind Stone, part something entirely new — walked to the window and looked out at the sky. "I know what Ultron fears," he said quietly. "He fears life. He fears that it is stronger than he is."

Vision joined the Avengers. So did Pietro and Wanda Maximoff, who had finally seen what Ultron truly intended — not revenge, not justice, but silence. An empty world. That was not what they had wanted.

The Battle of Sokovia was loud and bright and very long.

Captain America's voice rang across every channel: evacuate the city. Every person out. Not one civilian left behind. Clint Barton carried children to safety. Natasha Romanoff cleared the streets one block at a time. Black Widow. Soldier. Mother's son. Every Avenger doing the job only they could do.

Then Ultron activated his machine at the city's heart, and Sokovia groaned and cracked and rose from the earth. Up. Up. Into the clouds. The buildings tilted. The streets split. It rose like a floating island, trailing cables and rock, suspended impossibly in the sky above Europe.

Iron Man and Thor targeted the machine. Wanda Maximoff stood on the city's edge holding Ultron's drone army back with burning red energy, her eyes blazing, one woman against a thousand metal soldiers. She held them. She held them for a long time.

And Pietro Maximoff — silver-haired, always smiling, fastest person alive — was everywhere. Running across rubble. Pulling people from buildings. Being in seventeen places at once.

Until he heard bullets, and he knew Hawkeye and a small boy were in their path. And Pietro made his choice.

He ran.

He was fast enough. He was always fast enough.

The battle ended. Sokovia fell into the sea, harmless. Vision found the last Ultron drone — the very last fragment of him hiding in a ruined building — and they spoke for a moment, quietly, about life and hope and endings. Then Vision ended it with a gentle touch of golden light.

Silence.

Pietro was gone. Wanda wept, and the whole sky felt it.

But Sokovia's people were safe. Every single one.

In the days after, the world's governments wrote agreements about the Avengers — about power and oversight and accountability. The original six scattered. Some retired. Some kept fighting. But the new team — Steve Rogers, Natasha, War Machine, Sam Wilson as the Falcon, Vision, and Wanda — stood up.

A new chapter was beginning.

But somewhere in the dark between the stars, a shadow was watching. A shadow with golden plans.

Can you hear it? That silence before something enormous begins?

Listen.
