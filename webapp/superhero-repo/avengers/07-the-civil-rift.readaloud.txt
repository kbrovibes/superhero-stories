After the destruction of Sokovia, the world asked a hard question: who watches over the heroes? A document called the Sokovia Accords said that the Avengers must answer to a committee of world leaders. Tony Stark thought that was only fair — heroes should be accountable. Steve Rogers thought it was dangerous — what if the committee made the wrong call? Two good people, two good arguments, and no easy answer.

Then something terrible happened. Steve's oldest friend, Bucky Barnes — the man once known as the Winter Soldier — was blamed for a bombing at a meeting of world leaders. Steve believed Bucky was innocent. Tony believed in the law. At a great airport in Germany, the two factions of Avengers met face to face. Spider-Man, a young hero Tony had recruited, swung into the battle. Black Panther, whose father had been lost in the bombing, fought with grief and fury. It was spectacular and deeply sad all at once.

The worst moment came in a cold HYDRA base in Siberia. Tony discovered the truth about who had killed his parents years ago — it had been Bucky, acting under mind control. The pain was too much to hold in. He fought Steve and Bucky in the dark, not as Iron Man, but as a son who was heartbroken. When it was over, Steve left his vibranium shield on the floor and walked away. He could not carry it as someone else's soldier any longer.

The Avengers were broken apart. But a far greater danger was already moving through the universe. A powerful being called Thanos was collecting the six Infinity Stones one by one. Bruce Banner crashed through the roof of the Sanctum Sanctorum and gasped out the warning. And just like that, a divided world had to face its greatest threat — together or alone.
