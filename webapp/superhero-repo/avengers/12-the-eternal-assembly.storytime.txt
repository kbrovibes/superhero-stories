Let me tell you something about stories that never truly end.

They don't finish — they pass on. Like a torch held between two cupped hands, moving from one person to the next through the dark, never quite going out. That is the story of the Avengers.

It began with a conversation in a dark underground room: Nick Fury, a man in a long black coat with one sharp eye, looking at a file full of impossible people. He believed that those people — scattered across the world and the stars, different in every way that mattered — could become something more than the sum of their parts. He called it the Avengers Initiative.

He was right.

Steve Rogers was a skinny boy from Brooklyn who wanted, more than anything, to be useful. He became Captain America — and the thing that made him Captain America was never the serum, never the shield, never the costume. It was the choice he made before any of that, to get up one more time after being knocked down. He passed the shield to Sam Wilson, and Sam carried it forward — not as a copy, but as himself, which was exactly the right way to carry anything worth carrying.

Tony Stark was a man who built walls around himself — of sarcasm and cleverness and things that blinked and hummed — because the world inside those walls had hurt him. Slowly, imperfectly, magnificently, he tore those walls down. He built a family and kept it and chose them, over and over. And when the hardest moment arrived, he was ready. He passed his legacy to young protégés and students and dreamers who watched him and thought: I could try to be that good. That brave. That honest about being scared.

Thor, prince of Asgard, god of thunder — he learned that a hammer does not make a hero. Worthiness is not a title. It is a practice. He handed Stormbreaker forward and let his people grow beyond him, because the best leaders know when to step aside and cheer.

In New York, there is a monument. In Sokovia — rebuilt now, with clean streets and a park in the center — there is a monument. In Wakanda, carved from vibranium and basalt, there is a monument. Three places that remember what was lost and what was won, and why it mattered.

A young archer passes her bow across a rooftop to a student who pulls it back and finds the string sits perfectly in her grip, like it was always meant to be there.

A king in Wakanda sits in council with people who once had no voice, and listens, because that is what wisdom looks like up close.

A green-and-gold ranger tends to a city in space that was once just a dream in a young woman's heart, and the city hums with life.

Heroes do not retire. They change shape.

The Avengers were never one team. They were an idea: that when something terrible approaches — something that ordinary courage alone cannot stop — the right people will find each other. They will argue. They will misunderstand each other. They will make mistakes and have to ask for forgiveness. And then they will stand shoulder to shoulder anyway, because the alternative is unthinkable.

Nick Fury sat at his desk in a new Helicarrier — the fourth one; the others had not survived — and looked at a holographic display of dots across the globe. Each dot was someone. Each someone had chosen, on one particular day, to step forward instead of back.

"Avengers," he said softly to the empty room, the way you might say a word you've been holding close for years.

Then he smiled.

Somewhere in New Jersey, a girl's hands lit up with bright starlight and she gasped and laughed. Somewhere in the Pacific Ocean, a young man breathed underwater for the first time and looked up at the light refracted through the waves above him, astonished. Somewhere in a library in Harlem, a teenager lifted a stack of books telekinetically without meaning to, then looked around very quickly to see if anyone had noticed.

They were all going to be fine.

They were all going to be extraordinary.

The Eternal Assembly does not have a last chapter, because it does not end. It only ever begins — again, and again, and again — every time someone who has never been a hero before makes the same choice Steve Rogers made in a dark alley in Brooklyn.

The choice to be worthy.

To step forward.

To try.

Avengers Assemble. Now. Tomorrow. Always.

Can you picture it? All those lights, all those hearts, all those hands reaching out across the dark?

Good.

Now close your eyes, and dream about which one is yours.
