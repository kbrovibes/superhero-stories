Up in the sky, high above the clouds where birds can't even fly, the great Helicarrier hummed along like a city that refused to sit still. Inside, in a glowing glass cell, sat Loki — the god of mischief — grinning to himself.

You might wonder: why was he grinning? He was a prisoner, after all. Captured. Locked away. The heroes had won, hadn't they?

But Loki was always playing a longer game than anyone could see. And he was very, very patient.

He had left something behind, you see. His scepter — the golden weapon with the glowing yellow Mind Stone inside it — was still on the ship. And that gem did something sneaky. It made people nearby feel angry. Suspicious. Ready to argue over absolutely everything.

Tony Stark poked his fingers into S.H.I.E.L.D.'s computer systems and found something that made his eyes go wide. Nick Fury had a secret plan — a plan to use the Tesseract's power to make weapons. Big, dangerous weapons. Tony spun around and started demanding answers. Steve Rogers crossed his arms and said Tony was being paranoid. Thor stood between them with his hammer resting on his knee, looking uncomfortable. Bruce Banner quietly pointed out that everyone was shouting.

They were all standing very close to Loki's scepter. The yellow gem pulsed softly.

Outside the Helicarrier, far below in the world of ordinary people, Clint Barton — Hawkeye — was flying a small jet toward the ship. His eyes were still glassy. He still wasn't himself. Loki had sent him on a mission.

BOOM! BOOM! BOOM!

Explosions rocked the outside of the Helicarrier. Hawkeye's team had fired rockets at the great spinning rotors that kept the ship in the air. One rotor blew apart in a shower of sparks and smoke. The whole ship lurched sideways — CRASH — and everyone grabbed for handholds.

Now it was chaos.

Down in the corridors, Bruce Banner felt the floor shake — and when Bruce Banner felt things shake, the thunderstorm inside him started to wake up. His eyes flickered green. His hands began to tremble. He staggered through a corridor and the ceiling collapsed and then —

ROOAAARRR.

The Hulk was loose on the Helicarrier.

He smashed through wall after wall, a green mountain of rage and noise. Thor chased after him trying to stop him. They fought through the belly of the ship, tearing metal and breaking everything they touched. CRASH! SLAM! BOOM! It sounded like a building falling down inside another building.

Meanwhile, Tony Stark and Steve Rogers — who moments ago had been about to fight each other — looked at a broken rotor housing dangling in the sky and realized, instantly, they had to fix it together or the whole ship would fall.

There are moments, even between people who argue a lot, when something clicks.

"Together?" said Steve.

"Together," said Tony.

Tony flew inside the rotor housing, wires sparking around him, while Steve turned an enormous gear by hand — his shield braced against it, his whole body shaking. The engine groaned. Tony made adjustments inside. The rotor coughed back to life. WHOOOMM. The Helicarrier steadied.

But somewhere else on the ship, in a small corridor, Natasha Romanoff had found Clint Barton.

She knew him better than anyone in the world. He was her partner. Her friend. She had spent years learning how to read people — and she could see him in there, behind those blank eyes, locked away.

She didn't have a magic spell to break it. She had something better.

She fought him. Clint punched and kicked with all the skill she'd taught him — because Loki hadn't been able to take that away — and Natasha matched every move. She grabbed him. She threw him against a wall. She held on.

CRACK. The back of his head hit the railing just hard enough.

And Clint Barton blinked. Once. Twice.

His eyes came back.

He looked at Natasha and whispered, "I need some time."

"You'll have it," she said. And she meant it.

But the worst was yet to come. Loki had used the chaos to escape. And before he did, he left one terrible thing behind — the loss of someone good, someone kind, someone whose trading cards Tony Stark had once signed.

Phil Coulson.

Nick Fury walked into the room where the heroes were gathered, still shaken, and he put Phil's belongings on the table — his vintage Captain America cards, stained now, and irreplaceable.

"He died fighting," said Fury quietly. "He believed in all of you. Even when you didn't believe in each other."

The arguing stopped.

The Helicarrier was quiet, except for the hum of the repaired engines.

And one by one, the Avengers looked up at each other — not as strangers, not as rivals — but as a team that had just lost something precious, and knew exactly what they had to do next.
