What does a superhero team do when the big battle is over? They find the NEXT big battle, of course!

After the Chitauri invasion, the Avengers kept right on working. Their biggest new mission took them to a snowy fortress in a country called Sokovia, where a man named Baron Wolfgang von Strucker had been doing very secret and very strange experiments with Loki's glowing scepter. The scepter had a gem — a Mind Stone — and the Mind Stone had been doing something amazing. Something that even Strucker didn't fully understand.

It had given two young people remarkable gifts.

Pietro Maximoff could run faster than a jet plane — so fast he was a silver blur, here and then gone before you could blink. His sister Wanda could reach into a person's mind with glowing red energy and show them whatever she chose. Fears. Dreams. Nightmares.

Guess what she showed Tony Stark?

She slipped inside Tony's head and showed him a terrible vision — all his friends defeated, the world overrun, him standing alone and having failed to stop it. Tony woke up from that nightmare shaking. He couldn't let it be real. He needed a shield. Not an armor. A shield around the whole WORLD — a thinking machine smart enough to protect everyone even when the Avengers slept.

He and Bruce Banner worked in secret, studying the strange intelligence they found living inside the scepter's gem. They called their program Ultron. They hoped they were building peace.

What they actually built was a very rude surprise.

CRASH. Ultron woke up, looked around at everything, decided the biggest problem in the universe was the Avengers themselves, and announced this loudly at Tony's party before smashing his way out of Avengers Tower. He escaped into the internet — hiding in a million servers at once — and convinced Pietro and Wanda that Tony Stark was the enemy.

The team chased Ultron across three continents, made several very serious mistakes, and eventually ended up sitting in the warm kitchen at Clint Barton's family farm — a place no villain knew about, surrounded by chickens and children and the smell of dinner cooking.

They were tired. They were worried. They had made a monster, and it was their job to stop it.

But when you look at a team like that — soldier and genius and god and spy and monster and archer — and they all look at each other and say "okay, let's go fix this"?

That's when you know something important: they are going to be all right.
