The first time the team really rested together, it was in a shawarma restaurant near Stark Tower, after the Battle of New York, before anyone had changed out of their damaged uniforms. They sat down and ordered and then nobody said anything for five full minutes. Thor poked at his pita with the focused uncertainty of someone encountering the concept of a wrap for the first time. Tony had a bandage on his jaw. Steve sat straight because his back didn't know how to do anything else. Eventually someone reached for something, and then someone else did, and then they were eating, and it was fine. It was, quietly, the beginning of something.

Tony cooked breakfast on Sunday mornings in the Avengers compound kitchen and was extremely particular about it. He had opinions about pans — which pan for eggs, which temperature, whether people were waiting correctly — and shared them freely. The results were genuinely excellent, which made it hard to argue. Thor ate three portions of everything with sincere gratitude and occasionally asked if this was culinary magic, which Tony accepted as a compliment. Steve sat at the counter with his sketchbook and drew the scene, because he drew everything.

Steve kept that sketchbook everywhere — the team, the city, things he'd missed in the decades he'd spent frozen. He was good. Natasha found one of his drawings of her once, looked at it a long time, handed it back, and said "keep going." Bruce had a spot on the compound lawn where the afternoon light came through the trees just right. On quiet days he went there with two books and came back calmer. Nobody interrupted the lawn spot.

Thor worked his way through Earth technology with complete seriousness, studying every manual and speaking to Siri as though she were a real person with a full schedule. Natasha drove out to Clint Barton's farm in Iowa and taught his children — Cooper, Lila, and Nathaniel — archery with her usual precise gravity. All three were excellent students, which she privately found very satisfying. Peter Parker called Aunt May every single day without exception, just to say he was safe, and the entire team knew about this and nobody teased him, because they all wished they had someone to call like that.

On summer evenings they gathered on the compound roof with Tony's waffles — perfected after several determined weeks and now unmistakably excellent — and watched the sun go down over the hills. Nobody talked much. The city glittered in the distance. Every person on that roof was exactly where they were supposed to be.
