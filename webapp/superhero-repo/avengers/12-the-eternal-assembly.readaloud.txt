Every great story needs an ending. But the story of the Avengers is a little different — because it does not truly end. It just keeps being passed along, like a lamp carried from hand to hand through a very long night.

The original six were an unlikely family. There was Steve Rogers, the soldier from another era. Tony Stark, the genius who was never quite as reckless as he seemed. Thor, the god who learned what it meant to be humble. Bruce Banner, the monster who chose to protect instead of destroy. Natasha Romanoff and Clint Barton, the spies who proved that you don't need a superpower to be a hero. Together, they were flawed and brave and occasionally very loud — and they saved the universe.

When they were gone or retired, the shield and the armor and the hammer did not disappear. They were passed on. Sam Wilson carried the vibranium shield and made it his own. New heroes — young people from Jersey City and from Wakanda and from places in between — began to find their footing. An archer passed his bow to a new student who was ready to carry it. The assembly was growing.

In New York and Sokovia and Wakanda, monuments stand with names carved in stone. People come to those places to be quiet and to remember. Children who grew up in the shadow of titans come and look at those names and feel something — a small, warm certainty that the world is worth protecting.

And that is how the story of the Avengers continues. Not with explosions and portals, but with a choice — the same choice Nick Fury believed in from the very beginning. A choice to step forward when it would be easier to step back. A choice to be remarkable.

And that is how the Eternal Assembly was born — not in a single moment, but in every brave choice ever made. Avengers Assemble. Now and forever.
