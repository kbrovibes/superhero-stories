There are moments in stories — and in the world — when everything that has been building, for years and years, finally arrives at once. When every thread of every story pulls tight at the same time. When the scattered pieces of a great puzzle fall into place.

This was that moment.

The Quantum Realm time-travel plan was called the Time Heist. Teams of heroes slipped back through time like stones skipping across a pond — to the Battle of New York in 2012, to the golden halls of Asgard, to the rocky planet Morag. Each team had one job: find a stone, borrow it gently, come home.

It was not always smooth. Tony and Steve accidentally sent the 2012 Loki flying through a hallway with the Space Stone and lost him. Thor ran into his mother Frigga in Asgard's palace and cried, and she held him and told him the future was not set in stone — a kindness that he needed more than any hammer could provide. Tony had a quiet conversation with a younger version of his father Howard in a stairwell — a man who did not yet know that his son would one day save the universe — and learned something about where courage comes from.

Natasha Romanoff and Clint Barton went to Vormir.

Vormir is a world of cold cliffs and dark sky where a hooded figure guards the Soul Stone. He told them the rule: the Soul Stone can only be claimed through sacrifice. A soul for a soul.

Natasha and Clint looked at each other. They had been friends for longer than almost anyone. They both reached for the same terrible choice, because both of them thought the other mattered more. They fought — not to hurt each other, but to be the one who could give the other this gift.

Natasha was faster. She was always faster.

She held Clint's hand for one last moment and smiled at him. Then she leaped.

Clint came home alone, fist clenched around an orange stone, unable to speak.

Bruce Banner — Smart Hulk, calm and enormous — put on the new gauntlet that Tony and he had built together. Red and gold, like Iron Man's armor. Six stones glowing in a row: blue, yellow, red, purple, green, orange. He closed his fist.

The energy nearly destroyed him. It traveled up his arm like fire, and he held on, because that is what heroes do when holding on is the hardest possible thing. He snapped his fingers.

Nothing seemed to happen.

And then, from the quantum tunnels, from the ripples in reality, from every corner of a rebuilt world — people came back. Quietly. In their beds, on their streets, in their fields. Half of all life, returned.

But past-Thanos had been watching through Nebula's memories. He knew what they had done. He knew what they were trying to do. And he sent his warship.

BOOM. The Avengers Compound exploded. Rocket fire and stone and glass, a whole beautiful building crumbling into rubble in seconds. Tony crawled out of the dust. Steve crawled out. Thor crawled out. They stood in the ruins and looked up at Sanctuary II — Thanos's enormous warship — hovering in the grey sky.

Past-Thanos walked across the rubble toward them. Bigger than memory. Colder than space. He was carrying his double-edged sword and wearing no gauntlet, because this version of him had not yet collected the stones and didn't care — he had simply decided to destroy everything and start over.

Tony, Steve, and Thor. Three men against a god from the end of time.

Steve Rogers lifted his hammer. Mjolnir. Thor's hammer, which Steve had gotten to wobble years ago at a party, which he had lifted in Wakanda when everything was falling apart. He raised it and lightning poured down from the clouds through his arm and his shield and his voice roared across the battlefield. He was worthy. He always had been.

They held on. Barely. Thanos called his full army — Chitauri, Outriders, Black Order, enormous warships filling the sky from horizon to horizon — and even the three greatest fighters on Earth began to fall back.

Steve Rogers stood alone with a broken shield and a battered suit, facing the entire army, and he tightened his grip on the straps. Because Captain America does not step back. Not ever.

Then a hundred golden rings of light tore open across the sky.

Doctor Strange and the sorcerers of Kamar-Taj. King T'Challa and the armies of Wakanda. The Guardians of the Galaxy. Pepper Potts in her Rescue armor. Sam Wilson swooping on red wings. Wanda Maximoff with her hands alight. Scott Lang, enormous as a building, stepping over skyscrapers.

And the people who had been gone for five years — dusty and confused and blazing with life — pouring through every portal.

Steve Rogers breathed.

He pressed the comms button.

"Avengers," he said, the word quiet and enormous all at once.

"Assemble."

Two armies ran toward each other across a ruined field, and the sound they made was the loudest, most extraordinary sound the world had ever heard.

Captain Marvel punched through Sanctuary II and it fell from the sky in pieces. Wanda Maximoff cornered Thanos alone and for a moment made even him step back. Spider-Man held the gauntlet above his head, swinging it from web to web across the battlefield with the whole universe's armies trying to take it from him.

And then Thanos caught it.

He raised the stones above his head. He was going to snap everything away — everything, and start over, and this time build a universe that would thank him.

Tony Stark reached out with one gauntlet-covered hand. The nanobots moved faster than thought. Six stones, four colors, the weight of everything — into his hand.

Tony Stark looked at Thanos across the smoke.

"I am Iron Man," he said.

And he snapped his fingers.

Thanos and every soldier of his army — every Outrider, every Chitauri, every ship — turned to ash and drifted away on the wind. The sky cleared.

The battlefield went quiet.

Tony Stark lay very still.

The hardest, bravest thing anyone had ever done — he had done it. And now he rested. All around him were the people he had fought to protect, the world he had held together with his hands and his genius and his stubbornness and his love.

He had done it.

We love you, Tony. Three thousand.
