Long before the Avengers ever assembled, on a distant purple moon called Titan, a child was born to a family of the Eternals. His name was Thanos, and he was different from the rest of his people. While his planet bloomed with cities and gardens, Thanos saw a darker future approaching. Titan’s population grew faster than its resources, and famine, disease, and war began to creep into every corner of his world. He pleaded with the leaders to take a hard, painful path — to choose at random and reduce the population by half so that the rest could survive. The council called him mad and banished his idea. Years later, Titan was a silent, dust-blown ruin, and Thanos was the only one who remembered what it had once been.

That memory hardened him into something dangerous. He left Titan in a starship and traveled the cosmos with a single, terrible conviction — that he had been right, and that someone had to do for the universe what no one had done for his home. Across hundreds of planets, Thanos and his loyal army, the Black Order, arrived without warning. On worlds like Zen-Whoberi, Praxius IX, and the icy moons of Aakon, he gathered the people and divided them into two halves with a flip of a coin. One half went free. The other half he took away. He believed, in his cold, certain way, that he was an act of mercy.

It was on Zen-Whoberi that Thanos found a small green-skinned girl named Gamora. Her parents were among the half he had ended, and yet he lifted her up and called her his daughter. He taught her to fight, gave her blades that could cut starlight, and trained her to be the deadliest woman in the galaxy. He did the same with Nebula, a blue-and-silver child from another conquered world, rebuilding her body piece by piece every time she lost a sparring match. Thanos called this love. The girls, in their quiet hearts, called it something else.

For all his cruelty, Thanos did not act out of joy. He had no palace, no riches, no throne. He lived on a barge ship at the edge of the galaxy, in a wooden hut he had built with his own hands. He looked at the stars the way an old farmer looks at a tired field, certain that the universe needed a harvest. Somewhere in the dark, six small stones — older than the planets themselves — were waiting. And Thanos, the Mad Titan of dead Titan, was the only being patient enough, strong enough, and convinced enough to gather every one of them.
