The distress signal came from a mining station in the outer rings of the Andromeda passage on a Nova Corps frequency that should have been secure, which meant it was probably a trap. Gamora knew that frequency. She had used it herself once, when she was still working for Thanos and needed to draw a Nova patrol into an ambush. She said as much to the team. Peter said probably a trap. Drax asked what kind. Rocket checked the scanner.

It was Nebula. The signal was coming from an emergency capsule on the station's outer dock, and when they pulled it aboard, the capsule's occupant was blue, half-mechanical, and in worse shape than anyone had seen her — even accounting for the fact that Nebula's condition was always difficult to assess given how much of her was replaced parts. Thanos had recalled her after the failure on Xandar. The meeting had not gone well. She had not been punished in ways that were simple.

Gamora stood at the capsule door for a long time before she opened it. The rest of the team stood back, understanding that this moment belonged to something older than their own friendships.

Inside the capsule, Nebula was conscious and cold and did not say anything that could be repeated in polite company. She told Gamora she did not need help. She told Rocket not to look at her. She told Drax, who had moved toward her with what he considered a welcoming expression, to stop looking at her. She asked Peter, in a voice that was entirely flat, who had authorized him to pull her capsule.

"You did," Peter said. "You sent the signal."

Nebula was quiet for a moment. "That was a weak moment," she said.

"We all have them," Peter said.

The recovery was slow and uncomfortable and full of silences that had sharp edges. Rocket repaired the mechanical components that needed repairing, with commentary that everyone told him to stop and that he was constitutionally unable to stop. Groot brought Nebula water without being asked, setting it quietly beside her and retreating before she could object. Drax sat near her and said nothing for three hours, which was his way of saying she was welcome.

Gamora waited until the third day, when the Milano was running quietly through the dark and everyone else had found their sleep. She sat beside her sister and said, in a voice that was just between them: "You don't have to stay. But you can." Nebula did not answer. But in the morning she was still there, and she was wearing a clean jacket from the Milano's storage that someone — nobody would say who — had left outside her bunk.
