Are you comfortable? Good. Tuck in a little closer, because this story starts a long, long way from here — on the other side of the galaxy, in fact — and you will want to follow every step.

Once there was a boy named Peter Quill who grew up among the stars, even though his heart always kept a small, quiet room for Earth. His mother had given him a Walkman and a mixtape before she died, and Peter played it so many times the tape had gone soft and warm from love. He called himself Star-Lord, which made most people smile but which Peter said with complete seriousness, so you had to respect that about him at least.

Peter was what people in the galaxy called a Ravager — a scavenger and sometimes a thief. One day, on a dead orange planet called Morag, he danced through ancient ruins — truly danced, headphones on, arms out, voice echoing off stone walls — and he picked up a small silver Orb that had been sitting on its pedestal for centuries, waiting for the wrong person to find it.

He did not know what was inside the Orb. He only knew someone was paying well for it.

The trouble was, everyone else wanted it too.

On the bright, clean planet Xandar — home of the Nova Corps, the galaxy's peacekeepers, with their tall ships and their golden uniforms — a chase broke out that was really four chases at once. A green-skinned woman named Gamora, who moved like water and fought like a storm, tried to take the Orb by force. A small raccoon named Rocket, with patched fur and bright angry eyes and a blaster almost as tall as himself, tried to collect the bounty on Peter's head. And Rocket's best friend Groot — a gentle giant made of wood and bark and something that felt very like kindness — helped by simply picking everyone up.

The Nova Corps arrested them all.

And that is how four very different people ended up in the same prison cell in the Kyln, the most secure prison in the galaxy.

The fifth was already there, waiting.

His name was Drax, and he was enormous and tattooed from neck to ankle and very, very serious about everything. He had lost his family to the cruelty of a Kree warlord named Ronan, and he carried that loss like armor he never took off. Drax did not understand jokes. Drax did not understand figures of speech. If you said "I'm dying of laughter," Drax would ask if you needed a healer. But he was honest and loyal in a way that was beautiful, if you knew how to look for it.

Rocket looked at the Kyln and drew a plan on a napkin. It involved a prosthetic leg, a security tower, and precise timing. "What are the odds this works?" Peter asked. Rocket twitched his nose. "Terrible," he said. "Let's go."

What happened next was loud and fast and a little bit accidental. Groot started early. An alarm went off. Peter grabbed a guard's arm. Gamora found a door nobody knew existed. Drax held up a ceiling. Rocket laughed — a sharp, bright sound like a wire snapping — and called out coordinates over the noise.

They reached the Milano in the docking bay. Peter's ship: battered, paint peeling, cushions worn flat, and more beloved than almost anything in his world. They piled inside, Groot folding himself carefully through the hatch, and the Milano shot out of the Kyln and into the dark, open quiet of space.

Five strangers. One ship. An Orb full of terrible power.

For a while nobody spoke. Rocket cleaned his blaster. Gamora stared out the viewport. Drax polished one of his knives. Groot grew a small flower from the tip of one finger and offered it to no one in particular.

Peter put on the Walkman and pressed play.

"I am Groot," said Groot, very softly, as if the music made sense to him.

And something in that — the flower, the music, the small warm moment in all that cold dark — felt like the first quiet note of something that was going to grow into something very important.

They had not meant to find each other. But there they were, all the same.

Some families choose each other. Some just crash into each other on a distant planet and end up sharing a ship. Both kinds count.

Close your eyes now. Somewhere out there, a mixtape is playing, and five misfits are flying home together.
