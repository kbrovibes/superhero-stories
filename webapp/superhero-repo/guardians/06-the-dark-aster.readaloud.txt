Let me tell you something about the Dark Aster. It was covered — top to bottom — in old Kree war-scripture. Not because it needed to be. Because Ronan wanted you to know, the second you saw it, that you were looking at something that had never, in its entire existence, been in doubt about itself.

It was a city that flew. And flying the Milano into its shadow felt like flying into a thundercloud made of iron.

Peter turned up the Walkman. BOOM BOOM BOOM — the bass line kicked in, and somehow that helped.

The team split as soon as they were inside.

Gamora: bridge. She had walked these corridors before and she moved fast and certain, like water finding a crack. Drax: find Korath. He had a list of names and he was methodical about it. Rocket and Peter: the power coupling, the thing that kept the Stone's energy fields stable. Groot: everywhere else, silently, reaching down corridors, tripping pursuers, holding doors open and then closed with equal calm.

CRASH. Gamora turned a corner and found Nebula.

They stood for a second. Two sisters trained by the same teacher to fight the same way. And then Nebula reached for her weapon and the corridor lit up with the speed of it — blade against sword, fist against arm, old hurt against old hurt. Gamora fought like she was settling a debt she had carried for years. She won. Barely. Nebula fell through a grating and landed on a Ravager ship passing below and was gone into the battle, escaping to fight another day.

Gamora didn't stop. She kept moving.

Then Rocket did the thing.

He pushed his mining vessel up under the Dark Aster's belly for the second time that day — THOOM — blew through the engines, and the whole giant warship shuddered and went dark. No thrust. No engines. Just gravity.

The Dark Aster was falling.

Peter reached the bridge. Ronan lifted his hammer. Peter grabbed the Stone and the purple fire hit him like a wall and he was going to die and then — Gamora's hand on his arm. The fire split. Then Drax. Then Rocket, already running, already angry, already unwilling to lose another thing today.

Four people, one terrible power, shared until it was survivable.

The fire went outward. Ronan: gone.

Below on Xandar, dust settled on broken streets. People came out of shelters and looked up at the sky, where a warship used to be.

Four Guardians stood in the wreckage. Dusty. Quiet. Together.

Not one of them had made it through alone.

That was the lesson. That was always going to be the lesson.
