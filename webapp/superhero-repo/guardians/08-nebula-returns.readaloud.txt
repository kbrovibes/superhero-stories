A distress signal. Secure Nova Corps frequency. Coming from an outer-ring mining station that had been abandoned for years.

"Probably a trap," Gamora said. Immediately. Without hesitation.

"One hundred percent a trap," Peter agreed just as fast.

"What kind of trap?" Drax asked. He always liked to have this information before he arrived somewhere.

Rocket had pulled up the scanner. "One life sign. Emergency capsule on the outer dock. Single occupant. Whoever it is, they've been in there a while and they are not doing well."

Gamora was quiet. She knew that frequency. She had used it herself once, before she left Thanos — to pull an enemy patrol into a trap. She had already worked out whose capsule this was.

"Pull it aboard," she said.

Inside the capsule: Nebula. Blue. Mechanical. In far worse shape than you would have thought — although with Nebula, assessing condition was always complicated by how much of her was already replaced parts. Thanos had recalled her after the failure at Xandar. That meeting had clearly not gone well.

The team stepped back from the capsule hatch without discussing it. This was older than their story — a story between sisters that had started long before any of the rest of them arrived.

Nebula's eyes opened. "Where am I."

Gamora: "Safe."

Nebula: "I don't need this."

Gamora: "You sent the signal."

Nebula: "That was a moment of weakness."

Peter, quietly: "We all have them."

Rocket examined her arm. Some mechanical repairs were fusing wrong — the kind of error that gets worse the longer you ignore it. He started fixing them. She said stop. He kept going. Eventually she let him finish, the way you let someone do necessary work while making your feelings completely clear.

Groot brought her water. Set a cup beside her hand without meeting her eyes, then drifted away. She didn't drink it. An hour later: another cup. Then a third. On the third she drank it. Groot happened to be studying the ceiling.

Drax sat nearby and said nothing for three hours. For someone who always said exactly what he thought the moment he thought it, this was extraordinary restraint — and his way of saying: you are welcome, no strings attached.

On the third night, the ship running quiet, Gamora sat beside her sister.

"You don't have to stay," she said. "But there is room. And you can."

Nebula didn't answer.

In the morning she was still aboard the Milano. Someone had left a clean jacket folded outside her bunk — warm dark color, the right size, placed neatly. Nobody claimed credit. Everybody knew.

She put it on.

Some invitations leave something warm outside and wait to see if you reach for it.
