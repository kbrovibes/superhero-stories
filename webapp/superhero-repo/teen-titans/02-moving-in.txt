Titans Tower had twelve floors, a common room with a couch the size of a small boat, and a refrigerator that Cyborg immediately claimed as his personal project. The first week inside the T-shaped building was less like heroic headquarters and more like five strangers figuring out how to share a kitchen.

Victor Stone spent two full days rewiring the tower's security system. The original design was adequate, he explained to anyone who would listen. He was going to make it extraordinary. Sensor grids, biometric locks on every exterior door, an emergency beacon that could reach every communicator in Jump City. He worked through the night with his sonic-cannon arm folded carefully against his chest and a soldering iron in the other. When he finished, he crossed his arms and said simply, "Now we're safe."

Starfire decorated her room with photographs she had taken of every interesting thing she had seen since arriving on Earth: a bus stop, a grocery store, a fire hydrant, a dog wearing a sweater. She hung them carefully with tape she had asked Raven to explain, because on Tamaran pictures were displayed differently. She also filled a windowsill with small potted plants. The plants were mostly confused by the altitude.

Raven chose a room at the top of the tower and spoke very little about it. She shelved her books — there were many books — and placed a single candle on the windowsill that she lit each morning when she meditated. The others learned quickly that knocking before entering Raven's room was not a suggestion.

Robin mapped the tower on his first night, memorizing exits, corners, and sightlines out of pure habit. He set up a mission room on the fourth floor with maps of Jump City pinned to the wall and a whiteboard already covered in notes about Slade's last known movements. He told himself it wasn't obsessive. It was preparation.

Beast Boy immediately lost his room key, borrowed a screwdriver from Cyborg, and accidentally turned into a golden retriever halfway through fixing the lock. He barked once, surprised, then changed back. Cyborg made him a replacement key and attached a small green paw-print tag so it would be easy to find.

By the end of the week, the common room smelled like Starfire's cooking experiments, Cyborg's music hummed through the walls on Tuesday evenings, and there were stickers shaped like green animals on the underside of the kitchen table that nobody admitted to putting there. Raven's candle light flickered warmly behind her door each morning.

It was noisy and a little chaotic. It felt, unexpectedly, like home.
