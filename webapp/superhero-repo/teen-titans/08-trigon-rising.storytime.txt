Settle in, little one. This is a story about the bravest kind of trust — trusting people enough to let them help you carry something heavy.

Raven never talked about her father. The others had understood this without being told, though none of them had empathic powers — some things you can simply feel from the way a person carries themselves, and the wall Raven had built around the subject of Trigon was one of those things. His name appeared once in a mission briefing during their second month at Titans Tower. Raven left the room so quietly that it took a full beat before anyone realized she was gone. Robin started to follow. Cyborg put a hand on his arm. Robin stopped. Nobody pressed. Nobody called after her. The briefing continued.

They learned to read the shape of what she needed, even when she said nothing.

So when the sky above Jump City turned the color of a dying coal fire — deep, burning red, the light of another world bleeding through the barrier between dimensions — the Titans understood something enormous and important: this was different.

Trigon was Raven's father — an ancient being from a dark dimension who had been looking for a way into this world for a very long time. The connection he had found was the one that already existed: the thread of Raven's own empathic powers. Every time she used them to push back, the connection grew stronger. Her gift was the thing he was pulling on. She had to be precise and patient and hold a very thin line.

Four enormous red eyes appeared in the clouds above Jump City. Shadow-creatures clawed at the city's walls. The buildings around Titans Tower warped at their edges.

Raven stood on the roof and closed her eyes. Her dark energy blazed violet around her.

"I need forty minutes," she said. "Please keep the city from falling apart."

Nobody asked questions. Nobody needed to.

Starfire flew faster than she usually let herself, her starbolts blazing gold and scattering shadow-creatures from every rooftop she passed. Cyborg broadcast counter-signals on every frequency he had, slowing the warping of the city's streets block by block. Beast Boy became a gorilla and dismantled shadow structures at the waterfront before they could collapse into the streets — quietly, methodically, because he understood what this moment was. Robin stood at ground level, reading Trigon's pattern in the distortions and directing each Titan toward the next critical point. He didn't stop for forty minutes.

On the roof, Raven held.

Something enormous pressed against her and she pressed back, precise and patient, for forty minutes. Below her, the city held too — because her team was holding it. She could feel them through the empathic thread that always connected her to the people she lived with: Starfire's fierce brightness, Cyborg's steady focus, Beast Boy's quiet effort, Robin's unwavering attention. She was not alone.

She held.

And then — like a door slamming shut from the inside — Trigon was gone. The red drained from the sky. The shadow-creatures dissolved. His four enormous eyes blinked out, one after another, and the clouds above Jump City were just clouds. The city's lights came back on block by block as the grid restored. Jump City was quiet and intact and itself again.

Raven sat down on the roof of the tower. She was very tired, the kind of tired that lives in the bones. The others came up one at a time and sat beside her without ceremony — no announcements, no speeches, just arriving and sitting down, the way you do with people you trust.

No one spoke for a while.

"I'm sorry," Raven said at last. "That I never told you."

Cyborg considered this for a moment. "You told us what mattered," he said. "You said you needed time. We gave it to you."

Starfire sat close against Raven's side, warm and steady. Beast Boy sat cross-legged on the roof and looked at the sky and for once didn't say anything, which was its own kind of eloquence. Robin looked out at the city — intact, its lights coming back on block by block as the grid restored — and nodded once.

That was what they were for.

Sleep softly, little one. The people who show up for you without questions — those are the ones worth holding on to.
