The city was quiet on a Friday evening for the first time in three weeks, and Beast Boy declared it a pizza night. Nobody argued with this. Not even Raven.

They ordered from a place in Jump City called Chico's — four large pizzas, Cyborg's minimum — and arranged themselves in the common room with the comfortable disorder that comes from everyone having found their usual spot. Cyborg took the left side of the big couch. Starfire sat upright in the armchair by the window, pizza held at the altitude she had decided was optimal. Raven chose the floor with a book and one slice. Beast Boy draped himself over the couch like a decorative throw that someone had given a personality.

Robin ate standing up, which was one of his habits that the others had mostly stopped trying to cure.

The evening had no agenda. They watched a film that Beast Boy had chosen, which involved a sentient dog who solved mysteries, and Starfire asked forty-seven questions about it, which Beast Boy answered with increasing creativity and decreasing accuracy. Cyborg told Beast Boy his descriptions of how cars worked in the film were completely wrong. Beast Boy said his version was more interesting. Cyborg said interesting was not the same as correct. Raven, without looking up from her book, said, "He's right. It is more interesting." Beast Boy made a sound of vindication that annoyed Cyborg and delighted Starfire.

At some point, without anyone deciding to, the film finished and nobody turned it off and nobody started another one, and the room settled into a comfortable kind of quiet. Starfire told a story about a festival on Tamaran called the Blorthog — a day when Tamaraneans celebrated their closest companions. You brought them gifts and told them what they meant to you. It was considered one of the most serious days of the year.

"We should do that," Beast Boy said immediately.

"Now?" said Robin.

"The Blorthog is usually in spring," Starfire said, "but I believe the spirit of the thing is more important than the season."

There was a small, easy silence.

"Thanks for the pizza, Beast Boy," Cyborg said.

"Thanks for fixing my communicator fourteen times," Beast Boy said.

Raven put her book down. "Thanks for staying," she said. She did not specify to whom she was speaking. She meant all of them.

Outside the tower windows, Jump City glittered below in the warm dark. The team sat together — a Tamaranean princess, an empathic half-demon, a green shapeshifter, a brilliant half-machine, and a boy who had once been alone — in a place that belonged to all of them equally, and let the quiet be what it was.
