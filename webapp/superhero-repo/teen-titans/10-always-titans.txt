After a year together, Jump City knew the Teen Titans by sight. Citizens watched for Starfire's gold glow when the nights turned strange. They heard Cyborg's sonic cannon and knew the harbor problem was handled. When something small and fast ran across the rooftops at two in the morning, they knew it was Robin, and they went back to sleep.

Slade had returned four more times in the year since leaving that orange coin on Robin's windowsill, each attempt more intricate than the last, each one defeated — barely, sometimes — by the five of them working with a fluency that only comes from shared effort and shared failure. Robin had stopped thinking about Slade as a puzzle to be solved and started thinking of him as a reminder: the team had to keep getting better.

They did. Not because they were afraid of Slade, but because they were proud of each other.

There was a night in autumn when a fire broke out in a warehouse in the Jump City port district — not a mission, just a fire, the kind of accident that cities have — and the Titans arrived because they were the first ones there. Cyborg interfaced directly with the city's fire suppression system and activated it from six blocks away. Starfire carried three dockworkers out from a burning storage floor in a single pass. Raven contained a collapsing section of the roof with her dark energy while firefighters moved below it. Beast Boy became a Saint Bernard — it seemed right — and moved through the smoke, finding the two workers still inside, then became a dolphin in the harbor to help one of them stay afloat. Robin coordinated the evacuation on the ground until the fire department arrived.

Nobody was hurt. Nobody had asked them to come. They came because Jump City was their city, and that was reason enough.

In the T-car afterward, driving home through quiet streets, Beast Boy said: "Were we, like... always going to become this? Like, was it obvious from the beginning?"

Cyborg thought about it. "No," he said. "I don't think it was obvious."

"I did not know what we would become," Starfire said honestly. "I only knew I would try."

Raven was quiet. Then: "I think we became it because we kept showing up."

Robin drove. He was quiet for a block or two, and then he said: "Yeah. That's it."

The tower was lit up across the harbor, the letter T glowing against the dark sky, as it did every night — a signal, a landmark, a home. Five young heroes drove toward it together, the city quiet around them.

They were, and would always be, the Teen Titans.
