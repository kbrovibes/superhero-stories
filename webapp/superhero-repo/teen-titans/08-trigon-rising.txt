Raven never spoke about her father. The others had learned, in their first months together, that this was a hard boundary — not hidden, exactly, but sealed. The name Trigon appeared once in a mission briefing and Raven left the room. Nobody pressed.

So when Trigon reached into Jump City from his inter-dimensional prison and wrapped the city in a deep red energy field — warping buildings, clouding every sky, his four red eyes visible in the clouds above — the Titans understood immediately that this was different from every fight they had been in.

Raven was the center of it. Her father was using their dimensional connection as an anchor, pulling himself through. Every time Raven reached for her power to resist, the connection strengthened. The very gift that made her Raven was the thing he was using against her.

She stood on the roof of Titans Tower with her eyes closed and said, "I need time. Keep the city from falling apart."

They did.

Starfire flew through the warped skyline of Jump City, her starbolts cutting through Trigon's shadow-creatures — formless dark shapes that clawed at buildings — and drove them back over and over, her golden light holding steady against the red sky. Cyborg broadcast a counter-signal on every frequency he had to disrupt the dimensional distortion across the city's electrical grid, slowing the warping of the streets. Beast Boy became a whale and then immediately reconsidered — too many buildings — and switched to a massive gorilla, physically dismantling a shadow structure that was collapsing onto the waterfront. Robin directed all three of them from the ground, reading Trigon's pattern in the city's distortions and routing each Titan to where they were most needed each minute.

It took forty minutes. The roof of Titans Tower burned hot with Raven's dark energy, violet and absolute, pressing back against the red force with her own will. She did not call for help. She just needed the time.

The city held. The Titans held the city. Raven held the barrier.

And then — like a door slamming shut — the red left the sky. The shadow-creatures dissolved. Trigon's eyes blinked out of the clouds one by one and then were gone.

Raven sat on the roof and did not say anything for a long time. The others came up one at a time and sat with her.

"I'm sorry," she said eventually. "That I never told you."

"You told us what mattered," Cyborg said. "You said you needed time. We gave it to you."

Starfire sat close beside her. Beast Boy, for once, said nothing at all. Robin looked out at Jump City, quiet and intact, and nodded once.

That was what they were for.
