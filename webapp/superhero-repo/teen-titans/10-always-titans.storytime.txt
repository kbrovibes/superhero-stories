Are you comfortable? This is the last story in our collection, and it is a quiet one. It is about what happens after everything — after the missions and the arguments and the villains and the pizza nights — when you look around and realize what you have become.

After one full year together, Jump City knew the Teen Titans.

Citizens watched for Starfire's golden glow when things felt strange. They heard Cyborg's sonic cannon in the harbor and let their shoulders drop — it was being handled. When a fast shape crossed the rooftops at two in the morning, people glanced up, saw Robin's silhouette, and went back to sleep.

That kind of trust takes a year to earn. They had earned it.

Slade had returned four more times. Patient, persistent, clever — each attempt more intricate than the last, each one defeated. The team had grown in the spaces between challenges into something harder to unsettle. Robin had stopped thinking of Slade as a puzzle to solve and started thinking of him as a reason to keep getting better. They did.

And then there was an autumn night when none of that mattered — when the city needed them not because of a scheme or a plot, but simply because a warehouse in the Jump City port district caught fire. An accident. A real, ordinary emergency, with real people inside.

The Titans were the closest. They went.

Cyborg interfaced with the city's fire suppression network from six blocks away and activated it before the fire trucks had turned out of their station — a single quiet command and the water lines pressurized across the entire district. Starfire made three passes over the burning storage level and carried the dockworkers out through the heat and smoke in her arms, one and two at a time, setting them carefully on the dock below. Raven found a section of roof beginning to collapse and held it suspended in place with her dark energy — still and level and patient — while the firefighters moved beneath it in safety. Beast Boy went in as a Saint Bernard, moving through the smoke with a dog's extraordinary nose, finding the last two workers the fire had swallowed up. He brought them both to the exit, then turned into a dolphin in the harbor when one of them went over the side, and stayed with him until the rescue boat arrived. Robin stood at the edge of the lot and directed every piece of it from the ground, watching the whole picture, routing each Titan toward the next moment that needed them.

Nobody was hurt. Nobody had told them to go. Nobody had filed a mission report or designated a team leader for the occasion. They had gone because it was their city and these were their people and it was simply what you did.

In the T-car on the way home, driving through the quiet autumn streets with the city lights going by outside, Beast Boy was uncharacteristically quiet for a while. Then:

"Were we always going to become this? Like — was it always going to be us?"

Cyborg was quiet for a moment. "No," he said finally. "I don't think that was obvious, starting out."

"I did not know what we would become," Starfire said. She meant it honestly, with no sadness in it. "I only knew I would try."

Raven watched the lights pass outside the window. "I think we became it because we kept showing up," she said. "Every time. Even when it was hard. Especially then."

Robin drove for a full block. The harbor came into view ahead of them.

"Yeah," he said. "That's it."

Titans Tower was lit up across the water, the giant letter T glowing steadily against the dark sky as it did every night — a signal, a landmark, a home. It said to the city: someone is here. They are watching. You don't have to face it alone.

Five young heroes drove toward it together through the warm autumn dark. A Tamaranean princess who had crossed the stars to find a family. A half-machine boy who had rebuilt himself from the hardest moment of his life. A careful empath who had learned to let people in. A green shapeshifter who loved his team so much he turned it into jokes so it wouldn't overwhelm him. And a boy who had once been very alone, who had written four letters on a Tuesday and meant every word.

They were the Teen Titans.

They always would be.

Sleep well, little one. A family isn't always the one you start with. Sometimes it's the one you build, one shared choice at a time.
