After Eddie Brock gave up the Venom symbiote, the black goo did not simply disappear. It found a new host — a man named Mac Gargan, who had once been the villain called the Scorpion. Now Mac wore the Venom symbiote, and he used it not to protect anyone, but to frighten and to take. He was bigger and meaner than ever, prowling New York City as a brand-new Venom.

Eddie, of course, could not stand this. That black goo had been part of his own life for years. He knew it better than anyone. And now, as Anti-Venom, he had the one power in all the world that the symbiote truly feared: a touch that could dissolve it.

They met during dark and stormy days that people later called "New Ways to Die." Spider-Man was already fighting a whole team of villains hired to hunt him down, and Mac Gargan's Venom was the biggest and hungriest of them all. When Anti-Venom arrived, everything changed. He did not want to hurt Mac. He wanted to free him. Eddie had a strange, fierce hope: if he could peel the symbiote away, he could give Mac Gargan a second chance, the same way Martin Li had once given one to him.

The battle crashed across rooftops and alleys — CRUNCH, THWIP, BOOM! Mac's black tendrils lashed and grabbed, but wherever they touched Anti-Venom's white skin, the goo sizzled and shrank back. Eddie waded straight through the black symbiote's attacks, his white antibodies burning it away layer by layer. The Venom symbiote shrieked. It was being cured, and it hated it.

"Hold still," Eddie growled. "I'm helping you, even if you don't want it." He clamped his healing hands onto Mac Gargan and pushed the white light deep. The black goo boiled and thinned and began peeling away from Mac's body like old paint, dissolving into nothing. For one shining moment, Eddie nearly washed the whole symbiote clean off.

But the fight was chaos, and the other villains pulled Mac free before Anti-Venom could finish. The black symbiote clung on, wounded and weak, and Mac escaped into the storm. Eddie stood panting in the rain, frustrated that he had not saved the man — but certain, now, of who he was. He was not a monster hunting monsters. He was a healer. And to Eddie Brock, even the scariest black goo in New York was not something to destroy. It was something to cure.
