Long before the great sacrifice of Spider-Island, Anti-Venom had made himself a promise about the kind of hero he would be. He would not chase fame in the bright center of the city. He would guard the forgotten corners — the alleys, the shelters, the quiet blocks where ordinary people lived and no other hero ever seemed to patrol. Eddie Brock called himself a Lethal Protector, and to him the second word mattered far more than the first. He was there to protect.

Night after night, he swung across the rooftops of New York City on strands of white webbing, watching over the people below. He stopped robbers before they could frighten a shopkeeper. He pulled a lost child out of the path of traffic. He lifted a fallen beam off a trapped worker with his tremendous strength, and when the worker was hurt, Eddie laid a healing hand on him too. Where other heroes fought and flew away, Anti-Venom often stayed a little longer, to make sure the people he saved were truly all right.

One evening, his patrol crossed paths with Spider-Man's. The two of them had a long and complicated history — once bitter rivals, then wary allies against Carnage. But New York is a big city with plenty of trouble to go around, and that night a gang of crooks had more firepower than either hero could handle alone. So they teamed up, the way they had before. Spider-Man's spider-sense warned them of every ambush, and Anti-Venom's strength smashed through every locked door. THWIP, CRASH, THUD — the crooks never stood a chance.

When it was over, the two of them perched on a water tower and looked out over the glittering skyline. Spider-Man admitted something he had been thinking for a while. "You know," he said, "you're not so bad to have around." Coming from the hero who had once feared Eddie most, it meant a great deal. Anti-Venom rumbled a low, pleased laugh. Two men who had spent years as enemies were slowly, surely becoming something like friends.

Eddie swung home alone that night, over the sleeping rooftops of the city he had chosen to guard. He thought about how strange his life had become — how the man everyone once feared now spent his nights making sure strangers got home safe. He was not a perfect hero. He knew that. But he was a faithful one. And for the people of New York, especially the ones nobody else remembered to watch over, a faithful protector was exactly what they needed.
