Once upon a time, on the rooftops of the great city of New York, a huge white figure went swinging through the night.

And if you had seen him for the very first time, dear one, you might have gasped. Because at a quick glance, he looked a little bit like Venom — big, and strong, with enormous eyes and a wide toothy grin.

But look again. Look closer.

Because where Venom is black, this creature is white. Where Venom is a shadow, this one is a light. He is the mirror-opposite of the monster he used to be. And every single part of the way he looks tells that wonderful story.

Let us look at him together, slowly, from top to bottom.

His skin is living goo — glossy and gleaming and white, like fresh fallen snow, like a smooth polished pearl. And it ripples and flows softly as he moves, because the symbiote is always, always alive. It is never quite still, the way the surface of a quiet pond is never quite still.

Threading across all that lovely white are swirling black tendrils and veins. They curl along his arms and his broad shoulders like the branches of a winter tree drawn in dark ink. They are beautiful, in their way.

And spread across his big chest is a black spider shape. Now here is the clever part. It is the very same spider emblem that Venom wears — but flipped, dear one, turned right around. Venom wears a white spider on black. Anti-Venom wears a black spider on white. Mirror-opposites, you see, even down to the smallest detail.

Two enormous oval eyes, pure white and rimmed all around in black, sit wide upon his face. And his grin is broad and toothy — yet somehow, in a way that is hard to explain, far friendlier than Venom's grin ever was.

But that white color, dear one, is not just for show.

It is the color of the healing Lightforce that made him. It is the sign of all the gentle antibodies swirling around inside him. It is like a bright flag that says, to anyone who understands, "I am a symbiote who cures, instead of one who harms."

And so, when frightened people first catch sight of how very big he is, they sometimes shrink back and tremble. But then — they notice. They see the light instead of the dark. And slowly, they begin to understand that this great white creature has come not to scare them at all, but to help them.

Oh, and the living goo can do the most remarkable things.

It can flow up to cover Eddie completely, or peel gently back to show his kind human face whenever he wishes to speak plainly with someone. It can stretch and shape itself, sprouting extra tendrils to catch a person falling from a ledge, or forming a smooth shield to turn away a blow. And it can shift its color to melt into a wall or a shadow, so that a creature as large as Anti-Venom can disappear as quietly as a whisper in a library.

But do you know why Eddie is proudest of the white suit, dear one?

It is not because it looks fearsome. No, no.

It is because the white suit is the truest picture of who he has become.

The old black Venom goo had always reflected his anger, and his hunger, and his need for revenge. It was a mirror of his darkest days. But the white Anti-Venom goo reflects the very opposite — his mercy, and his healing, and his precious second chance.

And so, when Eddie catches his own reflection in a darkened window as he swings past, he no longer sees a monster staring back at him.

He sees a guardian. A protector. A helper.

Dressed, from head to toe, in the color of the very light that once saved his life.

And that, dear one, is the most important thing of all. For the way we look on the outside can change, and the way others see us can change too — but what matters most is the person we choose to be on the inside. Eddie chose to be kind. And little by little, the whole world began to see it.

So close your eyes now, little one, and let that gentle picture fill your mind — a great white hero, gleaming softly under the stars, watching over the sleeping city below.

Goodnight, Anti-Venom. Goodnight, guardian in white. Sleep well.
