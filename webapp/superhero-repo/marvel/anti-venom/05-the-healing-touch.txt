Most superheroes are known for how hard they can hit. Anti-Venom is known for something rarer — how gently he can heal. Of all the gifts the white symbiote gave Eddie Brock, the most wonderful was this: his touch could make people well.

It worked because of the white antibodies swirling inside the symbiote. Antibodies are the tiny helpers your own body uses to fight off sickness, and Anti-Venom's were extraordinarily powerful. When Eddie laid his glowing white hands on a person, those antibodies flowed into them and went hunting. They could wash away a bad illness. They could pull poison out of the blood, rinse away harmful drugs, and chase off disease, leaving a person cleaner and healthier than before. It was, in a way, the same gift Martin Li had once given to Eddie — passed forward, hand to hand.

Eddie did not save this power for big, dramatic battles. He used it in the quiet corners of New York City, in places where no cameras were watching. In a rundown shelter, he pressed his palm to the forehead of a sick woman who could not afford a doctor, and by morning her fever had broken. In a dark alley, he found a man who had swallowed something poisonous, and Eddie's white light drew the poison out like a magnet pulling iron filings. The man coughed, blinked, and sat up, amazed to be alive.

Word of the strange white healer spread through the poorest parts of the city in whispers. Some people were frightened by his size and his enormous eyes. But those who let him help never forgot it. Eddie found that healing an ordinary person — someone the world had forgotten — felt even better than defeating a villain. There were no headlines. There was only a person who had been suffering, and then was not.

Of course, the gift had a limit, and Eddie learned it the hard way. The healing was not endless. Each time he cured someone, a little of his power drained away, like water poured from a pitcher. If he healed too many people too quickly, the white glow would flicker and run low, and he would have to rest before he could help again. That limit made every healing a choice, and every choice mattered. Eddie Brock, who had once been feared across the city, had become something New York had never quite seen before: a monster whose greatest weapon was mercy, and whose strongest power was the simple, patient act of making people well.
