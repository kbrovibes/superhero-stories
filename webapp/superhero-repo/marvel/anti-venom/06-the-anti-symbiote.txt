Every symbiote in the universe has a family. They come from a distant world, they bond to a host, and they share powers like super strength and wall-crawling and living webs. Venom is one. Carnage is one. But Anti-Venom is different, because Anti-Venom is the one symbiote that is the enemy of all the others. He is the antidote — the cure that every black symbiote fears.

Here is why. When Martin Li's healing Lightforce mixed with the traces of Venom in Eddie Brock's blood, it did something no one had ever seen. It filled the new white symbiote with antibodies designed to fight symbiotes themselves. To an ordinary black goo, Anti-Venom's touch is pure trouble. Where his white hands land, the black symbiote dissolves, hisses, and pulls away, unable to bear the very cure it was never built to survive. That is why Venom and Carnage, the two scariest symbiotes in New York City, both back away when Eddie comes near.

But being the anti-symbiote is not his only trick. Eddie kept all the powers the goo is famous for, too. He has tremendous strength, enough to bend a steel door or lift a fallen beam off a trapped person. He can crawl straight up the side of a skyscraper, sticking to glass and brick as easily as a spider. He fires strong white webbing from his hands — THWIP — to swing across the skyline or to snare a fleeing crook. And he can shape-shift his living goo, flattening and shifting its color to blend into a wall or a shadow, so that he seems to vanish right before your eyes.

All of that makes Anti-Venom a mighty hero. But even mighty heroes have limits, and Eddie's is important to remember. His greatest gift — the healing, the curing, the dissolving of other symbiotes — is not endless. Every time he uses it, the white power inside him runs a little lower, the way a flashlight dims as its battery drains. Cure too much, too fast, and the glow can sputter and fade, leaving Eddie tired and ordinary until he rests and it slowly refills.

So Anti-Venom must be clever. He cannot simply blast every problem with his cure the way another hero might throw endless punches. He has to choose his moments, save his strength, and use his gift where it counts the most. It makes him thoughtful. It makes him patient. And it makes his power feel less like a weapon and more like a treasure — something precious that must never be wasted.
