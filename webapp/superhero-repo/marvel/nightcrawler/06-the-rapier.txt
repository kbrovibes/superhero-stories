The rapier had belonged to Margali Szardos before it belonged to Kurt. She had pressed it into his twelve-year-old hands on a morning when the circus was camped beside the Rhine, and told him that a swashbuckler without a blade was only half a swashbuckler. Kurt had practiced with it every day since — before breakfast, after breakfast, in meadows, in parking lots, on the rooftops of buildings in various countries. He had become, quietly and without fanfare, one of the finest fencers in Western Europe.

The rapier was slender and balanced, with a swept hilt that caught the light when he turned it. It was not heavy enough to break a Sentinel or punch through a tank. But it was not for that. The rapier was for precision: disarming a foe without injuring them, cutting wires in tight spaces, catching a latch that was almost out of reach, and occasionally — in the theatrical tradition of every great swashbuckler — making an entrance that nobody forgot.

In a mission in Madrid involving a group of thieves who had broken into the Prado Museum, Kurt's rapier proved more useful than any of the X-Men's more spectacular powers. The thieves had rigged the museum's security grid so that any large energy discharge would bring the ceiling down on a room full of irreplaceable paintings. Wolverine's claws were too loud. Storm's lightning was too destructive. Kurt moved through the darkened galleries on his own, three-toed feet silent on the marble floors, teleporting past laser beams and disarming four different guards using the flat of his blade — quick BAMF-and-tap combinations that left each guard sitting in confused disarray, their weapons well out of reach, before they'd quite understood what had happened.

He recovered the stolen piece — a small Goya portrait of a woman in a red dress — and teleported it back to its proper place on the wall before the police arrived.

After the mission, Storm asked him why he still carried the rapier when he had abilities that seemed far more powerful. Kurt ran a cloth along the blade and considered the question. "Margali gave it to me," he said at last. "It reminds me where I came from. A circus performer learns early that every tool has its right moment, and that the most powerful instrument is not always the best one for the job at hand." He sheathed the rapier and smiled. "Also, honestly — it is a great deal of fun."

Storm laughed. Between the laughter and the warm lamplight of the debriefing room, the rapier gleamed on the table between them, telling both stories at once: the boy from the Bavarian circus, and the hero he had become.
