The new student arrived in October — a girl named Petra, eleven years old, from a town in rural Montana. Her mutation had appeared three days earlier: she could move through solid walls. She sat in the Xavier Institute's front hallway on her suitcase, arms crossed, and looked at Kurt Wagner with an expression that said clearly she did not trust him.

Kurt sat down on the floor a few feet away. "I understand that look," he said. "I wore it myself, the first time someone brought me somewhere new and expected gratitude before I'd understood what I was grateful for."

She said nothing.

"When I was close to your age," he continued, "a mob in a town near Stuttgart cornered me. I had done nothing. They were afraid. I stood with my back against a wall and did not know what to do. Then Professor Xavier arrived and said something I needed to hear, and everything changed." He paused. "I had no idea this school existed. I had no idea there were people who understood. I am glad someone told me. I think eventually you will be too."

Petra looked at him sideways. "You teleport?"

"BAMF," he agreed — and vanished in a small friendly puff of smoke, reappearing on the bench at the end of the hall. She flinched, then almost smiled.

That was the beginning. Over the following weeks, Kurt became her guide — not her teacher, but the person who understood the specific loneliness of a power that couldn't be turned off. He told her what the circus had taught him: an ability that seems like a liability, used with skill and clarity of purpose, becomes an asset. He told her about Margali Szardos, the mob in Stuttgart, and every X-Man who had looked at him clearly and chosen to stay.

He told her: the hardest thing isn't learning to use the power. It's learning you deserve to be in the room.

By November, Petra was eating breakfast with the other students. By December she was in the Danger Room. By spring she was one of the school's most capable young mutants, and her arms were no longer crossed, and she had stopped sitting on her suitcase.

On the last evening before winter break, she found Kurt in the chapel at the end of the east-wing corridor and sat beside him. After a while she said, "I'm glad someone told me too."

Kurt felt the gold light of the candle on the old wooden walls. Outside, the pine trees held the first snow of the season. Somewhere upstairs someone was burning the toast.

Everything was exactly as it should be.
