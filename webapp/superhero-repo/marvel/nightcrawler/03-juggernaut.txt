Cain Marko had never liked his stepbrother Charles Xavier. He was the Juggernaut — enormous, armored, powered by the Crimson Gem of Cyttorak — and he came crashing through the Westchester woods like a living battering ram. His helmet was bolted shut so that Professor Xavier's telepathy could not reach him. His armor was nearly indestructible. And once the Juggernaut started moving, nothing could stop him.

The X-Men learned this quickly. Storm's lightning bounced off his armor. Colossus tackled him head-on and was tossed aside like a tin can. Wolverine's claws scratched the surface of his breastplate and left barely a mark. The Juggernaut walked through everything, heading for the main building, heading for Charles Xavier.

Kurt stayed at the edge of the battle and thought. His power could not stop the Juggernaut directly. But it could go where other things could not. He noticed two things: the back seam of the helmet was its weakest point, and the Juggernaut could not turn his head quickly inside all that armor. His peripheral vision was nearly nothing.

He waited. The Juggernaut paused to shove Colossus out of his path — three distracted seconds — and Kurt teleported onto the giant's shoulders. He pressed his fingers into the back seam and braced his feet against the pauldrons. BAMF — he yanked the panel with the sudden force of the jump. Something creaked. One bolt sheared.

Three more attempts, rapid and precise. BAMF, grab, pull. BAMF, grab, pull. Each time wrenching the helmet further, bolts groaning with metallic protest. The Juggernaut roared and spun, hands sweeping for Kurt — but Kurt was never there when the hands arrived.

The fourth bolt gave. The helmet came loose. Kurt BAMF-ed to the fire escape twenty meters away and gripped the railing, breathing hard.

Professor Xavier reached out with his mind and found Cain Marko's thoughts — the anger, and the old hurt beneath it, and the loneliness underneath that. He spoke to all of it, calmly, without cruelty. The Juggernaut halted mid-step. He stood very still for a long moment. Then he turned and walked back into the woods.

Kurt descended from the fire escape. Wolverine put one rough hand on Kurt's shoulder — a brief, wordless thank-you. Kurt accepted it with a grin. The fight had been about cleverness and patience, not power. He had waited for exactly the right moment, exactly the right point of leverage.

The circus had taught him that much. A performer knows: you do not need to be the strongest thing in the ring. You only need to be the most precise.
