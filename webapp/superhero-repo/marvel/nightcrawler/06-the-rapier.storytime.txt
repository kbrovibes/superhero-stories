Once upon a time, on a morning when the circus of Margali Szardos was camped beside the Rhine river in Germany, twelve-year-old Kurt Wagner was sitting on the steps of the wagon watching the water go by.

The Rhine was silver that morning, moving slowly, carrying the reflections of clouds. The circus was quiet. Kurt was the only one awake. He liked mornings — the stillness before the day began.

He heard Margali's footsteps and looked up. She was standing in the doorway holding something wrapped in cloth. She sat down on the step beside him, unhurried.

Then she unwrapped the cloth.

Inside was a rapier — slender, beautiful, its blade a long gleam in the morning light. The swept hilt was twisted iron, dark with age. The grip was worn smooth by many hands that had held it well.

"This belonged to my teacher," Margali said. "And to her teacher before her." She held it out to Kurt. "A swashbuckler without a blade is only half a swashbuckler."

Kurt took it in both hands. It was lighter than he expected — balanced to the point that it seemed to want to be held.

From that morning he practiced every day. In the dewy meadows where the circus made its camps. In the gravel car parks outside small German towns, the blade swishing while the circus horses watched from their pens. Later, when he had joined the X-Men and traveled further, on rooftops in Madrid and New York in the grey hour before cities woke — the rapier tracing the same arcs, morning after morning after morning, until the movements were as natural as breathing. He became, quietly and without announcement, one of the finest fencers in Western Europe.

The X-Men knew he carried the rapier. Many of them wondered why — when he could teleport, when he had abilities that seemed so much more powerful, why bother with a blade?

They understood after Madrid.

Thieves had broken into the Prado Museum and stolen a small Francisco Goya painting: a portrait of a woman in a red dress, small and exquisite, precious to the people of Spain. But the thieves had rewired the museum's security grid so that any large energy discharge — lightning, adamantium claws — would collapse the ceiling onto four galleries of irreplaceable paintings.

Storm looked at her hands. Wolverine looked at his claws. They both looked at Kurt.

He entered through a high window — BAMF — landing in absolute silence on a balcony above the darkened main gallery. The amber emergency lights along the floor turned everything gold and shadow. He could see the laser grid strung across the corridor below: thin red lines, precisely spaced. He BAMFed past them without disturbing a single beam.

The first guard was stationed at the entrance to the Italian wing. He felt a gentle tap on his wrist — the flat of the rapier blade, placed precisely where Margali had once shown him to place it — and then he was sitting against the wall, both hands empty and extremely confused. Second guard, the same. Third. Fourth.

Not a single painting moved on its hook. Not a ceiling tile cracked.

In the back room of the east wing, Kurt found the Goya wrapped in padded cloth, propped carefully against the baseboard. He carried it with the reverential grip that a circus performer learns for delicate things. He found the exact nail on the exact wall where it belonged — he had memorized the position from the mission photograph — and he hung it level and true.

He was in the courtyard watching moths circle the streetlamps when the police arrived through the front entrance.

Afterward, Storm asked him: "With everything you can do — why do you still carry the rapier?"

Kurt ran a cloth along the blade and thought about the morning by the Rhine and Margali sitting beside him on the step.

"It connects me to where I came from," he said. "When I hold it I am still Margali's apprentice. I still know who I am." He turned the hilt slowly. "And she taught me what the circus teaches: every act has its right tool. The most powerful thing you own is not always the right one for the job."

He sheathed it with a soft click.

"Also," he said, "it is genuinely a great deal of fun."

Storm laughed, and the rapier lay on the table between them, glinting in the lamplight — a gift from a circus morning by the Rhine, a reminder of who he was, a tool that did what nothing else could do.

In Kurt Wagner's three-fingered hand: exactly right.
