The woman in the doorway of the Bavarian lodge looked completely ordinary — brown hair, grey coat, a rucksack. But Kurt Wagner felt something strange the moment he saw her. A pull, like his teleportation instinct firing without permission, pointing him toward something he couldn't name.

Her name was Raven Darkhölme, known to the world as Mystique — a mutant who could reshape herself into any person she chose. She had come to this remote German village because the X-Men were passing through and she needed help. The Brotherhood of Mutants had stolen a device from a Genosha research station, and she had intelligence about where it was held.

Her information proved genuine and precise. It led the X-Men to Genosha, where they recovered the device and prevented it from being used against civilians. During the operation, Kurt pulled three hostages clear of a collapsing structure — BAMF after BAMF until all three were safe in the courtyard, the dust still falling behind them.

Afterward, while the team regrouped, Mystique found Kurt alone in the rubble. She looked at him — and let her disguise slip. Her natural skin went indigo blue.

The same color as his own.

"You should know," she said, "that I am your mother."

Kurt stood very still. He had suspected it, somewhere beneath the things he didn't let himself think about directly. Two people with the same impossible skin, both from Germany. The arithmetic was not hard.

He looked at her for a long time. There was no anger. There was something more complicated — a quiet acknowledgment of what had never been possible, and a steady certainty about what was real.

"I know that you gave me up," he said at last. "I believe you had reasons. I do not need to understand all of them. Margali Szardos was my mother — she found me, she kept me, she loved me. That is true. It was true when I was small and it is true now."

Something shifted in Mystique's expression — a softening in the hard, guarded lines around her eyes.

Kurt extended his three-fingered hand. She looked at it for a moment, then shook it. Not forgiveness — he had nothing to forgive. Not reunion — they were strangers, and might remain so. It was something smaller and more honest: acknowledgment. Two people with the same remarkable skin, in the ruins of a Genoshan building, choosing decency.

Kurt returned to the X-Men. He did not feel lost. He felt more precisely himself than ever.
