En Sabah Nur — Apocalypse — was the oldest mutant in the world, and he had seized a communications tower in Cairo, Egypt. He was broadcasting a signal that would disrupt electronic systems across three continents. The X-Men flew south in the Blackbird, Professor Xavier's supersonic jet, and landed at dusk on Cairo's edge.

The tower was defended by automated energy cannons and four Horsemen — powerful mutants stationed at the tower's corners. Any direct approach would be met with overwhelming force. Cyclops gave the briefing: Storm and Cyclops would draw the Horsemen away from the north and west; Jean Grey would use her telepathy to confuse the human operators; and someone would slip through the cannon ring from the east.

Every eye found Kurt.

He studied the surveillance photos. The cannons tracked physical mass — but his Brimstone Dimension hops fell below the tracking threshold. He could thread through the ring. He said so and asked for nothing more.

The plan deployed at midnight. Storm called lightning on the north side. Cyclops hammered the south. Three Horsemen moved toward the noise. Jean Grey fogged the operators' awareness. Kurt went in — BAMF, BAMF, BAMF — short precise hops, each one threaded past a cannon arc, until he materialized in the signal room.

Apocalypse was waiting. Seven feet tall, armored, ancient, and entirely calm. He had sensed Kurt's arrival.

"You are brave, little acrobat," the ancient mutant said. "But bravery is insufficient."

Kurt stood still. He knew he could not fight Apocalypse alone. But he didn't need to win a fight. He needed ninety seconds for Storm to reach the roof array.

So he talked. He talked about Professor Xavier and the Xavier Institute, about the children there who had been afraid when they arrived, about what mutants could be to the world if the world would allow it. He didn't perform courage. He simply had it.

At seventy-eight seconds, Storm's lightning came through the tower's roof array and destroyed the broadcast equipment. Silence replaced the hum of the transmitters.

Apocalypse looked at the dead equipment, then at Kurt. Something in the ancient face shifted — almost respect. He withdrew without a word.

On the Blackbird flying home, the Mediterranean black below and stars close against the windows, Kurt was quiet. He thought about standing in a room with the most powerful mutant alive, armed with nothing but words.

The words had been enough. Sometimes that was the whole lesson.
