Every X-Men mission had a debrief, and every debrief that involved Kurt Wagner included the same question from new trainees: how does the teleportation actually work? Kurt had thought about this a great deal, and his answer always surprised people.

"I travel through a place called the Brimstone Dimension," he explained one evening in the Xavier Institute's briefing room, drawing a rough diagram on the chalkboard with his three-fingered hand. "It is another dimension — not this world, but not quite another world either. A sort of corridor between places. When I teleport, I step into the Brimstone Dimension for a fraction of a second, then step back out at a different location." The smoke, he added, was residue from that other dimension bleeding through. The cracking sound — BAMF — was displaced air.

The limits were real and worth understanding. Kurt could travel about three kilometers in one jump before the strain became dangerous. He needed to be able to see — or very precisely remember — his destination, because appearing inside a wall or a floor was not an outcome anyone wanted. He could carry passengers if he was in contact with them, but each additional person added significant effort. After eight or ten rapid teleports in succession, he became dizzy and needed to rest.

During a training exercise in the Danger Room, Kurt demonstrated the full range. He moved three colleagues out of a simulated collapse in under ten seconds. He materialized on the back of a training robot and disabled its sensor array before it knew he was there. He used short teleports inside a tight corridor to effectively fly, bouncing off walls and ceiling in a kind of three-dimensional pinball that left the combat simulation entirely confused.

What no training exercise covered was the part Kurt privately thought of as the real power: the courage to jump when you couldn't fully see where you were going. Every teleport required a leap of faith. You aimed, you trusted, and you moved. Doubt in the middle of a jump was the one thing that could make it go wrong.

Professor Xavier once told him that this was, in fact, the most useful lesson his power taught: that commitment and clarity were more important than certainty. Not knowing exactly what was on the other side did not mean you stayed where you were. It meant you paid attention, prepared carefully, and then leaped with your whole heart.

Kurt kept this thought folded up somewhere near the center of him. It served him well in every fight, and in every situation that was harder than a fight.
