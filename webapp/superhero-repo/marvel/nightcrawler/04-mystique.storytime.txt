Once upon a time, in a small stone lodge tucked between two Bavarian mountains, the X-Men stopped for the night on a long mission across Europe.

The fire in the hearth was warm. The wind came down from the peaks in long steady gusts and pressed against the windows. Kurt was sitting by the glass watching the first stars appear above the dark ridgeline when the door opened and someone stepped in from the cold.

A woman. Brown hair, grey coat, a rucksack over one shoulder. Nothing unusual about her at all.

And Kurt felt something strange in his chest — a pull, like the feeling before a teleport when his body knows where it is going before his mind has caught up. He said nothing. He simply watched.

Her name was Raven Darkhölme, and she had information. A group called the Brotherhood of Mutants had stolen a dangerous device from a research station in Genosha, and she knew exactly where they were keeping it. She told the X-Men everything: the location, the layout, the schedule of the people guarding it. Her intelligence was detailed and precise.

Professor Xavier listened carefully and then nodded. It was genuine.

The next morning the X-Men flew to Genosha — a large island in the Mediterranean with a long and complicated history. The device was exactly where Mystique had said it would be. The operation moved quickly. The Brotherhood scattered. The device was secured.

But the building began to come apart under the stress of the fight. Old concrete, poor construction — walls cracking, a staircase groaning. Three innocent people were caught inside: a technician, an archivist who had been sitting at her desk when the alarms started, and an elderly man who had wandered in for shelter from the morning cold.

Afterward, while the dust settled and the team regrouped, Mystique came to stand beside Kurt in the rubble.

She was quiet for a moment. And then she let her disguise fall away.

Her skin went blue. Indigo blue — that specific, rare, twilight color that Kurt saw every time he looked in a mirror.

Her eyes, without the disguise, were gold-orange and sharp. His kind of eyes.

Kurt was very still.

He had wondered. Not always openly — it was the kind of wondering he kept in a quiet place and did not visit too often. But two people with the same impossible skin, both from Germany, both mutants: the arithmetic was not hard. He had suspected. Deep down, in the patient part of himself, he had known.

He let himself feel it for a moment — the strangeness of having the wondering confirmed — and then he listened to his heart, which was steady and certain.

"I believe you had reasons," Kurt continued. "I do not need to know them all. You were afraid, and it was a long time ago, and you are here now." He paused. "That is what I know."

Something in Mystique's face changed — that careful, guarded quality she wore like a second skin loosened slightly around the edges, the way frost loosens in the first warmth of morning.

Kurt extended his three-fingered hand. She looked at it for a moment — two seconds, three — and then she shook it.

Not forgiveness. Not reunion. Something quieter and more honest: two people with the same remarkable blue skin, choosing to be decent to each other in the ruins of a Genoshan building, in the middle of an ordinary afternoon.

Kurt walked back to the X-Men. He felt, to his mild surprise, completely whole. The question he had carried was answered. The answer confirmed what he had always known.
