Once upon a time, on a bright October morning when the maples around the Xavier Institute were turning copper and gold, a car came up the driveway.

Out came a social worker from Montana who had driven a very long way, and a girl named Petra.

She was eleven years old. Brown hair in a neat braid. A blue jacket. A suitcase slightly too large for her, which she had packed herself in a hurry, the zipper not quite closing all the way. She looked around at the big stone school, the wide lawn, the tall old oaks, and she was, to put it honestly, not at all happy to be there.

Three nights ago her mutation had arrived. She had walked through the wall of her bedroom into her neighbor's kitchen. Not on purpose. The neighbors were frightened. There were phone calls. There was this school in Westchester, New York, and she had not asked for any of it.

She set her suitcase in the front hallway and sat on it with her arms crossed.

The first person she met was Kurt Wagner.

He came around the corner humming a German folk song, stopped when he saw her, and she gave him the most thoroughly suspicious look an eleven-year-old face can produce. Blue skin. Pointed ears. Curling tail. Yellow eyes. She had not been prepared for this.

Kurt looked back at her.

And then he sat down on the floor, a comfortable distance away, legs crossed, tail curling beside him.

"I understand that look," he said pleasantly. "I made it myself once."

She said nothing.

"When I was close to your age," he continued, "a mob near Stuttgart cornered me against a wall. I had done nothing. They were afraid. I stood there and had no idea what to do." He looked at her. "Then Professor Xavier arrived and said something I needed to hear. I had no idea this school existed, or that anyone understood." He paused. "I am glad someone told me. I think eventually you will be too."

She looked sideways at him. "You teleport."

"BAMF," he said — and vanished in a small friendly puff of purple smoke, reappearing on the bench at the end of the hall.

She flinched. Then — just at the corner of her mouth — almost smiled.

That was the beginning.

Kurt became her guide. Not her teacher — that was Professor Xavier and the instructors — but the person who understood the loneliness of a power that announced your difference to every room you entered.

He told her about Margali Szardos and the circus: when the performers had first seen his blue skin and his tail, they had not wondered whether he belonged. They had argued about which act he would join. Not whether. Which. He told her about the mob in Stuttgart and Xavier arriving at the right moment. He told her about every X-Man who had chosen to stay.

And in the library one autumn afternoon, he told her the most important thing: the hardest thing isn't learning to use the power. It's learning you deserve to be in the room.

She looked at him when he said it. He could see her carrying it.

By November, Petra was eating breakfast with the other students. By December she was in the Danger Room, her ability becoming precise and intentional. By spring she was one of the school's most capable young mutants. Her arms were no longer crossed. Her expression had changed from suspicious to openly, genuinely alive.

On the last evening before winter break, Kurt was in the chapel at the end of the east-wing corridor, reading in the pew by the window. He heard light footsteps. The door opened.

Petra came in and sat beside him on the pew. She didn't say anything for a while. The candle burned in its glass. Outside, the pine trees held the first snow of the season. Somewhere upstairs someone burned the toast — inevitable.

After a while Petra said: "I'm glad someone told me too."

Kurt felt the warmth of the candle and the quiet evening and thought of all the kindness that had built his life.

Once upon a time, a boy no one wanted became someone everyone needed. And he never forgot what it felt like to be told: you belong here.

The candle burned. The pine trees held their snow. Everything was exactly as it should be.

BAMF. He never would forget.
