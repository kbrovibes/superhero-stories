The call from Tony Stark came on a Thursday. The Avengers had a problem that required teleportation, and the only person on the planet who could do what they needed was Kurt Wagner. Could he come to the Avengers Tower in Manhattan immediately? Kurt packed his rapier, said goodbye to Wolverine (who was unimpressed), and BAMF-ed to New York.

The problem was a device called the Oscillation Engine, a piece of advanced technology that had been planted inside the sealed reactor core of a power station in northern New Jersey. The core was surrounded by four feet of reinforced concrete and a magnetic containment field that would neutralize any large source of energy or matter transmission. No Avenger could reach the device. Iron Man's armor would be depowered by the field the instant he entered. Thor's hammer couldn't break the concrete without triggering the device. Captain America's shield bounced perfectly, but bouncing was no help here.

Kurt was the solution. The Brimstone Dimension was outside the field's operational parameters — his teleportation didn't interact with the magnetic containment the way normal energy did. He could jump in, collect the device, and jump out. Theoretically.

"I should mention," said Tony Stark, studying readouts on his StarkPad, "that you will have approximately four seconds inside the containment field before the emergency failsafe activates."

"Four seconds," Kurt repeated.

"At the outside."

He reviewed the blueprints for thirty minutes, memorizing the layout as he would a trapeze routine. He identified the device's likely location and ran the jump geometry twice.

Then he went in.

BAMF. The containment field was cold and absolutely silent — nothing like the Brimstone Dimension's warmth. The Oscillation Engine was exactly where the blueprints suggested, bolted to the secondary housing. Kurt's fingers found it in one second flat. He pulled. It resisted — the bolts were tight. Two seconds. He wrapped his tail around the housing for leverage and yanked harder. The device came free. Three seconds.

BAMF. He was in the corridor outside the reactor, holding the device, exhaling a long breath he hadn't known he was holding.

Tony Stark blinked. Captain America put down his shield. Thor nodded, deeply impressed.

"Four seconds was an overestimate," Kurt said mildly. He set the Oscillation Engine on the floor and smiled. The Avengers broke into laughter — sharp, relieved laughter that echoed off the power station walls.

Afterward, over coffee in the facility's break room, Captain America asked Kurt how he had prepared. Kurt answered honestly: the same way he prepared for any trapeze routine. You study the layout. You trust the jump. You don't hesitate in the air.

"Good advice," Steve Rogers said, and meant it for much more than trapeze work.
