The chapel at the Xavier Institute was a small room at the end of the east wing, with a wooden cross on the wall and four rows of pews that Kurt had installed at his own quiet request. Most mornings before dawn, when the rest of the school was asleep, he was there — sitting in the half-dark with a book of psalms open in his three-fingered hands.

Wolverine found him there once. Logan couldn't sleep — he rarely slept well — and was walking the perimeter out of old habit when he noticed the light under the chapel door. He knocked once and came in.

"You really believe all that?" Wolverine asked, nodding at the cross and the book.

"Yes," Kurt said simply. He moved over on the pew to make room.

Logan sat. Outside, an owl called from the pine trees. They were quiet for several minutes.

Kurt was, in this way, unusual among people who had been treated poorly by the world. He held no permanent bitterness. He had been driven from his village in Bavaria, cornered by mobs, dismissed his entire life. And yet he prayed each morning, laughed loudly at dinner, and argued with Storm about whether the queen or the knight was the superior chess piece. (He held firmly for the knight — the knight's L-shaped move was the most surprising. Who expected an L?)

He had thought about where this came from. The answer was the circus. Margali Szardos and her people had given him, alongside the rapier and the trapeze bars, something more valuable: daily proof that people could choose kindness. When they first saw him — his blue skin, his tail, his glowing eyes — they had not argued over whether he should join them. They had argued over which act he would join. Not whether. Which.

He told this to Wolverine in the quiet before sunrise.

"I got lucky early," Kurt said. "Good people showed me something worth believing in. I don't think I'm braver than you — I think the beginning was different for me."

Logan was quiet for a long time. Then: "That's not nothing."

"No," Kurt agreed. "It isn't."

The owl called again. Morning was coming. In twenty minutes the school would wake — kettles and footsteps and someone burning toast in the kitchen — and Kurt would be in the middle of it, laughing and eating an enormous breakfast, as he did every day.

But for now the chapel was quiet, and the light was gold, and two very different people sat together in the best kind of silence.
