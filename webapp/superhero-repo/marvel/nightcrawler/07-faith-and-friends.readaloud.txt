Here is something about Kurt Wagner that might surprise you.

Most mornings, before anyone else at the Xavier Institute is awake — before the kettles are boiling, before Wolverine has his first cup of coffee — Kurt is already up.

He walks quietly to a small room at the end of the east wing. Four rows of old wooden pews. A wooden cross on the wall. A book of psalms.

He sits. He reads. He prays.

One grey morning — very early, sky the dark blue of almost-dawn — Wolverine couldn't sleep. He was walking the grounds when he spotted a light under the chapel door.

He knocked. He went in.

He looked at the cross, the candle, the book in Kurt's hands. And he said — in the very direct way that is entirely Wolverine — "You really believe all that?"

Kurt looked up. "Yes," he said. Just that.

And he moved over on the pew to make room.

Wolverine sat.

Now here is what makes Kurt remarkable. He has been chased from his village in Bavaria. He has been cornered by mobs. People have called him a monster every year of his life.

And yet — he laughs at dinner. He argues with Storm about chess — is the queen or the knight the superior piece? Kurt holds FIRMLY for the knight. The knight moves in an L! An unpredictable, surprising L! Who expects an L?!

Wolverine asked him: how has none of the hard things made you hard?

Kurt thought carefully. "The circus," he said. "Margali Szardos and her people. When they first saw me — my blue skin, my tail, my eyes — they didn't wonder whether I should join them. They argued over which act I would join. Not whether. Which." He paused. "They assumed I was one of them. It was not a decision they worked toward. It was simply something they knew."

The owl called from the pine trees outside.

"I got lucky," Kurt said. "Good people showed me something worth believing in, early enough that it settled deep. I don't think I'm braver than you, Logan. I think the beginning was kinder to me."

Wolverine was quiet for a long moment.

"That's not nothing," he said.

"No," Kurt agreed. "It isn't."

The kettle began to boil upstairs. Footsteps on the floor above, voices drifting down the hall. The school was waking up around them, warm and full and alive. Two very different friends sat together in the quiet — a gruff loner and a gentle blue acrobat — not needing another word.
