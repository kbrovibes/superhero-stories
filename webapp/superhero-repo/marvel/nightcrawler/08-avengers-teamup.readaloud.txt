RING RING! The phone at the Xavier Institute is ringing!

Kurt picks it up. "Hello?"

"This is Tony Stark. I need your teleportation. Come to Avengers Tower immediately."

Kurt looks at Wolverine. Wolverine shrugs. Kurt packs his rapier. BAMF — Manhattan!

Here is the problem Tony explained. Inside a sealed reactor core in New Jersey, someone had hidden a nasty device called the Oscillation Engine. Four feet of reinforced concrete on all sides. A magnetic containment field that powered down any large energy source that entered it.

Iron Man's armor? Shut down by the field instantly. Thor's hammer? Too powerful — would trigger the device. Captain America's vibranium shield bounces beautifully, but bouncing couldn't help here.

Tony looked at Kurt. "Your teleportation travels through the Brimstone Dimension. The field doesn't register it until you materialize. You can go in, grab the device, and come back." He paused. "You have four seconds before the failsafe activates."

"Four seconds," Kurt said.

"At the outside," Tony confirmed.

The Avengers watched to see how he'd react.

Kurt nodded and asked for the blueprints.

He spent thirty minutes with those blueprints. Completely focused, completely still — his tail coiled in a neat circle beside him. He traced every wall, every pipe, every corner. He measured jump geometry in his head the way he measured a new trapeze routine — not mathematically, but in the body, in the instinct for space and timing that came from ten thousand hours of practice.

He closed his eyes. He pictured the interior until he could walk it in his mind.

He stood up. "I'm ready."

BAMF.

Cold. Silent. Nothing like the warm Brimstone Dimension. There — the Oscillation Engine! One second: fingers on the device. Two seconds: pulling — tight bolts, stubborn! His tail wrapped around the housing for extra leverage. Three seconds — one enormous yank —

BAMF.

He was in the corridor, holding the device.

Tony checked his watch. "Three seconds. I said four."

Thor crossed his great arms. "The acrobat is efficient."

Captain America started laughing — the kind of laugh that fills the whole room.

Later, over coffee, Captain America asked: "How did you prepare for that?"

Kurt thought about it. "The same as any trapeze act. Study the layout until you know it in your body. Trust the jump. Don't hesitate in the air — hesitation at the wrong moment is the only thing that can actually go wrong."

Steve Rogers looked at him. "Good advice," he said — meaning it for much more than trapeze work.

BAMF! Thanks, Nightcrawler.
