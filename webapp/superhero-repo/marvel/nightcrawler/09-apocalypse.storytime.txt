Once upon a time, the X-Men flew south through the night in the Blackbird — Professor Xavier's supersonic jet — over the dark Mediterranean and the coast of North Africa, toward Cairo.

In the center of Cairo, a communications tower blinked its lights as if nothing unusual were happening.

A great deal unusual was happening.

The man who had taken the tower called himself Apocalypse. His real name was En Sabah Nur, and he was very old — thousands of years old — the oldest mutant alive. He believed that only the strongest should survive, and he was using the tower to broadcast a signal that would disrupt electronic systems across three continents.

Cyclops gave the briefing in his careful, precise way, going through each element of the problem. The tower's perimeter was ringed by eleven automated energy cannons, arranged to cover every angle, their tracking systems detecting heat and mass and firing without hesitation. Apocalypse's four Horsemen — powerful mutants he had recruited and enhanced over centuries of searching for the strongest — were stationed at the tower's four corners.

Any direct approach would be overwhelmed immediately. The plan required precision and distraction working together.

Storm and Cyclops would make as much noise as they possibly could — real, unmissable noise — on the north and south sides of the tower. Loud enough to pull the Horsemen away from their positions. Jean Grey, the X-Men's telepath, would reach the tower's human operators from a distance and fill their minds with a gentle, harmless fog that made it impossible to focus on any one thing for long.

That would open a path to the east side.

But someone had to thread through eleven energy cannons to reach it.

Every face on the Blackbird turned to Kurt.

He was already studying the surveillance photographs, and he did not look up immediately. He had been noting the cannon specifications. They tracked physical mass above a certain threshold — but his Brimstone Dimension hops did not exist in this dimension until he materialized. They registered below the threshold. If he moved fast enough, and placed each jump precisely enough, he could thread through.

"I can do it," he said simply.

At midnight, Storm called lightning down on the north side. Cyclops struck the south. Three Horsemen moved toward the noise. Jean Grey reached the operators' minds from a distance and filled them with a soft, harmless fog.

Kurt went in. BAMF, BAMF, BAMF — short precise hops, each one threading through darkness between cannon arcs, until he materialized inside.

He went up three floors and stepped into the signal room.

Apocalypse was waiting. Seven feet tall, armored in ancient metal, with pale eyes already fixed on the doorway. He had sensed Kurt's arrival with senses sharpened over millennia.

"You are brave, little acrobat," Apocalypse said. "But bravery is insufficient."

Kurt stood still. He knew he could not fight this person alone. He did not need to. He needed ninety seconds for Storm to reach the roof array.

So he talked.

He talked about Professor Xavier — about the vision Xavier had held for years, of a world where mutants and humans found a way forward together. He talked about the children at the Xavier Institute, kids who had arrived frightened and found a home. He talked about what mutants could mean to the world if the world would allow it.

He spoke clearly and believed every word.

Seventy-eight seconds.

CRACK-BOOM. Storm's lightning came through the roof array and every transmitter screen went dark at once. The signal died.

Apocalypse looked at the dead equipment. He looked at Kurt. Something moved in the ancient face — something almost like respect.

He withdrew without a word.

Kurt exhaled and stood alone in the darkened room.

On the Blackbird flying home, the stars were thick against the windows above the dark Mediterranean. Kurt sat quietly and thought about standing in that room armed only with words.

The words had been enough. Not because they were perfectly argued — because they were true, and he believed them, and the believing had held him steady.

He thought of Margali at the Rhine, Xavier in the Bavarian square, every truth worth defending that he had gathered over the years. All of it had been preparation.

What you carry in your heart — the true things, the kind things — is sometimes the strongest thing you own.

He fell asleep above the Mediterranean. The Blackbird carried them all home through the stars.
