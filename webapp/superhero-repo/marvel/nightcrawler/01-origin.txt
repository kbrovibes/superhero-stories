Kurt Wagner was born in the Bavarian mountains of Germany, in a small village at the edge of an ancient forest. From his first breath, Kurt looked different from the people around him. His skin was the deep blue-indigo of a twilight sky. He had three fingers on each hand, two toes on each foot, pointed ears, glowing yellow eyes, and a long, whip-like tail that curled behind him whenever he was happy — which was often. He was also capable of something no one could explain: with a crack of sound — BAMF! — he could vanish in a puff of brimstone smoke and reappear somewhere else entirely. He could teleport.

The villagers were frightened, and his mother, the shape-shifting mutant known as Mystique, made the heartbreaking choice to give him up. A kind woman named Margali Szardos found the infant Kurt and brought him to live with her traveling circus, the Szardos family wagon trundling through the German countryside. The circus folk adored him. They had never seen a performer quite like Kurt. His prehensile tail could grip a trapeze bar. His agility was extraordinary — he flipped, tumbled, twisted in ways that made audiences gasp with delight. He became the star of the Munich circus, billed as "the Incredible Nightcrawler."

But audiences are fickle. When some began to whisper that Kurt was a real monster — not just a performer — the atmosphere grew dangerous. A mob surrounded him one terrible night in a town square near Stuttgart, demanding he be driven away. Kurt had no weapon and no army. He had only a brave heart and a gift he still did not fully understand.

That was the night Professor Charles Xavier arrived. Xavier was a brilliant, kind man who used a wheelchair and could communicate with his thoughts. He had come from his school in Westchester, New York, looking for young mutants in need of guidance. He spoke to the crowd calmly, but it was his gentle words to Kurt that mattered most: "You are not a monster. You are a gift to this world." Xavier offered Kurt a home — Xavier's School for Gifted Youngsters, a place where mutants could learn, grow, and be accepted.

Kurt Wagner joined the X-Men. He took the code-name Nightcrawler and trained alongside Wolverine, Storm, and Colossus in the Danger Room at the Xavier Institute in Salem Center, New York. He was devoted and brave in battle, and between missions he read poetry, practiced swordsmanship with a rapier, and led quiet prayers in the school's small chapel. He wore a blue uniform and a wide, warm smile. He had found his family. He had found his purpose. The boy the village had feared had become one of Earth's greatest defenders.
