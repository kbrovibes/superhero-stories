Sometimes the most frightening story is not about a villain from far away. Sometimes it is about what a hero could become if they stopped caring. That is the story of the Maestro — and it is one of the most sobering tales in all of the Hulk's long life.

In a possible future, a terrible war had left the world in ruins. Nearly all of its heroes were gone. The Hulk, however, had survived — not just survived, but absorbed more and more radiation over the centuries until he was stronger than he had ever been. But the kindness inside Bruce Banner had been worn away, year after year, by grief and hardship and loneliness.

The result was the Maestro — a Hulk who had lost his way completely. He built a city called Dystopia and ruled it with an iron fist, hanging the shields and helmets of fallen heroes on his walls like grim trophies: Captain America's round shield, Iron Man's helmet, even Thor's hammer. He called it proof of his supremacy, but really it was something sadder than that.

When the present-day Hulk was pulled forward in time to face this older self, he found someone who sneered at goodness and called heroism a fool's game. Their battle was the hardest the Hulk had ever fought — not because of raw strength, but because every blow felt like fighting his own reflection.

The Hulk won. But he walked away shaken to his core. Because do you know what the Maestro really was? A warning. A reminder that strength without kindness, without purpose, without love — is just power for its own sake, and power alone will always, eventually, turn cold.

And that is why Bruce Banner keeps fighting, every single day, to be better than the monster the world fears — and better, still, than the master he might have become.
