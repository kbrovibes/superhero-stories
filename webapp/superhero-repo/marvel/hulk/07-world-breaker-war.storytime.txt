When the Hulk came back to Earth from Sakaar, he did not come quietly.

He came with the rage of a man who had lost everything. He came with his Warbound at his side. He came in a ship that landed on the moon with a thunderous BOOM that astronomers could hear from Earth.

This was not just the Hulk anymore. This was the WORLD BREAKER.

He was bigger than he had ever been. Greener. Stronger. His footsteps cracked the rocks beneath him. When he breathed, the dust on the moon rose and fell like an ocean.

The first hero who met him was Black Bolt, the silent king of the Inhumans, whose whisper could level a city. Black Bolt was waiting on the moon to stop him. He spoke one word.

The Hulk knocked him down with a single mighty blow.

The Hulk was not interested in talking. He was here for his old friends. He was here for the heroes who had sent him away to Sakaar without asking.

He turned the ship toward New York City. He was coming.

The Avengers had no choice. They evacuated the city. Children, families, grandparents — they all packed into buses and trains and made their way to safety while the great green giant landed in the middle of Manhattan and began to walk through the empty streets.

His footsteps sounded like the slow tolling of a great bell. BOOM. BOOM. BOOM.

He carried a list of names. The Illuminati. The small group of heroes who had decided his fate. Iron Man. Mister Fantastic. Doctor Strange. Black Bolt was already down. He wanted the rest.

One by one, they came to meet him.

Iron Man flew at him in a special suit he had built for exactly this day — the Hulkbuster, an enormous towering armor as tall as a building. The Hulk tore it apart like tinfoil. Tony Stark crawled out of the wreckage, bruised, breathing hard, looking up at the green giant who used to be his friend.

"Bruce," he said quietly.

"You sent me away," the Hulk growled. And he set Tony down on the ground, not gently but not killing him, and moved on to the next.

Doctor Strange came in a cloak of red and a flurry of mystical glowing symbols, the wisest sorcerer on Earth. His magic was vast. His magic was old.

The Hulk's rage was bigger.

One by one, the Hulk gathered the heroes who had wronged him. He did not kill them. That was important — he did not kill them. But he wanted them to feel small. He wanted them to feel the way he had felt in the slave pens of Sakaar. So he marched them into Madison Square Garden, and he set up an arena, and he made them fight there. Slaves. Gladiators. Performers in his great sad show.

He wanted them to UNDERSTAND.

The conflict reached its peak when one final, mysterious hero arrived. The Sentry, the most powerful being on Earth — a man with the power of a thousand exploding suns. He flew down to meet the Hulk in the broken streets of New York. The two of them collided with a shockwave that shook the whole eastern coast.

They were like two suns slamming together.

The buildings around them trembled. The sky darkened. The Hulk was matched, perhaps for the first time in his life, by someone of equal strength.

But the most important moment of World War Hulk was not the punching. It was the truth.

As the great battle raged, the Warbound were not idle. Miek, the small insect-like warrior who had been the Hulk's loyal brother, was hiding something. A terrible secret. And when at last it came out, the truth broke over the Hulk like a wave of icy water.

The bomb that had destroyed Sakaar — the bomb that had taken Caiera, that had taken the Hulk's paradise — had not been planted by the Illuminati. It had been planted by Miek himself, who had wanted the Hulk to stay angry forever. Who had believed that the only Hulk worth following was the broken one.

The Hulk staggered. He had built this whole war on a lie.

His rage drained out of him like water from a cracked vase. In its place came something worse — guilt. Sorrow. The terrible sad weight of every blow he had landed in the name of a wrong he had never been wronged about.

He fell to his knees in the broken streets of New York. Around him, the surviving Avengers gathered, exhausted and battered but not unkind. They did not strike him while he was down.

Bruce Banner came back, then. The man inside the monster. He shrank down to his small human form, kneeling in the rubble in torn clothes, weeping into his hands.

"Stop me," he whispered to the heroes around him. "Please. Stop me before I do this again."

They did. Gently.

World War Hulk had been one of the most terrible events in the history of the Marvel Universe. It had shown that the Hulk was, without a doubt, the strongest one there is. He could break the world if he chose to.

But it had also shown something else. Something quieter. Something more important.

The Hulk's greatest enemy had never been the heroes of Earth. It had never been the Red King. It had never been Miek.

The Hulk's greatest enemy was the lie that had told him his rage was the only thing he had left.

Bruce Banner, sitting in a quiet cell while the city was rebuilt around him, made a vow then. A small private vow. He was going to find a different way to be the Hulk.

It would take years. It would take many wrong turns.

But the seed of that change was planted, that day, in the rubble of New York.
