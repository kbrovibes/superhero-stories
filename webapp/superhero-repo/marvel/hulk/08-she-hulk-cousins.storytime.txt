For a very long time, Bruce Banner believed he was the only Hulk in the world.

The only one whose blood had been changed by gamma radiation. The only one who had to live with a green giant sleeping inside him. The only one who could turn from a small quiet scientist into a roaring mountain of muscle when something went wrong.

He was wrong.

Bruce had a cousin. Her name was Jennifer Walters.

Jennifer was bright and sharp and funny, with dark hair and a wide easy smile. She had grown up in California while Bruce had grown up wherever his unhappy father had dragged the family, but the two of them had always written letters. They had shared secrets. They had been each other's closest friend across the long miles between them.

Jennifer became a lawyer. A very good one. She wore tailored suits and carried a worn brown briefcase, and she stood up in courtrooms defending people who could not afford to defend themselves. She loved her work. She loved the LAW. She loved the idea that even the smallest person could find justice if they had a clever enough lawyer at their side.

Then one terrible night, Jennifer was hurt very badly in an accident. She was rushed to a hospital. She needed blood, and she needed it fast, and she needed a very specific type that almost no one had.

Bruce had it. Bruce was nearby.

He looked down at his unconscious cousin in the hospital bed, and he hesitated. He knew what his blood carried. He knew the gamma radiation that had changed him might change her too.

But she would die without it.

He let the doctors take the transfusion.

Jennifer lived. The blood saved her.

And when she woke up, days later, her hair was still dark and her smile was still wide and her wit was still sharp — but she was also seven feet tall, with skin the color of new spring leaves, and muscles that bent the hospital bed beneath her.

Jennifer Walters had become the She-Hulk.

Here is where the story takes a beautiful turn.

When Bruce had become the Hulk, his transformation had been a curse. He lost himself. He raged. He hurt people he loved. He hid from the world.

When Jennifer became the She-Hulk, something different happened. She kept HERSELF. Her personality. Her intelligence. Her humor. Her love of law. The gamma radiation in her cousin's blood, mixed with whatever was already in HER blood, made her something new. She was strong, yes. Incredibly strong. She could lift cars. She could leap rooftops.

But she was still Jennifer. She was just MORE Jennifer.

She liked being She-Hulk.

She liked being tall. She liked being strong. She liked the way her opponents in court suddenly took her arguments more seriously. She liked the confidence it gave her, the sense that she could finally take up the space she had always deserved to take up.

She bought new suits — green ones, because they matched. She kept practicing law. She joined the Avengers and the Fantastic Four. She represented superheroes in courtrooms, because superheroes sometimes need lawyers too. She made the She-Hulk into a hero who fought with both her fists and her arguments, and she was beloved by everyone who met her.

For Bruce, all of this was a wonder.

He and Jennifer were the only two people in the world who could truly talk about what it was like to live with gamma radiation in their cells. They could sit at a kitchen table together and share things they could not share with anyone else. The fear. The strange new sensations. The quiet weight of being different. Jennifer understood Bruce in a way no one else ever had.

She was, in many ways, his ANCHOR.

When Bruce was hunted, when he was lonely, when he was afraid of the Hulk inside him, Jennifer would show up. She would sit with him. She would crack jokes until he laughed in spite of himself. She would remind him, "Bruce. We are not curses. We are still us. Look at me. I am still here. So are you."

In return, Bruce became a brother she could count on. When she was hurt — and she was, sometimes, badly — Bruce was there. When she had questions about her own changing body, he had answers. They held each other up.

They did not always agree. That happened too. Jennifer believed in the LAW. She believed in working through the system. Bruce, who had spent so much of his life RUNNING from the system, sometimes operated outside it. There were even hard moments when they ended up on opposite sides of a conflict — moments that broke Bruce's heart, even when he knew his cousin was right to stand where she stood.

Once, during a difficult time called the second superhero Civil War, Jennifer was hurt very badly. Bruce thought, for a terrible day, that he might lose her. The grief that filled him was as big as any rage he had ever felt — and it led to one of the saddest transformations of his life.

She recovered. She always recovered. Jennifer was tough.

Today, Bruce and Jennifer remain two of the most important heroes in the gamma-irradiated world. They visit each other when they can. They exchange long phone calls. They send postcards. They tease each other the way cousins always have, and they protect each other the way family always should.

She-Hulk proved something important. That gamma radiation did not have to mean a monster. It could also mean a HERO who took her difference and turned it into a power for good.

For Bruce, that was the greatest gift his cousin ever gave him.

The reminder that being changed did not have to mean being lost.

And every now and then, on a quiet Sunday in a quiet kitchen somewhere, you might find the two of them — Bruce small and gentle in his glasses, Jennifer enormous and green in her favorite green sweater — sitting at a table together, drinking coffee, laughing about something silly.

The heart and soul of the gamma-irradiated world.

Family.
