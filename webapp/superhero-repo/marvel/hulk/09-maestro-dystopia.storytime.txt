This is a story that almost never gets told, because it is a what-if story. A story about a future that has not happened yet. A future that Bruce Banner is doing everything in his power to make sure NEVER happens.

But the future is a strange and stubborn thing. And in one of the many possible futures, there lives a version of the Hulk who frightens even the present-day Hulk.

His name is the Maestro.

Let me tell you about him, so you can understand why Bruce works so hard to make sure he never comes to be.

Long, long from now, in a future the Hulk visited once and hopes never to see again, the world has changed terribly. A great catastrophe happened — a war that the heroes could not stop. Most of humanity was lost. Most of the great heroes were lost too. The cities lay broken under sad orange skies. The seas had grown strange. The very rocks of the Earth had been changed.

In all that wreckage, only one being walked the land in his old green skin.

The Hulk had survived.

But the centuries had changed him. He had absorbed more radiation than any creature should. He had outlived everyone he had ever loved. He had outlived Tony Stark. He had outlived Steve Rogers. He had outlived Jennifer. He had outlived Betty Ross. He had even outlived the Avengers as an idea.

And being alone for so long, with no one to remind him who he was supposed to be, had hollowed him out.

The Hulk of the future became the Maestro.

The Maestro was bigger than the Hulk. His muscles were even greater. His skin was a darker, harder green. His hair had gone gray. His eyes were tired and cold and clever, because the Maestro had the Hulk's strength AND Bruce Banner's brilliance, both in one body, both serving only himself.

He ruled what was left of the world from a city he had built in the wasteland, a place he called Dystopia.

He wore terrible trophies. He had taken Captain America's shield from a museum and hung it on his wall. He had Iron Man's helmet on a shelf. He had — and this is the hardest one to picture — Thor's hammer, Mjolnir, propped up by the door, as if to remind himself that he had outlived even gods. He had taken these things, not to honor his old friends, but as a quiet bitter way of saying that he was the only one left.

He ruled with an iron fist. He crushed any rebellion. He believed that might was the only thing that mattered, and that he was the only one fit to lead what was left of the human race.

He was what happens when a hero lives too long, all alone, and forgets why he was ever a hero.

The present-day Hulk met him only once.

A strange experiment pulled the Hulk through time and dropped him into Dystopia. He stood in the broken city, looking up at the throne where his own future self sat in royal silks, and he could not believe what he was seeing.

"Look at us, Hulk," the Maestro said with a slow tired smile. "Look at what we become."

Their battle was terrible. The Maestro was older. The Maestro was stronger. The Maestro had had centuries to learn every trick the Hulk had ever known.

But the present-day Hulk had something the Maestro did not. He had HOPE. He had friends back home. He had Jennifer. He had Betty. He had the small fragile possibility of a different future.

He fought for that future.

The battle lasted a long time. Buildings fell. The ground cracked. The orange sky shook. And in the end, the Hulk won. Just barely. He did not destroy the Maestro completely — that was not really possible — but he sent him into a deep, long sleep, sealed away from the broken world.

Then he came home.

When Bruce Banner shrank back to his small human form on a hospital bed in his own time, he could not sleep for many nights. He had seen his own face on the body of a tyrant. He had heard his own voice ruling a wasteland. He had looked into his own future eyes and seen nothing kind looking back.

It changed him.

From that day on, Bruce Banner worked even harder to be a different kind of Hulk. He chose his friends carefully. He held them close. He fought to keep his humanity alive, even when his green form was loose, because he had SEEN what happens when humanity is lost.

The Maestro became the ghost in Bruce's mind. The cautionary tale. The warning he carried with him on every mission, every battle, every quiet evening alone.

Power, the Maestro had taught him, is not the danger. Power WITHOUT KINDNESS is. Strength without family is. Living forever without anyone you love is the truest sad ending of all.

So Bruce wrote letters. He called his cousin Jennifer. He apologized to people he had hurt. He let himself be loved, even when it was scary. He kept reaching for connection, even when the Hulk inside him wanted to go off alone and brood.

And somewhere, in the long shimmering branching maze of possible futures, the city of Dystopia grew a little dimmer. The Maestro stirred in his long sleep, perhaps, and frowned at the shape of a future that was not coming to him.

Because Bruce was choosing, every single day, to take a different road.

The future is not fixed. The future is a garden. And every kind choice you make is a seed.

That is the lesson the Maestro taught.

Even if he never meant to.
