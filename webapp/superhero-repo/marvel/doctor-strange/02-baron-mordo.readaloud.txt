Among all the students at Kamar-Taj, there was one who trained the hardest, studied the longest, and burned with a quiet, intense dedication. His name was Karl Mordo — Baron Mordo — and he was extraordinary. The Ancient One trusted him. Everyone expected him to one day become the Sorcerer Supreme, the greatest protector Earth had ever known.

Then Stephen Strange arrived. Mordo was meant to be his guide. He showed Strange the ropes, helped him through those first confusing lessons, and watched as Strange — stubborn, brilliant Strange — began to catch up and then pull ahead. Mordo told himself it was fine. But inside, something grew tight and cold.

What truly broke Mordo was learning that the Ancient One herself had bent the very rules she taught. He believed in the laws of magic completely, the way other people believe in gravity: no exceptions, no excuses. When he discovered those rules had been bent, his world cracked apart.

He left Kamar-Taj. And he decided that too many sorcerers existed in the world — people who used powers they had not earned or did not deserve. He took up the Staff of the Tribunal and began hunting them, one by one, stripping their magic away. In his own mind, he was saving the multiverse. To everyone else, he had become a danger.

Strange still hopes, sometimes, that the man he once called a friend is still in there somewhere beneath the cold certainty. But Mordo will not bend.

And that is how a hero became a warning: that the very thing that makes someone strong — a fierce belief in what is right — can become a trap if it never leaves room for doubt.
