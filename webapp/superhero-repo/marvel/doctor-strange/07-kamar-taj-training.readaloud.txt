High in the Himalayas, where the air is thin and cold and the mountains seem to touch the clouds, there is a place that does not appear on any map. Its gates are plain and old, its courtyard made of worn stone. But inside, something remarkable hums in every corner. This is Kamar-Taj.

When Stephen Strange first arrived, the Ancient One did not hand him a spell book or teach him an incantation. She told him to forget. To set aside every rule he thought he knew about what was real and what was impossible. For a man as proud and precise as Strange, that was the hardest lesson he had ever faced.

Slowly, he began to see. He read ancient texts by lamplight — the Book of Cagliostro, scrolls older than any library he had visited. He practiced the intricate hand gestures of the Mystic Arts until his arms ached, because every movement had to be exactly right. He wore a Sling Ring on his fingers and learned to open portals between places, golden sparks spinning in the air until a doorway appeared.

And do you know what? That surgeon's brain that used to map every nerve in a patient's body turned out to be wonderfully good at memorizing the geometry of spells.

He trained with fellow students. They learned to raise shields of light, to move as one in battle, to defend each other against threats from dimensions no one else even knew existed. Kamar-Taj was not a school he ever wanted to leave.

From that day on, Strange understood that the greatest education is not the one that fills your head with facts, but the one that teaches you how much larger the world truly is.
