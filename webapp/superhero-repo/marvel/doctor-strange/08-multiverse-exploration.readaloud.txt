Imagine that our world is one room in a house with no walls and no ceiling — a house that goes on forever, with billions and billions of rooms, each one different. In some of them, history turned a different corner. In some of them, the heroes you know made different choices and became very different people. This is the multiverse, and Doctor Strange has walked through many of its doors.

With his Sling Ring — two metal bands worn on his fingers — Strange can spin open a portal between worlds. Golden sparks circle in the air, a window appears, and he steps through. But the journey is never simple. Every reality he visits has its own rules. Its own gravity. Its own version of what is true. He has to hold onto who he is, very tightly, so that the shifting worlds do not pull him apart.

Strange has met other Stephens. There was one who never had his accident, who was still a surgeon, still proud and unbroken. There was one who had fallen into darkness, using his power selfishly until the world around him crumbled. Each encounter was a mirror, and Strange had to look clearly at what he saw.

He has also seen terrible things: two universes beginning to crash into each other like enormous ships, threatening to erase both. He raced to stop those collisions, sometimes with the help of heroes from other realities — people with familiar faces and very different stories.

Back in the Sanctum Sanctorum, Strange keeps watch over the multiversal borders, alert to any tremor in the fabric between worlds.

From that day on, he understood something no other hero could: every choice matters, not just for one life, but for all the lives that branch out from that single moment — across infinity.
