Some enemies are large. Some enemies are enormous. And some enemies are so vast, so ancient, so absolutely unlimited in power that the very idea of fighting them — in the ordinary sense of fists and spells and strategy — is simply impossible.

Dormammu was the third kind.

He lived in the Dark Dimension — a place beyond time, beyond light, beyond any ordinary understanding of space. If you could look at it, which you probably couldn't for very long, you might see swirling colors that weren't really colors, shapes that changed every time you tried to name them, and a sky that felt like the inside of a scream. The Dark Dimension was Dormammu's kingdom, and Dormammu was made of it entirely — pure mystical energy shaped into something enormous and hungry that had one goal above all others.

Consume everything.

He wanted to absorb every dimension in the multiverse into his own — to erase their individuality and pull them into himself like a dark ocean swallowing every smaller sea. He had been doing this for a very long time. He worked patiently, sending his power to willing servants in other worlds — people who traded their loyalty for fragments of his limitless strength. They would weaken the barriers between dimensions, and then he would simply flow in.

Earth was next.

Strange went to the Dark Dimension to stop him. Not with an army — he didn't have one. Not with the most powerful spell in the library — those wouldn't work against something of Dormammu's scale. Strange went alone, with a simple plan that was either brilliant or completely ridiculous. Even he wasn't entirely sure which.

"Dormammu," he said, appearing in the Dark Dimension where no human had business being. "I've come to bargain."

Dormammu was not impressed. He destroyed Strange instantly.

And then — Strange appeared again. Standing in exactly the same spot. Saying exactly the same words.

"Dormammu. I've come to bargain."

Dormammu destroyed him again.

And again, Strange appeared.

Can you picture it? The enormous, ancient, dimension-devouring Dormammu — who had conquered realities beyond counting — now trapped in the same moment, repeating endlessly, unable to move forward or backward in time. Because the Dark Dimension existed outside of time. And Strange had used the Eye of Agamotto — the Time Stone — to create a loop that imprisoned them both in the same instant, over and over and over.

Strange died in that loop. Again and again and again. Each death was real — CRASH, gone — and each return was the Eye simply winding back to the beginning. Dormammu raged. He killed Strange in every way imaginable. He froze him, crushed him, burned him away. And Strange came back every single time.

"Dormammu. I've come to bargain."

Dormammu could not leave the loop. He could not end it — only Strange controlled the Eye, and Strange refused to stop. He had come ready to endure as long as it took.

"This is all you'll ever have now," Strange told him, between one death and the next. "You are trapped. You'll be trapped here forever. Unless you agree."

The bargain was simple: Dormammu leaves Earth alone. Dormammu takes his servants — the Zealots who had opened pathways between the dimensions — with him when he goes. Earth is free. The loop ends.

For a being of infinite power who had never once been told no, this was unbearable.

He agreed.

Strange walked out of the Dark Dimension. He didn't announce it as a victory. He didn't bow or celebrate. He simply came home and found Wong making tea in the Sanctum Sanctorum, and reported that the situation had been resolved. Wong nodded and poured him a cup.

Sometimes the strongest weapon is not strength at all.

It is the willingness to endure. To keep standing up, again and again, no matter what the Dark Dimension throws at you. To be patient enough to outlast what you cannot outfight.

Dormammu is still out there, vast and watching, looking for a single moment of weakness in Earth's dimensional barriers. Strange knows this. He guards those barriers every day.

And somewhere in the Dark Dimension, a being of infinite power still remembers the one sorcerer who came to bargain — and would not stop.
