Sorcerer Supreme. It sounds like the most magnificent title in the world, doesn't it? And it IS magnificent. But I'll let you in on a secret: it is also very, very heavy.

Every single morning, before New York City has even finished waking up — before the bakeries have opened or the pigeons have started cooing or the first school bus has rumbled down the street — Doctor Strange is already awake. He is meditating. He is reading ancient texts. He is practicing spells in the Sanctum Sanctorum, because the moment his knowledge slips — the moment he stops being perfectly prepared — something terrible from another dimension might slip through the barriers he holds in place.

Are you ready for this? The whole city goes about its ordinary days — eating breakfast, playing in parks, going to school — with absolutely NO idea how many extraordinary threats Stephen Strange quietly turned away overnight. He protects a world that will never know his name. CRACK! And he does it anyway, every single day.

It can be lonely. Sometimes Strange has to make choices that look wrong from the outside — letting something small go bad to prevent something enormous from going catastrophic. He sees possible futures the way a doctor reads a map of the body: paths that lead to health, paths that lead to disaster. His teammates trust him, but they don't always understand why he does what he does. He carries that weight alone.

He also watches himself very carefully. Magic has a PULL to it. The more power you hold, the louder a small sneaky voice can get — whispering that you know best, that the rules don't quite apply to you. Strange remembers the arrogant surgeon he used to be, and he works every single day not to become that person again. In the comics, he even LOST the title of Sorcerer Supreme once — because he refused a war the Vishanti asked him to fight. He had to earn it back. And he did.

But on the hardest days, Strange rests his hand on the Eye of Agamotto and feels its gentle golden pulse — a heartbeat linking him to every guardian who came before, all the way back to Agamotto himself. FWOOM. And he remembers: every peaceful morning in New York City, every child who sleeps safely through the night, every ordinary wonderful day that the world gets to have — that is a victory he personally helped win.

The most important tools aren't the ones you hold in your hands. They are the ones you carry in your heart.
