Being a Sorcerer Supreme sounds very grand. And it is — but it is also heavy in a way that most people never see.

Stephen Strange spends many hours every single day in study. He reads ancient texts. He meditates. He practices spells not because he enjoys showing off, but because the moment his knowledge slips, the moment he stops being prepared, something terrible might slip through the barriers he maintains. He is protecting a world that goes about its ordinary mornings and school days and dinner times without the slightest idea how much darkness Strange holds at bay.

He cannot always explain his choices. Sometimes he makes a decision that looks, from the outside, like he has given up — like he has let something bad happen. But Strange sees the paths ahead in ways others cannot, and sometimes a small loss is the only way to prevent something much, much worse. He lives with that knowledge alone. It can be lonely.

He is also watchful of himself. Magic has a pull to it. The more power you hold, the more your pride can grow, whispering that you know best, that the rules don't quite apply to you. Strange remembers who he used to be — the brilliant surgeon who was too proud to listen — and he works every day not to become that person again. Humility, for him, is a practice, not a given.

But on the hardest days, Strange walks into the Sanctum Sanctorum, rests his hand on the Eye of Agamotto, and remembers: every moment of peace in New York City, in every city, is a victory he personally won.

And that is how a man who healed one patient at a time became the guardian of every life on Earth.
