Close your eyes for a moment. Now imagine that every decision anyone has ever made — every choice, every turn taken and not taken, every moment when something happened differently than expected — created a new reality. A whole separate world, running alongside your own. And those worlds ran alongside other worlds, which ran alongside others, until the number of worlds was so vast it had no name for the size.

This is the multiverse. And it is Doctor Strange's responsibility to protect it.

He travels through it with the Sling Ring — a quick rotation of his hand, a burst of golden energy, a circular portal opening in the air with a crisp WHOOSH — and steps through into somewhere that looks like his New York but isn't. The same streets, perhaps. The same buildings, roughly. But the sky the wrong shade of blue. The newspaper headlines describing events that never happened in his world. People moving through their days with the easy confidence of people who don't know they're in an alternate reality, which they are.

Strange has to anchor himself during multiversal travel. Not physically — that part is handled by the Sling Ring and the Eye of Agamotto. Mentally. Because the multiverse tests you in a very specific way: it shows you who you could have been.

He has met alternate versions of himself. Not once — many times. There is the Stephen Strange who never had the accident, who kept his perfect surgeon's hands and his perfect surgeon's career and his perfect surgeon's arrogance, who never went to Kamar-Taj and never learned that reality was larger than his certainties. There is the Stephen Strange who went darker — who chose power over wisdom, who bent the rules until the rules broke, who lost himself in the process of saving everyone. Both versions were recognizably him. Both were uncomfortable to face.

Every decision ripples outward, Strange understands now. Your choices in this reality create the shape of who you are — and somewhere in the vast multiverse, the versions of you who made the other choices are living different lives with different consequences. Character is not just what you do when it matters. Character is what you practice every day, in every small decision, because those small decisions are what you are made of when the large ones arrive.

The most dangerous multiversal threat is called an incursion — when two universes come too close and begin to overlap, threatening to destroy each other in the collision. Strange has felt the edges of incursions: a wrongness in the air, a sense of two incompatible realities trying to occupy the same space. The sound, if you could call it that, is something between a hum and a crack, like the universe clearing its throat before something very bad happens.

He works with heroes from other realities when this occurs. Two groups of Avengers from two different worlds, learning to trust each other despite the deep strangeness of looking at your teammate and knowing they are — almost — someone you know. The coordination required is extraordinary. The trust required is even more so.

The Sanctum Sanctorum serves as the hub for all of this monitoring. Inside its shifting rooms and impossible geometry, detection systems built into the walls — some ancient and incantation-based, some developed by Strange and Wong working together — alert him when multiversal activity is occurring without authorization. The universe is large, Strange has said, but it is not unmaintained.

And through all of it — through every alternate Strange he has met, every wrong-sky world he has visited, every incursion he has helped prevent — the lesson stays the same: this world, the one he returned to from Kamar-Taj, the one where Wong keeps the library and the Cloak has opinions and New York City hums with eight million people who don't know what lives behind the door on Bleecker Street — this world is precious.

Not because it is the only one. But because it is his.

Exploring infinite realities teaches you, Strange would say, not how small your home is.

It teaches you how irreplaceable.
