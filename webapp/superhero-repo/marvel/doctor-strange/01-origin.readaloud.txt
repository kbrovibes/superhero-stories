There was once a doctor named Stephen Strange, and his hands were the most famous in the world. He was a neurosurgeon — someone who performs very delicate operations on the human brain — and he was extraordinarily good at it. People flew from faraway countries just to have him help them. He was also, it must be said, quite proud of himself.

Then one night, driving too fast on a dark and winding road, Stephen had a terrible accident. His hands were badly hurt. The small, precise nerves that gave them their magic were damaged in ways no other doctor could fix. He spent all his money traveling to every hospital and every specialist he could find. Nothing worked.

One day he heard a whisper about a hidden place in the mountains of Nepal called Kamar-Taj. He was desperate enough to go. He arrived tired, poor, and angry — not looking for wisdom, just looking for a cure.

But the teacher who met him there, the Ancient One, had other plans. She showed him something astonishing: that the world Stephen thought he knew was only one thin layer of something vast and strange, a multiverse with more dimensions than anyone could count. And do you know what happened next? She pushed him off a cliff — not a real one, but into that vast world, to see it with his own eyes.

Stephen had to start over, studying ancient spells and learning to see with his mind and spirit rather than just his clever hands. Slowly, stubbornly, he did it. He grew into a sorcerer, then a master, then the Sorcerer Supreme — guardian of all Earth against things that go bump in the dark places between worlds.

From that day on, Stephen Strange traded his operating room for the Sanctum Sanctorum, and he discovered that healing the world was an even greater calling than healing one person at a time.
