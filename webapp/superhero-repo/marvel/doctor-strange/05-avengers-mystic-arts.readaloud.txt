When the Avengers face a problem that a fist or a shield cannot solve, they look for the man in the crimson cloak.

Doctor Strange brings something no one else on the team can offer. He opens shimmering golden portals in midair so the whole team can leap from one side of the planet to the other in a single step. He weaves shields of glowing light that spring up between his friends and danger. He can bend time itself to reverse a catastrophe or sneak a precious extra second into the middle of a fight.

Strange is what you might call the wild card. He solves problems that no one else even realizes are problems — until the moment they become very, very urgent.

Sometimes this makes things complicated. Strange sees many possible futures at once, and sometimes he makes choices that worry his teammates, choices that look wrong until much later, when it becomes clear that he was protecting something much larger than any one battle. He and Tony Stark understand each other that way — two brilliant, stubborn men who carry burdens they cannot always explain. They respect each other deeply for it. And Strange has great admiration for Steve Rogers, whose moral compass never wavers.

He also taught the Avengers. He showed them how to handle magical artifacts safely. He explained the dangers of the supernatural and helped them prepare for the impossible. Because of Strange, they could face threats from other dimensions, not just other countries.

And that is how Stephen Strange — the doctor who once only healed one patient at a time — became the guardian who kept the whole world from falling into darkness, one careful, extraordinary act at a time.
