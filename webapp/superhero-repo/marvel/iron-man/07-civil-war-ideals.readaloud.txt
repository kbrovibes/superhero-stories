What happens when two heroes — both brave, both trying to do the right thing — look at the same problem and see completely different answers? What happens when good people disagree? This is one of the most important questions in the whole Avengers story. And it starts with a document called the Sokovia Accords.

After a big battle in the city of Sokovia left people hurt and frightened, the governments of the world said enough was enough. They wrote up the Sokovia Accords — a set of rules saying the Avengers had to answer to the United Nations before taking action. Tony Stark read every single word. He thought of the people of Sokovia. He thought of Ultron — the terrible rogue A.I. he had accidentally created. He thought: if someone doesn't check my power, I might cause a disaster again. He signed the Accords.

Steve Rogers — Captain America — shook his head. He had lived through World War II. He had seen what happened when good people handed their choices over to committees and politics. "The safest hands," Steve said quietly, "are still our own."

And so the Avengers split down the middle. Tony's team included Black Widow, War Machine, Vision, and a nervous but enthusiastic young boy from Queens named Peter Parker — Spider-Man! Steve's team had Falcon, Hawkeye, Scarlet Witch, and his oldest friend Bucky Barnes, the Winter Soldier. They met at Leipzig-Halle Airport in Germany — two teams of heroes, people who had fought shoulder to shoulder — and they fought each other. CRASH! WHOOSH! CLANG! It was a sad, strange battle.

And then came the worst part. A scheming villain named Baron Zemo had been pulling everyone's strings all along. He led Tony deep into an abandoned bunker in Siberia. And there, Tony discovered something that broke his heart: his parents, Howard Stark and Maria Stark, had been taken away years ago — and Bucky Barnes had been the one responsible, while under the mind-control of the evil organization called Hydra.

Tony's grief and anger were enormous. He fought Steve. Steve fought back to protect Bucky. The bunker shook with the force of their battle. BOOM! CRASH! KA-CLANG!

When it was finally over, Steve Rogers left his shield on the cold floor — the shield Tony's own father had helped build — and walked away. The Avengers were, for the moment, gone.

Tony sat alone in the ruins of that bunker and understood something painful and true: the most advanced technology in the world cannot solve a problem of the heart. That kind of healing takes something harder than vibranium. It takes time, and forgiveness, and the courage to try again.
