The hardest battles Tony Stark ever fought were not against monsters or machines. They were against the people he cared about most.

After the events of Sokovia — where a battle gone wrong had hurt many innocent people — the leaders of the world decided that the Avengers needed rules. They wrote up the Sokovia Accords: a set of agreements that would put the team under the oversight of the United Nations. Tony agreed with every word. He had seen what unchecked power could do. He had built Ultron and watched what happened. Accountability, he believed, was the only responsible path.

But Steve Rogers, Captain America, shook his head. He had lived through too much history to trust any committee with the safety of the world. "The safest hands are still our own," he said.

Neither man would back down. Tony led Black Widow, War Machine, and a nervous young Peter Parker from Queens. Steve gathered his own team — including his old friend Bucky Barnes, the Winter Soldier. They met at an airport in Leipzig, and what happened there was the saddest fight in the Avengers' long history.

Then came the bunker in Siberia, where Baron Zemo had been pulling strings all along. There, Tony learned the truth: his parents, Howard and Maria Stark, had been taken from him years ago — and Bucky's hands had been the ones responsible, while he was under the control of the terrible organization called Hydra.

Tony's grief became a storm. He fought. Steve fought back to protect his friend. And when it was over, Steve walked away — and left his shield on the cold floor behind him.

And that is how Tony Stark learned the most painful lesson of all: that the greatest things in life cannot be built. They can only be earned, and lost, and slowly — if you are very lucky — found again.
