Somewhere by a quiet lake, in a little cabin surrounded by trees, a little girl named Morgan Stark was eating breakfast with her dad. The morning light came through the windows. The world was peaceful. And Tony Stark — genius, hero, Iron Man — had finally, finally found a moment of rest.

Five years had passed since the villain Thanos snapped his fingers and made half of all life in the universe disappear. Gone. Tony had survived, and he'd found Pepper, and they'd built a home by the lake, and Morgan had come along, bright as a sunbeam. Tony held onto this small, beautiful life with everything he had.

Then his old friends came knocking. They had a plan — a wild, impossible, mind-bending plan involving time travel — to bring everyone back. Every child. Every parent. Every friend taken away.

Tony sat alone at his workshop bench that night, the water dark and still. He turned the problem over and over in his mind. The others had tried to solve time travel and failed. But Tony — being Tony — saw something no one else had seen. He worked until morning. And he solved it.

He held the answer in his hands and thought about a photo of himself with young Peter Parker — his kid, his protégé — and all the children out there who had lost someone. He couldn't stay by the lake. Not really. Not while they were still gone.

Tony Stark flew to the battle.

The fight that followed was the biggest the Avengers had ever faced. Thanos came back with his entire army, and the odds seemed impossible. But Captain Marvel tore through the sky like a comet. Pepper flew in her Rescue armor. Every hero stood together.

And in the final breathless moment — when Thanos reached for the six Infinity Stones — Tony stretched out his nanotech gauntlet and pulled them away. All six. The stones locked onto his armor with a blinding surge of light and power. Tony looked at Thanos, who had called himself inevitable, and said the same four words he'd said all those years ago in front of the cameras: "And I am Iron Man."

SNAP.

Thanos and his armies turned to dust and drifted away on the wind. The universe was safe.

Pepper knelt beside Tony as the light faded. She took his hand. "You can rest now," she said.

His first Arc Reactor — inscribed "Proof That Tony Stark Has a Heart" — was placed on the still water of the lake at his funeral. It floated there, glowing softly, as every hero he had inspired stood on the shore. Tony Stark's greatest invention was plain for all of them to see: not the suit, not the technology. The people. The people he had brought together to protect the world he loved.
