If you ask Tony Stark to tell you the most important person in his life, he will not say Iron Man.

He will not say Captain America. He will not say Rhodey, his best friend. He will not even, perhaps, say his father.

He will say Pepper.

Virginia "Pepper" Potts had walked into Tony's life one ordinary morning many years before, when she was a young accountant who noticed a math error in the Stark Industries books. She walked straight up to Tony — past the security guards, past the assistants, past everyone who had told her she could not see Mr. Stark — and she put the corrected ledger on his desk.

Tony hired her on the spot.

She became his executive assistant. She kept his schedule. She kept his promises. She kept his life from falling completely apart on the many days when he forgot to eat or sleep or come home.

She also — and this was the most important thing — kept his EGO in check. Pepper was not afraid of Tony Stark. She told him when he was being brilliant. She also told him when he was being ridiculous. She was the only person in his entire company who could walk into his workshop, look him in the eye, and say, "Tony, that is a terrible idea, and you know it."

He loved her for it. He had loved her, in fact, for a very long time before he ever managed to say so.

Eventually he did say so. And eventually she said it back. They became a true couple — Tony and Pepper, Pepper and Tony, two halves of one tired beautiful whole.

But here is the thing about being the most important person in Tony Stark's life. You become a target.

Bad guys learned, again and again, that the way to hurt Tony was to hurt Pepper. She had been kidnapped. She had been threatened. She had been swept up in battles she never asked to be in. Each time, Tony had charged in to save her. Each time, the experience left him a little more terrified.

He could not stop worrying about her. He would sit in his workshop late at night, staring at his glowing screens, and his mind would run in fearful circles.

So Tony did what Tony always did when he was worried. He BUILT something.

He built her armor.

It was called the Mark 49. But almost no one ever called it that. Everyone called it by its more important name. RESCUE.

The Rescue armor was nothing like Tony's Iron Man suits. Tony's suits were red and gold and built to PUNCH things. Rescue was purple and silver and built to PROTECT. It had advanced shielding technology that could surround a falling person mid-air and bring them safely to the ground. It had non-lethal weapons — stunners, restraints, holding fields — because Pepper had always preferred diplomacy over violence, and Tony had built her armor to match her values, not his.

It had medical kits inside it. It could carry wounded people. It could create force fields. It could, in a pinch, fly very, very fast.

Tony placed it on a special stand in his workshop. He never told anyone about it. He hoped, with all his heart, that Pepper would never have to use it.

For years, she did not.

Then the Mad Titan Thanos came.

When the final battle for the universe arrived at the ruins of the Avengers compound, Tony was already on the front lines. The fight was the biggest, hardest, scariest thing the heroes had ever faced. Half the universe had already been lost. The other half was hanging by a thread.

Tony Stark fought. He fought, and he fought, and he fought.

And then, in the middle of all of it, a streak of purple light arrived from above.

Pepper Potts had finally put on the Rescue armor.

She fell from the sky, weapons humming, shields up, eyes blazing. She landed beside her husband on the smoking battlefield, and for the first time in their long story, the two of them stood together, suit beside suit, defending the world side by side.

"Hi," Tony said through his helmet, almost laughing through his exhaustion.

"Hi," Pepper said back, calm as ever.

They fought together that day. Red and gold beside purple and silver. The genius and the executive assistant who had grown into a hero in her own right.

It was, perhaps, the proudest moment of Tony's life.

After the great battle was over, after the sad and beautiful ending that came that day, Pepper's part of the story did not stop.

She kept the Rescue armor. She kept the work. She raised the daughter she had had with Tony — a bright brave little girl named Morgan — with the same patient steady strength she had brought to everything else. She helped lead the rebuilding of the world. She used Stark Industries resources to help survivors of the Blip find their footing again.

She became, in her own way, a symbol of resilience. People who had loved Tony loved Pepper too, and they trusted her to carry his legacy forward.

But more than any of the public things, Pepper kept the small private things. She kept the photographs. She kept Tony's old workshop tools. She kept his arc reactor in a quiet box on the mantelpiece. She told Morgan stories about her dad at bedtime — funny stories, mostly, the kind of stories that made the little girl laugh until she fell asleep clutching her stuffed bunny.

Pepper Potts was always the heart of the Stark legend. She made sure that Tony's story was not just about the suits. It was about the people the suits were built to PROTECT.

Tony used to say, in his more honest moments, that the best thing he ever built was not the Mark 49. It was not even the Arc Reactor.

The best thing he ever built was the life he got to share with her.
