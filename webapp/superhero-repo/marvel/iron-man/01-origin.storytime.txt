Tony Stark had everything.

He had a house on the cliffs in Malibu, California, where the ocean crashed against the rocks below and the sunset painted everything gold every evening. He had a workshop full of every machine and tool a brilliant mind could desire. He had Stark Industries — the most famous technology company in the world, a company his father Howard Stark had built and Tony had made even bigger and brighter. He had the name "Tony Stark" on the cover of every magazine, and everywhere he went, people cheered.

He had everything, and he had not thought very carefully about how he had gotten it.

Then came the day in the desert, and Tony Stark was forced to think about absolutely everything.

He had been traveling to demonstrate a new Stark Industries weapon — a massive missile called the Jericho — in the mountains of Afghanistan. His convoy was moving through a valley of red sand and ancient rocks when — BOOM. An explosion. Smoke. Fire. And Tony Stark woke up in a dark cave with a deep pain in his chest and the sound of dripping water in his ears.

There was a car battery next to him, wired to a device that had been placed just beneath his skin near his heart. It was keeping him alive. The shrapnel from one of his own company's missiles was trying to reach his heart, and only the electromagnet in his chest was holding it back.

And beside him was a man named Ho Yinsen.

Ho Yinsen was a physicist — a scientist who understood things that most people could not begin to imagine. He was gentle and calm, and he had been a prisoner in that cave for longer than Tony. He had used his skill and care to save Tony's life. And when Tony asked why, Yinsen looked at him quietly and said: "So that your life might be worth saving."

The people who held them prisoner wanted Tony to build weapons for them. But Tony Stark had seen what his weapons did when they were used this way, in the wrong hands, in the wrong places. And for the first time in his life, he could not look away from it.

So instead, secretly, working in the flickering light of the cave while their captors slept, Tony and Yinsen built something different.

First, they built a tiny Arc Reactor — a miniaturized version of the great power source Tony knew from Stark Industries — round and glowing a soft blue, powerful enough to light a city block and small enough to sit in Tony's chest. It replaced the car battery. It kept him alive.

Then they built the Mark I.

It was not beautiful. It was scrap metal and welded seams and mismatched parts. It was heavy and loud and crude. But it was armor — a full suit of iron armor, big enough to wear, with rocket boots for thrust and weapons in the arms.

They worked and they worked. And when the captors discovered what they were doing — when the shouting started outside the door — Yinsen ran out into the corridor to give Tony the last few minutes the suit needed to power up.

Tony found Yinsen dying in the passageway, and Yinsen looked up at him and smiled a very tired, very peaceful smile and said: "Don't waste it, Tony. Don't waste your life."

Then the armor powered up.

BOOM. WHOOOOOOSH. Fire poured from the palms of the Mark I as Tony crashed through the cave walls and out into the blazing Afghan sunlight. He flew — rough and rattling and barely controlled — and when he was clear of the mountains, he landed in the desert and the Mark I fell apart around him.

He sat in the sand, alone, under the enormous blue sky.

He thought about Ho Yinsen. He thought about everything he had made, and where it had gone, and what it had done. He thought about the word "waste" and the word "life."

Back in Malibu, he shut down the weapons division of Stark Industries. Then he went down to his workshop — the one with the ocean view and the sunset and all the machines — and he started building.

Not weapons.

A new suit. Sleeker. Faster. Red and gold, like the sky at sunset over the Pacific.

He called it the Mark III. And the man who stepped out of the workshop in that gleaming, beautiful suit — the man who looked up at the California sky and ignited his boot jets and rose into the air with a sound like a roar of thunder — was no longer just Tony Stark, billionaire.

He was Iron Man.

And somewhere, in the silence of a cave far away, he kept a promise.
