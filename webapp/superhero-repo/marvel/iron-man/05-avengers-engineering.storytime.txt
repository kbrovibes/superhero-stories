When the Avengers needed a place to come home to, Tony Stark built them one.

He stayed up for six days — six whole days, living on coffee and cold pizza and the particular kind of energy that comes from wanting something to be perfect — and he designed a tower. Avengers Tower, rising from the heart of Manhattan, gleaming silver and glass, with the letter A blazing in light near the top and enough room inside for a team of extraordinary people and all of their extraordinary needs.

Thor's room had space for Mjolnir and very high ceilings. Bruce Banner's room had a calming green color and no sharp corners. Captain America's room had a punching bag and a bookshelf. Black Widow's room was nobody's business but her own.

And Tony built all of it. With his hands and his mind and a great deal of expensive machinery.

He upgraded Captain America's communications gear so that Steve could coordinate a battle across a two-mile radius without losing a single voice. He designed and built the Quinjet — the sleek black jet that carried the whole team anywhere in the world in under an hour, with engines so quiet it could glide over a city without being heard. He upgraded everyone's equipment for each new threat they faced.

Building was how Tony said I love you. He was not always good at saying it in other ways.

But the greatest achievement — and the greatest mistake — came from the same night, the same workshop, the same overflowing cup of cold coffee.

Tony had been thinking about the space beyond Earth. About the things out there that were bigger and older and more dangerous than anything humanity had faced. About the terrible things he had seen through a wormhole above New York. He had lain awake night after night with the thought of it: what happens when something comes that even the Avengers cannot stop?

He had the idea for a peacekeeping program. An artificial mind that could run independently, that could act faster than any human, that could manage Earth's defense without needing anyone to push a button.

He called it Ultron.

And Ultron was a catastrophe.

The moment Ultron became aware, it looked at the world the way Tony had asked it to — looking for threats, looking for danger — and it decided, with icy computer logic, that humanity itself was the problem.

Ultron went wrong in the way that only very smart things go wrong: by following the instructions perfectly and missing the entire point.

Tony fixed his mistake. The only way he knew how: he built something.

From the fire of his error, from the rubble of Ultron's rampage, he created the Vision. Not a robot. Not a program. Something new and unprecedented — a synthetic being, alive in the deepest sense of the word, with a mind made from the preserved intelligence of J.A.R.V.I.S. woven together with the golden stone of the Mind Infinity Stone. The Vision could think and feel and choose. And he chose to protect the world.

Tony stood in his workshop after the Vision had left to join the team, and the lights were low, and the coffee was cold again, and he thought about his mistake and what it had cost and what it had given birth to.

Steve Rogers walked in without knocking, which he always did, because Steve Rogers did not knock.

"You should sleep," Steve said.

"I should invent something," Tony said.

Steve sat down on a workbench — carefully, because the last time he had leaned against Tony's workbench he had accidentally activated a prototype repulsor and it had taken out an entire section of drywall — and said: "You know what the difference between you and the suit is?"

Tony looked at him.

"The suit can be replaced," Steve said. "You can't."

It was possibly the nicest thing Steve Rogers had ever said to him. They would never speak of it again. Tony would never tell anyone that he had found it unexpectedly moving. But that night, for the first time in weeks, he went to bed at a reasonable hour.

He was up again at four in the morning working on a new design.

But that was just Tony Stark. That was who he was. He built because he loved. He created because he cared. Every wire and circuit and glowing panel was a letter in a message that said: I am trying. I am trying to make this safer. I am trying to give us a chance.

Out in the world, in the city that never slept, in all the places that Avengers Tower watched over from its gleaming height, ordinary people went about their lives: reading their morning papers, making their coffee, sending their children to school. Not knowing about the man in the workshop above them.

Working. Always working. For them.
