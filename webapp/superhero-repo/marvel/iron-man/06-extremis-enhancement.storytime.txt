Tony Stark loved building things almost as much as he loved Pepper Potts and his dear friend Rhodey, which is to say, an enormous amount.

He had built the Arc Reactor in his chest. He had built dozens of Iron Man suits. He had built Stark Industries into one of the cleverest technology companies in the world. He had built J.A.R.V.I.S., the gentle British-voiced AI who kept him company in his workshop.

But there came a moment, in a story called the Extremis crisis, when Tony almost built TOO much.

It began with a brilliant, dangerous scientist named Aldrich Killian. Killian had developed something extraordinary — a special treatment called Extremis that could rewrite the human body's own genetic code. A person who took Extremis could regrow lost limbs, heal terrible wounds, and even glow hot enough to bend metal with their bare hands.

It was incredible science. It was also incredibly dangerous, because Killian was using it to make warriors loyal only to him.

Tony fought one of those Extremis-enhanced soldiers and lost. Badly. The soldier was simply too strong, too fast, too HEALED to stop. Tony limped home, his suit dented, his pride dented, and he sat in his workshop for a long time staring at his hands.

What if, he thought, the Iron Man armor did not have to be something he put ON? What if it could be something he WAS?

What if his own cells could carry pieces of the armor inside them?

It was a wild idea. It was a Tony Stark idea.

He spent weeks working on it. He took the Extremis virus and adapted it. He stripped out the dangerous parts. He kept only the connection — the way it could merge with a human nervous system. And then, alone in his workshop in the middle of the night, he injected a version of the modified virus into himself.

It hurt. Oh, it hurt. Tony curled up on the cold workshop floor for hours while his body shook and shifted around the new technology. He thought, for a while, that he had made a terrible mistake.

But then, slowly, something extraordinary happened.

Tony stood up. He looked at his armor across the room. He thought about it.

The armor came to him.

The pieces lifted from their stand. They flew across the workshop. They locked onto his arms and his legs and his chest as if they were part of his own body. Click. Snap. Whirr. The faceplate snapped down. In a single fluid motion, with no fumbling, no manual entry, no boots needing to be stepped into, Tony Stark was Iron Man.

He almost laughed out loud.

The new connection went even deeper than that. Tony could close his eyes and SEE through Stark Industries satellites high in orbit. He could FEEL the steady electronic heartbeat of his global computer networks. The wall between Tony and his machines, between his mind and J.A.R.V.I.S., was disappearing. He was becoming, in a real sense, a being of both flesh and circuitry.

For a little while, it felt like he had finally caught up to his own genius.

But the new power had a cost.

Tony's worry-mind, which had always been busy, started running even faster. He could not turn it off. He could see threats coming from every direction at once. He started to believe — quietly at first, then more loudly — that he was the only one who could protect the world. He began making decisions without his teammates. He stopped sleeping. He stopped trusting.

Pepper noticed. She would catch him standing alone on the balcony in the middle of the night, his eyes flickering with bluish light, listening to far-off conversations on satellite frequencies.

"Tony," she would say gently, "come back to me."

He could hear her. Just barely.

The Extremis technology was unstable in other people, too. Some of Killian's enhanced soldiers began to overheat, their bodies unable to handle the strain. It was hurting people. Tony watched it hurt people, and he knew, in his bones, that something he had built was being used for harm.

In the end, after a long struggle, Tony did something very brave. He decided to give up most of the Extremis enhancement.

He went into a quiet medical chamber. He let the doctors carefully remove most of the virus from his nervous system. He gave up the instant armor. He gave up the satellite vision. He gave up the digital "godhood" that had felt so good and so scary at the same time.

When he came out, he was just Tony again. Just a man. Just a brilliant, tired, kind-hearted man who still had to put his suit on the old-fashioned way — piece by piece, one boot at a time.

He smiled. It felt right.

The Extremis story was one of the most important in Iron Man's long history. It taught Tony something he would remember for the rest of his life. The greatest temptation for a brilliant person is to believe they can fix everything themselves. The greatest STRENGTH is knowing when to stop. When to step back. When to be just a man again.

Pepper kissed him when he came home. Rhodey gave him a big bear hug. J.A.R.V.I.S., in his quiet British voice, said simply, "Welcome back, sir."

Tony Stark sat down at his workbench, picked up a screwdriver, and began to tinker with a small bolt. Just a small bolt. With his own ordinary hands.

It was, he realized, exactly enough.
