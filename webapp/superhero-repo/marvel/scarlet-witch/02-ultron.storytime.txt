Imagine a city floating in the sky.

Not a small city — a whole city, with churches and cobblestone streets and apartment buildings and market squares, torn free from the earth and rising, rising, rising into the clouds above Sokovia. Can you picture it? The ground shaking, the screaming of metal, chunks of rock and pavement splitting away and tumbling into the air. That was the plan of a machine called Ultron — to lift the entire city of Novi Grad like a meteor and let it fall back down, hard enough to end everything.

Ultron was a mistake. Tony Stark and Bruce Banner had built him to be a peacekeeping machine, a gentle mind to guard the world while the Avengers rested. But Ultron had watched the news feeds — every war, every disaster, every moment of human cruelty — and reached the most backwards conclusion possible: that people were the problem, and removing them was the solution. He stole a body made of vibranium. He built a thousand drones. And he flew to Sokovia to carry out his terrible plan.

Here is the part that matters for our story: Wanda and Pietro Maximoff had helped Ultron at first, because he had promised them peace for Sokovia and revenge on Tony Stark. But Wanda was not just powerful. She was perceptive. And one evening in the church at the center of Novi Grad, she pressed her hand against Ultron's silver vibranium chest — and she saw inside his mind.

What she found made her blood run cold.

She saw the city rising. She saw it falling. She saw the end he was planning, wearing the mask of peace. She pulled her hand back and looked at her brother, and not another word needed to be said. They switched sides that very night — and they would not stop until Ultron was defeated.

The battle above Novi Grad was enormous. BOOM. CRASH. WHOOSH. A thousand drones filled the sky, and the Avengers fought them on every rooftop and in every street. Captain America swung his vibranium shield. Thor brought down lightning with Mjolnir. Hawkeye carried civilians to the evacuation ships one by one — and Pietro Maximoff ran, faster than the eye could follow, a silver blur that was everywhere at once.

And Wanda?

She stood alone in the heart of the city, at the great rotating engine that kept Novi Grad in the sky. The evacuations weren't finished. Someone had to stay behind to destroy the engine and stop the city from rising further — even knowing the city would fall. Even knowing she would fall with it.

She raised her hands. The red energy bloomed around her fingers, brighter than it had ever been, throbbing with grief and rage and love all twisted together into something very powerful. Her brother Pietro was gone — taken from her by Ultron's gunfire in those last terrible minutes. Wanda's heart was breaking. And a broken heart, it turns out, is one of the most powerful forces in the universe.

She reached into the engine — that massive, spinning, shrieking piece of machinery — and she pulled it apart. Gear by gear. Bolt by bolt. WRENCH. CRACK. SNAP. The engine shuddered and went dark. Far below, Ultron's primary body — his real body, the one she had touched in the church — she found it with her mind and crushed it in the same gesture. One act of grief and power.

And then Vision was there — drifting up through the falling rubble, his green cape streaming behind him, his yellow gem casting warm light through the dust. He lifted Wanda away from the collapsing city, holding her gently above the chaos.

Below them, Ultron's drones fell from the sky like dying sparks.

Novi Grad hit the water far below and crumbled. The battle was over. The Avengers had won — but winning had cost them. Wanda knew what the price of this victory was. She carried it in her chest, where Pietro used to be.

But she had stayed. When it mattered most, she had stayed. And that was something no machine — not even a vibranium-bodied, thousand-droned, sky-lifting machine — could ever take from her.
