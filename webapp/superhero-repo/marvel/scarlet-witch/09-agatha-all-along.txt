Long before Wanda Maximoff was born, the witches of the world wrote in their oldest books about a person who had not yet arrived. They called her the Scarlet Witch. She would not study magic; she would simply make it. She would not borrow power from other dimensions like the sorcerers of Kamar-Taj; the power would grow inside her like a second heart. The witches were so frightened of this future person that they begged the universe to never let her be born. They wrote her into their prophecies in red ink. They underlined her three times.

Agatha Harkness had been alive for three hundred and forty years. She had survived the Salem witch trials by stealing the magic of the witches who tried to punish her. She kept her power neat and hidden, lived in pleasant houses, baked snickerdoodles, and always — always — kept one eye on the news. When Agatha first felt the red glow of Wanda's chaos magic ripple across the East Coast of the United States, she packed her bags, drove to a sleepy town called Westview in New Jersey, and rented the house right next door to the brand-new Maximoff family. She wore pearls. She baked her famous snickerdoodles. She called herself Agnes. She smiled all the time.

For weeks, Agatha pretended to be just a friendly neighbor. She watched Wanda raise Billy and Tommy. She listened. She measured the size of Wanda's power. And then, when Wanda was tired and grieving and tangled in her own story, Agatha pulled back the curtain. Her cozy basement turned into a candlelit witch's cellar full of runes. Her eyes flashed purple. She sang her famous, cheerful little song — "It was Agatha all along!" — and Wanda's whole life suddenly looked very different.

Agatha did not want to hurt Wanda exactly. She wanted Wanda's power for herself. She told Wanda, in the middle of their fight through the streets of Westview, what the old witches' books had said. She showed Wanda the name of the Scarlet Witch, written in red ink and underlined three times. "You were a myth, Wanda," she said. "You aren't supposed to exist." For one long moment, Wanda almost believed her. The witches were afraid. Maybe Wanda should be too.

But Wanda Maximoff had already been afraid of herself for too many years. She did not need a three-hundred-year-old witch to tell her she was dangerous. She had decided in Sokovia, and in Wakanda, and in Westview, that she would choose what to do with the power inside her. With a single flick of her hand she painted Agatha's runes in red light across the ceiling of the Hex, so that no witch trapped within could use her magic except for Wanda herself. Agatha froze. Wanda lifted into the air. Her hair grew long and dark. A scarlet headpiece formed around her brow like a crown. And the Scarlet Witch, the one the old books had warned about for centuries, opened her eyes for the very first time and chose, very quietly, to be good.
