Once upon a time — or maybe it wasn't quite real, maybe it was something in between — there was a perfect little town called Westview, New Jersey, and inside it everything was simple and warm and good.

Wanda Maximoff lived there with Vision. They had two sons named Billy and Tommy who ran through the house being loud and wonderful. The town was quiet and kind. Neighbors waved from porches. There were picket fences and friendly mailboxes and a woman next door named Agnes who was always smiling, always wearing her pearl necklace, always arriving at just the right moment with a plate of snickerdoodle cookies still warm from the oven.

Everything in Westview was like a TV program from long ago — black and white at first, then slowly filling with color. Wanda and Vision moved through it like actors who didn't quite remember learning their lines, because somewhere underneath the story, in the real world outside the glow, something enormous was happening.

Wanda was grieving. Not quietly — loudly, in a way that bent reality itself. The loss of Vision, her loss, her love — had poured out of her as a shining red bubble of magic that had swallowed the entire town of Westview. The people inside were playing parts in a show Wanda hadn't known she was writing. Outside the bubble, S.W.O.R.D. agents crowded the boundary. A brave woman named Monica Rambeau pushed her way through the barrier, again and again, trying to reach Wanda and understand.

But Agnes kept smiling. Agnes always seemed to know things just a moment before she should. Agnes was always nearby when things got strange.

One day the curtain came down.

Agnes — always smiling Agnes — stepped forward and let her smile fall away. And what stood behind it was something much older, much stranger, and much more powerful. Her name was Agatha Harkness. She had been alive for centuries, surviving trials and catastrophes and the burning of Salem itself by stealing magic from other witches. She had learned to smell power the way other people smell rain: from far away, before it arrives.

And from the moment she had sensed Wanda's red bubble appear over Westview, Agatha had known exactly what she was dealing with. The Scarlet Witch — a being spoken of in ancient books written by witches who had been gone for hundreds of years. A prophecy of someone whose magic was not borrowed or learned but grown from within, capable of rewriting the laws of reality itself. Chaos magic. The rarest, wildest, most dangerous kind.

Agatha wanted it.

The pleasant street outside her house rewrote itself — runes carved into the walls, candles blazing, the cheerful porch becoming a dark and crackling place of old power. Agatha hurled purple lightning. CRACK. ZAP. The bolts crashed through the town's neat illusion.

And Wanda answered. Red energy bloomed from her palms, building walls of force through the streets of Westview, pressing back against the purple with everything she had.

The battle moved through the hexagonal bubble of the town — two witches, one very old and one discovering her true nature, throwing their power at each other across the streets where the sitcom neighbors stood frozen. BOOM. FLASH. The red and purple light painted the whole sky above Westview.

Then Wanda did something clever.

She had noticed the runes — the ancient carved symbols that Agatha had placed all through the hex bubble, the symbols that channeled and controlled magical energy. And instead of simply fighting Agatha's power, Wanda had been painting her own runes, quietly, into the walls of the bubble itself while they fought. The whole hex was lined with them now.

When Agatha reached out to take Wanda's chaos magic — to steal it the way she had stolen from witches before — Wanda activated the runes.

The power snapped back. Agatha's hands went still. Her eyes went wide. The runes of the Scarlet Witch trapped her, powerless, inside her Agnes costume — the smiling neighbor, the pearl necklace, the snickerdoodle smile — unable to do harm.

Wanda stood in the middle of the battle-cleared street and for the first time said the name aloud. The Scarlet Witch. That was who she was. Not a HYDRA experiment, not a grief-stricken woman who had made a terrible mistake — though she was all of those things too. She was the Scarlet Witch, written of in ancient prophecy, older than any spell book Agatha carried.

And later, when she returned to her quiet cabin in the mountains, she found a book on the table. Dark leather. Strange symbols. The Darkhold — the book of the damned — had come to her.

She opened it. Because knowing your power is only the beginning. Understanding it is the work that never ends.
