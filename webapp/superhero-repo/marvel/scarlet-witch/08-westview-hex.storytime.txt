This is the story of the strangest, warmest, saddest dream Wanda Maximoff ever had. And how she chose, in the end, to wake up from it.

The story begins after Wanda had lost almost everyone she loved. Her parents in Sokovia. Her brother Pietro on the bridge in Novi Grad. And then twice, twice, she had lost Vision — once in Wakanda when she had to destroy the Mind Stone herself, and then again when the universe gave Vision back as a quieter, paler version and then took him away again too.

Wanda was very, very tired. The kind of tired that lives deep down in your bones and won't go away no matter how long you sleep.

One quiet afternoon she got into a small car and drove east. She drove for hours. Eventually she came to a sleepy little town in New Jersey called Westview. It had one main street, a few shops, a tidy little neighborhood with picket fences, and an empty lot at the end of one of the streets.

Vision had bought that empty lot, once, in secret. He had been planning to surprise Wanda with it. In her pocket was a folded piece of paper she had found in his things after he was gone. On it, in Vision's careful handwriting, were just four small words.

"To grow old in."

Wanda stood on the empty lot and read those four words. Something inside her broke very softly, the way a vase falls in slow motion. And her chaos magic — that strange, wild magic that listens to feelings as much as it listens to thought — overflowed.

A great red bubble bloomed outward from where she stood. It expanded across the street, across the houses, across the whole town. The bubble was so big it swallowed Westview entirely. From the outside, nobody could see in. From the inside, everything was about to become very strange and very wonderful.

Wanda did not know she was making the magic. That is the most important thing to understand. Her grief was so big it was building something all by itself, without her telling it to.

When she opened her eyes inside the bubble, the world was black and white.

The empty lot had become a sweet little house with a white picket fence. The doorbell played a cheerful tune. The neighbors waved as they walked their dogs. A laugh track giggled in the background whenever somebody told a joke. And there, coming through the front door with a briefcase in his hand, was Vision. Alive. Smiling. Saying, "Honey, I'm home."

Wanda hugged him so tightly that her arms ached. For the first time in years, she was not alone.

Inside the bubble, the years passed very quickly. The black and white softened into color. The 1950s slid into the 1960s, with grooving music and bright orange furniture. The 60s slid into the 70s, with brown wallpaper and bell-bottom pants. The 70s slid into the 80s, with big hair and bright pink jackets. The 80s slid into the 90s, with chunky sneakers and silly sitcom jokes. Every decade Wanda's house got a new look. Every decade Vision got a new sweater. Every decade the neighbors waved a little differently. But the love at the center of the show stayed exactly the same.

Then one day — and this was the most magical day of all — Wanda discovered something incredible. She was going to have babies. Not one. TWO. Twin boys, just like she and Pietro had been twins.

She named them Billy and Tommy.

They were born on a single magical afternoon, in a kitchen full of light. By dinnertime they were already toddlers, crawling under the table. By bedtime they were five years old, in matching pajamas, asking for one more story. By the next weekend they were ten years old, racing each other down the sidewalk on their bicycles.

Because Wanda's magic does not run on calendars. It runs on love. And love grows children very quickly when it wants to.

Billy was the quieter twin. He could hear what other people were thinking. He could feel his mother's sadness from the next room, even when she was trying very hard to hide it.

Tommy was the louder twin. He could run faster than the eye could follow. WHOOSH! He would zip from the kitchen to the front yard before his shoelaces had even finished tying. He ran exactly the way his Uncle Pietro used to run, although nobody inside the bubble could remember the name Pietro.

Wanda had her whole little family. Vision. Billy. Tommy. A house. A street. A town. A life. It was not real, exactly. But it was made out of the realest thing Wanda still carried inside her — her love for all the people who were gone.

Now. Outside the red bubble, things were not so happy.

The real people of Westview were trapped. They were playing parts in Wanda's show that they had not chosen. They could not see their real families. They could not eat the foods they wanted. They could not say the things they wanted to say. Wanda had not meant to trap them. But her grief had spread out into them, and now they were stuck.

A group called S.W.O.R.D. — that's an organization that watches out for strange things on Earth — set up cameras at the edge of the bubble. An Air Force captain named Monica Rambeau, a brilliant scientist named Darcy Lewis, and an old friend of Thor's named Jimmy Woo all stood at the boundary and tried to figure out who, exactly, was running the show.

When Monica was eventually pulled inside the bubble herself, she met Wanda. And she said something very kind. She told Wanda, gently, that the real people of Westview were hurting.

Wanda did not want to hear it. Inside her show, she had a husband and two little boys. Outside the show, she had nothing. Who would willingly give up everything?

But Wanda Maximoff was not just a witch. She was a hero. And heroes, when they finally see what they have done, choose to do the right thing even when it breaks their hearts in half.

Wanda kissed Billy and Tommy on the forehead and tucked them into bed one last time. She told them how proud of them she was. She told them she loved them, and that one day, somehow, they would find each other again.

She walked downstairs. Vision was waiting at the door. He smiled at her in his calm, gentle way. He said, "Goodbye, Wanda." She held his hand. She said, "Goodbye, my love."

Then Wanda lifted her arms and pulled the bubble down. The red glow shrank. The 1990s wallpaper vanished. The white picket fence dissolved. Billy and Tommy and Vision and the cheerful doorbell all dimmed like a fading television set.

When the bubble was gone, the real town of Westview blinked back into the warm afternoon sun. The real people of Westview hugged each other and went home to their real families. They were free.

And Wanda Maximoff walked alone down the empty street, with a long red coat trailing behind her, leaving the warmest dream she would ever have.

Because real people, she had decided, mattered more than even the most beautiful dream in the world. And that — listen to this carefully — that is what made her not just a witch with magic, but a true hero. The kind who knows when to wake up. The kind who chooses the truth, even when the truth is the loneliest thing in the world.
