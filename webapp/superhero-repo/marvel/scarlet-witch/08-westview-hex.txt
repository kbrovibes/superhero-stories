After Wanda Maximoff lost Vision to Thanos, the universe slowly stitched itself back together. The Avengers won. The dust came back. But the Vision who returned was a quieter, paler version of himself, and not long afterward, he was gone again too. Wanda drove east in a small car, all alone, until she reached a sleepy New Jersey town called Westview. She got out at an empty lot Vision had once bought for the two of them. In her hand was a folded piece of paper. On it, in his careful handwriting, was a single line: "To grow old in." Wanda's grief was so large that it overflowed her on that empty lot, and her chaos magic spilled out in a glowing red bubble that swallowed the whole town.

Inside the bubble, Westview became something new. The first day, everything was black and white, like a 1950s television show. There was a sweet little house with a picket fence. There was a Vision who was alive and well, smiling at her in the doorway. There was a doorbell that played a cheerful tune. The neighbors all wore aprons and laughed at the right times. Wanda did not know she was making the show. She only knew that for the first time in years, she was not alone.

The decades passed quickly. The black and white softened into color. The clothes changed from poodle skirts to bell bottoms to shoulder pads to bright nineties prints. The little house got new wallpaper every era. And one day, in the middle of a 1970s episode, Wanda discovered something incredible — she was pregnant. Inside her belly were two impossible little boys. She named them Billy and Tommy. They were born in a single, magical afternoon and were toddlers by dinner. They grew to ten years old over a single weekend, because Wanda's magic ran on love, not on calendars. Billy could read minds, like his mother. Tommy could run faster than the eye could follow, like his uncle Pietro.

For a little while, in that strange red bubble outside Westview, Wanda had everything she had ever lost. A husband. Two sons. A neighborhood. A life. It was not real, exactly. But it was made of the realest thing she carried — her love for the people who were gone. Outside the bubble, a group called S.W.O.R.D. and an Air Force captain named Monica Rambeau pressed their faces against the boundary and tried to figure out who, exactly, was running the show.

When Wanda finally understood what she had done — when she saw the real people of Westview trapped inside her story, hurting, frozen mid-sentence — she did the bravest thing she could think of. She let the bubble go. She kissed Billy and Tommy goodnight one last time. She watched Vision dim like a fading light. The hex closed. The real town blinked into the sun. And Wanda Maximoff walked away from the warmest dream she would ever have, because real people, she had decided, mattered more than any dream she could make.
