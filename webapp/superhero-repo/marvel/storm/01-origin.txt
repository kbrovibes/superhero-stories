Ororo Munroe was born beneath a wide blue sky, and from her very first breath, the weather seemed to notice her. Her mother, N'Dare, was a princess from Kenya, and her father, David Munroe, was an American photographer. The family lived happily in Cairo, Egypt, until a terrible accident took both her parents away when Ororo was only six years old. She was left alone in a foreign city, bewildered and frightened, with no one to look after her.

To survive, Ororo learned to pick pockets in the crowded bazaars of Cairo. A clever thief named Achmed el-Gibar took her in and taught her every trick she would need to stay alive on the city's streets. Ororo was quick-fingered and sharp-eyed, and she never stole from anyone who could not afford to lose what she took. But even as she darted through the marketplace, something was stirring inside her — a strange warmth that gathered at her fingertips whenever the wind picked up, a flicker of silver in her dark eyes whenever clouds rolled in from the desert.

When Ororo was a teenager, she left Cairo and walked south, drawn by an instinct she could not name. She traveled all the way across the Sahara Desert until she reached the open, golden Serengeti plain of East Africa. There, for the first time in her life, she felt the sky answer her. She lifted her hands, and rain fell. She whispered to the wind, and it carried her upward. The people of a nearby village watched in wonder and gave her a name that meant everything: Goddess.

For years Ororo lived on the Serengeti, tending the land with gentle rains and steering fierce storms away from the villages that depended on her. But one day, a man named Professor Charles Xavier came to find her. He explained that she was a mutant — that the power within her was real, inherited, magnificent, and that she was not alone. At his school in Westchester, New York, called the Xavier Institute, others like her had gathered: Cyclops, who fired optic blasts; Jean Grey, who could move objects with her mind; and Wolverine, whose senses were as sharp as the blades in his fists.

Ororo said farewell to the Serengeti and flew north. She became Storm, wearing white in honor of the clouds she commanded. Under Professor Xavier's guidance she learned to aim her gift rather than just feel it — to call down a precise bolt of lightning, to stir a single cloud without disturbing the sky beyond it. The X-Men became her family. And the girl who had once survived by picking pockets in Cairo was now a hero who could hold back a hurricane.
