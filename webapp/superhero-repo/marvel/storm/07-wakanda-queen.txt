The first time Ororo Munroe met T'Challa, Prince of Wakanda, she was a teenager crossing East Africa on foot, still learning who she was and what her gifts might mean. T'Challa was a young man moving through the same region, testing himself against the same open world. They spent only a few days together before their paths divided, but something was planted in that brief meeting that did not disappear.

Years passed. Ororo became Storm, a cornerstone of the X-Men. T'Challa became the Black Panther, warrior-king of Wakanda — the most advanced nation on Earth, hidden in the mountains of East Africa and built on a mineral called Vibranium that no other country possessed. When their paths crossed again, the years between them dissolved quickly, the way clouds clear before strong sunlight.

T'Challa proposed marriage at the Great Mound, the sacred Vibranium deposit at the heart of Wakanda, surrounded by the red earth and the close green hills of the nation he protected. Ororo said yes.

The wedding was one of the great events in the memory of Wakanda. Leaders and dignitaries from across the African continent came to Birnin Zana, the Golden City, which was alive with color and ceremony. The X-Men attended: Professor Xavier, Cyclops, Jean Grey, Wolverine — all of them watching Ororo take her place as Queen Consort of Wakanda. Storm wore white and gold, and the sky above Birnin Zana was, by general agreement, the most beautiful it had been in living memory, which was not a coincidence.

As queen, Ororo found a different kind of purpose. Wakanda was already extraordinary, but it was also isolated, and a queen with Storm's gifts and history — orphan, street child, African goddess, global hero — could speak across boundaries that a king occasionally could not. She brought the wide, inclusive lens of the Xavier Institute's ideals to a nation accustomed to secrecy. She argued for Wakanda's engagement with the rest of the world.

She was also still Storm, in every sense. She kept her place among the X-Men. When duty called her back to Westchester, T'Challa understood — he was the same. They were two people who each carried enormous responsibilities, and they had chosen to carry them together, which made both of them stronger. The Serengeti girl from Cairo and the warrior-king of the most advanced nation on Earth, side by side at the edge of Wakanda's great escarpment, looking out at a continent that had always been home to both of them.
