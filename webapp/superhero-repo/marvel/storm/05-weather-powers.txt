On a clear Tuesday morning at the Xavier Institute, Professor Charles Xavier asked Storm to explain her powers to the newest X-Men students. She took them outside to the wide lawn that rolled down toward the Hudson River and stood barefoot in the grass, because she always said she thought better when she could feel the earth.

Ororo described it this way: she did not control the weather the way you control a light switch. She felt the weather — every current of air, every charged particle, every shift in temperature and pressure. The atmosphere was like a great living thing, and she was fluent in its language. She could sense a thunderstorm building three hundred miles away the way you might feel a change in mood in a room before anyone spoke.

She demonstrated. She raised a warm updraft that lifted three students two feet off the ground before she gently set them back down. She pulled moisture from the air until a small cloud formed above the lawn, no wider than the shadow of a house. She made it rain on one square yard while the yard beside it stayed completely dry. The students who got wet laughed. It made very clear why her powers were considered among the most formidable in the world.

But Storm was careful to explain the limits too. Her power was tied to her emotional state. When she was angry, she had to work hard not to summon a storm that could harm the very people she was protecting. When she was frightened, the wind could turn erratic. Professor Xavier had spent years teaching her meditation and breathing exercises to keep her feelings steady so her weather-work remained intentional. The most dangerous storm she had ever called, she told them, was one she had not meant to call — she had been fourteen and grieving, and the sky went dark for forty miles.

She also spoke about flying. She was not technically flying under her own power — she generated and rode a column of air, adjusting it constantly to steer. On a clear day she could reach sixty miles per hour. Inside a good tailwind she had hit ninety. The closest comparison was surfing: you did not fight the wave, you read it and worked with it.

One of the students raised a hand. "Do you ever get tired of it?" she asked.

Ororo looked up at the sky for a quiet moment. "Never," she said. "Every sky is different." She smiled, called a gentle gust that ruffled every notebook on the lawn, and walked back inside.
