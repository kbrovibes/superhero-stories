Once upon a time, hiding in the dark space behind every sleeping mind, there lived a creature made entirely of shadows and fear.

His name was the Shadow King. Long ago, before he became a monster of the mind, he had been a real man named Amahl Farouk — a crimelord who ran Cairo's underworld from the shadows of its oldest streets. Professor Charles Xavier had defeated him once, and Farouk's body was gone. But his mind had survived, slipping away into the astral plane — that invisible, dreamlike world that exists behind the ordinary one, woven out of thoughts and memory and feeling.

And there he had waited. Patient and cold. Choosing the right moment.

Then he found Storm.

She was sleeping soundly in her room at the Xavier Institute one autumn night, the window cracked open just enough for the cool Westchester air to come in, when she felt something at the edge of her dream. A shadow that did not belong to anything.

The next morning Storm was quiet at breakfast. Her tea went cold.

"You all right?" Wolverine asked from across the table, his amber eyes calm and watchful.

"Fine," said Ororo. But she was not fine.

The second night was worse. The Shadow King went deeper, and he found the thing he had been looking for — Storm's oldest, most private fear. Claustrophobia, the terrible dread of tight and enclosed spaces, left over from a day long ago in Cairo when she had been very small and the walls of a collapsing building had pressed in around her. That fear lived in a quiet corner of her heart, and the Shadow King wrapped himself around it like winter around a fire.

She woke with a GASP that night, certain the ceiling was moving down. Her room was perfectly ordinary. Nothing was wrong. But her heart was pounding so hard she could feel it in her fingertips.

She did not sleep again that night. Or the next.

On the third day, Wolverine found her on the roof of the Xavier Institute before dawn, sitting with her knees pulled up, watching the sky go from black to deep blue to pale gold. He sat down beside her. He did not ask questions. He just sat there, warm and steady as a rock, until Storm said quietly, "I think something is in my dreams."

Jean Grey reached out with her telepathy and searched — gently, carefully — and there it was. Coiled around Storm's mind like a cold vine, feeding on her exhaustion. The Shadow King.

Professor Xavier's face went very still when Jean told him. He had faced Farouk before. He knew what it would take. He settled into his chair, closed his eyes, and followed the Shadow King into the astral plane.

There, in that strange invisible world of thought and memory, they fought. You could not have seen it with ordinary eyes. But Storm could feel it — a distant rumble behind her eyes, like thunder twenty miles away.

And while Xavier fought, the Shadow King pressed down on Storm with everything he had. Tight spaces. Locked rooms. The rubble of Cairo. The dark. The walls closing in. He wanted her to give up. He wanted her to make herself very small and stay that way.

Ororo closed her eyes.

She felt small. She felt trapped. She felt the old Cairo fear rising up inside her like floodwater.

And then she remembered.

She had walked across the Sahara Desert alone. She had stood on the Serengeti plain and made the rain come down. She had looked up at the open sky and felt it answer her. She was not a small girl in a dark room. She was Storm.

She took a breath, and in her mind she spread her arms as wide as they could go — and the sky came in. The Serengeti sky, enormous and golden and full of wind, so wide and bright and FREE that the Shadow King's darkness had nowhere left to live. It dissolved. It thinned. It became nothing.

Xavier struck in that instant. He scattered the Shadow King's consciousness across the astral plane like dandelion seeds in a gale.

Storm's eyes opened.

Real sunlight lay across her bed. A bird was singing somewhere in the elm tree by the window. The room was exactly the right size.

There was a knock at the door, and Wolverine came in holding a mug of tea. He set it on the nightstand and said nothing at all — which was exactly the right thing to say.

Storm wrapped her hands around the warm mug and laughed. It was a bright, real, surprised laugh, the kind you laugh when you have just found your way home after a very long walk.

She was all right. She was more than all right.

She was Storm. And she had won.
