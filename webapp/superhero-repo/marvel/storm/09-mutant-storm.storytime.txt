Once upon a time, at fourteen minutes past five in the morning, every alarm in the Xavier Institute went off at once.

The sound pulled the X-Men awake in an instant. Ororo was on her feet before the second pulse.

In the control room, Professor Xavier read the incoming reports with a very still face.

"Thirty Sentinel units," he said. "Mark VII. Deployed simultaneously. Targeting every known mutant location in the northeastern United States." He looked up. "This is the largest Sentinel assault ever attempted."

Storm looked at Cyclops, and Jean Grey, and Wolverine.

"Cyclops — ground defenses," she said. Her voice was steady. "Jean — telepathic network. Find every mutant in the region and warn them before the Sentinels do. Wolverine — perimeter."

She walked out the front door and flew straight up.

Up past the dark treelines. Up past the low winter clouds. Up into the iron-grey sky where the temperature was minus twenty and the wind was forty miles an hour, and the whole enormous dome of the atmosphere surrounded her in every direction.

She felt the weather. Not just the local weather — all of it. The cold mass of Arctic air pressing down from Canada. The warm wet breath of the Atlantic rolling up from the south. The pressure difference between them, the enormous gathering energy that had been building for two days.

All that energy, waiting.

She reached for it.

What followed, over the next four hours, was the largest weather event Storm had ever attempted. Not the most dramatic — she had called fiercer lightning before. But nothing had ever required this level of sustained control across this much space. She built a nor'easter — the churning giant storm born from Arctic cold and Atlantic warmth — and held it in her mind like a living thing needing constant attention. Layered snowfall to blind the flying Sentinels' cameras. Ice and wind into the joints and sensors of the ground units. Lightning in careful sequence: CRACK, each bolt precisely placed.

An hour in, her arms ached.

Two hours in, she was genuinely exhausted in a way she rarely admitted to herself.

Three hours in, she was running on pure training and pure will. The same will that had walked the Sahara. The same will that had crawled through the dark tunnel to reach Skids. THAT will.

Jean Grey's voice reached her through the telepathic network: "Sixteen down." Then: "Twenty-four down. Six remaining."

Storm stopped generating and started descending.

She dropped through her own storm — through the cloud layer, into the near-zero visibility below, wind screaming sideways and snow coming in horizontal. She extended her electromagnetic senses, looking for the mechanical signatures of the last six Sentinels.

One of them found her first. A containment blast lit the cloud white and missed her by two feet.

She rolled right. Another blast. She banked hard left. A third caught the edge of her cape and knocked her sideways and she went through a snowbank at speed, tumbling, cold exploding around her in every direction.

She sat up. Arms working. Legs working. Power still present.

She stood up.

She raised her arms above her head and called the last six bolts — one through six — spaced three seconds apart, each finding its target by electromagnetic sense rather than sight.

Six distant sounds. Six machines going dark.

She let the storm go gently, the way you put down something heavy by finding the floor rather than dropping it. The nor'easter folded back toward the Atlantic.

She descended through clearing clouds.

The Xavier Institute appeared below, its windows warm and lit. Four figures on the front steps, growing larger as she came down. Cyclops. Jean Grey. Wolverine. Professor Xavier in his chair.

She landed in the snow. It crunched under her feet. The air was cold and clean.

"Are you all right?" Professor Xavier asked.

Ororo looked at each person she had been fighting for. All safe. All here.

"Yes," she said. She was quiet for a moment. "I could use some tea."

Wolverine, who had already gone inside, reappeared thirty seconds later with a mug.

He handed it to her without saying anything. Because some things don't need words.

Above them, the sky continued to clear, and the morning light came in slowly, warm and patient.

The Xavier Institute stood exactly where it always had.
