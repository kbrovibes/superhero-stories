The year came when the Xavier Institute filled with a new class of young mutants who had arrived scared and full of questions. There was Suri, who could change the temperature of anything she touched but had burned through three sets of doorknobs since arriving. There was Emile, who could generate gusts of wind but had no idea how to stop them. And there was twelve-year-old Petra, who could sense electrical fields and had spent a week not touching anything at all, just to be safe.

Professor Xavier asked Storm to work with them.

She began the way she always began: she took them outside. They sat in the grass by the Hudson River, and she told them about Cairo — being six years old and alone. About Achmed el-Gibar and the bazaars and the long walk to the Serengeti. About the claustrophobia she still carried. About the Shadow King. About the first time the rain came when she called it, and how she was not sure at all if it was real.

She told them because they needed to know that a person could be frightened and powerful and still learning all at the same time, and that those three things did not cancel each other out.

Over the following weeks she worked with each of them carefully. She showed Suri that temperature was a spectrum with control points at both ends, not an on-off switch. She took Emile to the roof and showed him that wind was pressure — that slowing a gust meant equalizing the pressure around it, not fighting it. For Petra, she sat silently beside the Hudson River for an hour, both of them feeling the electrical hum of the water, until Petra realized that sensing something did not mean being overwhelmed by it.

In December all three gave a demonstration for the Institute. Suri held a snowflake on her palm for thirty seconds without it melting. Emile lifted two inches off the grass on his own column of air and held there. Petra mapped the Hudson River's electrical field from memory, accurate within ten feet.

Storm stood at the back of the crowd and said nothing. When it was over, Petra found her.

"How did you know we could do it?" she asked.

Storm looked at the three of them — uncertain, new, already beginning to be extraordinary.

"I knew," she said, "because I was you."
