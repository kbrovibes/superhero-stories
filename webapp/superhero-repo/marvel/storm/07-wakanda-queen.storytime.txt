Once upon a time, two young people crossed paths in the wide green heart of East Africa.

One was a girl named Ororo — still learning, still discovering, walking south through the grasslands toward a destiny she could not yet name. The other was a young man named T'Challa — a prince, though you would not have known it from looking at him, quietly testing himself against the great open world.

They were together for only a few days. They talked about the land and the sky and what they believed the world could be. And then their paths divided, as paths do when you are young and the world is wide.

But something stayed.

Years passed. Ororo became Storm, and the X-Men became her family. T'Challa became the Black Panther — king of Wakanda, the most magnificent nation on Earth, hidden in the mountains of East Africa, built on a miracle mineral called Vibranium that gave Wakanda technology and strength no other country possessed. Birnin Zana, the Golden City, shone beneath the African sun, and T'Challa ruled it with a fierce and careful heart.

When their paths met again — adults now, both carrying the weight of responsibilities they had chosen with open eyes — it was as if no time had passed at all.

The world they moved in was complicated. There were always battles to fight, always people who needed protecting. But in the evenings, when the work was done, they talked the way they had in the grasslands long ago — about the land and the sky and what they believed the world could be.

T'Challa brought Ororo to the Great Mound.

The Great Mound was the sacred heart of Wakanda: a vast deposit of Vibranium buried in the red earth, ancient and precious and unlike anything else in the world. To bring someone here was to say: this is the most important thing I have. I want you to see it.

He asked her to marry him, standing in the warm red earth with the green hills of Wakanda all around them.

She said yes.

The wedding was held in Birnin Zana, and it was everything a wedding in the Golden City should be: colors as rich as the African earth, music that carried for miles, dignitaries and diplomats from a dozen nations, and the X-Men — Professor Charles Xavier, and Cyclops, and Jean Grey, and Wolverine, all of them in their finest, all of them watching with full hearts as Ororo walked forward in white and gold.

She was radiant. That is the word that every person who was there would use later, when they told the story: radiant. Not just the dress or the ceremony. Her, entirely.

And the sky above Birnin Zana that day was — by the honest account of every person present — the most beautiful sky in living memory. Soft rose and warm lavender and a deep, particular blue that had the quality of a held note. Something about the light. Something about the air. A few people glanced up at the new queen and smiled quietly to themselves, because of course. Of course it was beautiful. She had been the Goddess of the Serengeti. Did you expect ordinary weather?

As Queen Consort of Wakanda, Ororo found a kind of purpose that complemented, rather than replaced, her life as Storm. Wakanda was extraordinary — its science, its art, its people, its principles — but it had spent centuries protecting itself through secrecy, keeping its gifts hidden from a world that might covet them. Storm had lived differently. She had grown up on the streets of Cairo with nothing, had slept under the open sky of the Serengeti, had stood in the middle of the Xavier Institute and learned that you could protect people by trusting them. She brought that perspective to Wakanda gently but persistently, arguing for a little more openness, a little more sharing.

T'Challa listened. He did not always agree, but he listened the way you listen to someone who has earned the right to be heard.

She remained an X-Man. When the call came from Westchester — when the Sentinels appeared on the horizon or the Shadow King stirred or any of the other thousand threats that never entirely went quiet — Storm went. T'Challa never asked her not to. He understood the call. He answered the same kind of call himself, in his own armor, on his own continent.

Two people, each carrying enormous responsibility, choosing to carry it together.

Some evenings, when the day's work was done, they would stand at the edge of Wakanda's great escarpment and look out at the continent below — the forests and the plains and the rivers catching the last of the day's light — and Ororo would feel, as she always felt in the wide open air, completely and entirely herself.

The girl from Cairo. The Goddess of the Serengeti. The X-Man. The queen.

All of them the same person.

All of them, at last, at home.
