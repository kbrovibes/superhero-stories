Once upon a time, three frightened children arrived at the Xavier Institute in the golden middle of September, when the leaves were just beginning to turn.

Their names were Suri, Emile, and Petra, and they had very different stories but one important thing in common: they were each afraid of the gift they had been born with.

Suri was fourteen, with quick eyes and careful hands. Her gift was temperature — she could raise or lower the warmth of anything she touched. It sounded wonderful, except that she hadn't yet learned to keep it steady. She had burned through three sets of doorknobs and taken to keeping her hands in her pockets.

Emile was thirteen, and when he was nervous — which was frequently — a gust of wind would simply appear around him, strong enough to scatter papers. He had no idea how to stop it. His room had become a small permanent weather event.

Petra was twelve and the quietest of the three. She could sense electrical fields in the walls, the ground, other people — and when she'd first discovered this, it had been so loud and new that she'd spent a week not touching anything, just sitting still and hoping the world would quiet down.

Professor Xavier observed them for a week. Then he found Storm.

She took them to the lawn by the Hudson River. Storm sat down in the grass without ceremony, and after a moment they all sat too.

She told them about Cairo — being six years old and alone, learning to survive in the bazaars with Achmed el-Gibar. About the strange warmth in her fingertips when the wind came. About walking the Sahara on instinct. About the Serengeti opening around her — the wide gold plain, the sky so large it almost hurt to look at — and raising her arms for the first time and feeling the rain come, not knowing if it was real.

"You can be frightened, and you can be powerful, and you can still be figuring it out — all at the same time, in the same body," she said. "They are not contradictions. Every person with a gift begins exactly where you are."

She paused. The river moved below them, patient and unhurried.

"Including me," she said. "I was you."

With Suri, Storm sat at the kitchen table and explained temperature as a spectrum — not a switch but a long gradual range with a thousand stopping points. They practiced moving to one point and holding it, over and over. Three weeks later Suri shook Storm's hand — warm, ordinary, steady.

Suri's eyes filled. "That's excellent work," Storm said. It was simply the truth.

With Emile, she took him to the roof on a cold clear day and explained something that changed how he thought about his gift entirely: wind is not force — it is pressure, always moving from high to low, seeking balance. You stop it not by opposing it but by equalizing the pressure around it. He furrowed his brow. He concentrated. The anxious habitual gust that had followed him for months went quiet. He stared at the still air.

"Oh," he said.

"Oh," Storm agreed.

With Petra, she did something different. She sat with her for an hour beside the Hudson River and neither of them spoke. They simply sat, and Storm let her attention rest on the electrical hum of the water — the slow, ordinary hum of flowing river. After a while Petra's careful breathing became easier.

"It's just there, isn't it," Petra said. "It's not coming for me."

"It's just there," Storm agreed. "Sensing something isn't the same as being swept away by it."

In December, Professor Xavier organized a small demonstration on the lawn.

Suri held out her palm. Someone placed a snowflake on it. Thirty seconds. Not melting. Not refreezing. When she looked up the smile on her face made everyone around her start smiling without knowing why.

Emile rose two inches off the grass on his own column of air, steady and intentional. When he came down his hands were shaking, but from effort, not fear.

Petra stood at the whiteboard and drew the electrical field of the Hudson River from memory. Storm's instruments confirmed she was accurate within ten feet.

Storm stood at the back and did not clap. What she felt didn't fit inside a clap.

Afterward, Petra found her in the hallway.

"How did you know we could do it?" she asked.

Storm looked at her — small and serious and already becoming someone extraordinary.

"Because I was you," she said. "Frightened, and learning, and not yet sure what I was capable of."

Petra was quiet for a moment.

"And look what you became," she said softly.

Storm smiled — a real one, full and warm. She put her hand on Petra's shoulder and they walked in together, out of the December cold, into the bright hum of the Xavier Institute on a winter evening, full of people becoming.

Always becoming. Always, already, enough.
