Some stories begin not with a villain choosing to be bad, but with a person who has simply been hurt. Ava Starr's father, Elihas Starr, had once worked alongside Dr. Hank Pym. When he was dismissed for dangerous experiments, he kept working alone — and one day his laboratory exploded. Ava survived, but the blast filled her body with quantum energy and left her in a condition called molecular disequilibrium. She was constantly flickering in and out of reality, like a television signal going fuzzy. Every single moment of her life was filled with pain.

Ava could walk through walls, turn invisible, and strike enemies before they could see her coming. She called herself Ghost. But she wasn't hunting treasure or trying to take over the world. She only wanted to stop hurting.

And do you know what she believed could save her? Energy from the Quantum Realm — the same subatomic world that Scott Lang and the Pym family were working to protect.

Fighting Ghost was unlike anything Scott had faced. How do you hit someone who keeps disappearing? His ant allies helped — they could sense the tiny vibrations Ava left in the air even when she was invisible. But the more Scott learned about her, the harder it was to simply fight her. She wasn't a villain. She was a young woman who had been failed by the grown-ups around her.

In the end, it was Janet van Dyne — who had survived decades alone in the Quantum Realm and carried its energy inside her — who gave Ava the stability she needed. From that day on, Ghost became proof that sometimes the bravest thing a hero can do is choose to understand, rather than simply to win.
