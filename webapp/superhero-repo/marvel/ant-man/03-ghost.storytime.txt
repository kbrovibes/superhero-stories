Imagine that your own body is the thing you cannot trust.

That every moment — standing still, reaching for a glass of water, sitting in a chair — your cells are tearing apart and reassembling, shifting in and out of phase with the physical world around you. You can pass through walls, yes. You can turn invisible, yes. These things sound like powers. From the inside, they feel like pain. Every second of every day, your molecules flickering, your body never quite settling into solid existence, the constant sensation of being halfway between here and somewhere else.

This was Ava Starr's life. This was what had been done to her.

Her father, Elihas Starr, had been a scientist working in the same field as Hank Pym — quantum physics, dimensional energy, the territory of the very small. Hank Pym had dismissed him years earlier for methods he considered too reckless. Elihas had continued anyway. And the experiment that went wrong took him and his wife in a single terrible moment — and left Ava, their young daughter, alive. But changed.

Molecular disequilibrium. The technical term for something that had no adequate description beyond this: the explosion had left her simultaneously in phase and out of phase with physical reality, constantly, without end.

She had been found by people who saw the utility in this condition before they thought about the cost to her. She had been trained. She had been given a suit that managed the disequilibrium to some degree — allowing her to control the phasing, to aim it, to use it. She became Ghost: invisible, intangible when she chose, able to walk through security systems and armored walls and anything else that stood between her and what she needed.

What she needed was quantum energy. The specific energy of the Quantum Realm — the dimension below the subatomic where time and space worked differently. It was the only thing that might stabilize her cells permanently. And the people who could provide access to the Quantum Realm were Hank Pym and Hope van Dyne — who had their own complicated reasons to be cautious about that particular dimension.

Scott crossed paths with her at the worst possible moment, when Ghost had decided that waiting for cooperation was taking too long.

The challenge of fighting her was this: she was invisible when she wanted to be. She was intangible when she wanted to be. Normal sensors were useless. But Scott noticed something: the ants sensed her anyway. The tiny vibrations her partial-phase existence created in the air were below the threshold of human detection — but not below the threshold of ant detection. While Ghost moved unseen, Scott's ant allies tracked her movements by the disturbances she made in the world, and relayed the information to him in the form of direction and distance. It was, in its way, a kind of sonar built from arthropods.

This bought him fractions of seconds. In a fight against someone who can turn intangible on command, fractions of seconds were everything.

But the harder challenge was different. As Scott fought and evaded and tried to manage the situation, he kept seeing it clearly: Ava was not a conqueror. She was not trying to take over anything. She was a person in constant pain, trying to find the one thing that might end it. The label of "villain" didn't fit what he was looking at.

He said so. Out loud. In the middle of a fight. Scott Lang's approach to combat included a perhaps excessive amount of talking, and he deployed it here.

The resolution came not through defeating Ghost but through understanding what she actually needed. Janet van Dyne — Hope's mother, who had survived decades in the Quantum Realm by adapting to its unique energies — had absorbed enough quantum energy to provide temporary relief. She placed her hands on Ava's, and the constant flickering of Ava's cells steadied. Not permanently. But enough.

Heroism is as much about healing as fighting.

Scott had known this about himself since the day he first chose to put on the suit — not to fight, but to protect. Not to defeat, but to solve.

Ava Starr needed a solution, not an enemy.

He tried, imperfectly and with a lot of explaining, to be that instead.
