Five years is a long time to be lost.

For Scott Lang, those five years happened in what felt like five hours. He stepped into a tiny van with a Quantum Tunnel built inside it, activated the system, and was — from his perspective — absent for about a third of a workday. He came back to a world that had moved on without him, a daughter who had grown up five years without her dad, and the quiet, devastating realization that the people he had known had experienced time he couldn't share.

He also came back with something important: information.

In the Quantum Realm, time did not work the way time worked everywhere else. Down at the subatomic scale, the concepts of past and present and future became... suggestions. Scott had experienced this. He had felt the wrongness of time flowing in the wrong direction, and the rightness when it steadied, and the distinct sensation of a different temporal current nearby.

He drove to the Avengers' headquarters in upstate New York with an idea that was either brilliant or completely unhinged, and he wasn't entirely certain which. He sat down with Tony Stark — who was, at this point, a retired man living on a lake with a daughter, and who had very specifically decided to be done with impossible ideas — and explained the concept of a Time Heist.

Go back through history. Collect the six Infinity Stones before Thanos did. Bring them forward to the present. Use them to reverse what Thanos had done.

Tony said no. Tony went home and stayed up all night working on the math.

Tony came back with: it might work.

They built the tools together. Tony Stark and Bruce Banner — who had spent the five years becoming something between Bruce and the Hulk, a thoughtful giant with all of Bruce's intelligence and most of the Hulk's biceps — designed the Time Space GPS that would anchor a traveler in the right era. Scott's knowledge of Quantum Realm navigation — what the micro-singularities felt like, where the quantum vortexes appeared, how to recognize a stable temporal corridor — was the essential ingredient that no one else had.

Can you picture the team assembling? Veterans of battles across galaxies, sitting around a table with Scott Lang — a thief from San Francisco with an ant suit — as the navigation specialist, nodding seriously at his explanations about the subatomic currents of time travel.

He loved every second of this.

In 1970 New York, Scott's mission required a very specific kind of Ant-Man move: get inside Tony Stark's arc reactor — the past version of Tony Stark, who was going about his day entirely unaware — and interrupt its function long enough to create a diversion. Not damage it. Not harm anyone. Just introduce enough interference to pull that Tony's attention and create the window the team needed.

This meant Scott Lang, shrunken to microscopic scale, operating inside the personal power source of one of the smartest men alive, improvising the timing of the disruption while simultaneously managing not to get caught.

He executed it perfectly. The Space Stone was secured in that window. The heist worked.

He served as primary navigator through the Quantum Tunnel for the team's returns — feeling the currents of the quantum passages, steering through micro-singularities with the confidence of someone who had spent an unexpected five years (five hours) learning the terrain. He brought everyone back. He kept the timeline intact. He did the math in real time, which was difficult, and he got it right.

The smallest Avenger was essential to the largest operation the Avengers had ever attempted.

Scott Lang, who started this whole journey by accidentally shrinking himself in someone else's bathroom, helped restore half the universe.

He thought Cassie might be proud of him when she found out.

She was.
