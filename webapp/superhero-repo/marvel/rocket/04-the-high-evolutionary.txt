The being who made Rocket had a name: the High Evolutionary. He was a scientist who believed that the universe was imperfect and that it was his right to improve it — to take living creatures and redesign them, granting them intelligence, changing their bodies, creating what he called Higher Species. He worked on a space station called the Arête, and his experiments were conducted without mercy or warmth. He was not cruel for pleasure; he was cold for purpose, which in some ways was worse.

Rocket had spent most of his life not knowing exactly what had happened to him on Halfworld. He knew he was different. He knew it had been done to him, not chosen by him. But the full truth — the laboratories, the surgeries, the precise and methodical process by which a small raccoon-like creature had been transformed into the being he was — those memories were buried and painful, and Rocket had spent years outrunning them.

When the High Evolutionary came looking for him, Rocket could not run from that truth anymore.

The Evolutionary wanted his research back. He considered Rocket a specimen — inventory, not a person — and he was determined to reclaim what he had created. He sent teams to intercept the Guardians, nearly killed several of them, and threw the entire team into chaos. Rocket lay gravely injured in the hours that followed, and his friends — Peter Quill, Nebula, Drax, Mantis, and Groot — tore themselves apart trying to find a way to save him, chasing through the Evolutionary's operations and records to find a code buried deep in his systems, a code that was the only thing that could help.

What Rocket found, in those fractured moments of consciousness, was memory: a small creature, barely made, lying in a laboratory on the Arête, and three others there with him — friends he had made and lost before he was old enough to understand loss. What the High Evolutionary saw as successful test subjects, Rocket had seen as family. The grief of that old loss was the thing he had been carrying longest.

The Guardians reached the Arête. They fought through everything the Evolutionary had built — his creations, his guards, his enormous pride — and Rocket was there for the end, standing on his own feet, channeling every ounce of that old grief into something that felt, for once, like resolution. The animals caged inside the station were freed. The High Evolutionary's empire was dismantled. No one else would be made the way Rocket had been made.

He walked out into the open dark of space, older and freed of a weight he had carried for his entire life.
