The ship that carries the Guardians of the Galaxy is called the Milano, and Rocket has opinions about every single centimeter of it. It was originally Peter Quill's ship — Star-Lord claimed it on one of his early Ravager missions — but over years of Rocket's constant improvements, it has become something considerably better than it started out as. Rocket considers this a joint ownership situation. Peter disagrees, mildly, on a recurring basis. They have this argument every few months and it resolves nothing.

The Milano is a modified M-class starship, roughly the length of a city bus, with a wing configuration that allows atmospheric flight as well as deep-space travel. In its original factory specification, it was a capable but unremarkable vessel. Under Rocket's supervision, it became something else. He rebuilt the engine coils twice, pushed the drive output to its maximum safe threshold and then slightly past it, and modified the thrust-vectoring array to allow maneuvers that the ship's original designers had not thought to make possible. The Milano can now accelerate to sublight speed in approximately eight seconds, bank at angles that would shake most ships apart, and — in an emergency — achieve a brief burst above its nominal speed rating that Rocket calls the "oh no" mode and uses only when genuinely necessary.

The ship's interior is cluttered in a way that reflects its crew. The cockpit has two seats and a navigation system that argues back. The cargo bay holds supplies, weapons, spare parts, and an ongoing disagreement about whose responsibility it is to sort them. Rocket's workshop occupies the rear starboard compartment. The crew quarters are small but functional, and Groot has chosen a spot near the engine bay where it is warmest, which Rocket engineered specifically, though he has never said so out loud.

Rocket has twice rebuilt the Milano from near-total destruction. The first time, on Contraxia, took three days with borrowed tools and an argument about whether salvaged Ravager parts were acceptable substitutes for genuine components. The second time, after the Battle of Ego's planet, took two weeks and cost every credit they had. Both times, when the Milano was back in the black, Rocket ran a full diagnostic sweep from the cockpit, let the readings scroll past his eyes, and allowed himself a quiet satisfaction that he shared with no one.

He would say, if you asked, that a ship is just a machine. But he spends more careful hours on the Milano than on any other machine he has ever touched.
