Imagine waking up and finding that while you were asleep, seventy years had gone by. That is exactly what happened to Steve Rogers. During the final days of World War II, his plane went down over the freezing Arctic Ocean, and Steve was lost beneath the ice. When scientists finally found him and brought him back, he opened his eyes to a world he barely recognised.

The Brooklyn he had grown up in — the crooked streets, the familiar faces, the simple corner shops — was gone. In its place stood towering glass buildings, tiny glowing screens in everyone's pockets, and news that moved faster than he could follow. Everything he had known, everyone he had loved, was part of history.

It was a very lonely feeling. But here is what Steve discovered as he walked through this strange new world: people were still the same deep down. They still wanted to be safe. They still wanted to be free. They still needed someone to stand up for what was right. And Steve Rogers was very, very good at that.

Do you know what he brought to his new friends — to Tony Stark and Natasha Romanoff and all the Avengers? A perspective no one else could offer. He had seen what people were capable of at their best and their worst. He reminded them of the human cost of every choice, the value of simple honesty, and why the mission always came back to protecting real people.

And that is how a man frozen in time became the one person the modern world needed most — because the things he believed in were never out of date.
