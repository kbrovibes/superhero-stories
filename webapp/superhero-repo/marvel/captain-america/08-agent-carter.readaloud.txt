Long before Captain America was famous, before the shield and the uniform and the cheering crowds, there was a woman who already believed in Steve Rogers. Her name was Peggy Carter, and she was one of the finest intelligence officers in all of World War II.

Peggy saw what most people missed. While others looked at scrawny little Steve and saw someone too small and too sick to matter, she looked into his eyes and saw a good man — someone brave and true. That belief meant everything to Steve. With Peggy beside him, he found the courage to become the hero he was meant to be. She stood right alongside him and the Howling Commandos on the most dangerous missions, offering sharp plans and steady wisdom when the fighting was fiercest.

They promised each other a dance. Just a simple dance. But then came that terrible day when Steve's plane went down over the Arctic Ocean, and the dance never happened. For decades, the memory of Peggy Carter was the warmth that kept Steve's heart alive while he slept beneath the ice.

When Steve finally woke up in the modern world, he learned that Peggy was still alive — older now, her hair gone silver — but still the same brilliant, dignified woman. Their reunion was one of the quietest, most tender moments in the story of Captain America. She reminded him that the world had changed, but kindness and justice never would.

And that is how Peggy Carter — spy, leader, founder of S.H.I.E.L.D. — became the beating heart behind everything Captain America ever stood for, and the reason he never stopped dancing toward a better world.
