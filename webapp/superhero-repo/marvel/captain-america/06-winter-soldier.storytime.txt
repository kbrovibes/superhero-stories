Before Steve Rogers was Captain America — before the serum, before the shield, before the star on his chest — he was a skinny kid in Brooklyn named Steve.

And he had a best friend.

His name was James Buchanan Barnes. Everybody called him Bucky.

Bucky was everything young Steve was not. He was tall. He was strong. He was the kind of boy who walked into a room and made everybody laugh. He had a crooked grin and a way of slinging his arm around Steve's narrow shoulders that said, very clearly: nobody messes with this kid. Not while I am here.

And nobody did.

When bullies cornered Steve in alleys behind the Brooklyn Bridge — and they often did, because Steve was small and Steve never knew when to stop standing up to people — Bucky was always the one who came running. Fists swinging. Voice booming. "Pick on somebody your own size!"

When the war came and Steve became Captain America, Bucky put on a uniform and stood beside him on the front lines, just like he always had. They fought side by side in a special unit called the Howling Commandos — a small fierce team of soldiers from many countries who took on Hydra's worst.

They were the best of friends. They were the best of soldiers. They were brothers in everything but blood.

And then, on a snowy mountain in the middle of a high-stakes mission, on a speeding train high above an icy gorge, something terrible happened.

Bucky fell.

Steve reached for his hand. Steve's fingers brushed Bucky's glove. And then Bucky was gone, tumbling away into the white whirling snow far below.

Steve searched. He searched the whole gorge in the days after. He found nothing. And for the rest of the war — and for the long decades of frozen sleep that followed — Steve Rogers believed his best friend was lost forever.

He was wrong.

Bucky had survived the fall. Barely. He was found, broken and unconscious, by a secret organization that did not want to save him. They wanted to USE him.

They put him in a metal chair. They wiped his memory away, page by page, until he could no longer remember Brooklyn or Steve or his own name. They replaced his lost arm with a powerful cybernetic one of cold black metal with a red star painted on the shoulder. They put him in deep sleep between missions, freezing him for decades at a time, only thawing him out when they needed him.

They taught him to be silent. They taught him to be deadly. They called him the Winter Soldier.

For seventy years, the Winter Soldier walked the world. He carried out terrible missions for terrible people. He did not remember who he had been. He did not know there had ever been a Steve Rogers, or a Brooklyn, or a brother who would have given anything to find him.

When Steve finally woke up in the modern world, he had no idea any of this had happened. He grieved Bucky as he always had — a quiet steady ache in the center of his chest.

And then, one day, on a mission, Captain America came face to face with the Winter Soldier.

The masked assassin knocked his shield away. His mask slipped. And Steve saw the face he had been mourning for seventy years.

"Bucky?" he whispered.

The Winter Soldier stared at him with cold empty eyes. "Who the hell is Bucky?"

It was the worst moment of Steve's long life.

But it was also the moment everything changed.

Because Steve Rogers, who had never given up on anything good in his life, was not about to start with Bucky Barnes.

The hunt that followed was not a hunt to STOP the Winter Soldier. It was a hunt to SAVE him.

Steve refused to fight Bucky the way he would have fought any other enemy. He took the punches. He spoke gently. He said Bucky's name out loud, over and over, like a key turning in an old lock. "Bucky. It's me. It's Steve. You know me. You knew me before you knew anybody."

His friends — Sam Wilson, the Falcon; Natasha Romanoff, the Black Widow — stood beside him through all of it. They could not understand at first. They thought Cap was being foolish. But they trusted him.

Slowly, painfully, the conditioning began to crack.

Bucky started to remember small things. The smell of the old Brooklyn fishmonger. A song his mother used to sing. The way Steve coughed in the cold. Each memory was a thread, and Steve gently pulled on every one.

It was not fast. It was not easy. There were many setbacks. Bucky carried terrible guilt for the things he had been forced to do, and that guilt was almost as heavy as the brainwashing had been. He would disappear for months. He would refuse to be called a hero. He would whisper, "I'm not him anymore, Steve. I'm not Bucky."

And Steve, every time, would answer the same way. "You don't have to be the boy you were. But you are still you. And I am not going anywhere."

With time, and with the help of brilliant healers in Wakanda who carefully cleaned the dark code out of his mind, Bucky began to come back.

He chose a new path. He became a defender of justice in his own right. He used his fearsome skills to make amends, one good deed at a time. He was not the laughing boy from Brooklyn anymore. He was someone new — quieter, sadder, wiser. But the GOODNESS at the bottom of him was still there.

It had been there all along.

Today, Bucky Barnes fights beside the Avengers as a trusted ally. His metal arm gleams. His eyes have softened. And whenever Captain America raises his shield in battle, you can almost always find the Winter Soldier at his back, guarding him exactly the way he did seventy years ago, in an alley behind the Brooklyn Bridge.

Some friendships, it turns out, can survive anything.

Even time.

Even ice.

Even all the long dark years between.
