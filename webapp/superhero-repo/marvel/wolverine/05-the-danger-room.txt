Beneath Xavier's School for Gifted Youngsters, hidden behind reinforced steel doors and accessible only by a private elevator, lay the most remarkable training facility in the world: the Danger Room. It was built by Professor Xavier and designed by Hank McCoy, and it could simulate nearly any environment or threat that the X-Men might face in the field. Crumbling buildings, extreme weather, holographic opponents, collapsing bridges — all of it perfectly safe, all of it perfectly realistic. If you were not paying attention, your brain could not tell the difference.

Logan had trained in difficult places before. Lumber camps, military drill grounds, mountainous wilderness — but the Danger Room was something else entirely. The first time he walked in, a holographic forest materialized around him and within seconds he was dodging falling trees, tracking three opponents by scent, and navigating a river crossing that had no obvious safe route. He finished the scenario in four minutes. Cyclops, watching from the control room above, raised one eyebrow behind his visor. Most recruits took twelve.

But the Danger Room was not just for solo performance. Professor Xavier used it to teach the X-Men how to fight as a team — how to coordinate, communicate, and trust. Logan had always been a solo operator by instinct. Asking for backup felt like admitting weakness, and Logan had spent too many years relying only on himself to shed that habit quickly. One particularly grueling session changed his mind.

The scenario placed the team in a collapsing underground tunnel with five holographic hostages to evacuate. Logan immediately moved toward the first blocked passage. Storm was clearing debris with a focused wind funneled through a narrow gap. Jean Grey was holding up a falling section of ceiling with pure telekinesis. Cyclops was calling out directions — "Logan, passage three, left side, now!" — and for once, Logan didn't argue. He went left. The passage opened. The hostages got out.

After the lights came back up, the team stood together in the bright, empty room, breathing hard. Logan looked around at his teammates — at Jean, who had held up that ceiling by herself; at Storm, precise as a surgeon; at Cyclops, who had seen the whole picture when Logan only saw the next ten feet ahead. He nodded slowly. He had learned something. In a Danger Room, and in a real fight, you could be the toughest person present and still need the people beside you.
