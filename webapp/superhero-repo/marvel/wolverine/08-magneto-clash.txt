There is no enemy more dangerous to Wolverine — and more complicated — than Erik Lehnsherr, the man called Magneto. Magneto is a master of magnetism, capable of bending, lifting, and hurling metal objects of any size with terrifying precision. For most opponents, this is a serious tactical problem. For Logan, whose entire skeleton is coated in adamantium, the world's most magnetic metal, it is deeply personal.

The confrontation took place near the coast of Nova Scotia, where Magneto had seized control of an offshore research platform and threatened to destabilize its power supply — which would have sent a catastrophic surge through the undersea cable networks of the eastern seaboard. The X-Men arrived in the Blackbird, touching down on a reinforced landing pad as storm clouds gathered overhead. Storm was managing the weather, trying to give them a ceiling advantage. Cyclops was coordinating. Jean was shielding. Logan was up front — where he always was.

Magneto appeared on the upper deck of the platform with his helmet — a deep crimson that blocked all psychic perception — and his hands already moving. The first thing he did, precisely and deliberately, was lift Logan off his feet.

Not anyone else. Logan.

Suspended thirty feet in the air by the pull of his own skeleton, Logan understood the message immediately: you are at a disadvantage here, and I want you to know it. He hung in the air, entirely calm, claws extended. He was not afraid. He had been in worse positions. He waited.

What Magneto had not accounted for was what the rest of the X-Men would do with the minute he spent making his point.

Cyclops fired a precise optic blast that sheared through the control housing on the platform's main antenna. Storm sent a focused lightning bolt into the backup generator. Jean Grey worked quietly to move the crew — who were all unharmed, because Magneto in this moment was an idealist making a point, not a villain making victims — to the secure lower deck.

By the time Magneto turned his attention back to the broader fight, the X-Men had dismantled his leverage entirely. He lowered Logan to the deck — a grudging acknowledgment. Logan landed steady, retracted his claws, and said quietly: "It doesn't have to be this way, Erik."

Magneto looked at him for a long moment. Then he left, vanishing into the cloud bank Storm had been maintaining. The platform was safe. The crew was safe. And Logan stood on the deck in the wind, wondering, not for the first time, how a person with such capacity for greatness could spend so much of it in opposition.
