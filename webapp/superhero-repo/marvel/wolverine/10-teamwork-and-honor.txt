Professor Xavier had a practice he kept at the end of every school year: a gathering in the main hall of Xavier's School for Gifted Youngsters, where students and X-Men sat together and reflected on what the year had taught them. He called it simply the Gathering, and he had been holding it since the very first class at the school. This year, for the first time, he asked Logan to speak.

Logan stood at the front of the hall for a long moment before he said anything. He looked at the rows of young faces — students of every kind, some newly arrived, some who had been at Xavier's for years — and he thought about what he actually wanted to say, rather than what might sound impressive.

He told them about a mission from early in his time with the X-Men, when the team had gone to Muir Island, off the coast of Scotland, to assist Dr. Moira MacTaggert — one of the world's foremost geneticists and a close friend of Professor Xavier's. A secondary systems failure at her research facility had trapped three of her staff in the lower labs, and every automated response had misfired. The X-Men arrived in the middle of a North Sea storm.

Cyclops had organized the response instantly. Storm had stabilized the weather enough to let the Blackbird land. Jean Grey had linked the team telepathically so they could coordinate without communication devices in the interference-heavy facility. And Logan had gone down into the lower labs — the section most compromised by the failure — because he could function in conditions that would slow anyone else down.

What he did not tell the students was how afraid he had been. Not of the danger — he had long ago made his peace with danger. But afraid of getting it wrong. Afraid that his instincts would fail him, that the years of trusting only himself would, in this crucial moment, make him the weak link in the chain his teammates had built around those three people.

What he told the students was what actually happened. He had followed his training. He had followed his team. He had extended his claws only when the work called for them, retracted them the instant it did not, and come out the other side with all three researchers safe. Dr. MacTaggert met him on the tarmac in the rain and shook his hand with both of hers. He had not known what to do with that. He still was not entirely sure.

He looked out at the hall. He said: "I spent a long time believing the only thing I had to offer was the ability to take a hit and keep going. The X-Men showed me that taking a hit and keeping going is worth something only because of what it protects. It is never the point by itself. The point is the people."

Professor Xavier, from the front row, smiled the smile he saved for moments that required no comment.
