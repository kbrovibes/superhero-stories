Once upon a time, on a Thursday afternoon in late October, Jean Grey found Logan sitting alone on the back porch of Xavier's school.

He was not doing anything in particular. He had a cup of coffee, going cold, and he was looking at the maple trees at the edge of the lawn, which had gone brilliant red and amber in the autumn. He was wearing his jacket and his expression of mild thoughtfulness, which, on Logan's face, looked from a distance like the expression of someone contemplating a difficult strategic problem.

Jean sat down next to him without asking if it was all right. She had learned, some time ago, that asking Logan if anything was all right was the quickest way to make him say it was.

She poured herself a cup of tea from the pot on the porch table — the kind of thing that somehow always appeared at Xavier's school when it was needed — and looked at the maple trees too.

They sat quietly for a little while.

"I know you know," Logan said eventually.

"Know what?" said Jean, which both of them knew was not entirely honest.

Jean Grey was a telepath. This meant that thoughts, to her, were something like weather — she did not go out of her way to observe them, but they were simply present, the way the wind is present. She could feel the surface-level moods of the people around her the way you can feel the temperature of a room. She could go deeper if she chose to, reading specific thoughts with precision — but she had a strict sense of when that was and was not appropriate, and she never went looking in places she hadn't been invited.

With Logan, she rarely had to.

He was, underneath everything, remarkably easy to understand. The gruffness was a kind of shorthand — a way of not wasting time on pleasantries when directness worked better. The crossed arms were a habit from too many years of being the only person he could rely on. And underneath all of it was a heart that was, at its core, fiercely and entirely oriented toward protection. He would walk into every difficult situation first. He would not leave until everyone was out.

She had known all of this within a week of his arriving at Xavier's school.

She had also known, within that same week, that he would not want her to say so. So she hadn't.

But over the months — over missions and training sessions and early mornings in the kitchen when the school was quiet and they were both awake before anyone else — a friendship had grown. The quiet kind. The kind that doesn't announce itself but is simply, solidly there.

Logan had begun to trust her senses the way she trusted his. In a crowded situation, he would move toward a threat he'd detected by scent while she tracked the layout of the room with her expanded awareness, feeding him information so naturally that it felt like thinking. She covered the angles he could not. He covered the ground she needed time to assess.

They fit together, as a team, the way two halves of a useful tool do.

The best illustration of this was the evening in Manhattan.

A structural collapse in the old Flatiron District — a section of a century-old building had given way, and thirty-two people were trapped in the lower floors. The rest of the X-Men were on separate assignments. Logan and Jean arrived first, before any emergency services had fully organized themselves.

Logan looked at the building. Jean looked at the building. They exchanged exactly four words.

"I'll go in," said Logan.

"I'll hold," said Jean.

That was it.

He went in. She stood at the edge of the debris field, arms extended, eyes bright with concentration, and she held. The compromised walls. The cracked ceiling joists. The section of stairwell that was hanging at a twenty-degree angle and would have gone in about ninety seconds if left alone. She held all of it, simultaneously, the way a juggler holds objects in the air — not effortlessly, because it wasn't effortless, but with the kind of trained focus that makes difficult things look smooth.

Inside, Logan moved quickly and precisely. He cut through a jammed door frame with a careful diagonal stroke — two cuts, door gone. He went through a blocked corridor, slicing at the obstruction not randomly but with the understanding of what could be removed without causing more collapse. He called back to the trapped people in a steady, calm voice — "I'm here, follow my voice, stay together, I'm going to bring you out" — and they followed, because when a voice sounds that certain and that safe, you follow it.

It took nineteen minutes.

Thirty-two people came out.

Logan came out last, the way he always did.

He walked to where Jean was standing on the sidewalk. She let her arms fall. She exhaled. The building groaned behind them, settling, but it did not fall further.

She put her hand on his shoulder.

He looked at her.

They stood there on the Manhattan sidewalk while the city hummed around them and the last of the evening light turned everything warm gold.

He understood what she was saying with the hand on the shoulder. She understood what he was saying by not shrugging it off.

Some friendships are very loud. They come with big declarations and public moments and a lot of words.

Some friendships are very quiet. They live in the trust between two people who have stood beside each other when it mattered, and they do not need saying.

Jean and Logan had the second kind.

It is, in the end, rather a good kind to have.

Dream quietly, little one. The best friends are the ones who simply show up.
