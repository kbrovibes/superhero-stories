Among the darkest chapters of Logan's long life is the story of Weapon X. Somewhere in the frozen wilderness of Canada, a secret government program had been watching Logan for years. Scientists and soldiers had studied reports of a man who could not be wounded, who never aged, who moved through danger the way a river moves around stone. They had a plan for him — a plan that was not his to refuse.

Logan was captured and brought to a cold, gray facility buried deep in the Canadian Rockies. The scientists of Weapon X had discovered a near-indestructible metal called adamantium, forged from one of the rarest alloys on Earth. They believed that if they could bond this metal to Logan's skeleton — coating every bone, filling every claw — they would have the ultimate weapon. They were right about the result. They were wrong to think it gave them any power over Logan himself.

The process was agonizing. Logan endured it with a courage no one in that room fully understood, because they did not know that beneath the pain, Logan's healing factor was working harder than it had ever worked before — fighting to keep him alive while his body was changed forever. The procedure was a success. When it was finished, Logan's skeleton was sheathed in adamantium. His claws — three on each hand — were no longer just bone. They were metal, gleaming and permanent, capable of cutting through almost anything.

When Logan finally came back to himself, he was in a strange place between fury and grief. The scientists had given him a new body — but they had also taken pieces of who he was. His memories were scrambled. He did not know his own name. He ran into the wilderness with nothing but the cold air of Alberta and the instinct that he was more than what those people had made him.

It took time, kindness, and the patient guidance of Professor Charles Xavier to help Logan reclaim himself. At Xavier's School for Gifted Youngsters in Westchester, New York, he found something the Weapon X program had never offered him: choice. He could put down his claws or extend them at will. He could fight for something he believed in. The adamantium was part of him now, and always would be — but it belonged to Logan, not to a program, not to anyone else.
