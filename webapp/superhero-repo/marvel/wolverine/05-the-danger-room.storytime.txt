Once upon a time, deep beneath Xavier's School for Gifted Youngsters, past the reinforced steel doors at the end of the long corridor, there was a room that looked entirely ordinary.

White walls. Smooth floor. Two small observation windows high up near the ceiling. Nothing else.

Logan had seen it exactly twice before he was told what it actually was. He had walked past it, glanced in through the small window, and thought: empty room, nothing of note.

He was more wrong than he had been in a very long time.

The Danger Room had been designed by Professor Charles Xavier and built by Hank McCoy — Doctor McCoy — who had a PhD in biochemistry AND the engineering instincts of someone who had needed to solve problems very quickly on more than one occasion. It could simulate, with extraordinary precision, almost any environment or threat the X-Men might face in the real world.

Collapsing buildings. Arctic storms. Dense jungle. City rooftops. Underground tunnels. Fires. Floods.

And holographic opponents who moved and reacted like real ones — because the room's systems adapted in real time to the student inside it.

The first time Logan stepped through the door alone, the floor seemed to fall away. He barely had time to register the change before a forest had grown up around him — tall Douglas firs, frost on the ground, a river rushing fifty yards ahead, and three sources of movement in the undergrowth: two at his two o'clock, one directly behind.

He turned, assessed, and moved.

Four minutes and eleven seconds later, the forest dissolved. The white walls were back. Logan stood in the center of the room, breathing quietly, not even breathing hard.

Up in the observation room above, Cyclops made a small note on his clipboard. Most new X-Men took between ten and fifteen minutes on that particular scenario.

Logan did not know this, and would not have said anything about it if he did. He was not the kind of person who mentioned things like that.

The solo exercises were one thing. The team exercises were another.

Professor Xavier believed, with the kind of absolute certainty he applied to very few things, that the most important thing an X-Man could learn was not how to use their own power. It was how to use it alongside the people beside them. Power, on its own, was simply energy. It was the combination — the way Storm's wind and Jean's telekinesis and Cyclops's precise direction-calling fit together like the pieces of a lock — that made the team extraordinary.

This was a harder lesson for Logan than the combat scenarios.

He had spent the better part of a century doing everything himself. He had trusted himself when there was no one else. He had learned to rely on no one because, in many of the places he had been, there was no one to rely on. Asking for help felt like dropping your guard. And dropping your guard, in his experience, had consequences.

Professor Xavier was patient about this. He never said "you need to learn teamwork" — he simply kept designing scenarios that couldn't be solved alone, and waited.

The scenario that finally got through to Logan was the one called Deep Passage Seven.

The setup: a collapsing underground tunnel, walls cracking, ceiling dropping dust, five holographic civilian figures trapped at various points along the route. The team had ten minutes. Go.

Logan was first through the door. He immediately identified the nearest blockage and moved toward it — but the passage was solid, packed debris from wall to wall. He could not cut through it fast enough alone.

Fifteen feet behind him, Ororo Munroe — Storm — had closed her eyes. The air shifted. She was drawing air from a gap he hadn't even seen, funneling it with extraordinary precision through a crack in the rubble, using the pressure to ease the stones apart. Her face was absolutely calm. She was doing something that required tremendous focus, and she was doing it without anyone asking.

On his right, Jean Grey had both arms extended, and a fourteen-foot section of ceiling that had been about to come down was simply … staying up. Suspended in midair by her telekinesis, trembling slightly, but holding. She was breathing quickly and her eyes were very bright.

From the observation window above — because even underground, Cyclops found a way to be in the observation room — his voice came through the earpiece: "Logan. Passage three. Left side. There's a gap behind the third support. Go left. Now."

Logan opened his mouth. He had a different idea. He could see what looked like a shorter route along the right wall, and he trusted his own eyes and his own instincts, and—

He went left.

The third support was cracked all the way through. One carefully placed shoulder drove it clear. The passage beyond opened up like a held breath releasing. Three of the five holographic civilians were through in under thirty seconds.

Two minutes later, the lights came back up.

The team stood together in the bright, empty room. The holographic civilians had dissolved. The cracked ceiling was smooth and white again. Outside the observation window, Cyclops was still making notes.

Logan looked at Jean. She unclenched her hands and exhaled slowly — holding that ceiling had cost her something. He looked at Storm, who was already breathing normally, her composure perfectly restored. He looked at the passage he had gone through — the left one, not the right one, the one Cyclops had seen from above because Cyclops could see the whole picture.

He nodded once.

He didn't say much. That was not his way.

But Professor Xavier, watching through the observation window, folded his hands in his lap and let himself smile.

He had known, from the moment Logan walked through the front gate, that this would happen eventually. The gruffness was real. The stubbornness was real. But so was the goodness. You couldn't have a heart as fundamentally protective as Logan's and not, eventually, learn to trust the people protecting alongside you.

It just took the right tunnel at the right moment.

Isn't that usually the way?

Sleep well, little one. Some lessons take time. The best ones are worth waiting for.
