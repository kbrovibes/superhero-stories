The day Logan walked through the front doors of Charles Xavier's School for Gifted Youngsters in Westchester, New York, he was not entirely sure he belonged there. The mansion was enormous — wide halls, high ceilings, and rooms full of young people doing the most extraordinary things without seeming to notice. A girl floated a book off a shelf with her mind. A boy left a trail of ice across the floor tiles, then melted it with a sheepish grin when a teacher walked by. Logan stood in the entryway with his jacket and his stubble and his crossed arms, looking very much like a man who had spent more of his life in forests than in schools.

Professor Xavier introduced him to the team. There was Scott Summers — Cyclops — a disciplined young man with ruby red sunglasses who took his role as team leader with total seriousness. There was Jean Grey, warm and thoughtful, with telepathic and telekinetic gifts that made her one of the most powerful mutants Logan had ever encountered. There was Ororo Munroe, called Storm, who could call lightning from a clear sky and speak to the wind. And there was Hank McCoy, also known as Beast, who was as brilliant in a laboratory as he was agile in a fight.

Logan was not immediately easy to get along with. He was direct to the point of bluntness, impatient with rules that seemed invented for their own sake, and quietly skeptical of teamwork — a man accustomed to relying only on himself. Cyclops and Logan locked horns almost immediately, their approaches to leadership and strategy about as compatible as fire and a filing cabinet.

But Logan learned. He watched how the X-Men cared for one another — genuinely, freely, without keeping score. He watched Professor Xavier treat every student as someone worth believing in. And slowly, like frost thawing off a window, Logan let himself belong.

The day he put on the X-Men uniform for the first time — blue and yellow, fitted and purposeful — he stood in front of the mirror for a long, quiet moment. Behind the gruff exterior, behind all the years of wandering, he had always wanted what the X-Men had: something worth fighting for, people worth protecting, a name that meant more than survival. He had found it, at last, here in Westchester.
