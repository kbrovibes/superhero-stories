Once upon a time, in a faraway corner of Canada, on the edge of a wide wilderness where pine trees grew tall as church steeples and the Rocky Mountains glowed purple at sunset, there lived a small, quiet boy named James Howlett.

James lived in a grand house on a sprawling estate in Alberta, and the house had long hallways and creaking staircases and enormous windows that looked out over rolling green hills. Because James was often sick, he spent many of his days inside those long hallways, reading books and watching the wind bend the grass in waves. He was not a loud boy. He was not a strong boy. But he had a good heart — the kind that notices things, the kind that cares.

His very best friend was a girl named Rose. Rose had red hair and a quick laugh, and the two of them would wander the quieter parts of the estate together, inventing stories about the animals in the forest and the shapes of clouds.

And then — one stormy night — everything changed.

James could never quite explain what happened that night. There was a terrible fright, a crash of thunder that filled the house, and a moment of overwhelming fear and sadness. And then — all at once — SNIKT. Three claws, sharp and solid, punched right through the skin between his knuckles. Three on the left hand. Three on the right.

Can you imagine? It hurt. Of course it hurt. James gasped and stumbled back. But then the most extraordinary thing happened. The small wounds around those claws began to close. The skin knit itself back together. His body was healing itself — faster than any doctor's medicine could manage, faster than any bandage could help.

James stared at his hands. He had no words for what had just happened.

He and Rose slipped away into the night, and James took a new name. He called himself Logan. It was a strong, plain name — a name that matched the life he was about to live.

Logan and Rose traveled far from Alberta, all the way west to the lumber camps of British Columbia, where tall men with broad shoulders swung heavy axes in the cold mountain air. Logan worked alongside them. He chopped. He hauled. He listened. The others thought he was simply a sturdy young worker with more energy than most. But Logan could smell the pine resin in the air from half a mile away. He could hear the creak of a branch snapping in the forest before anyone else. His eyes could find shapes in the darkness that most people could not see at all.

Oh, and the claws. He kept those very, very quiet.

Seasons passed. Then years. Then many, many years. Logan was not like other people, and little by little he began to understand that he was not going to grow old the way they did. He would travel, and they would stay. He would move on, and the world would change around him. He fought in great, terrible wars, always doing his best to look after the soldiers beside him — the ordinary men and women who had no healing factor, no claws, no second chances. Each goodbye left a mark on his heart, even when no marks stayed on his skin.

He became quieter. Rougher around the edges. Harder to know. But the goodness was still there, buried under years of hard roads and harder memories — warm and steady as an ember in the dark.

And then one day, a man named Charles Xavier came to find him.

Professor Xavier was not a tall or loud man. He traveled in a wheelchair, and he had the kindest, sharpest eyes Logan had ever seen. He ran a school in the state of New York — a school for extraordinary young people who had gifts they did not fully understand yet. Xavier had a gift of his own: he could sense the hearts of people, and he could tell in a moment whether a person was good.

He told Logan what he saw.

Logan was quiet for a long time. He looked at his hands. He thought of every person he had not been fast enough to protect, every moment he wished he had done more. He thought of Rose, and Alberta, and the long-ago night of thunder when everything began.

Then he looked back at Professor Xavier.

And he said yes.

So if you ever see a short, sturdy man with wild dark hair and thick sideburns walking through the halls of Charles Xavier's School for Gifted Youngsters — with three sharp claws ready to spring from each fist and a gruff voice that hides a very warm heart — you will know exactly who he is.

That is Logan. That is Wolverine.

Sleep tight, little one. The best adventures are just beginning.
