Of all the enemies Gambit had faced, none unsettled him as deeply as Mister Sinister. The villain's real name was Nathaniel Essex, and he had lived since the Victorian age — kept alive by a twisted genius and a hunger to collect and experiment on mutant DNA. Sinister dressed in black armor with a red diamond on his forehead, and his pale face never showed anything like doubt or mercy. He was not interested in gold or power the way ordinary villains were. He was interested in mutant genetics — in cataloguing and controlling the most gifted people on Earth. And long ago, before Remy had ever heard of the X-Men, Sinister had found the young thief from New Orleans and made him a terrible bargain.

Gambit did not speak about that bargain. But the day came when the X-Men traced a series of attacks on mutants across the American South back to a research facility hidden in the Louisiana bayou — a dark building half-swallowed by cypress trees and Spanish moss. Remy recognized the facility the moment they landed. His jaw tightened. He told Cyclops and Storm everything.

Inside, Sinister had been capturing mutants and studying them against their will, using technology that could read and copy any mutant's abilities. He had already collected data from seven captives, including a young girl named Louisa who could generate intense heat and had been kept in a glass chamber without sunlight for three weeks. Gambit felt fury burn through his chest like a charged card on the verge of exploding.

The battle through the facility was fierce and fast. Cyclops cut power to Sinister's containment fields with a precise optic blast. Storm called down lightning that cracked through the roof and fried three of Sinister's robotic sentinels. And Gambit moved through the corridors like the thief he had been trained to be — silent, quick, reading every room before he entered it. When Sinister appeared in the central laboratory, tall and cold and utterly calm, Remy was already there, a glowing card resting between two fingers.

"You made a mistake coming back to Louisiana," Gambit said.

"Did I?" Sinister replied, almost bored.

The card flew. BOOM. The laboratory lights exploded. In the confusion, Gambit freed Louisa and the other captives while Sinister retreated into a secret tunnel — because villains like Sinister always had secret tunnels. But the facility was empty and the prisoners were free, and that was what mattered.

Back outside in the warm bayou night, standing under a sky full of stars, Remy looked at the glowing lights of New Orleans on the horizon. He was not done with Sinister. But Sinister, he told himself, was not done worrying about Gambit either.
