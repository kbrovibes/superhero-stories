Once upon a time, the most useful thing Remy LeBeau owned was a stick.

A very good stick, to be fair. But still.

He had been given it when he was nine years old, growing up in New Orleans with the Thieves Guild. His trainer's name was Marcel — a lean, unhurried man with calloused hands — and on the morning Marcel decided Remy was old enough for weapons training, he had not handed the boy anything complicated. He handed him a smooth hardwood pole, roughly six feet long, perfectly balanced.

"A bo staff," Marcel said.

Remy looked at it. "It's just a stick," he said.

Marcel smiled patiently. "Any fool can fire a gun. A bo staff requires thought. It is a weapon that makes you better before it makes you dangerous. You will train with this every day until the day you leave New Orleans. And after that, if you are sensible, you will train with it still."

Remy began to train.

He learned the basic sweeps — the low arc aimed at the ankles, the one Marcel called the "good morning" because of how quickly it put someone on the ground. He learned spinning blocks, the staff rotating so fast it deflected anything thrown toward him. He learned overhead strikes and how to plant the staff and vault over an obstacle twice his own height. He practiced until his shoulders ached and his palms were rough.

Years of practice changed the staff from an object he carried into something that felt like another arm. When he needed it, his hand found the grip without looking. The right movement arrived before his mind finished the question.

Celestine Arceneaux made Remy his staff. She was a Guild artisan on Magazine Street in New Orleans — a woman of considerable skill and very few unnecessary words. She chose white ash wood, kiln-dried until the grain was dense and the flexibility exactly right. She finished it with linseed oil that kept it slightly grippy even in rain. Near the base, she burned a small fleur-de-lis — the symbol of New Orleans — into the wood.

Remy had kept that same staff for years, repairing small cracks and refreshing the oil. The fleur-de-lis was still perfectly clear.

After he joined the X-Men, someone asked why he still bothered with the staff when he had mutant powers. He could blow things up, after all. Why carry a piece of wood?

He thought about it seriously. Cards were ranged weapons — superb at distance, fast, precise, versatile. But they required a moment of charging, required his hands to be free. And against certain opponents — someone strong and fast enough to close the distance before a card was ready, or someone in armor that absorbed the blast — the cards alone were not enough. The staff was everything the cards were not: immediate, requiring nothing except a good grip and the right timing. Six feet of reach created automatic standoff space. Spinning in a wide arc, it cleared a perimeter that made closing in costly. Together, staff and cards made Gambit one of the most adaptable fighters anyone faced.

His signature low ankle sweep had become something of a legend. It had worked on Sabretooth in Audubon Park. It had worked on four Weapon X soldiers in a corridor in Manitoba. It had worked on Wolverine during a training session — which Wolverine referred to as "a slip caused by an uneven floor mat." Remy had never corrected this.

Late one evening, Storm found him outside cleaning the staff with a cloth and linseed oil. She sat beside him in comfortable silence.

"Marcel gave you that philosophy," she said. "Thought before force."

"Every day," Remy said. "Said any fool could blow something up. The harder thing was knowing when not to."

Storm looked at the evening light settling into the gardens.

"He sounds like he was very wise," she said.

Remy ran the cloth along the staff and held it up, checking the grain. The fleur-de-lis near the base was clear as the day Celestine had burned it.

"He was a thief," Remy said. "But the kind who gave things back when he could. I've met worse teachers."

He stood, slung the staff across his back, and went inside.

The gardens were quiet. The evening light faded.

The fleur-de-lis caught one last glint of gold before the dark came in.
