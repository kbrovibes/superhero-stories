Here's a question for you: what does a superhero do when there's no villain to fight?

He teaches.

This is the story of Gambit and a boy named Marcus, and it might just be the most important story of all.

Marcus is fifteen years old. He lives in Atlanta, Georgia. And one Tuesday afternoon, he startles himself reaching for a juice box — and three scorch marks appear on the kitchen wall. His hands were warm. More than warm. Actually very hot. Red-orange hot.

His mutant power had arrived.

Marcus shows up at Xavier's School with a small backpack and a lot of embarrassment. Professor Xavier gives him a room. Then Xavier asks Gambit to work with the new student.

Remy shows up at Marcus's door the next morning with two cups of coffee and says, "Tell me what you can do and how much control you have."

Marcus shows him. A burst of red-orange heat from his palms — then FLASH, it slips. Lost focus. Too much.

Remy watches. He nods slowly.

"I did that with kinetic energy for months," he says. "Charged everything I touched by accident. Blew up a lamp. A very nice chess set. About eleven playing cards that I was just TRYING TO HOLD."

Marcus looks up. "What fixed it?"

"Practice," Remy says. "And deciding that the power was yours. Not the other way around."

They work together for SIX WEEKS. Every afternoon. Practice room. Gambit is patient — like, REALLY patient, the kind that doesn't make you feel bad for struggling, but also doesn't pretend things are easier than they are.

Bad sessions happen. Remy lets Marcus be frustrated for exactly the right amount of time, then redirects: don't think of your power as something to LOCK DOWN. Think of it as something to SPEAK WITH. Like adjusting the volume, not covering your ears.

Week one: three seconds of control.
Week two: eight seconds.
Week three: fourteen seconds.
Week five: forty-two seconds!

Week SIX —

Marcus holds ninety seconds. Steady. Controlled. Glowing warm orange in the practice room, not a flash, not a spike, just a steady beautiful glow.

He looks at his hands. He looks at Gambit.

He doesn't say anything. His eyes are very bright.

Remy is thinking about a nine-year-old in New Orleans, terrified of his own glowing fingers. He's thinking about the day Charles Xavier told that boy his power was a GIFT. Every step between then and now.

"You're going to be very good at this," he says. And he means it completely.

Marcus grins. Then he asks: "Can you teach me to throw a card?"

And Gambit reaches into his coat pocket.

"We'll start with the ace of diamonds," he says. "It flies truest."

THAT is what heroes do when there's no villain. They pass it on.
