Once upon a time, in Atlanta, Georgia, a fifteen-year-old boy named Marcus reached for a juice box and accidentally put three scorch marks on the kitchen wall.

He stared at the marks. He looked at his hands. His palms felt warm — more than warm. Like a small, insistent fire that had decided to exist.

His mutant power had arrived.

Marcus was embarrassed about the wall. He was embarrassed about the scorch marks on his jacket sleeve two days later, during a math test. He was embarrassed about being very unusual at exactly the moment in life when most people were trying hard to feel ordinary.

He arrived at Xavier's School for Gifted Youngsters with a small backpack and no good idea of what happened next. Professor Xavier gave him a room, a class schedule, and a genuine reassurance that being unusual was something he would have in common with everyone else. Then Xavier asked Remy LeBeau to work with the new student.

Remy said yes without hesitating. He didn't explain why it mattered to him. He simply showed up at Marcus's door the next morning with two cups of coffee, sat down, and said: "Tell me what you can do and how much control you have. No judgment. Just show me."

Marcus demonstrated: a heat output from both palms, red-orange, warm as a campfire. Two seconds, three — then an involuntary flash as his focus slipped. He pulled his hands back and looked at the floor.

"Sorry," he said.

"Don't be," Remy said. "How often does it slip?"

"When I get startled. Or frustrated."

Remy nodded. "I charged everything I touched for three months. An ordinary lamp — gone. A chess set Wolverine was attached to — also gone, which was awkward. Eleven playing cards I was simply trying to hold."

Marcus looked up. "What fixed it?"

"Practice," Remy said. "And deciding that the power is yours. Not the other way around. Right now it feels like something that happens to you. We're going to make it something that comes from you. There's a difference."

They started the next afternoon, in the practice room in the mansion's lower level. Every afternoon after classes.

Remy was not the kind of teacher who made things sound easier than they were. When a session went badly and Marcus sat on the floor looking defeated, Remy let the frustration be real for exactly the right amount of time, then redirected. He taught Marcus to think of his power like his own voice — you don't cover your mouth to stop speaking, you learn volume. Control comes from using it, not suppressing it.

Week one: three seconds of steady output. Week two: twelve. Week three, he held fourteen seconds and looked so surprised that he broke his own focus — but the flash was twenty percent smaller than it used to be.

Week five: forty-two seconds. Beautiful and controlled.

Week six: a gray January afternoon.

Marcus took a slow breath, let his power come forward — speaking, not shouting — and the warmth in his palms built steady and full. Ten seconds. Thirty. Sixty. Seventy — his mind offered to wander, and he caught it. Held it. Brought it back.

Ninety seconds.

He let it ease down gently.

He looked at his hands. Then at Gambit.

He didn't say anything. His eyes were bright.

Remy thought about a boy in New Orleans, nine years old, terrified of his own glowing fingers. He thought about Charles Xavier in the French Quarter, looking at those red eyes and saying the word gift. He thought about every step between that day and this one.

"You're going to be very good at this," he said. Not encouragement — observation. A fact he had watched become true.

Marcus's shoulders relaxed. Something careful in him let go.

Then he asked: "Can you teach me to throw a card?"

Remy smiled — the genuine one.

"We'll start with the ace of diamonds," he said, reaching for the worn deck. "It flies truest. I've never quite figured out why, but it does."

He held the card out. Marcus took it in two fingers, and looked at it.

Outside the practice room, the mansion was full of its usual sounds. Inside, Gambit and Marcus practiced.

Every good teacher's student teaches someone.

That is how the good things last.
