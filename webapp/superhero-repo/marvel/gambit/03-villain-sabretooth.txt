Victor Creed — the feral, snarling mutant called Sabretooth — was everything Gambit refused to become. Creed had all the cunning of a trained fighter and none of the conscience, using his retractable claws and superhuman senses to hunt without regret. He had clashed with Wolverine for decades, and the Thieves Guild had unwisely hired him once to recover something that belonged to them. That job put Sabretooth on a collision course with Gambit.

The trouble started in Audubon Park, New Orleans — a sweep of old oaks and wide green lawns beside the Mississippi — where Sabretooth had cornered a young Guild courier named Beau Mireaux. Beau was seventeen and fast and terrified, clutching a sealed envelope that Creed wanted because someone, somewhere, had paid him to want it. Gambit arrived just as Creed backed Beau against a wall of ancient roots.

"Put him down, Creed," Remy said, pulling his bo staff from across his back and letting it settle into both hands. "He's a kid."

Sabretooth grinned — a wide, unpleasant expression full of too many teeth — and lunged.

The fight moved through the park in a blur of motion. Sabretooth was stronger and faster and healed instantly from every glancing blow, his claws leaving long gouges in the oak trunks when Gambit dodged. But Remy was smarter. He had grown up in these streets. He knew every alley, every root cluster, every place where the ground was soft or the branches hung low. He charged a handful of cards as he ran, leaving a trail of small glowing explosions that peppered the ground around Sabretooth with smoke and distraction — CRACK, CRACK, KRAKOOM. Creed roared. He lunged again.

Gambit waited. He let Creed's momentum carry him exactly two steps too far, and then brought his bo staff around in a sweeping arc that caught Creed across the ankles. Two hundred and sixty pounds of feral mutant hit the earth hard. Remy pressed a single charged ace of spades against the back of Creed's collar — glowing bright pink — and held it there.

"You want to finish this," Remy said, calm as a summer morning, "or you want to walk away?"

Sabretooth growled. Then he got up and walked away, because Gambit's charged cards at that range were not something even a fast healer wanted to test. Beau Mireaux kept his envelope. The park went quiet again, Spanish moss swaying in the river breeze.

Remy had not defeated Sabretooth forever — no one did that. But he had done what mattered. He had protected someone who could not protect himself, in the city he loved, with the skills he had been given. That was the difference between a thief and a hero.
