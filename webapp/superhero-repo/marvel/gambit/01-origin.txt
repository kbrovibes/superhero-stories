Remy LeBeau grew up in the narrow, lamp-lit streets of New Orleans, a city of jazz music and sugary beignets and wide brown rivers sliding past ancient oak trees. He had no mother and no father he could remember — just the Thieves Guild, a secret brotherhood of the cleverest thieves in Louisiana. The Guild raised Remy like a son, training him to pick locks, move in silence, and speak three languages with a charming smile. He learned to use a bo staff, a long smooth pole he could spin so fast it became a blur, and he learned that loyalty to the Guild was the most important thing in the world.

But Remy had a secret the Guild could not explain. When he held an ordinary object — a coin, a stone, a playing card from a worn deck — and concentrated just so, the object began to glow with a soft pink-purple light. The air around it crackled and hissed. And when he let go, BOOM — the object exploded in a bright flash of kinetic energy. This power made the Guild proud. It also made Remy lonely, because the light in his eyes — glowing red irises, black sclera — frightened people who did not know him.

The day that changed everything came when Remy was a young man exploring the French Quarter on a gray February morning. He had just slipped a stolen pocket watch back into its owner's coat — he had stopped being comfortable keeping things that were not his — when a tall man in a dark coat appeared from a doorway. The man's name was Charles Xavier, and his school for mutants in Westchester, New York was the reason he was in New Orleans. Xavier told Remy that his ability to charge objects with kinetic energy was a mutant power — a gift woven into his cells — and that there were others like him, gifted people called the X-Men who used their powers to protect a world still learning not to be afraid.

Remy had grown up being told that the Guild was his only family. But Xavier offered something different: a family that did not ask him to steal. He packed a travel bag, tucked a fresh deck of playing cards into his coat pocket, shouldered his bo staff, and boarded a train north to Westchester. He did not know the names Cyclops, Storm, or Wolverine yet. He only knew that for the first time, someone had looked at his glowing red eyes and seen a hero inside.

At Xavier's School for Gifted Youngsters, Remy LeBeau became Gambit — a name that means a bold, clever opening move in chess. The boy from New Orleans had made his opening move.
