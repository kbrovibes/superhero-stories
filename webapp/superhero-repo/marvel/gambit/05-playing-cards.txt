The most recognizable thing about Gambit — other than his glowing red eyes — was always the playing cards. A fresh deck, fanned between his fingers. A single card glowing pink and crackling with kinetic energy before it left his hand. The explosion when it arrived. But the cards were never the real story. The real story was the power behind them.

Remy LeBeau's mutant ability was the power to charge objects with kinetic energy — the energy of motion, the invisible force that builds up in anything when it is stressed or accelerated. When Remy touched an object and concentrated, he pushed kinetic energy into it the way you might pump air into a balloon, faster and faster until the balloon could barely hold it. When the object left his hand, all of that built-up energy released at once in a bright, precise explosion. The flash was always that distinctive pink-purple, the color of the energy itself.

He could charge almost anything. Coins, pebbles, door hinges, a length of rope. But he chose playing cards because they were perfect for his style. A standard deck weighed almost nothing in his coat pocket. Cards were flat and aerodynamic — he could throw them with the same flick of the wrist he had practiced since childhood, the same motion the Thieves Guild had used to teach him to deal a hand or palm a chip. When charged, a single playing card hit with the force of a small explosive, but because the charge was contained and directional, Gambit could control exactly where and how it detonated.

He kept a worn deck in his left coat pocket and a fresh deck in his right. The worn deck was for practice — he charged those cards until they glowed, then released the charge without throwing them, keeping his focus sharp. The fresh deck was for emergencies. His favorite card was the ace of diamonds, which he said flew truest.

At the training room in Xavier's mansion — the Danger Room, a remarkable space that could project any obstacle or opponent — Remy spent hours after sessions practicing the precision of his charges. He could ignite a card with just enough energy to create a flash and no detonation, like a camera burst. He could charge a chain of cards with a slight delay between each, creating a sequence of explosions that walked across a surface like footsteps. He could even charge an object and then carefully drain the charge back out of it, defusing his own explosives — a skill that had saved lives more than once.

The cards, he once told Bobby Drake, were not the weapon. He was the weapon. The cards were just how he introduced himself.
