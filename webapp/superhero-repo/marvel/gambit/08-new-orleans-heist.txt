The call came from Thieves Guild Master Etienne Marceaux on a Tuesday in October, which meant trouble, because Etienne only called when the trouble was real. A rogue faction inside the Guild — four experienced thieves who called themselves the Crimson Tide — had stolen something that did not belong to them: a sealed container from a university research lab on Tulane Avenue, containing an experimental compound that could, when dispersed into a water supply, temporarily strip mutant abilities. The Crimson Tide planned to sell it to someone who had made clear they intended to use it against the X-Men.

Remy went to New Orleans alone. He did not tell Cyclops where he was going — which he would apologize for later — because New Orleans was his city and the Guild was his complicated history and he needed to handle this the way he knew how. He crossed the French Quarter on foot in the early evening, relearning the streets with his feet, smelling the city he had grown up in.

He found the first Crimson Tide member, a man called Thierry, in a gambling hall above a restaurant on Bourbon Street. He did not fight Thierry. He sat down at his table, dealt him a hand, and in the course of the game — which Remy won, but graciously — gathered the information he needed. Thierry thought he was just talking.

He located the container in a warehouse on the edge of the Warehouse District, guarded by the other three members, who were significantly less willing to have a conversation. The fight was short and precise: Gambit came through the skylight, charged a series of cards in quick succession — CRACK CRACK KRAKOOM — and while the guards scrambled for cover in the smoke, he swept the two closest with his bo staff and was at the container before the third recovered. He took it. He left each of the three a playing card — just an ordinary one, uncharged, from his worn deck — tucked into their collar. Let them find it. Let them know he had been close enough.

He delivered the container to Professor Henry McCoy — Beast — back in Westchester, who spent three days analyzing and safely neutralizing the compound. Then Gambit called Etienne and told him that the Crimson Tide had been disqualified from Guild membership, effective immediately, and that the Guild's reputation for keeping agreements had been upheld.

"You didn't have to handle it personally," Etienne said.

"Yes," Remy said, "I did." New Orleans was where he came from. Protecting it — protecting the people in it and the ones connected to it — was not an obligation. It was something closer to love.
