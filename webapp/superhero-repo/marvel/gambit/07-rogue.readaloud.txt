Okay. This is a different kind of story. No explosions. Well — almost none. Stay with me.

Her name is Rogue. Real name: Anna Marie. Her mutant power is one of the most difficult to live with of anyone at Xavier's school. When she touches another person's bare skin — just a touch — she absorbs their memories and abilities. She can't turn it off. Since she was a teenager, she has worn GLOVES. Always. Because accidentally absorbing someone's memories hurts both people, and Rogue never wanted to hurt anyone.

She was fierce, and funny, and stubborn, and quietly lonely.

And then Remy LeBeau arrived from New Orleans with his deck of cards and his charming smile and his habit of leaving small notes in people's doorways.

Rogue was NOT impressed. She had seen charming people before. Charming people usually wanted something.

But here is the thing about Remy: he didn't want anything she couldn't give. He sat beside her without touching. He talked to her. He listened. He found out which songs she liked and then went to the mansion's VERY out-of-tune piano and played them BADLY. Really, quite badly.

Wrong notes. Wrong tempo. Wrong octave sometimes.

Until Rogue came over and said "that's not how it goes" and sat down and showed him the right way.

Which was, as she eventually realized with great fondness, EXACTLY what he had been doing on purpose. He had wanted her to come over. He had wanted to share the piano bench with someone who knew music better than he did. He had made himself need her help just a little, so she would give it.

That is a VERY clever, VERY warm, VERY Remy thing to do.

They had arguments too — real ones. They ended in long silences. And then one of them would leave a glowing pink card outside the other's door, or a bowl of gumbo, or both, and the silence would soften.

What Rogue needed was not someone who felt sorry for her. She needed someone who took her seriously, exactly as she was.

Remy gave her that.

And she gave him something back: she was the first person who could see through his charm — past the smile, past the cards — and choose him anyway. The real him. The boy from New Orleans still figuring things out.

They held hands sometimes, at the window of the mansion, both wearing gloves. Looking out at the wide green grounds together.

It was not everything they might have wished. But it was warm. And real. And it was theirs.

And sometimes, that is more than enough.
