Once upon a time, in the most musical city in all of America, there was a boy who glowed.

His name was Remy LeBeau, and he lived in New Orleans, Louisiana, where the streets were narrow and paved with old cobblestones, and music drifted out of every open window, and the smell of beignets — little fried pastries dusted in powdered sugar — floated through the morning air like a gentle invitation. The mighty Mississippi River rolled just beyond the levees, wide and brown and slow, and Remy had grown up watching it from iron balconies draped in flowering vines.

Remy did not have a mother or a father he could remember. Instead, he had the Thieves Guild.

The Guild was a secret brotherhood — clever and careful — and they raised Remy as one of their own. They taught him to pick any lock, to move through a room without making a single sound, to speak in English, in French, and in the warm rolling Cajun dialect that tasted like home. They taught him to use a bo staff — a long, smooth pole, perfectly balanced — and to spin it so quickly that it hummed in the air. They told him that loyalty was the greatest treasure, and for a long time, Remy believed them.

But Remy had a secret, even from the Guild.

When he held an ordinary object — a coin, a stone, a single playing card from a worn and shuffled deck — and breathed slowly and concentrated very carefully, the thing in his fingers began to change. A soft light crept into it. Pink first, then purple, crackling at the edges like a tiny thunderstorm being born in the palm of his hand. The air smelled of electricity. And if he let go —

BOOM.

A brilliant, bright explosion of kinetic energy, the kind that rattled windows and sent pigeons spiraling up from the rooftops into the warm Louisiana sky.

It was a wonderful and startling power. But there was something else, too. Remy's eyes were not like other people's eyes. His irises glowed a deep, burning red. The whites of his eyes were black as midnight. When strangers saw them, they sometimes stepped backward without meaning to.

Remy had grown up used to that. He smiled extra wide to make up for it. He said kind things. He told jokes in three languages. And quietly, secretly, he had started to feel uncomfortable about the stealing. When the Guild sent him to take things from people who needed them, Remy would sometimes find a way to slip the item back. A pocket watch returned to a grandfather's coat. A ring put back on a market table when no one was looking. He was a thief who gave things back — which made him a very confusing member of the Thieves Guild.

One February morning — the air smelling of chicory coffee and rain on warm stone — Remy was walking through the French Quarter when a man in a dark coat stepped quietly out of a doorway. He was older, sitting in a wheelchair, with kind eyes and a calm, unhurried voice.

"Remy LeBeau," the man said. "My name is Charles Xavier. And I believe you've been waiting for someone to say what I'm about to say."

Xavier explained everything. The glowing cards. The crackling energy. The red eyes. These were not curses, not accidents. They were a mutant gift — abilities woven into Remy's very cells, as natural to him as the color of his hair. And there was a school, Xavier said. A school in Westchester, New York — a big house full of people who could do impossible things, who understood what it felt like to be different, who used their gifts not to take from the world but to protect it.

The X-Men.

Remy was quiet for a long moment. He looked down at the playing card in his hand — one card from the deck he always kept in his coat pocket — and he watched it glow faintly pink at the edges.

Then he looked up and smiled.

He packed his battered travel bag that afternoon. Deck of cards in his coat. Bo staff across his back. A last look at the iron balconies and the wide brown river he had grown up beside.

He got on a train heading north.

When he arrived at Xavier's School for Gifted Youngsters, he was given a room, a uniform, and a question: what name would he like to go by?

He thought about the Thieves Guild. He thought about the locked doors he had opened and the things he had put back. He thought about opening moves and second chances.

"Gambit," he said. It was a chess word, meaning a bold and brave first move, the kind that changed everything that came after.

The boy from New Orleans had made his move.

And now the adventure could begin.
