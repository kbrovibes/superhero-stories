Once upon a time, there was a villain who had lived for a very, very long time — and who had used all of those years to become extraordinarily, terrifyingly clever.

His name was Mister Sinister, though his real name, in another life, had been Nathaniel Essex. He had been alive since the Victorian age — a time of top hats and gaslit streets and horse-drawn carriages — and he had spent every one of those long years studying mutants. Cataloguing them. Collecting samples of their abilities the way a child might collect shells from a beach, except that the shells did not consent, and the beach was the whole world.

Sinister wore black armor that seemed to drink in the light around him. There was a red diamond at the center of his forehead, like a jewel that had been pressed there by something cruel. His face was pale as chalk and showed no warmth at all. He did not laugh like other villains. He simply watched, and calculated, and waited.

He had found Remy LeBeau once before.

When Remy was young, still living in New Orleans, still running errands for the Thieves Guild, Sinister had appeared and offered him something. An arrangement. A bargain Remy had agreed to because he did not yet know better — and had quietly carried like a stone in his pocket ever since.

Remy did not talk about that bargain. But he had not forgotten it.

One spring evening, word reached Xavier's school that mutants were disappearing across the American South. Small towns, bayou roads, the back streets of Baton Rouge and Shreveport. People with abilities, gone without explanation. The X-Men assembled: Cyclops in his red-visor helmet, Storm in her white cape, and Remy with his bo staff and his coat full of playing cards.

They found the facility at the edge of a Louisiana swamp, half-hidden by curtains of Spanish moss and cypress trees whose roots twisted out of the dark water like knotted fingers. The building was gray and windowless and hummed with machinery.

Remy went still when he saw it.

"You've been here before," Storm said, reading his face.

"A long time ago," he said quietly. "Let's make sure no one ever comes here again."

Inside, Sinister had built a laboratory for his collection. Containment chambers. Equipment that could read and record every detail of a mutant's power. Seven people were being held inside, frightened and cold and wondering if anyone was coming for them. Among them was a girl named Louisa, twelve years old, who could generate warmth and light from her hands — a small sun in human form — and who had not seen real sunlight in three weeks.

BZZZT — Cyclops sent a precise optic blast into the power relays, and the containment fields flickered and died.

CRACK — Storm summoned a bolt of lightning down through the roof, and three robotic sentinels clattered to the floor in sparking heaps of metal.

And Gambit moved through the corridors the way he had been taught in New Orleans, a long time ago, in a life that felt both distant and very close. Silently. Reading each room before he entered. Finding the doors that mattered.

Sinister was waiting in the central laboratory. Tall. Still. Pale as January moonlight.

"You made a mistake coming back to Louisiana," Remy said, a glowing card resting between two fingers.

"Did I?" Sinister replied, with the bored tone of a man who has been told the same thing many times.

Remy let go of the card.

BOOM.

The laboratory filled with brilliant pink-purple light. Machinery scattered. Windows cracked. In the confusion of smoke and sparks, Remy ran to the cells and opened every one of them. Louisa stumbled out, squinting in the glow of Remy's kinetic energy, and he guided her gently toward the exit.

When they emerged into the warm bayou night, the air smelled of rain and cypress and the distant sweetness of jasmine growing along the levee. Sinister had slipped away through a tunnel — he always had an escape planned — but every prisoner was free, and the facility was empty, and Louisa stood on the grass and lifted her face toward the stars.

Remy watched her and felt something loosen in his chest. Not quite forgiveness of himself. But something like the beginning of it.

Above the tree line, the lights of New Orleans shimmered orange and gold on the horizon.

He was not finished with Sinister. But for tonight, that was enough.

Louisa created a small warm glow in her palm — like a candle in the dark — and smiled at Remy.

He smiled back.

And the night, just for a moment, felt a little less heavy.
