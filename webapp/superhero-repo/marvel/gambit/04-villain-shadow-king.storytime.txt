Once upon a time, a shadow came to the mansion.

Not a shadow from the sun — not the comfortable kind that follows you home on a late afternoon. This was a shadow with intentions. This was the Shadow King.

His real name had been Amahl Farouk. He had no body anymore, not in the ordinary sense. He existed in the psychic realm — the invisible world of thoughts and dreams that lives inside every person's mind. He was very old and very patient, skilled at sliding unseen into places he was not wanted. He had fought Charles Xavier himself, once, in a battle that took place entirely inside the mind. He had survived. And he had been scheming in the psychic dark ever since.

He came to Xavier's mansion in Westchester on an autumn evening, when the leaves were orange and gold. He came without sound. One by one, he dimmed the X-Men — not hurting them, just switching something off behind their eyes, smoothing the sharp edges of their thoughts flat.

Storm stood in the back garden, her white cape motionless, staring at the sky. The clouds moved, and she did not command them.

Wolverine sat at the kitchen table with a fork in his hand, turning it over and over, saying nothing. Logan, who always had something to say. Saying nothing.

Remy LeBeau walked into the kitchen and felt the wrongness immediately. He had been trained to read rooms. And this room told him, clearly, that something had stolen the light from inside his friends.

He charged a card. Pink-purple. Crackling. And looked around at the empty, still room. Who was he charging it for? The Shadow King had no body. No ankles to sweep.

He put the card away. He set his bo staff against the wall.

He thought about something Jean Grey had told him — that strong emotions create turbulence in the psychic realm. Like dropping a stone into still water, the rings spread outward. A person who feels very deeply can disturb the psychic plane.

Remy sat down on the kitchen floor, cross-legged, and closed his eyes.

He was not always comfortable with this. He wore a smile like a comfortable coat — useful for keeping his own feelings at a safe distance. But now he let himself simply be.

He thought of New Orleans. The trumpet from an open window. The wide Mississippi catching the last of the day's light. The iron balconies draped with vines.

He thought of Beau Mireaux in the park, the relief on his face when Sabretooth walked away.

He thought of Louisa lifting her face to the bayou stars.

He thought of the X-Men — Cyclops, steady and principled, Storm with her magnificent fearlessness, Wolverine who growled at everything but showed up every time.

And then, carefully, because it was the biggest feeling of all, he thought of Rogue. Toward whom he felt something so warm and complicated and full that it was a great deal like holding a very powerful charged card and not quite letting it go.

He felt it all. He let it all be there, without pushing any of it away.

From somewhere that was not exactly the kitchen — somewhere in the invisible space between thoughts — there came a sound like a mirror cracking. A retreat that felt unmistakably like something leaving. Room by room, something lifted from the mansion.

Storm blinked, and the clouds shifted in response.

Wolverine set down the fork and looked at it as if surprised to find it in his hand.

Jean Grey came running from upstairs, her eyes sharp and clear. She stopped at the kitchen door, staring at Remy on the floor with his eyes closed.

"Someone just sent a wave through the psychic plane," she said. "Like an enormous stone thrown into a still lake. Was that you?"

Remy opened his eyes.

"I felt things at him," he said simply. "Seemed rude to hold back."

Jean sat down on the kitchen floor beside him and started laughing — not from surprise, but the laugh of someone who has just seen something genuinely wonderful.

The mansion was quiet again. The right kind of quiet. The kind with people in it.

Outside, the autumn leaves fell in the late light, and Storm raised her face to the sky and smiled.

Sometimes the bravest thing you can do is simply feel everything — and not look away.
