Before Remy LeBeau ever charged a single playing card, there was the bo staff. It was a simple weapon — a smooth hardwood pole, roughly six feet long, no edge, no trigger — and the Thieves Guild of New Orleans had put one in young Remy's hands before he was ten years old. "Any fool can fire a gun," his trainer Marcel had told him. "A bo staff requires thought. It is a weapon that makes you better before it makes you dangerous."

Remy had taken those words seriously. He spent years learning the staff until it was an extension of his own body — spinning it in sweeping arcs to deflect incoming attacks, planting it to vault over obstacles, using its length to keep opponents at a distance where his kinetic charging was most effective. A charged card was a ranged weapon; the bo staff was everything else. Together, they made Gambit one of the most versatile fighters on any team.

His staff had been crafted in New Orleans by a woman named Celestine Arceneaux, a Guild artisan who worked from a workshop on Magazine Street. The wood was white ash, kiln-dried until perfectly balanced between flexibility and rigidity. Celestine finished it with a light oil that made the surface warm to the touch and slightly grippy even in rain. Near one end, she burned a small fleur-de-lis — the symbol of New Orleans — into the wood. Remy had kept this same staff for years, repairing small cracks with a wood glue mixture Marcel had shown him, maintaining the finish with the same oil Celestine used.

In combat, the staff allowed Gambit to manage space. Against a stronger opponent — Sabretooth, for instance — he could stay at striking range without closing to grappling distance, where Creed's raw power was overwhelming. Against multiple opponents, the sweeping arc of a six-foot pole created a wide defensive perimeter that made clustering around him costly. Against armored enemies who might be resistant to cards, the staff could be used as a ram or a lever.

But Remy's most trusted use for it was the thing Marcel had described first: the sweep. Low, fast, perfectly timed, catching someone's feet at the exact moment their weight committed forward. It had worked on Sabretooth in Audubon Park. It had worked on four Weapon X soldiers in a corridor in Manitoba. It had worked, he was mildly embarrassed to admit, on Wolverine during a training session, though Wolverine maintained it had been a slip.

The staff was not glamorous. It was not radioactive or enchanted or capable of flight. It was carefully made wood and years of practice. And in Remy LeBeau's hands, that was more than enough.
