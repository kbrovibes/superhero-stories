Of all the extraordinary people at Xavier's School for Gifted Youngsters, the one who changed Remy LeBeau most was Anna Marie — the mutant called Rogue. Her power was one of the most isolating anyone could imagine: when she touched another person's bare skin, she involuntarily absorbed their memories, their personality, and any mutant abilities they possessed. The person she touched was weakened; the absorbed traits faded from Rogue over time. She had worn gloves since she was a teenager, had kept a careful distance from every person she had ever cared about. She was fierce and funny and stubborn and deeply, quietly lonely.

When Remy arrived at the mansion from New Orleans — charming, warm, fond of impractical gestures like leaving playing cards in people's doorways with small notes on them — Rogue was skeptical. She had seen charm before. It usually wanted something.

But Remy didn't want anything from her that she couldn't freely give. He was happy to sit beside her without touching. He was happy to talk and let her talk back and find the conversation worthwhile in itself. He learned which songs she liked and played them badly on the mansion's out-of-tune piano until she came and corrected him, which was, she eventually realized, the whole point. He shared his beignet recipe with her and did not pretend the results were better than they were. He was present, warm, and unhurried.

Their friendship grew slowly, the way trust grows in people who have been given reasons not to trust. There were arguments — Rogue was not a person who avoided disagreements, and neither was Remy. There were long silences after those arguments, and then someone would leave a charged card glowing softly outside someone else's door as a peace offering, or a bowl of gumbo, or both.

What Rogue needed, Remy had quietly understood from the beginning, was not someone who pitied her power or tried to fix it. She needed someone who took her seriously as she was: formidable, feeling, complicated. He gave her that. And she, in turn, gave him something he had needed since New Orleans: a person who could see through charm to the real person underneath and choose to stay anyway.

They held hands sometimes, both wearing gloves, standing at the window of the mansion looking out at the wide grounds. It was not what either of them might have wished. But it was warm, and real, and it was theirs. In a life full of extraordinary things, Remy had come to believe that ordinary warmth — two people who had chosen each other, deliberately, despite everything — was the most extraordinary thing of all.
