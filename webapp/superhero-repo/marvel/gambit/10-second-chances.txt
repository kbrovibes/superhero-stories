On a cold January afternoon, a fifteen-year-old boy named Marcus arrived at Xavier's School for Gifted Youngsters with a small backpack and no good explanation for three scorch marks on the kitchen wall of his apartment in Atlanta. His mutant ability had just emerged: he could generate heat from his hands, enough to warp metal, and it came out when he was startled or upset. Professor Xavier gave Marcus a room and a class schedule, then asked Gambit to work with the boy.

Remy was not obvious about why this particular assignment meant something to him. He simply showed up at Marcus's door the next morning with two cups of coffee, sat down at the desk in the boy's room, and said, "Tell me what you can do and how much control you have."

Marcus demonstrated: two seconds of concentrated heat that glowed red-orange at his palm, followed by a sudden flash when he lost focus. Remy watched carefully.

"I did that with kinetic energy for months," Remy said. "Charged everything I touched by accident. Blew up a lamp, a very nice chess set, and approximately eleven playing cards just trying to hold them."

Marcus looked up. "What fixed it?"

"Practice," Remy said. "And deciding that the power was yours, not the other way around."

He spent six weeks working with Marcus every afternoon in the mansion's practice room. He was a patient teacher who never pretended the work was easy. When Marcus's control slipped and he was frustrated with himself, Remy let him be frustrated for exactly the right amount of time before redirecting. He taught Marcus to think of his power as something to speak with rather than contain — adjust the volume of your voice, not cover your mouth.

Six weeks in, Marcus held a sustained, controlled heat output for ninety seconds without a flash. He looked at his hands, then at Gambit. He did not say anything. His eyes were bright.

Remy thought about the boy he had been at nine years old, learning the bo staff, terrified of his own glowing fingers. He thought about the day Charles Xavier had come to New Orleans and told him his power was a gift. He thought about every version of himself between that day and this one — the mistakes, the complicated choices, the long walk toward becoming someone he was proud of.

"You're going to be very good at this," he told Marcus. It was not encouragement for its own sake. It was what he actually believed.

Marcus nodded. Then he asked if Gambit would teach him to throw a card.

Remy smiled and reached into his coat pocket. "We can start with the ace of diamonds," he said. "It flies truest."
