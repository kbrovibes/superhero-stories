Once upon a time, a blade was forged on a world called Godthab Omega.

The people of Godthab Omega had been making swords for so long that the craft was in their blood — passed from hand to hand, generation to generation, the way some families pass down recipes or songs. The forges were deep in the mountain, where the rock stayed warm all year around. The smiths worked by firelight, turning the metal over and over, finding the balance point the way a sculptor finds a face inside a block of stone: by removing everything that isn't it.

The blade they called Godslayer came out of those forges single-edged and curved, dark and patient. It was not decorated with jewels. It did not glow. It simply held its edge the way a river holds water — naturally, without effort, as though it had been made for nothing else.

It was passed from warrior to warrior over many years, and each one learned from it: that a sword teaches you as much about yourself as it teaches you about fighting. How you plant your feet. How quickly you decide. How much patience you have when the fight goes longer than you expected.

And then the sword came to Gamora.

Gamora had been training since she was small enough that a full-length blade was taller than she was. Thanos had put a weapon in her hand almost before she could walk steadily, because that was how Thanos thought about children: as things to be sharpened. She had started with shorter blades, training sticks, practice weapons with dulled edges. Every morning on the Sanctuary's training deck, she worked through forms and footwork and combinations, the metal ringing against metal or against the targets that swung back when you hit them, reminding you to stay alert.

By the time Godslayer came to her, her hands already knew what to do.

But the sword changed how she practiced.

Every morning now — even out among the stars, even on the battered deck of the Milano where Rocket's half-finished inventions cluttered one corner and Groot's small potted plant sat on the windowsill — Gamora found her space and moved through her forms. Step, pivot, extend. Step, pivot, extend. The way a musician practices scales not because they cannot play the notes but because playing the notes every day is what keeps them true.

Rocket watched once, arms folded, from the doorway.

"You do that every morning," he said. "You're already the best fighter on this ship."

"That is why I do it every morning," Gamora said, without stopping.

Rocket was quiet for a moment, and then he went back to his workbench, thinking about that.

Godslayer's edge never needed sharpening. The metal held it the way a very calm person holds a difficult feeling — not tight, just present. It did not scratch or dull against the steel of ordinary weapons. It turned heat aside. When an energy blast caught the flat of the blade, the sword shed the heat the way a duck sheds water and stayed cool.

But the thing about the sword that Gamora thought about most was not the edge or the balance or the way it moved in her hand like an extension of her thinking.

It was the rule she had made for it.

She had drawn Godslayer in battle against Ronan's soldiers. Against Nebula. Against creatures and threats across a dozen star systems. Every single time, it had been in defense — her own safety, her teammates', the people who had no one else to stand for them.

Godslayer had never been drawn in anger alone. Never in cruelty. Never because Gamora wanted to prove something to anyone, including herself.

She was not the fastest sword fighter in the galaxy. She would tell you that herself, honestly, the way she said everything. There were warriors who trained for nothing else, who had devoted their entire lives to speed and form in ways that she had not, because she had also been learning everything else: star navigation, languages, strategy, how to be a person in the world rather than just a weapon in it.

But a blade guided by wisdom? A blade that only comes out when it truly needs to?

That, she thought, was something rarer than speed.

She finished her forms. She slid Godslayer back into the scabbard at her hip, smooth and quiet.

Groot reached over from the windowsill and offered her a small leaf — a gift, because Groot was always paying attention to the people around him.

"Thank you, Groot," Gamora said.

She tucked the leaf into her belt pocket and went to see what breakfast Star-Lord had somehow managed to ruin this time.

Outside the porthole windows, the stars drifted past: bright and patient and very old.

And Godslayer rested at Gamora's hip, edge perfect, waiting.
