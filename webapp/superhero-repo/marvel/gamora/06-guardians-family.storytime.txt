Once upon a time, there was a spaceship called the Milano.

It was not an especially beautiful ship. The paint was scuffed in several places. The engines made a low, contented rumble when they ran well, and a slightly different sound — more of a complaint — when they were asked to do something extra. The hold smelled like engine fuel and old food and something that Rocket had once described as "pure engineering genius," by which he meant his own projects.

The Milano had five people aboard, and they were an unlikely combination.

The first was Peter Quill — who called himself Star-Lord, which most people found a bit much, though he insisted it was exactly right. Peter had grown up on a street in Missouri, been taken to the stars by a crew of rough nomadic traders called the Yondu Ravagers, and had tumbled from one adventure to the next ever since. He had tremendous courage and an equally tremendous confidence in himself that was sometimes, not always, earned.

The second was Rocket, who looked like a raccoon and moved like lightning and could turn any collection of spare parts into something inventive and slightly alarming. He protected himself with layers of sarcasm and the suggestion that he did not care about anything very much. But if you watched carefully, you could see him watching his friends — making sure they were all right before they had to ask.

The third was Groot: tall and wooden and gentle in the way old forests are gentle — broadly, quietly, without needing to make a point of it. He spoke only three words. But in those three words he contained happiness and concern and the kind of loyalty that simply shows up whenever it is needed.

The fourth was Drax, who had lost his family long ago and was slowly learning — with some difficulty — that there was a difference between justice and vengeance, and that letting people be close to you was one of the bravest things a person could do.

And the fifth was Gamora.

Gamora had grown up in order and discipline on Thanos's Sanctuary — a ship that was precise and controlled, where everything had a place and everyone was expected to stay in it.

The Milano was nothing like that.

Rocket's tools were distributed across three surfaces and one emergency locker. Drax had never rotated his chair from the best position on the bridge. Groot's rock collection lived on the corner of the navigation console, arranged in a row that only Groot understood. Star-Lord's music — carried on a device he called a Walkman — was almost always too loud.

And Gamora — who had been raised in precision and control — found, to her own surprise, that she did not want to change any of it.

She was the one who held things together. When a mission was coming, she read every briefing before anyone else was awake. She mapped the exits and the three most likely things to go wrong, so that when those things went wrong — and they always went wrong — she already knew the next step. When Star-Lord's charm ran out in the middle of a negotiation, Gamora said the exact words that finished it.

But she also paid attention in a way no one had trained her to do.

She learned the rhythm of the ship at night when everyone slept — the sounds that meant all was well. She knew that Rocket's jokes got louder when he was frightened, the volume rising in direct proportion to how much he did not want anyone to know. She sat with Drax sometimes in the evenings when the day had been hard — not always talking, just present, because she had learned that sometimes the most important thing you can give someone is the evidence that you are not leaving.

Thanos had built her into a weapon.

But the Milano had taught her something different. A team was not a collection of weapons. A team was a living thing — with its own language and rhythm and shared memory that grew a little longer every time they came through something together.

One evening, Groot crossed the hold and tucked a small blue flower — grown somehow from one of his own twigs — behind Gamora's ear. She looked at it in the porthole glass, stars drifting behind it.

"Thank you, Groot," she said.

"I am Groot," he said warmly.

She smiled. A small smile, but a real one.

She was exactly where she was supposed to be.

Home.
