The day the Guardians of the Galaxy crossed paths with the Avengers was not a day anyone had planned. It began with a distress signal, and it ended with one of the strangest introductions in the history of two of the universe's most determined groups of heroes.

Gamora had been tracking a surge of cosmic energy near the planet Titan — the ancient, devastated homeworld of Thanos, now a ruin of crumbling towers above orange skies. She recognized the signature of an Infinity Stone immediately; she had made it her business to know every frequency they emitted. The signal was not the Stone itself, but something feeding off its residual energy, something moving toward it like a tide toward the shore.

They arrived to find a second ship already there. Aboard it: Tony Stark, the Iron Man, in a suit that made even Rocket's best work look modest. Doctor Strange, Sorcerer Supreme of Earth, who opened portals through space the way ordinary people open doors, and who carried a relic called the Eye of Agamotto that held a power Gamora respected immediately. And Peter Parker, Spider-Man, who was the most talkative person she had ever met and who asked her seventeen questions in the first four minutes, including one about whether her green skin was a natural color.

"It is," she said.

"That's so cool," he said.

The Iron Man and Gamora reached an understanding relatively quickly, because Tony Stark could read a room and Gamora had no time for posturing. There were more immediate concerns: Thanos was moving toward Titan, and between the Guardians' knowledge of his tactics and the Avengers' knowledge of the Stones and their effects on Earth, the combined group had more information than either team had alone.

Gamora laid out everything she knew: his patterns, his weaknesses, the exact conditions under which he would negotiate versus escalate. Tony laid out the Stone's properties and the engineering insights from the Gauntlet's known design. Strange said little but listened with the complete attention of someone calculating several steps ahead. Even Spider-Man contributed — a fresh perspective, unclouded by years of history.

For a single afternoon above the ruins of Titan, heroes from opposite ends of the universe sat together and built something neither could have built alone: a real understanding of the threat they faced. Gamora felt the particular satisfaction of information shared and combined — the knowledge that the sum of two teams, talking honestly and fast, was more powerful than any single hero with the mightiest weapon.

The battle that followed was hard. But it was fought with better maps than anyone had ever carried into that fight before. Because two families of heroes had recognized each other as allies.
