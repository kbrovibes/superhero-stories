The Milano is not a tidy ship. Rocket's spare parts live in three different corners. Drax has claimed the largest chair on the bridge and will not negotiate on this point. Groot's collection of interesting rocks sits on the navigation console, and Star-Lord's music player is always too loud. It is cramped and a little chaotic and nothing like the clean, ordered corridors of Thanos's Sanctuary that Gamora grew up in.

She loves it.

The Guardians of the Galaxy are, by most accounts, an unlikely team. Star-Lord — Peter Quill — is a human who grew up in Missouri and was then swept up to the stars by a Ravager crew called the Yondu Ravagers, bounced around the galaxy for years, and now leads with a combination of genuine heart and spectacular overconfidence. Rocket is a tactical genius who hides behind a wall of sarcasm so you cannot easily tell how much he cares. Groot communicates only in three words — "I am Groot" — but says everything he needs to through those words and through the quiet, steady way he shows up when you need him most. Drax lost his family and is rebuilding himself, one fight at a time, learning that justice and vengeance are not always the same thing.

And Gamora — Gamora is the one who holds the thread.

She is the planner, the strategist, the person who actually reads the mission briefing before the Milano lands. When Star-Lord's charm runs out before the negotiation is over, Gamora steps in with three precise sentences that finish it. When Rocket disappears into his own cleverness and loses sight of the goal, Gamora sets the course correction. When Drax goes still and sad in the evenings, she sits with him in the quiet — not talking, just present.

What she has discovered is something she could not have predicted in her years aboard the Sanctuary: that a team is not a collection of individual fighters loosely pointed at the same enemy. A team is a living thing, with moods and rhythms and a language that develops over time. She knows, now, the exact sound of the ship when everyone is asleep and all is well. She knows the way Groot rustles when he is worried. She knows that Rocket hides his fear under jokes and that the best response is not to call attention to it.

She learned all of this the same way she learned the sword: by paying attention every day.

The Guardians are her family. Not the family she was born into — Zen-Whoberi is a quiet memory now, warm and distant as old starlight — and not the family she was assigned. A family she chose, and who chose her back.
