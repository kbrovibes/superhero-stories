Once upon a time, in a far corner of the universe, there was a small and peaceful planet called Zen-Whoberi.

The people of Zen-Whoberi were gentle. They grew things and made music and watched their three moons rise every evening in a row, like lanterns on a string. They did not build weapons. They did not go to war. They just lived, quietly and happily, the way people do when the world is kind to them.

There was a small girl there. She had green skin and steady hands and eyes that noticed everything — the way a butterfly moves, the way water finds the lowest path, the way the sky changed color just before a storm. She did not yet have a name that the whole galaxy would come to know.

And then, one day, the sky changed in a different way. A shadow fell over Zen-Whoberi — the shadow of a very large, very dangerous man named Thanos.

When Thanos left, the little girl went with him.

He took her to his great ship, the Sanctuary, which traveled the stars like a slow dark whale through deep water. He gave her food and training and a room of her own. He called her his daughter.

Her name was Gamora.

Every morning, while the stars slid past the porthole windows, Gamora trained. She had an adopted sister, Nebula, who trained beside her. The two of them practiced every morning — swords and staffs and quick, spinning kicks — until the sweat ran down their foreheads and their arms ached all the way to the shoulder.

Gamora was very, very good. Then she became better than very good. Then she became the best.

Warriors across dozens of star systems learned her name. They said it softly, the way you say something you respect and are a little bit careful about. The deadliest woman in the galaxy, they called her.

But Gamora, being very smart and also very honest with herself, noticed something as the years went by.

Thanos was not teaching her to protect people. He was not teaching her to be kind, or brave, or good. He was teaching her to be a weapon — his weapon — for plans that had nothing to do with helping anyone.

She lay on her bunk one night, the stars wheeling past outside, and she thought about Zen-Whoberi. She thought about the three moons rising in a row. She thought about what she had been, and what she was being turned into.

And she made a decision.

She would leave.

It was the hardest thing she had ever done — harder than any sparring match, harder than any trial Thanos had put her through. Nebula did not come with her. The distance between them felt like a crack in glass, not quite broken but never quite whole. Gamora carried that sadness the way you carry a stone in your shoe: always there, always felt.

But she walked anyway.

She took her twin blades and her long, famous sword called Godslayer, and she crossed the cold dark of space on her own, following her conscience instead of orders.

The universe, it turned out, had been waiting for her.

In a gray and rattling prison called the Kyln, she met a man named Peter Quill, who everyone called Star-Lord — though most people thought that was a bit too grand a name for someone who got into so much trouble. She met Rocket, a small raccoon-like creature with enormous cleverness and an even more enormous opinion of himself. She met Groot, a tall tree-being who spoke only three words but somehow managed to say everything he needed to. And she met Drax, a big warrior with sad eyes behind all that fierceness, searching the galaxy for his own kind of justice.

None of them trusted each other at first. None of them planned to become a team.

But when danger came — and it always does, sooner or later — they stood together. Side by side, back to back, watching out for each other the way families do.

The Guardians of the Galaxy were born.

And Gamora, the last daughter of Zen-Whoberi, who had been taken away from everything she loved, found something she had not expected to find out here among the stars.

She found a home.

Not a planet. Not a ship. A home made of people who chose each other.

She looked around at her strange, noisy, wonderful new family — Star-Lord, Rocket, Groot, Drax — and something quiet and warm settled in her chest, right where the old loneliness used to live.

She was not a weapon.

She was a Guardian.

And somewhere out among the three-mooned skies of a memory, the gentle people of Zen-Whoberi were, perhaps, just a little bit proud.
