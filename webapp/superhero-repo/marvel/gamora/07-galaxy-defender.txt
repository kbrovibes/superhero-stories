The galaxy is large beyond imagining. Between the familiar planets of the Nova Corps' jurisdiction and the wild, unmapped edges of known space lie hundreds of civilizations — some thriving, some desperate, some simply trying to hold on. The Guardians of the Galaxy have flown across most of it: through the golden cloud-cities of Contraxia, past the ancient carved columns of Morag, around the enormous slow curve of the Andromeda frontier. Gamora has seen more of the universe than most people who live in it.

This is what those travels have taught her: the galaxy does not protect itself. The Nova Corps does its best. Xandar's officers are brave and dedicated. But one organization cannot be everywhere at once, and the spaces between order and chaos are vast. There are places where a single ship — the Milano, dented and reliable — showing up at the right moment makes all the difference.

The Guardians are not official. They have no badge from the Nova Corps, no charter from any government. What they have is a reputation built from action: the planet Xandar, saved from Ronan. A dozen smaller interventions that never made the headlines. Gamora keeps a quiet record — not for glory, but because it matters to her to know that the work is real, that it counts.

What she offers the team is not just skill with Godslayer, though that is considerable. It is clarity of purpose. She grew up being trained to serve someone else's mission. She knows what it feels like to fight without knowing why, and she will not do it again. Every time the Guardians take a job, Gamora asks the same question: who does this help? Not who pays best, not who has the most impressive ship — who does it help?

This standard has cost the Guardians easy money on several occasions. It has also led them to exactly the right places at exactly the right times. On the ice world of Praxius IX, they arrived to find colonists trapped by a corporate mining operation and left with the colonists freed and the mining equipment in three very satisfying pieces. At the relay station above the Kree border, Gamora talked down a standoff that had been building for weeks with forty minutes of careful, patient negotiation.

She is not famous the way some heroes are. There are no statues of her on Xandar — though the people of Xandar would build one gladly. She does not want statues. She wants the galaxy to be better than it was yesterday, one careful decision at a time. That is a longer and quieter kind of heroism than most stories celebrate. It is also, she has decided, exactly the right kind.
