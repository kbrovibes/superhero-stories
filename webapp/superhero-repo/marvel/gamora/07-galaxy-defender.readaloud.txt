How big is the galaxy? Let me put it this way: if you flew the Milano at full speed and never stopped, you would be flying for a VERY long time before you ran out of places to explore. Billions of stars. Thousands of planets. Strange cloud-cities. Ancient ruins. Ice worlds and jungle moons and places that do not have names yet.

Gamora has seen a big chunk of it. She has flown past the golden cloud-cities of Contraxia, and the towering carved columns of Morag, and all along the sweeping curve of the Andromeda frontier. And here is what all those miles of space have taught her:

The galaxy does NOT take care of itself.

The Nova Corps tries. Xandar's officers are incredibly brave. But one group of officers cannot be everywhere at once. There are spots between here and there, in the wide wild dark between planets, where nobody official is watching. Where someone needs help and there is nobody coming.

EXCEPT — a dented little ship that shows up at exactly the right moment.

The Milano has no official badge. No government charter. No permission slip from anybody. What it has is a reputation built from ACTION. The Guardians saved Xandar from Ronan. They have shown up for smaller emergencies that never made the news, on stations and moons and colony ships that needed exactly one crew willing to ask: WHO does this help?

That question is Gamora's. Every single mission, she asks it.

Not who pays the most. Not who has the biggest ship. WHO. DOES. THIS. HELP.

On the ice world of Praxius IX, that question led the Guardians to a group of colonists trapped by a mining company that was taking more than its share. CRASH — the mining equipment ended up in three very satisfying pieces, and the colonists went home safe.

At a relay station above the Kree border, the same question led Gamora to sit across a table from two very angry groups of people and talk — patiently, carefully, for forty minutes — until the standoff that had been building for weeks was over and everyone went home unhurt.

She is not famous. There are no statues of her on Xandar.

She does not want statues.

She wants the galaxy to be better today than it was yesterday. A little safer. A little kinder. Not all at once — just one careful decision at a time.

That kind of heroism does not make a lot of noise.

But it makes a very big difference.

Zoom — there goes the Milano!
