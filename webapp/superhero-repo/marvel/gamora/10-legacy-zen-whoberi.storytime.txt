Once upon a time, when all the big battles had quieted and the universe had begun to breathe again, Gamora set a course for a set of coordinates she knew by heart.

She had never written them down. They lived in her the way some things live in you — not in your head, but deeper, in the place where you keep the things that were yours before you were old enough to choose what to keep.

The coordinates were for Zen-Whoberi.

She flew alone, which she almost never did. The others offered to come. She said no, gently, and they understood — some things need to be done alone the first time.

When the Milano dropped out of warp, she looked out the forward viewport.

The space was empty. Not the normal empty of space, which is always a little full of light and dust and the faint memory of ancient things. This was the particular kind of empty that exists when something has been removed.

Gamora breathed.

Then she put on her suit and flew down to what remained — a fragment, a trace, enough to land on if you were very careful. It did not smell like anything anymore. The sounds were gone.

But above her, the three moons of Zen-Whoberi still traveled their ancient path. They rose in a row as the coordinates moved through their cycle — one, then two, then three — like lanterns on a string, the way they always had.

Gamora sat on the ground of her world.

She thought about the people who had lived here. She could not remember their faces — she had been too young, and so much time had passed. But she remembered the feeling of the place, the way you sometimes remember a song without remembering the words: something gentle, something that moved slowly and chose kindness and understood that what you did for your neighbors mattered.

The Zen-Whoberi had known something Gamora spent her whole life rediscovering: that the universe is made of connections. That what happens to one world sends ripples through all the others. That gentleness is not weakness — it is the thoughtful, careful, deliberate choice to use strength only when truly needed, and with as much care as possible.

She had been given enormous strength. She had been trained to use it without hesitation or questioning, in service of someone else's plan.

She had spent twenty years learning to use it differently. Learning to ask first. Learning to consider who would be hurt and who would be helped. Learning, slowly and with great effort, to be the kind of person her first people would have recognized.

She thought about the Guardians — Peter, Rocket, Groot, Drax — and the family she had built aboard the Milano through five years of shared mornings and hard missions and meals and arguments and small kindnesses. She thought about the dozens of planets they had visited and the hundreds of people they had helped and the choice they made, every single morning, to ask: who needs help today?

She thought about Nebula, finally making her own choices. She thought about Spider-Man, who asked seventeen questions in four minutes and wanted to share tacos with the entire galaxy.

All of it was her inheritance from Zen-Whoberi. The principle her people had known and that she was passing forward: build family. Choose connection. Use what you have been given to protect the ones who cannot protect themselves, without spectacle, without waiting for credit.

She stayed until the three moons completed their arc.

Then she got up, and she flew back to the Milano.

She found them all waiting: Rocket pretending to be busy with something technical; Drax sitting with unusual stillness; Groot turned toward the door with his head tilted, the way he always waited for someone he was worried about; Star-Lord holding a cup of something hot that was clearly meant for her.

She took the cup. She sat down.

Nobody asked. Nobody needed to.

Groot reached over and placed one small new leaf on the console in front of her. Green and new and growing.

Gamora put her hand over it gently.

Outside the porthole, the stars moved in their great slow wheel. Somewhere among them, the three moons of Zen-Whoberi made their quiet circuit, carrying the memory of a gentle world.

And aboard the Milano, the last daughter of Zen-Whoberi sat with her family and held that memory forward like a lantern.

For the Zen-Whoberi. For what they had been. For what she was still becoming.
