Once upon a time, a signal traveled across the dark between the stars and found its way to Gamora's instruments.

She was sitting at the navigation console of the Milano, reading the data on screen with the focused calm of someone who has studied a great many sensor reports and knows exactly what to look for. And there it was — a pulse of cosmic energy, faint but unmistakable. She had spent years learning the frequencies of the Infinity Stones, the way a musician learns the notes of a scale: carefully, until recognition was instant.

This frequency was coming from the ruins of Titan.

Titan was a sad, beautiful place — a moon that had been, long ago, a whole world. Now it was crumbling towers and orange sky and dust that had once been cities. The kind of place that reminds you that things do not last forever — which can be sad, or, depending on how you look at it, a reason to make the time you have count for something.

"We should go," Gamora said.

"Going," said Rocket, already adjusting the heading.

They dropped out of warp near Titan and found, to their complete surprise, that they were not alone.

Another ship was there. Earth design, Gamora determined quickly. The person who stepped out first wore a suit of red-and-gold armor that was clearly extraordinary by any standard. His helmet opened to reveal a face that was alert, intelligent, and taking in the Milano with exactly the same speed that Gamora was applying to him.

Tony Stark. Iron Man.

Behind him came a man in a flowing dark cloak — a cloak that moved on its own, making small adjustments as if it had opinions. He carried a circular pendant of gold and green that Gamora recognized immediately: the Eye of Agamotto, housing its own Infinity Stone. He was called Doctor Strange, and he had the manner of someone who has seen a great deal and learned to be very calm about it.

And then, clambering down with considerable enthusiasm, came a young person in a red-and-blue suit who was clearly delighted to be anywhere in the universe.

"Hi! I'm Spider-Man — and you're Gamora, right? The deadliest woman in the galaxy? Is the green skin natural? What does Godslayer weigh? Have you tried a taco?"

"Yes," Gamora said. "Yes. It is. I don't know. No."

"You should really try a taco."

"Focus," said Tony Stark, not unkindly. Then he looked at Gamora with the direct attention of a person who understands that time is limited. "You know what sent that signal."

"Yes," she said.

"Tell me everything."

"Tell me everything about the Gauntlet," she replied.

It was, she thought, the beginning of a very productive conversation.

They gathered in the ruins of one of Titan's old buildings, where the walls still stood high enough to block the orange wind. Tony and Gamora talked fast and honestly, the way two engineers talk when they have a shared problem and a narrow window.

Gamora told him about Thanos: his patterns, the situations that triggered caution versus overwhelming force, the map she had built of his mind across twenty years of study. She held nothing back. Shared knowledge was a multiplier.

Tony told her about the Gauntlet — how the Stones interacted when combined, the theoretical weaknesses in its design. Doctor Strange, who had said almost nothing, suddenly spoke with precision about the Time Stone and the way it could be manipulated.

Even Spider-Man contributed. He had a way of asking questions that everyone else had stopped asking, and one of his questions revealed something neither Gamora nor Tony had considered.

Fresh eyes are always worth something, Gamora thought.

For one hour above the ruins of Titan, two groups of people who had never met became something neither had expected: a team. Not through a formal agreement. Through the simple act of putting what they knew on the table and trusting each other with it.

The battle that followed was one of the hardest any of them had ever fought. The noise of it echoed off Titan's broken stones. But they fought with knowledge. They fought connected.

Afterward, Gamora looked at Tony Stark across the ruins and understood something she had not quite put into words before.

The universe's heroes were not separate groups, each protecting their own corner of the sky. They were pieces of something larger — something that worked far better when it worked together.

Two families of heroes, she thought.

Same sky. Same side.
