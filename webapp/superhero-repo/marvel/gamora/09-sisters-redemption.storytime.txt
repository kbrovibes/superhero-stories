Once upon a time, two girls woke up every morning on a cold ship and walked to the training deck together.

They were not sisters by birth. But they were sisters by everything else — by shared mornings and shared meals and the particular knowledge that comes from years of living side by side, knowing exactly how someone moves and breathes and guards their left side when they are tired.

Their names were Gamora and Nebula.

And their story took a very long time.

You know how some journeys go? You think they are heading one direction, and then they turn, and turn again, and by the time you arrive somewhere, you realize the road has taken you somewhere you never could have guessed at the start. That is the kind of journey Gamora and Nebula had.

After all the battles and chaos that swept through the universe, the world needed time to quiet down and heal. Things that had been broken were being put carefully back together, piece by piece.

And in the middle of that slow work, Gamora found herself on a battlefield above Earth, standing beside someone she had not expected.

Nebula.

Not the cold, mechanical Nebula who had hunted her across the drifting rock-city of Knowhere. This Nebula was still herself — still carrying all of it, the metal joints and memories and complicated past. But she was here, on this side, by her own choice.

She had seen the full shape of Thanos's plan from inside it, and she had decided she wanted no part of it.

Gamora knew exactly what that choice cost. She had made it herself.

The battle was the kind that fills the sky and every space in between with noise and motion. Wonderful and terrifying and hard to keep track of.

In the middle of it, Gamora moved to block an attack from one direction and found Nebula moving to block one from the other.

They ended up back to back.

Swords raised. Feet planted. Covering each other's blind spot without discussion, because their bodies remembered what their history had complicated: they knew each other's fighting like they knew their own heartbeats. Years of mornings on the Sanctuary had put that knowledge in their muscles, and it did not care about the years of anger that followed.

"You have my back," Nebula said. Her voice was careful. Surprised.

"I always did," Gamora said.

For a moment it was almost quiet between them in all that noise.

Then Nebula said, with great care: "I blamed you. For every replacement. For every time he made me into something else. I blamed you because you won."

"I know," Gamora said.

"I understand now that it was not only your fault."

"I know," Gamora said. "I know."

The battle called them back, and they answered. Side by side, with the easy coordination of people who have always known each other's next move.

When it was over, they sat together in the stillness that follows very large things. Two women, an arm's length apart.

Gamora looked at her sister — at the blue skin and the metal and the eyes that had, for the first time in a long while, something other than fury in them — and thought about the strange roads that life takes.

She thought about Zen-Whoberi, where she had started. She thought about the Sanctuary, and the day she walked away. She thought about the Kyln and the Milano and all the mornings since: the sword forms, the missions, Groot's small quiet gifts, Rocket's loud jokes, Drax's slowly healing heart.

She thought about how much family she had found, the long way around.

And she thought: perhaps there was still room for one more.

Not simple. Not quick. But real.

"I never stopped hoping you would find your way here," Gamora said.

Nebula looked at her. The metal parts of her face could not show expression the way Gamora's could. But her eyes did something quiet and complicated.

"Here is a very strange place to end up," she said.

"Yes," Gamora agreed. "It usually is."

They sat together a little longer. The sky above Earth began to clear.

And two sisters — who had taken the longest road anyone could imagine — rested.

Even the long way around is still a way home.
