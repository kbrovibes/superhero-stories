Of all the rivals Gamora has ever faced, none are more complicated than Nebula. They trained together in the same ship, ate at the same table, and sparred every morning on the same cold floor of the Sanctuary. They were sisters — not by blood, but by the fierce, difficult bond that forms when two people survive the same hard childhood side by side. And they were rivals, because Thanos made them into rivals, always pitting one against the other.

Nebula lost those fights, most of the time. And every time she lost, Thanos replaced a part of her with machinery — a new eye, a new arm, new circuits woven into her brain — until she was more metal than flesh. She was brilliant, ferocious, and furious at the whole universe, but mostly at Gamora, who she blamed for every upgrade and every loss. The anger between them was real. So was the grief underneath it.

When Nebula hunted Gamora across the galaxy, she came not just for revenge but for the satisfaction of finally winning. She flew a sleek, blue-black ship and carried twin blasters and an energy blade, and she was nearly as fast as Gamora and twice as relentless. In a battle over the planet Knowhere, the two fought so hard that the air around them seemed to spark. Nebula knew every one of Gamora's moves — she had trained against them for years.

But Gamora had learned something Nebula had not: how to stop fighting alone. She had teammates now. She had people watching her back. When Nebula gained the upper hand and it seemed like the duel was over, Drax and Rocket arrived — not to fight Nebula, but to buy Gamora a breath, a moment, a chance to regroup. It was enough.

Gamora did not defeat her sister with strength or speed. She defeated her by not giving up — by getting back up one more time, planting her feet, and looking at Nebula with steady eyes until even Nebula's fury ran out of fuel. Nebula retreated, and her hatred curdled into something Gamora recognized — not just anger, but pain. The kind of pain that comes from feeling unseen.

Gamora began to understand that their rivalry was never entirely about winning. It was about wanting to matter to someone. She held that understanding quietly, like a candle in a storm, and hoped that someday — in a better year, in a calmer corner of the universe — Nebula might find her way home.
