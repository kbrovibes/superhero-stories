When Hope van Dyne first crossed paths with Ghost, she expected a fight. Ghost — whose real name was Ava Starr — had been stealing pieces of the Pym Particle research, phasing through walls and vanishing before anyone could land a hand on her. But the moment Hope actually looked at Ava, in the harsh laboratory light, she did not see a villain. She saw a young woman in terrible pain.

Ava had not chosen her powers. Years before, her father, Elihas Starr, had run a dangerous quantum experiment at S.H.I.E.L.D., and something had gone wrong. The blast killed her parents and saturated little Ava with raw quantum energy. From that moment on, Ava's molecules were never quite settled — she flickered in and out of phase with the real world. She could pass through walls. She could become invisible. She could deliver strikes that no armor could stop. And every second of every day, it hurt.

Ava had been raised, in secret, by Dr. Bill Foster — a kind, retired scientist who had once been Hank Pym's research partner. Bill Foster, the world knew him as Goliath, had been one of the original size-changing heroes, growing to towering height alongside Hank in his younger days. Bill knew the technology. He had built a chamber to ease Ava's pain. But the pain kept coming back, and Ava was running out of time.

Hope might have hated Ava for the missions she had ruined and the laboratory she had stolen. Instead, when Hope understood Ava's story, she did something her younger self might not have done. She helped. With her father, with Bill Foster, with newly rescued Janet, Hope worked out a plan. Janet — who had spent thirty years in the Quantum Realm — could safely transfer healing quantum energy from her own body into Ava's, stabilizing Ava's molecules without anyone else getting hurt.

It worked. Ava's body settled. Her pain quieted. She still flickered sometimes, on bad days, but she was no longer dissolving. She did not become Hope's best friend overnight — too much had happened — but she stopped being an enemy. Bill Foster shook Hank Pym's hand for the first time in many years. And Hope learned a lesson her mother had quietly modeled her whole life. Sometimes the strongest move a hero can make is not a punch or a stinger. Sometimes it is choosing to understand.
