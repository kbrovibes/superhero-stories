The first time Hope van Dyne flew, she cried. Not from sadness — from joy. Her father had finally finished the new Wasp suit, and the wings were the part Hope had wanted most. They were two pairs of clear, dragonfly-thin panels that folded out of housings on her back, shimmering in the workshop light. Scott Lang's Ant-Man suit had no wings. He had to ride a flying carpenter ant named Ant-thony for air support. But Hope? Hope could rise straight off the ground on her own.

The wings were not the only thing that made the Wasp suit special. Built into the wrists were two bio-electric blasters, called stingers. They were Hope's own design — yellow flashes of focused electricity that could fire from full size or from ant size with the same precision. A single stinger could short out a security camera, or stun a guard, or punch a clean hole through a steel door. Two stingers at once, properly aimed, could drop an armored truck.

Hope had practiced for years before her father trusted her with the suit. She had studied martial arts since childhood — boxing, jiu-jitsu, escrima — and she had drilled her shrinking technique alone in the Pym Tech parking garage at midnight, popping in and out of size with the precision of a metronome. When Scott Lang had needed a teacher, she had been ready. When Hank Pym had finally needed a Wasp, she had been ready.

Her combat style is something completely her own. She fights mostly in the air, hovering just behind a thug's shoulder before SHRINKing down to a thumbprint, dodging the swing, and reappearing at full size with an elbow at the back of his head. Sometimes she shrinks her body but keeps her fist full-sized for a split second — landing a giant punch that ends the fight on impact. Sometimes she shrinks an entire room around her and zips through the gap. Scott calls it her "tactical music." He says she is the only person he knows who can do calculus while throwing a kick.

Once, on a mission to recover a stolen Pym disc, Hope flew through an entire warehouse of armed mercenaries without ever touching the ground. She came home with the disc, a bruised elbow, and a small, satisfied smile. Her father met her in the kitchen. He poured her a cup of tea. He looked at her — really looked at her — and said, "Your mother would be very proud." Hope did not say anything. She did not need to.
