After her mother came back from the Quantum Realm, Hope van Dyne expected things to feel different. She just did not realize how MANY ways they would feel different. The first morning after the rescue, she walked into the kitchen and found Janet standing at the window with a coffee cup in both hands, watching the fog roll across the bay.

"It's still here," Janet said quietly.

"What's still here?" Hope asked.

"The fog. The bay. Coffee."

Hope laughed. She had not yet started to understand how strange ordinary things looked to someone who had spent thirty years in a place where ordinary things did not exist.

That was how the first few weeks went.

Janet had been gone since 1987. She had no concept of the internet. She did not know what a cell phone was. She had never seen a flat-screen television, never used a microwave, never been to a Trader Joe's. She had missed every president, every world cup, every Olympic games, every Marvel movie, every Pixar movie, every Beyonce album. She did not know what an emoji was, and the first time Hope tried to explain emojis at breakfast, Janet stared at the phone in genuine confusion.

"So this little smiling sun-thing means... what, again?"

"It just means you're happy."

"You could just SAY you're happy."

"Yes, but then you'd have to use words."

"...Hope. People have stopped using words?"

Hope laughed so hard she almost spilled her tea. Janet, after a long second, started laughing too, and the kitchen filled with the sound of two people who had not been able to laugh together for thirty years finally doing it.

The teaching went both ways.

While Hope taught Janet about smartphones and Wi-Fi and the modern world, Janet taught Hope about the Quantum Realm — and what Janet knew about the Quantum Realm was beyond anything any scientist on Earth had ever guessed. Janet had walked across landscapes made of pure probability. She had met creatures who spoke only in patterns of light. She had learned to read the strange shimmering equations that wrote themselves across the air down there. She had befriended a kind of luminescent ant-creature that she still missed.

She filled notebooks. She filled chalkboards. Hope, who had spent her whole life studying her father's Pym Particle research, was suddenly studying her MOTHER'S quantum biology, and the two of them spent late nights in the laboratory drawing diagrams while Hank looked on, baffled and proud.

It was inevitable that they would start working together as Wasps.

The Wasp suit Hope had been wearing was a brand-new, third-generation design — red and gold and black, sleek and modern, with bio-electric stingers Hope had designed herself. But Janet's original suit was still in storage at Pym Technologies, locked in a climate-controlled vault. It was the suit she had worn back in the founding days of the Avengers, alongside Captain America and Iron Man and Thor. It was faded red-and-black, with thin glassy wings, a round retro helmet, and stingers that pulsed in a bluish-white tone you do not see on suits made today.

Hank dug it out. He brushed off the dust. He repaired the wings — both pairs were cracked, after thirty years in a vault — and replaced the stinger capacitors with modern ones. The suit still fit Janet, who had not aged as fast in the Quantum Realm as she would have on Earth.

The first time Janet stepped into the workshop wearing it, Hope just stood there. She did not move. She did not even breathe for a second. Janet was a Wasp again. Hope was a Wasp. Both of their wings were folded. Both of their stingers were charged. The same workshop light glinted off both helmets.

"You okay?" Janet asked, very gently.

"Yeah," Hope said. "Just. I have wanted this since I was seven."

Janet walked over and hugged her, suit and all.

After that, they started taking missions together. They were a remarkable team. Janet brought thirty years of survival instincts and a deep, instinctive understanding of quantum physics. Hope brought thirty years of training, engineering, and modern combat skills. On a mission they split the work the way only family can split it. Janet led the science, scanning for energy signatures, identifying threats Hope could not see. Hope led the tactics, picking the angles of attack, calling the timing. They never had to argue about who did what. The roles just fit.

When they fought, the rhythm was extraordinary.

Janet's combat style was the older, more graceful Wasp style — efficient, conservative, almost dance-like. She had learned to fight in an era when the Avengers wore wool capes and posed for newspaper photographers. She moved like she was conserving every drop of energy. Hope's combat style was newer, faster, sharper — she shrank and grew and shrank and grew, weaving in and out of fights like a hummingbird. Together they were one of the strangest and most effective duos in the superhero community.

Stingers in the air made a beautiful pattern. Janet's pulsed in a bluish-white tone, the color of old fluorescent bulbs. Hope's were bright yellow-gold, the color of new sunshine. When the two Wasps zipped past each other in midair, their stingers crisscrossed like fireworks.

One early mission stuck in Hope's memory in particular. A small group of arms dealers had stolen a single Pym disc from a Pym Tech shipping yard. One Pym disc could shrink a panel van down to the size of a sneaker. The arms dealers planned to sell it overseas. Hope and Janet went together. Janet shrank to the size of a beetle and zipped through a keyhole. Hope flew through a vent. They met in the middle of the warehouse, exchanged the briefest of nods, and went to work.

It took two minutes.

Janet's bluish stingers dropped three guards on the left. Hope's yellow stingers dropped four on the right. They circled each other in midair the way two birds circle each other in a thermal — perfectly synchronized, perfectly silent, completely deadly to anyone who tried to lay hands on the Pym disc. By the end of the two minutes, eight arms dealers were unconscious on the floor, and the Pym disc was tucked safely into a compartment on Hope's belt.

Hank watched the security footage later, sipped his coffee, and said, "I should have introduced you two to each other years ago." It was a joke. It was also not really a joke.

But honestly, the missions were not the best part.

The best part was the small things.

The morning walks through the garden, where Janet would sniff every flower she had not smelled in thirty years and ask careful questions about which ones had been new since she left. The pasta nights, where Janet would try to explain to Hope that the Quantum Realm has no concept of spaghetti, and Hope would try to explain to Janet that Trader Joe's has invented seventeen different kinds of dried mushroom risotto since she left. The Sunday afternoons, where Janet would sit on the back porch with a hairbrush and brush out Hope's long hair the way she had not been able to do since Hope was seven years old.

The house in San Francisco — the big, quiet, eucalyptus-scented house that had carried the sadness of a missing person for three decades — was finally full again. There were three Pyms at the dinner table. There were two Wasp suits in the workshop. There was laughter at breakfast and arguments about TV shows and arguments about science and tea poured for three.

Hope sometimes caught her father just watching them all from the doorway with the look of a man who could not believe his luck. Hank Pym had lost a lot of things in his life. He had lost his wife. He had lost his teammates. He had lost his old partner Bill Foster for years. He had even lost a little of his own kindness, behind years of grief.

He had not lost his daughter. And now, miraculously, he had his wife back too.

For Hope, the lesson of those months was simple and quiet. The new Wasp had spent her whole life training to be a hero like her mother. What she discovered, when her mother came home, was that being a hero like her mother was easier than she had thought — because her mother was still her mother. She still laughed at jokes. She still cooked terrible pasta. She still brushed Hope's hair on Sundays. The Wasp was always going to be a costume. The mother was the real thing.

And after thirty years, Hope finally had her back.
