Okay, brave readers — take a deep breath. This is the BIG one. This is the story Hope van Dyne had been waiting for since she was seven years old. Are you ready? Because we are going to bring her mom home.

For THIRTY YEARS, Hope thought her mother was gone forever. Janet van Dyne — the original Wasp — had shrunk past the subatomic barrier to save the world, and nobody had heard from her since. Hope grew up. Hank Pym grew old. The house in San Francisco stayed quiet. That was the story Hope thought she was living.

But then, one evening, Hank sat his daughter down and told her the truth he had been keeping for years. Janet was NOT dead. Janet had survived. She was alive — somewhere in the Quantum Realm — and Hank had been secretly building a machine that might bring her home.

Hope did not yell. She did not cry. She just stood up, walked straight into the workshop, and said, "Show me the plans."

The plan was something called a Quantum Tunnel. It was a glowing doorway big enough to send a person down through the subatomic barrier and pull them back up again. Hank and Hope built it together inside that rolling-suitcase laboratory Hope had designed — pop, big! pop, small! They tested. They tuned. They got SO close.

And then disaster. A sneaky thief named Sonny Burch tried to steal the technology to sell it. A mysterious fighter called Ghost — who could phase right through walls — was after the same machine. They snatched the laboratory and took off through San Francisco.

What followed was the wildest chase the city had ever seen. Hope and Scott and Hank were popping cars in and out of full size — VROOM, tiny! VROOM, huge! VROOM, tiny again! They shrank a delivery truck to the size of a toy and rolled it under a parked van. They grew a Hello Kitty Pez dispenser into a giant boulder and sent it rolling down a hill. They flew. They shrank. They drove. They flew. And somehow, by the end, they had the laboratory back.

The Quantum Tunnel was almost ready. But how would they FIND Janet? She was so impossibly small, so impossibly far away?

The answer was surprising. Scott Lang had visited the Quantum Realm once before — and while he was down there, Janet had brushed against his mind, planting a message inside him like a little glowing seed. Now Scott was a kind of phone line straight to her. When Hope and Hank tuned the tunnel correctly, they could trace the signal Janet had left.

Hope sat at the console. Her hands were perfectly steady, even though her heart was not. Hank put on his old diving suit. Scott helped with the dial. The tunnel began to hum. The light went from yellow to green to blue to purple to a bright shimmering BLUE-PURPLE swirl. The Quantum Realm opened up like a flower.

And out of it stepped Janet van Dyne.

She had quantum energy on her fingertips — silver and softly glowing — like she was painted in starlight. She was older. She was wiser. She had spent THIRTY YEARS in a place where time does not move in straight lines.

But the moment she saw Hope, none of that mattered.

Janet crossed the room in three steps. She put her hands on Hope's face. She said her daughter's name very, very softly — "Hope" — like the word was a song she had been humming to herself all those long years.

Hope had become a brilliant scientist. A genius fighter. A precise, determined hero. But the bravest, hardest, most wonderful thing she ever did was just this: she believed her father. She helped him build a door. And on a foggy night in San Francisco, she brought her mother home.
