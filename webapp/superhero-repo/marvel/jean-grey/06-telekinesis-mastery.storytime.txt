Once upon a time, Jean Grey reached for a pencil across the table — and the pencil came to her.

She had not told it to. She had not even quite meant it. But her mind had wanted the pencil, and the pencil had moved, and there it was in her fingers, and for a long moment she simply sat and looked at it.

Then she laughed.

It was a surprise, telekinesis. She had arrived at the Xavier Institute knowing she was a telepath — that much was undeniable, that much had already turned her whole world upside down. But this was different. This was not about hearing. This was about doing.

Professor Xavier noticed the next day when a teacup lifted three inches from his desk as Jean reached for it across the room. He set down his book and looked at her very thoughtfully.

"We have more to explore," he said.

He started her small. A book from a shelf. A training disc sailing across the Danger Room. Jean found it easy — too easy, almost, like using a muscle that had been waiting to be used. Professor Xavier pushed her further, asking for heavier objects, then for precision: not just lifting something, but placing it exactly, gently, in a specific spot without disturbing anything around it.

A chair lifted and set down without a sound.
A heavy training beam caught mid-fall and lowered like a feather.
A car — an entire car — raised three feet off the ground, held steady, then set back down without a scratch.

Jean worked until catching falling objects became pure reflex. She did not have to think about it any more than you think about breathing. Something falling? Her mind was already there, steady as a pair of hands.

The development she was most proud of was what she called telekinetic shielding. It came to her during a training exercise when a blast was heading for two teammates at once and she threw her concentration between them before she had any conscious thought. The blast stopped. Not deflected sideways — stopped. There was simply an invisible wall where there had been nothing, solid enough to take the full force of the impact.

She practiced the shielding until she could wrap it around a whole group, layering it the way you layer blankets: firm enough to resist, light enough to hold without exhausting herself. During a mission in New Mexico, she held a telekinetic dome over the entire X-Men squad for eleven minutes — eleven full minutes — while Storm worked to resolve a dangerous weather situation overhead. Hank McCoy calculated afterward, with great enthusiasm and a lot of mathematics, that she had been intercepting several hundred kilograms of debris the whole time.

Jean said only: "How's your math, Hank?"

He said: "Impeccable." And they both laughed.

She also discovered, almost by accident, that telekinesis could be applied at the very smallest scales. She had been looking at one of Professor Xavier's oldest books — a beautiful, fragile thing — when she noticed a page had been torn, a long diagonal tear through a paragraph of important text. She looked at it for a moment, then looked more carefully, focusing not on the tear but on the individual fibers of the paper itself.

She pressed them back together. Very slowly. Very gently. With her mind.

The tear closed, seamlessly, as if it had never been there.

Professor Xavier came in as she was finishing. He looked at the page. He looked at Jean. He sat down quietly and was still for a moment in the way he was still when he was genuinely moved.

"That," he said at last, "is extraordinary."

Jean never used her telekinesis carelessly. There was a moment before every use — just a breath, just a pause — when she asked herself whether this was the right thing. Whether this was what the moment needed. Not out of fear. Not out of doubt. But out of the deep, considered respect for a gift that could do a great deal and therefore deserved a great deal of thought.

Professor Xavier had taught her that. And she had never forgotten it.

You can do so much with careful hands — even when those hands are made of thought.

Sleep well, dreamer. You are surrounded by things that hold you gently.
