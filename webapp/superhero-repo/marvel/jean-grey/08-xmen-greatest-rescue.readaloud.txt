Are you ready for the X-Men's greatest rescue mission? Then hold tight — because this one goes underground!

The island of Genosha was in trouble. A giant sentinel robot — one of those enormous mutant-hunting machines — had been reactivated and was stomping around wrecking the power grid while dozens of civilians were trapped below the city in collapsed transit tunnels. The X-Men had been called in.

Professor Xavier gave everyone their assignment at TWO IN THE MORNING. Scott — take out the optical systems. Bobby Drake — freeze those joints. Ororo Munroe — ground the sensors. Hank McCoy — climb up and cut the signal. And Jean?

Jean gets the hardest job.

Jean goes underground.

Down she went, into the dark tunnels, with cracked stone walls and water rising around her boots. But Jean didn't need lights. She was using her TELEPATHY as a map — reaching ahead through the dark, feeling for frightened minds.

One. Five. Twenty. FORTY-THREE people, two levels underground, in a flooded transit platform.

Some were hurt. Many were terrified. But the moment Jean reached them, she sent something ahead of her first — not words, not a command. Just warmth. Just the feeling of: someone is here, someone has come, you are not forgotten.

She felt forty-three waves of fear ease, just a little.

"This way," she said. "I know another route."

She found an alternate passage — longer, but solid. She led the group forward. Three people were too injured to walk easily, so Jean wrapped them in gentle telekinetic stretchers — FLOAT — and they glided forward beside the others.

RUMBLE — the ceiling groaned. Jean reached up with her telekinesis without breaking stride. The stones held.

BOOM — BOOM — BOOM. Above them on the street, the rest of the X-Men were fighting the sentinel. The ground shook like a drum. Jean kept walking. Kept leading. Kept holding.

Twenty minutes. Forty-three people. A maintenance staircase rising up to — the night air!

Jean brought the last person up to the surface just as — CRASH — the sentinel went down above the city.

She sat on the curb. She breathed. The telekinetic stretchers drifted gently to the ground.

Hank appeared beside her almost immediately. "All forty-three?" he asked.

"All forty-three," she said.

Ororo landed. Scott jogged over. And the five X-Men stood together in the wreckage while forty-three people found each other in the light of emergency lanterns and called out the names of the people they loved.

That is what it is all for. Every last bit of it.
