The villain called Mastermind had a talent that made him one of the most dangerous tricksters the X-Men had ever faced. His real name was Jason Wyngarde, and his mutant power was illusion — he could make any person see anything he wanted them to see, as vividly and convincingly as if it were real. He had spent years using that gift to deceive, to confuse, and to make himself look more impressive than he actually was. Now he turned that power toward one target: Jean Grey.

It began subtly. Jean started noticing flickers at the edges of her vision — shadows that moved strangely, doors she did not recognize, a hallway at the Xavier Institute that seemed longer than it should be. She told herself she was tired. She told herself it was nothing. But Mastermind was patient, and he had been practicing his art for a long time. He wove illusions into Jean's surroundings the way a spider weaves a web: one thread at a time, almost invisible, until the whole structure was complete.

Professor Xavier noticed something was wrong before Jean did. He watched her pause in conversations, her eyes going distant. He knew that look — it was the look of a mind being pulled in two directions at once. He called the X-Men together and explained what he suspected. Scott Summers was on his feet before the Professor finished speaking.

The confrontation came in the streets of Manhattan, where Mastermind had built an elaborate illusion to make Jean believe she was trapped in a maze of mirrors with no way out. Jean could hear Scott calling for her, but each time she reached toward his voice she saw only her own reflection staring back. Mastermind laughed from somewhere in the false shadows, confident that a girl lost in her own mind could not fight back.

He had underestimated Jean Grey entirely. She closed her eyes and reached outward with her telepathy — not toward the images her eyes were showing her, but toward the real minds in the space around her. There was Scott, steady and determined. There was the thin, slippery thread of Mastermind's concentration, coiled like a wire under the illusions. Jean followed that thread directly to its source and pushed, not cruelly but firmly — a mental nudge that shattered his focus like a stone through glass. The maze of mirrors vanished in an instant.

Mastermind stumbled backward, genuinely surprised. He had never encountered a telepath who could dismantle an illusion from the inside. Jean stood in the sudden daylight of Midtown, Scott's hand finding hers, and she allowed herself one quiet moment of satisfaction. She had fought the trick without ever needing to see through it — she had simply reached past it to find what was real.
