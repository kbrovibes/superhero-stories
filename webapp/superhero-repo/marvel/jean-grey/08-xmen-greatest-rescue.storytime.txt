Once upon a time, the X-Men received an emergency call from a place called Genosha, and they answered it in the middle of the night.

Genosha was an island nation, and something had gone very wrong there. A sentinel robot — one of the enormous mechanical giants once built for hunting mutants — had been reactivated by an unknown signal. It was moving through the streets, cutting power and communications, and in its wake the transit stations beneath the city had collapsed, trapping people in the dark far below ground.

Professor Xavier briefed the team at two in the morning. His voice was calm and steady, as it always was in difficult moments. He described the sentinel's systems and its weaknesses and gave each X-Man a specific task. Ororo Munroe — Storm — would use her command of weather to ground the sentinel's atmospheric sensors and blind it. Bobby Drake — Iceman — would coat its joints in thick ice to slow its movement. Hank McCoy — Beast — would climb to its communication array and sever the signal controlling it. Scott Summers would target the optical systems.

And Jean? Jean would go underground, find the trapped civilians, and bring all of them out.

She went down alone into the dark.

The tunnels were narrow and cracked, and water was rising around her ankles. Jean moved without hesitation, because she did not need light. Her telepathy was her compass — reaching ahead through the stone and the dark, feeling for the particular texture of many frightened minds pressed close together.

She found them. Forty-three people in a flooded platform at the lowest level: cold, frightened, some of them injured, all of them waiting for something that had not come yet. The fear in that enclosed space was like a weight pressing on the air.

Jean stopped before she entered.

She breathed. She reached. She sent warmth ahead of her — not a command, not a direction, nothing that told them what to do. Just a presence. The feeling of: I am here, I have come, you are not forgotten.

She felt it move through the room like a candle lit in a dark corner.

She stepped inside and spoke clearly and calmly: "I know the way out. Follow me."

She had sensed an alternate passage — longer than the direct route, but solid and uncompromised. She led the group forward, checking the ceiling as she went, maintaining the calm she had sent ahead so it would not fade. Three people were too hurt to walk the full distance. Jean lifted them gently onto telekinetic stretchers — invisible supports that carried them at walking height, steady as boats on still water.

Halfway through, the passage groaned.

Jean raised her telekinesis to the ceiling and held it, the way you hold a door open for someone coming through behind you — unhurried, reliable. She stood perfectly still for a long moment until the groaning quieted. Then she said, calmly: "Keep going." And they kept going.

Above them, the battle shook the earth. BOOM — the sentinel's enormous steps. CRACK — the X-Men meeting it head-on. The ground vibrated and the loose stone chattered against the walls. Jean walked and held and guided for twenty steady minutes without once letting her calm waver.

At the top of a long maintenance staircase, she pushed open a heavy door and the night air of Genosha came rushing in, cool and wide and real.

Jean brought the last person through the door just as she felt, far above in the street, the sentinel fall.

She sat on the curb. She breathed. The telekinetic stretchers settled their passengers gently to the ground.

Hank McCoy appeared at a fast run and stopped, looking at Jean and the forty-three people gathered behind her.

"All of them?" he said.

"All of them," Jean said.

Ororo arrived on a breath of wind. Scott came from the direction of the wreckage, his visor cracked from the fight, and looked first at the crowd and then at Jean.

The five X-Men stood together in the broken street while forty-three people sorted themselves out — finding friends, calling out names, reaching for the ones they had been afraid they would not see again. Emergency lanterns cast warm circles of light in the dark city.

Jean sat on the curb and watched them find each other.

That was what it was all for. The training and the hard choices and the long nights. All of it — every bit — was for this moment: people finding each other in the light.

Sleep well, dear heart. Someone is always finding the way out of the dark.
