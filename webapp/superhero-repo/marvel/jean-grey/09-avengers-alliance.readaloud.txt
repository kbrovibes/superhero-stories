Picture this: a Kree warship hovering above the Atlantic Ocean, pointing a very nasty weapon at the eastern coast of North America. Not a laser. Not a missile. A PSYCHIC disruptor — designed to scramble the brainwaves of millions of people at once and leave them completely unable to think clearly.

The Avengers were in trouble. Thor's Mjolnir? Magnificent against robots. Not great against a psychic signal. Iron Man's repulsors? Tony Stark is brilliant — brilliant enough to know this was not a problem his armor could punch. Captain America's shield? No.

They needed a telepath. They called the X-Men. Jean Grey picked up the phone.

She flew out to the S.H.I.E.L.D. Helicarrier floating above the Atlantic — and wow, the team waiting for her. Tony Stark, who gave her a firm handshake and immediately started explaining his schematics. Natasha Romanoff, who asked three precise technical questions about her telepathic range before they reached the briefing room. And Captain America — Steve Rogers — who looked at Jean and simply said: "We are very glad you're here."

Jean felt that one.

The plan came together fast. Thor and Iron Man would take their Asgardian thunder and their arc reactor blasts to the front of the Kree ship, keeping the defense drones busy and making a lot of very dramatic noise. That was their job: be loud, be impossible to ignore.

Jean's job was the opposite: be very quiet, and very precise.

She sat cross-legged on the Helicarrier's deck, eyes closed, the Atlantic wind moving her hair, and she reached outward with her telepathy. Past the ocean. Past the interference from the ship's engines. Into the hull itself, searching for the particular psychic frequency of the disruptor — the narrow, specific signal she had identified in Tony's scans, a note in a register only she could hear.

She found it.

She jammed it. Her own telepathy, pushing directly against that frequency, like putting your thumb over the end of a garden hose.

Ten seconds. Twenty. Thirty.

CLICK — the disruptor shut down.

Forty-seven seconds total. That was all it took.

Across the whole eastern seaboard, millions of people felt lighter without knowing why.

Captain America found Jean on the observation deck afterward, looking at the ocean. He sat beside her and told her that what she had done mattered — not just the victory, but the example.

Jean thought of Professor Xavier. She smiled. "That's the oldest idea at our school," she said.

Avengers and X-Men. Together. You love to see it!
