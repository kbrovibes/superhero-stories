Scott Summers had been at the Xavier Institute longer than Jean, and from the day she arrived he had been the kind of steady presence that made a new place feel less unknown. He carried a quiet seriousness about him — a boy who measured every word and every step — that Jean recognized as the kind of person who has learned, through experience, to be careful with the world. Scott's eyes were always hidden behind ruby-quartz glasses or a red visor, because without them the powerful optic beams he generated could not be contained. Jean never thought of those glasses as a barrier. She thought of them as part of who Scott was — like a musician who never goes anywhere without their instrument.

Their friendship developed slowly and honestly, the way good friendships do. They trained together in the Danger Room, where Jean's telepathic warnings and Scott's precision made them a remarkable team. She would sense movement before it happened; he would act on her warnings before anyone else had processed them. They developed a shorthand — a glance, a nod — that the other X-Men sometimes could not follow. Hank McCoy once joked that Jean and Scott had their own private language and he was not sure whether it was telepathy or just knowing each other very well. Jean smiled and said it was a little of both.

What drew Jean to Scott was not just his steady courage — it was that he was one of the very few people who was not at all unsettled by her telepathy. Some people, when they learned Jean could read minds, became self-conscious, watching their own thoughts the way a person watches their reflection in a shop window. Scott simply went on being himself. If anything, he was more transparent with her than most, as if the existence of her gift gave him permission to be honest in a way that was harder when speaking aloud.

They faced dozens of missions together before either of them said a word about what the other one meant to them. Jean could have known, of course — she could have listened. She never did. There are things a telepath chooses not to hear, because some things belong to the person who is feeling them until that person decides to share them.

Scott told her eventually, in the garden at the Xavier Institute, on a quiet evening when the Hudson Valley sky was turning the color of embers. Jean already knew how she felt. She had known for a long time. She listened to every word he said — out loud, the way he meant to say it — and then she told him exactly how she felt in return.

Some of the most important conversations are the ones you let happen in the right time, in the right way, without rushing them.
