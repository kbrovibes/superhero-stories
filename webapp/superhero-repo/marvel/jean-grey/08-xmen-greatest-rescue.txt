The city of Genosha had been calling for help for three days before the X-Men could get there. An enormous mechanical sentinel — one of the giant mutant-hunting robots the government had deployed in past years — had been reactivated by an unknown signal and was systematically cutting off the island nation's power grid, trapping civilians underground in collapsed transit stations while defense systems fired uselessly against its armored shell.

Professor Xavier briefed the team at two in the morning: Scott, Jean, Ororo Munroe, Bobby Drake, and Hank McCoy. The plan was layered. Storm would ground the sentinel's atmospheric sensors. Iceman would freeze its joints to slow it down. Beast would climb to its communication array and sever the controlling signal. Scott would hit the optical systems. Jean's role was the one nobody wanted — she would go below ground and find the trapped civilians while the rest drew the sentinel's attention above.

Below ground was dark and full of cracked stone and rising water. Jean moved through the tunnels using her telepathy as a map, reaching ahead for the distinct texture of frightened minds pressed together in small spaces. She found a group of forty-three people in a flooded transit platform two levels down, twelve of them injured, some badly frightened, several of them losing hope. She reached into the space above her panic-swept crowd and sent warmth first — not a command, just presence, the lighthouse feeling — and felt the group's collective fear ease by several degrees.

She got them moving through an alternate passage she had sensed: longer but intact, coming up through a maintenance corridor to street level. She levitated the injured on gentle telekinetic stretchers, held the passage ceiling when it groaned, and guided forty-three people through the dark for twenty minutes while above them the X-Men and the sentinel traded enormous impacts that shook the ground like thunder.

The sentinel went down just as Jean led the last person up the maintenance stairs into the night air of Genosha. She sat on the curb for a moment, telekinetic stretchers still floating softly, and let herself breathe.

Hank McCoy appeared beside her almost immediately. "All forty-three?" he said.

"All forty-three," Jean said.

Ororo landed beside them in a rush of wind, and Scott jogged across the debris-strewn street, and for a moment the five of them stood together in the wreckage while the people they had rescued gathered in the light of emergency lanterns and called for the people they loved.

That was what the X-Men were for. Not the enormous battles — although those mattered too. The forty-three people finding each other in the light.
