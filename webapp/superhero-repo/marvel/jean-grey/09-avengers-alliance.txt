The Avengers and the X-Men were not always natural allies. They operated in different worlds — the Avengers visible, celebrated, based in New York's Stark Tower; the X-Men quieter, working in the margins of a world that was still deciding how it felt about mutants. But crises have a way of pushing people through doors they might never have opened on their own, and when a Kree warship deployed a psychic disruptor weapon above the Atlantic seaboard, Captain America called Professor Xavier directly.

The disruptor was designed to scramble human neural signals across a thousand-mile radius. It was not lethal, but it would leave millions of people unable to coordinate thought or movement — helpless for as long as the weapon remained active. The Avengers' problem was that their best weapons were physical: Thor's Mjolnir, Iron Man's repulsors, the Hulk's enormous strength. None of those would shut down a psychic device. They needed a telepath.

Jean flew to the Helicarrier that S.H.I.E.L.D. had positioned above the Atlantic, where she met Tony Stark, who was polite and professionally respectful in the careful way of someone who knows they need your help and is smart enough to say so. She met Natasha Romanoff, who shook her hand and asked three technical questions about the range of Jean's telepathy in the time it took to walk from the landing pad to the briefing room. She met Captain America, Steve Rogers, who simply looked at her and said: "We're very glad you're here."

Jean worked alongside Tony's analysis of the Kree ship's systems to identify the disruptor's mental signature — a narrow-band psychic frequency she could recognize the same way a musician recognizes a note. While Thor and Iron Man kept the warship's defense drones occupied with a frontal assault, Jean focused her telepathy on a single point inside the ship's core: the psionic emitter at the heart of the disruptor. She reached through the hull, through the interference, and jammed that frequency with her own signal.

The disruptor collapsed after forty-seven seconds. Across the Atlantic seaboard, millions of people felt the pressure lift without ever knowing it had been there.

Afterward, Captain America found Jean sitting at the edge of the Helicarrier's observation deck, watching the ocean. He sat beside her and told her what she had done mattered — not just the outcome, but the precedent. A mutant and an Avenger working together, demonstrating that extraordinary people with different histories could choose the same direction.

Jean thought about Professor Xavier's dream. She smiled. "That's actually the oldest idea at our school," she said.
