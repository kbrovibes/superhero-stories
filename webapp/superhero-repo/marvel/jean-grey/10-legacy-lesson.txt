The Xavier Institute accepted a new class of students every year, and every year Jean found herself drawn back to help with the early weeks of training. She remembered exactly what it felt like to be new — the noise, the disorientation, the fear that her gift was more burden than blessing. She remembered Professor Xavier sitting beside her, not across from her, on a quiet Tuesday morning in Annandale. She tried to offer new students the same thing: presence before instruction, steadiness before explanation.

One student in particular stayed in her mind. A twelve-year-old from Chicago named Maya, whose empathic abilities were so raw that she cried when the cafeteria was crowded — not from sadness, but because she was absorbing the combined emotions of thirty people simultaneously without any filter. Jean recognized that immediately. It was the closest thing to her own beginning she had ever seen.

Jean spent four weeks working with Maya outside the regular training schedule. They sat in Professor Xavier's garden, where the natural world's quieter emotional weather gave Maya's mind space to rest, and Jean guided her through the same exercises Professor Xavier had once used with her: find one thing, hold it softly, step back. Maya kept a journal, just as Jean had. Jean made a point of asking about her entries, about what had been hard and what had started to feel easier, because she knew from experience that being asked about progress made the progress feel real.

She also told Maya something Professor Xavier had not told her until much later: that the goal was never to stop feeling. A telepath who switches herself off from the world is not safer — she is lonely. The goal was fluency, the ability to move through the world with awareness without being dismantled by it. Empathy was a strength, not a weakness. The noise was information, and information could be used.

By the fifth week, Maya came to breakfast without crying. By the eighth week, she was working independently through the basic exercises. Jean watched her from across the cafeteria one morning and felt something she could only describe as the specific satisfaction of a circle completing: the girl she had been once, frightened and overwhelmed, was now the steady presence. The thing that had been done for her was now hers to pass forward.

Professor Xavier had built his school on exactly that idea: that the people who most understood what extraordinary gifts felt like from the inside were the ones best equipped to help others carry them. Jean Grey had been that student once. She was now, quietly and with great care, becoming the teacher.
