Once upon a time, in a big warm school called the Xavier Institute in a place called Westchester, New York, a girl named Jean Grey was trying to find her footing in a new life.

She had her training. She had her classes. She had Hank McCoy, who always made her laugh with a well-timed observation, and she had Professor Xavier's patient guidance, steady as a lighthouse.

And she had Scott Summers.

Scott had been at the Xavier Institute longer than Jean, and he had the quiet, settled air of someone who knew where everything was and had already worked out most of the hard questions for himself. He wore his ruby-quartz glasses every moment of every day — the special lenses that contained the powerful optic beams his eyes could produce — and he spoke carefully, choosing each word, as if he understood that words were tools and deserved to be used precisely.

Jean never thought of Scott's glasses as strange. She thought of them the way you think of a musician's instrument: not separate from the person but part of them, an extension of something important.

From the beginning, Scott was easy to be around.

That sounds simple. But for a telepath, it is not simple at all. Many people became self-conscious around Jean once they understood what she could do. They began watching their own thoughts, the way you suddenly become aware of your hands when someone mentions you have hands. They were friendly, but they were also guarded in a particular way that Jean could always feel, even when no one said a word.

Scott was different.

Scott just went on being himself. Completely, naturally, without adjustment. He did not watch his thoughts when Jean was near. He did not become careful in that self-conscious way. He was simply — honest. More honest, perhaps, than with anyone else, as if the fact of her gift gave him permission to stop translating.

They trained together in the Danger Room, and the teamwork they built there was something remarkable to watch. Jean would sense movement a half second before it happened — something telepaths can do, picking up the intention in a mind before the body acts on it. She would send Scott a warning, sometimes just a look or a slight tip of her head. He would act on it instantly, before anyone else had processed that anything was happening at all.

They developed a shorthand that was so complete and so quiet that Hank McCoy once observed them training for ten minutes and then said, very seriously: "I cannot tell whether you two are communicating telepathically or whether you simply know each other better than two people should know each other after this short a time."

Jean had laughed. Scott had almost smiled, which from Scott was the same thing.

"A little of both," Jean said.

Through missions and training and the long evenings of an unusual school where everyone had extraordinary gifts and was still, somehow, very much an ordinary teenager — through all of it, Jean and Scott grew to know each other the way only true friends do. Not by telling each other every single thing. But by being present. By showing up. By trusting without requiring proof.

Jean could have known at any time, of course. She was a telepath. She could have reached toward Scott's thoughts the way you reach for a book on a shelf and simply — checked. Seen how the story was going.

She never did.

Some things belong to the person feeling them. Some stories deserve to be told aloud, in the teller's own words, when the teller is ready. A telepath who uses her gift to skip ahead in a story that is not hers is not being brave — she is being impatient. And Jean Grey was many things, but she had learned, through a great deal of practice, to be patient.

So she waited.

Scott told her one evening in early autumn, in the garden of the Xavier Institute where the maple trees were just beginning to change color and the Hudson Valley sky was turning the particular warm amber-rose it turns for only a few weeks every year. He stood beside her and he said the true thing, carefully, in his own words.

Jean listened to every syllable. Out loud. The way he had chosen to say it.

Then she told him exactly how she felt.

It was the most honest conversation either of them had ever had with another person. It was also, for two people who were used to fighting enormous battles together, somehow the bravest thing either of them had ever done.

Some of the most important stories happen very quietly, in gardens, at the end of ordinary days.

Sleep softly. The best conversations always find their right moment.
