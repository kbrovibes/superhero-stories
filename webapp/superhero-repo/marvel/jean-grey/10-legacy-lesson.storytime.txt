Once upon a time, Jean Grey came home to school.

Not as a student this time. As something new: a guide, a steady presence, the kind of person who knows what the beginning feels like because she has lived it herself.

The Xavier Institute welcomed new students every autumn, and every autumn Jean asked Professor Xavier if she could help with the early weeks of training. He always said yes.

She remembered everything about being new — the cafeteria noise arriving in waves, the feeling of other people's emotions sliding into her own like water under a door, the long evenings pressing her pillow over her ears and wondering whether she would ever feel quiet again. She remembered Professor Xavier sitting beside her in Annandale, and what it had meant that he sat beside and not across from. She tried to offer every new student the same thing: presence before instruction, steadiness before explanation. Just: I am here, you are not alone, this is manageable.

This year, one student needed that more than the others.

Her name was Maya, from Chicago, with a wide serious face and careful eyes and empathic abilities so raw that sitting in the breakfast room made tears spill down her face — not from sadness, but from absorbing thirty people's feelings simultaneously with no filter and no way to sort out which were hers.

Jean saw her crying at breakfast one morning and recognized the expression from the inside.

She sat down beside Maya — beside, not across — and said quietly: "Would you be willing to train with me, outside the regular schedule? I think I might know how to help."

Maya said yes.

Jean took Maya to Professor Xavier's garden in the early mornings, before the rest of the school was fully awake. The garden was good for telepaths. The natural world generated simple, ancient feelings — the contented sleepiness of birds settling in hedges, the quiet aliveness of soil and grass — and its emotional weather was a calm harbor compared to the open sea of a crowded dining hall.

They sat in the garden and Jean taught Maya what she knew.

Find one thing. The goldfinch on the fence post. The sound of the river, far away. One thread. Hold it without grasping — the way you hold a soap bubble: firmly enough that it doesn't float away, gently enough that it doesn't pop.

Maya kept a journal with a green cover. Jean asked about her entries every day, because she knew that being asked about progress makes the progress feel real. It stops being a solitary effort and becomes something shared.

On the fourteenth day, Jean told Maya something that Professor Xavier had waited much longer to tell her.

"The goal," Jean said, "is not to stop feeling. A telepath who switches herself off from the world is not safer. She is lonely. What we are learning is fluency — to move through the world with awareness without being dismantled by it. Empathy is not a weakness. The noise you hear is information. And information can be used."

Maya sat quietly for a long time. Then she said: "That's the first time this has sounded like something I could actually do."

"It is something you can do," Jean said. "I know, because I did it. And I was very much where you are."

By the fifth week, Maya came to breakfast without crying. By the eighth week, she was working through her exercises independently in the garden before Jean even arrived.

One morning Jean came around the corner of the hedge and saw Maya sitting alone in the early light, her green journal in her lap, a small smile on her face that meant she had done something she was proud of.

Jean stopped and watched from a distance.

She felt what she could only describe as a circle completing — the slow, quiet satisfaction of something done for you, long ago, passing forward to exactly the person who needed it.

Professor Xavier had built his school on that idea: that the people who understood a difficult gift from the inside were the ones best equipped to help others carry it.

Jean Grey had been that student once, overwhelmed in a small bedroom in Annandale.

She was becoming, now, the steady presence who walked in and sat beside.

Sleep soundly. The best things done for you — you can give them away, and giving them away makes them larger, not smaller.
