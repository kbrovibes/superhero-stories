Once upon a time, the Avengers had a problem that none of their powers could solve — and they had to ask for help.

This is actually a remarkable thing. The Avengers were extraordinary heroes: Thor of Asgard, whose hammer Mjolnir crackled with lightning; Tony Stark, whose Iron Man armor could think faster than almost any computer; Steve Rogers — Captain America — whose courage and judgment were his greatest weapon. Natasha Romanoff, whose skills and steadiness made her irreplaceable in any situation. Together they had faced threats from across the galaxy.

But the problem in the Atlantic required something none of them had.

A Kree warship had taken a position above the ocean and deployed a psychic disruptor — a device that sent a carefully targeted frequency into human neural signals, scrambling coordination and thought across a thousand-mile radius. It was not a physical attack. There was nothing to punch, nothing to deflect with a shield, nothing that responded to lightning. Thor could hit the ship as hard as he liked; the disruptor would keep broadcasting.

The Avengers needed a telepath.

Captain America called Professor Xavier. Professor Xavier called Jean Grey.

Jean flew to the S.H.I.E.L.D. Helicarrier that hovered in the gray Atlantic air, and she stepped off the landing pad into a world very different from her own. The Avengers moved through that floating ship with the ease of people who had learned each other's rhythms over many missions. But they turned to meet her warmly.

Tony Stark shook her hand and immediately began talking about his analysis of the Kree ship's signal architecture, pointing to a holographic display that rotated in the air between them, because Tony Stark communicated primarily through holograms and certainty. Jean listened carefully and asked three questions that made him pause mid-sentence and say, with genuine appreciation: "That is a very good question."

Natasha Romanoff shook her hand and, in the time it took them to walk from the landing pad to the briefing room, asked Jean four precise technical questions about the range of her telepathy, what interference it could penetrate, and what its limits were under psychic disruption. She was gathering information, Jean understood. Not because she was unfriendly — because she was thorough, and thorough was how she stayed safe.

And Captain America simply looked at Jean Grey and said: "We're very glad you're here."

Jean felt that in a way she had not expected.

The plan was clear and good. Thor and Iron Man would go to the front of the Kree warship with all the noise and power they had — lightning and repulsor blasts and the sheer magnificent spectacle of an Asgardian god and a billionaire inventor going head-to-head with alien defense drones. Their job was to be entirely impossible to ignore.

Jean's job was to be invisible.

She sat on the Helicarrier's deck, cross-legged, while the battle unfolded in the sky ahead. She closed her eyes against the Atlantic wind. She breathed slowly. She reached.

Tony had given her the disruptor's frequency — a narrow, specific psychic signal, measurable but not human. Jean listened for it the way a musician listens for a single instrument in an orchestra, tuning out everything else until she found that one voice in the noise.

There it was.

She focused everything she had on that single point, inside the hull of the Kree ship, at the core of the emitter. She pushed her own telepathic signal against it — not a blast, just a presence, steady and immovable, like a thumb pressed over the end of a garden hose.

The disruptor struggled. Ten seconds. Twenty. Thirty.

On the fortieth second, something in its mechanism gave way under the pressure.

CLICK. Silence. The frequency collapsed.

Across the entire eastern seaboard of North America, millions of people felt lighter without knowing why — a pressure they had never noticed until it was gone.

Jean opened her eyes. The sky above the Atlantic was wide and cold and the sun was low on the water and everything was very still.

Captain America found her on the observation deck. He sat beside her, his shield resting against the railing, and for a while neither of them said anything. Then he spoke carefully, as he always did, about what mattered: not just the outcome, but the meaning. A mutant and an Avenger, working together. Demonstrating that people who came from very different worlds and carried very different histories could still choose the same direction. Could still, when it mattered, be on the same side.

Jean thought about Professor Xavier. She thought about the very first day she had arrived at his school, a frightened girl from Annandale with her pillow over her ears, trying to quiet the world. She thought about everything the Institute stood for.

She smiled.

"That's actually the oldest idea at our school," she said.

Captain America smiled back. That was enough.

The stars come out over the ocean the same way they come out everywhere. They don't need to be invited. Goodnight.
