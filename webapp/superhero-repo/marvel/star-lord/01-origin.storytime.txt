Let me tell you about the night Peter Quill's whole life changed — and it began not in the stars, but in a small hospital room in Missouri, in the year 1988.

Peter was eight years old. His mother, Meredith Quill, lay in a hospital bed, her hand stretched toward him. She wanted to hold on. She had golden hair and a smile that could warm up a rainy afternoon, and she had made Peter a mixtape — a little cassette of her favorite songs, wrapped in her favorite stories about dancing at summer festivals and falling in love. She called it Awesome Mix Vol. 1. She pressed it into his hands just before she closed her eyes for the last time.

Peter was too scared to take her hand. He ran.

He burst through the hospital doors into the parking lot, gasping in the cold night air, tears streaming down his cheeks — and then a beam of blue light poured down from the sky, lifting him right off the ground. WHOOOOSH. Before he could even scream, he was inside a spaceship, surrounded by alien faces, staring up at a blue-skinned man with a red fin on his head and a sharp, knowing look in his eyes.

That man was Yondu Udonta, captain of a crew of space pirates called the Ravagers. He had been hired to pick Peter up and deliver him to his biological father — a mysterious being named Ego. But Yondu looked at the sobbing eight-year-old boy in his cargo hold, and something in his chest changed. He didn't deliver Peter anywhere. He kept him.

Can you picture that? A pirate crew sailing the stars, and in the middle of it all, one small boy from Earth, clutching a mixtape cassette like a life raft.

Peter grew up among the Ravagers — a wildly colorful, multispecies crew of outlaws, traders, and explorers. He learned to fly every kind of ship in the galaxy. He learned to negotiate with alien merchants and talk his way out of tight spots. He learned to fight, to run, to vanish like smoke when things went wrong. And through all of it, he listened to his mother's mixtape every single day. The songs were the one piece of Earth he had left.

Yondu trained him with a special Yaka Arrow — a dart that followed the sound of a whistle, darting and spinning through the air like a silver fish. ZAP. ZIP. The arrow could change direction mid-flight, threading through obstacles as neatly as a needle through cloth.

When Peter was old enough, Yondu gave him an old M-class starship — a little wedge-shaped vessel with worn red paint and two stubby wings. Peter named her the Milano, after his second-grade crush from an Earth television program. He never told anyone why. He just kept flying her across the galaxy, calling himself Star-Lord, because it sounded magnificent even if nobody agreed.

Then one day, a mission took him to the ruins of a planet called Morag, where a strange object called the Orb was buried in a flooded temple. Peter grabbed it — not knowing what it was. Not knowing that inside the Orb was the Power Stone, one of the six most powerful objects in all the universe. Not knowing that grabbing that Orb would land him in a prison called the Kyln, where four very strange beings would crash into his life and never, ever leave.

There was Gamora, a deadly green-skinned warrior. Rocket, a furry creature who could build anything from spare parts. Groot, a giant tree-person whose entire vocabulary was three words: I. Am. Groot. And Drax the Destroyer, who was exactly as loud as he sounds.

Five strangers. A cramped prison. One very bad plan to escape. And somehow — CRASH, BOOM, WHOOSH — they made it. They flew together through the stars and discovered, slowly and then all at once, that they were something more than strangers.

They were the Guardians of the Galaxy.

Peter Quill had run out of a hospital in Missouri with tears on his face and a mixtape in his hands. But the universe — vast, strange, loud, and full of wonders — had been waiting for him all along.
