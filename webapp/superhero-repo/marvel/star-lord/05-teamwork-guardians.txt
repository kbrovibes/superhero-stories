The Guardians of the Galaxy were not, at first, a team. They were five strangers who happened to be locked in the same prison block. Peter Quill, a half-human pirate. Gamora, an assassin trained by Thanos. Drax the Destroyer, a man with no sense of metaphor. Rocket, a genetically modified raccoon with an attitude almost as big as his gun. And Groot, a tree-like Flora colossus who could only say three words. On paper, they should have ended each other in the first hour. Instead, somewhere between escaping the Kyln and rescuing Xandar, they accidentally became a family.

The strange thing about the Guardians was that none of them were the leader. Peter pretended to be, because he had the ship and the mixtape and the loudest voice. Gamora actually was, in any moment that required strategy. Rocket was, when there were engines or weapons involved. Drax was, when the only choice left was to charge. Groot was, when someone needed to hold the rest of them together with his branches. They argued about every plan and then carried it out perfectly anyway. The first time they all linked hands on the streets of Xandar to share the burden of the Power Stone, none of them realized they were promising to do the same thing for the rest of their lives.

When the Avengers crashed into the Guardians’ corner of the universe — Thor falling onto the windshield of the Benatar with his eyes wide open, Tony Stark and Spider-Man landing on Titan — the two teams clashed before they cooperated. Peter Quill nearly came to blows with Tony Stark over who would lead the Titan mission. He punched Thanos in the face when he heard about Gamora, and accidentally ruined the plan to pull the gauntlet off. The lesson was hard but clear. Being a Guardian was not about being right. It was about staying steady when the people around you were not, and forgiving them later when they came back to apologize.

After the snap was reversed and Gamora returned in a strange, half-remembered form from the past, the Guardians flew on. Peter Quill kept his mother’s mixtape running. Rocket built better and better guns. Drax laughed at jokes that were not jokes. Mantis, who had become one of them after Ego, held a small Groot sapling on her lap and translated his three words for everyone else. The Benatar pulled out of orbit at the end of every adventure with five — sometimes six, sometimes more — misfits leaning out the open cargo bay, watching the next planet rise in front of them. Peter Quill looked at them and finally understood, after all these years, what his mother had once meant when she had called him her Star-Lord. He had been hers first. Now he was theirs.
