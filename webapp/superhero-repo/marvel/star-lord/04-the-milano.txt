Every great hero needs a great ride, and Peter Quill’s was the Milano — a small, wedge-shaped M-class starship with a worn red paint job, two stubby wings, and the words "Knowhere Cantina" still half-stenciled on her belly from a previous owner. Peter had not bought the Milano. He had won her in a game of high-stakes Sakaaran dice at a moon-base bar when he was nineteen years old. He had renamed her after his second-grade crush, a girl named Alyssa Milano he had watched on Earth television before Yondu had taken him away. He had never told the rest of the crew where the name came from. They thought it sounded space-y.

The Milano was not designed for combat. She had two pulse cannons, a small cargo hold, and a single sleeping bunk. But she was, in Peter’s hands, a miracle. He could fly her through asteroid fields without scratching the paint. He could drift her sideways between two cargo cruisers in deep space. He had once landed her on the back of a flying Knowhere whale while listening to "Come and Get Your Love" by Redbone. The Milano was the only thing in the galaxy he had ever truly owned, and he treated her the way a sailor treats a beloved boat — with constant complaining and absolute loyalty.

Rocket modified the ship the way he modified everything else, by hot-wiring extra parts onto the back of her engines whenever Peter wasn’t looking. Drax kept his collection of knives stuck into a panel above his bunk. Gamora kept the medical bay neat. Groot, in his original size, lived in a flower pot in the cockpit. After Groot’s sacrifice on Xandar, the pot rode on the dashboard with Baby Groot inside it, dancing to Peter’s music whenever the ship lifted off. The Milano was small, but somehow she always had room for one more weirdo.

The Milano did not survive Ego. She was torn apart in the battle for the Celestial’s planet, and Peter watched her break in half in the upper atmosphere. He cried about it for two days. But the Guardians did what they had always done — they put together a new ship out of the spare parts of a Sovereign Quadrant and a Ravager hauler, and they painted her red again. They called the new ship the Benatar, and Peter Quill flew her with the same off-beat grace he had flown the Milano. The lesson stuck with him. Homes can be rebuilt. Families can survive losing the buildings around them. What mattered was who was inside the ship, not the ship.
