Let me tell you about a ship the way you would tell someone about a home. Not because of what it could do — though she could do quite a lot — but because of who she held inside.

Her name was the Milano. She was an M-class starship, which is a technical way of saying she was wedge-shaped and stubby-winged and painted a deep, worn red that had seen better days. The words "Knowhere Cantina" were half-stenciled on her belly from a previous life that no one talked about. She had two pulse cannons, one small cargo hold, a single sleeping bunk, and exactly enough room for extraordinary things to happen inside her.

Peter Quill had named her after Alyssa Milano, his second-grade crush from an Earth television program. He had never told the crew. Not once. Not even when Rocket asked six times why the ship was named after a human girl from a planet none of them had been to.

Peter had won her in a game of dice at a moon-base bar when he was nineteen years old. She was not built for combat, technically speaking. She was built for cargo runs and quick escapes and getting from one side of the galaxy to the other without attracting too much attention. But Peter flew her like a miracle. He knew every squeak in her floorboards, every patch in her hull, every moment where the engines needed a certain exact combination of throttle and angle to find their smoothest hum.

He could thread her sideways between cargo cruisers in a crowded spaceport. He could bank through an asteroid field without a scratch on the paintwork — or at least almost. He once landed her on a flying Knowhere whale to the sound of "Come and Get Your Love" by Redbone playing from the tinny ship speakers, and it was, by anyone's measure, extremely cool.

The Milano wasn't just Peter's ship. She became everyone's home.

Rocket lived in her engines and understood every system better than her manufacturer had. He modified the propulsion in ways that were probably technically illegal in fourteen star systems. He also kept a very large gun on a magnetic clip just inside the cargo bay door, and he was not sorry about it.

Drax the Destroyer had stuck three of his favorite knives into a panel above his bunk, and they were still there, rattling gently in turbulence, after every battle and every close call.

Gamora kept the medical bay very neat. She could find the antiseptic with her eyes closed, which turned out to be important fairly often.

Groot lived in a flower pot in the cockpit. Not because he was placed there — because he chose it. He wanted to watch the stars go by, and from the cockpit he could see everything. He sat in his pot and watched nebulae and asteroid belts and the long, impossible dark between galaxies, and said three words about all of it, and meant everything.

After Groot sacrificed himself wrapping his great wooden arms around all of them to survive the crash on Xandar, a twig remained. Just a small green twig with a few tiny leaves. And every time the Milano lifted off after that, Baby Groot was dancing on the dashboard in the same flower pot, to whatever song happened to be playing. This was not negotiable. This was policy.

Then came the battle against Ego, and the Milano was destroyed. Not damaged — destroyed. Torn apart over a planet that turned out to be a living, terrible, selfish being. Peter cried for two days. He did not think it was embarrassing to cry about a ship, and he was correct.

The crew built a new one from the parts of a Sovereign Quadrant ship and a Ravager hauler they had lying around. They called her the Benatar. They painted her red again, because of course they did. Baby Groot's flower pot moved to the Benatar's dashboard without any discussion.

But here is what Peter understood, sitting in the Benatar's pilot seat for the first time: the ship was not the home. The people were the home. The ship was just the place that held them all in one piece while they flew from one impossible thing to the next.

His mother had called him her Star-Lord. He had been hers first — before the galaxy, before the Ravagers, before any of it. And now he was theirs. The ragged, mismatched, magnificent family of people who had been strangers in a prison cell and ended up choosing each other for the rest of their lives.

That was worth more than any ship. Even the Milano.
