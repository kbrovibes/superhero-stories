Peter Quill had spent thirty years thinking he was just a human. A scrappy, half-decent thief from Missouri with a cassette tape, a pair of red boots, and a lot of attitude. The day Ego showed him otherwise, Peter held out his hands and watched a small ball of blue-gold light gather between his palms. The light was warm. The light pulsed in time with his heartbeat. The light was a piece of a Celestial — one of the oldest and most powerful beings in the universe — and that piece lived inside Peter. He had laughed when he first saw it. He had been so excited. He had imagined the things he could do, the heroes he could save, the planets he could light up like fireworks.

Then he had learned what his father wanted to use that light for, and Peter Quill had made a decision that almost no one in his shoes would have made. He chose to set it down. On the hillside of the dying Celestial planet, with Ego whispering into his ear that they could be gods together, that they could remake the galaxy in their own image, Peter looked at the small glowing ball in his hand and thought of Yondu. Thought of Meredith. Thought of Rocket and Groot and Gamora and Drax and Mantis crashing through the gold-light sky behind him in a Ravager ship. And he chose them. He chose to be the human boy his mother had loved.

The light did not go away completely. Peter still carried a spark of it inside him, the way a hearth keeps an ember under the ash. He could feel it warm in his chest when the Guardians were in danger. He could see the world a little more clearly than other humans — could feel the curve of gravity on a planet's surface, could sense when a stranger meant the team harm. He never made the light grow large again. He never pulled it back into his hand and tried to use it as a weapon. The hardest power to wield, he had learned, was the power not to.

Years later, Mantis asked him, quietly, if he ever missed it. Peter thought for a long time before he answered. He was standing in the cockpit of the Benatar, watching the stars wheel slowly past the window. He said no. He said that the Celestial part of him had never really been his — it had been Ego's, an inheritance from a father who had not deserved to be one. The human part of him, the Missouri-boy part of him, the part that loved his mother's songs and a blue-skinned pirate's last words and a tree-shaped friend who could only say three words — that part was his. That was the part he wanted to keep. Mantis nodded gently and rested her hand on his sleeve. She had felt the truth of it before he had finished speaking.
