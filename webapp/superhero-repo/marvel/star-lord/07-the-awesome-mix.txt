Before Peter Quill was Star-Lord, he was a small boy in Missouri with a Sony Walkman and a cassette tape his mother Meredith had made him. The tape was labeled, in her own careful hand, "Awesome Mix Vol. 1." It had songs on it from the years she had been young — "Come and Get Your Love" by Redbone, "Hooked on a Feeling" by Blue Swede, "Cherry Bomb" by The Runaways, "I Want You Back" by The Jackson 5, "Fooled Around and Fell in Love" by Elvin Bishop. She had recorded the songs one at a time from her own radio, calling out the titles to little Peter as the tape spun. The night she died, the cassette was inside a wrapped package on her hospital bedside, waiting for him. He took it with him when the Ravager beam pulled him into the sky.

That tape went everywhere with Peter for the next thirty years. He played it in the cockpit of the Milano while flying through asteroid fields. He played it in his ear during prison breakouts. He played it in the middle of fights, dancing on broken concrete on the planet Morag, twirling small lizard creatures by their tails to the opening guitar of "Come and Get Your Love." Rocket complained that the songs were ancient. Drax tried to learn the words and got them all wrong. Groot bobbed his small wooden head perfectly on the beat. It was, Peter said, the only piece of Earth he had ever managed to keep.

When the original Walkman was broken in a fight on Xandar, Peter Quill almost cried. The Nova Corps gave him a new device — a sleek silver music player called a Zune — at the end of the Ronan trial. Peter listened to it suspiciously at first. It held five hundred songs instead of twelve. "Earth music has gotten weirder," he announced to the team after one afternoon of scrolling. He kept the original Awesome Mix Vol. 1 cassette in a small case, taped to the back of the Zune, for safekeeping.

Years later, after the Snap, after the return of half the universe, after the long quiet trip home from the battlefield, Peter sat alone in the cockpit of the Benatar and unwrapped a second package. It was Awesome Mix Vol. 2. Meredith had made two tapes. She had put the second one inside the first envelope, years ago, marked "For when you're a little older." Peter had been thirty-six years old by then, well past the age she had imagined. He pressed play. He heard his mother's handwriting in the song choices — "Father and Son" by Cat Stevens, "Brandy" by Looking Glass — and he understood, in one perfect quiet moment, that even now she had not finished raising him. Some mothers send love forward in time. Meredith Quill had sent it forward on tape.
