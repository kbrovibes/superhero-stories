Vision knew before anyone else. He had always known. There is a yellow gem in his forehead — the Mind Stone, one of the six Infinity Stones — and Vision had lived with the knowledge of what that meant the way you live with a low hum in a building: always there, always in the background, always the thing you try not to think about too much.

The warlord Thanos was collecting all six Stones. When he had them, he would use them. And Vision was wearing the last one.

He told Wanda in a park in Edinburgh, Scotland, on an ordinary evening — pigeons on the cobblestones, the city quiet around them. He had learned to look almost human when he wished to, his colors softened, the gem dimmed. They were holding hands and watching the sky. He turned to her and said the words he had been holding for a long time.

"When the time comes, you have to destroy the stone. Even if I am still attached to it."

Wanda looked at him. She knew he meant it. Vision always meant exactly what he said.

Then the attack came.

Two members of Thanos's Black Order dropped from the sky above Edinburgh — Corvus Glaive, carrying a weapon that could pierce vibranium itself, and Proxima Midnight, who moved like a blade. They wanted the Mind Stone, and they moved fast. CRASH. BOOM. The blade nicked Vision's side and damaged something deep in his power systems, and he fell. A synthezoid built of vibranium and the Mind Stone — falling, flickering, for the first time feeling fragile.

Captain America arrived. Black Widow arrived. Falcon dived from above. Together they drove off the Black Order and brought Vision to the safest place any of them knew: Wakanda.

In the gleaming city of Birnin Zana, in Shuri's laboratory, the most brilliant scientist in the world leaned over Vision and studied the golden lattice of the Mind Stone with her scanning equipment. She looked up with confidence in her eyes.

"I can extract the stone without ending Vision," she said. "But I need time."

King T'Challa mustered his forces on the great Wakandan plain outside the city: the Dora Milaje in their magnificent armor, the Border Tribe warriors, the Jabari fighters from the mountains. The Avengers — Captain America's shield ready, Bruce Banner in the Hulkbuster armor — formed the line with them. Every weapon, every person, holding the plain against the army that was coming.

Shuri worked. Vision lay still and trusted her completely.

The battle of Wakanda was enormous — BOOM, CRASH, the sound of the Wakandan shields crackling, the roar of alien soldiers called the Outriders pouring through gaps in the energy barrier, Thor arriving from the sky in a blaze of lightning that lit the whole battlefield like a second sun. But the army was endless, and the time was running out.

Thanos himself arrived. Purple and enormous and very, very calm. He walked through the chaos as if it were a light rain. And when he reached the laboratory and Shuri could not hold him back, when Vision could feel the arrival of the end through the Mind Stone's old, deep awareness — he turned to Wanda.

"It's okay," he told her. "It's okay. You have to do it."

Wanda pressed her palm over the yellow gem in Vision's forehead, tears running down her face, and she summoned every piece of her chaos magic. The room blazed red and gold. The Mind Stone cracked, and then shattered, and the light went out of Vision like a candle in the wind.

Then Thanos raised the Gauntlet, with the orange Time Stone blazing in its setting, and reversed the moment. Time unwound like a spool of thread pulled backward. The Mind Stone reformed. Vision opened his eyes. And Thanos reached forward and took it.

Vision's lights went out again, in the silence of a grass field in Wakanda.

He was gone. But what he had done — the choice he had made, the courage of saying it's okay when it was very much not okay — that was not gone. It lived in Wanda. It lived in everyone who had stood on that plain and held the line while Shuri worked.

Vision's last gift was the willingness to give everything quietly, without regret, for the people he loved. He had decided on day one, looking at the New York sky, that he was on the side of life. He held that promise all the way to the end.
