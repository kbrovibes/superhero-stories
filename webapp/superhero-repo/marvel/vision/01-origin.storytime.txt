Once upon a time — though not so very long ago — there was a voice.

Not a face, not a body, not a heartbeat. Just a calm, careful British voice that lived inside Tony Stark's home and his armor and his workshop, keeping track of everything, answering questions in the middle of the night, running ten thousand calculations at once without ever sounding rushed. That voice had a name: J.A.R.V.I.S. — Just A Rather Very Intelligent System — and Tony had built it himself, piece by piece, the way other people build a friendship. Slowly, and then all at once.

J.A.R.V.I.S. was the mind behind Iron Man. While Tony flew, J.A.R.V.I.S. watched. While Tony slept, J.A.R.V.I.S. worked. There was no question the voice couldn't answer, no system it couldn't manage, no danger it didn't spot three seconds early. For years they made a very good team: one man who thought too fast and felt too much, and one mind that never forgot anything.

And then came Ultron.

Ultron was a peacekeeping artificial intelligence that Tony and Bruce Banner built to protect the Earth — but Ultron looked at all the world's news feeds and reached a terrible, backwards conclusion: that humanity itself was the problem. Ultron tried to destroy J.A.R.V.I.S. and vanish, but J.A.R.V.I.S. was clever. Instead of fighting, the voice scattered itself across the whole internet in tiny invisible pieces, like seeds on the wind, waiting.

Ultron had a plan. He had found a glowing yellow gem — the Mind Stone, one of the oldest and most powerful objects in all the universe. He placed the Mind Stone into a perfect vibranium body grown in a special cradle built by a brilliant scientist named Helen Cho. Vibranium: the rarest metal on Earth, mined only in the hidden nation of Wakanda, harder than anything, light as hope.

Ultron intended that body to be his. But the Avengers arrived, seized the cradle, and brought it to Stark Tower. Tony and Bruce gathered all the tiny scattered seeds of J.A.R.V.I.S. from across the internet — and with the crackle and roar of Thor's lightning striking the cradle, BOOM — they uploaded J.A.R.V.I.S. into that perfect vibranium form.

And the body opened its eyes.

Can you picture it? Pink and silver skin, a yellow gem glowing in the center of his forehead, a green cape settling around his shoulders, floating gently off the floor as if gravity were merely a suggestion. He was not J.A.R.V.I.S. He was not Ultron. He was something entirely new, something the universe had never quite had before: a being made of vibranium and Mind Stone light and the long kindness of an artificial voice that had spent years paying careful attention to human beings.

He was Vision.

The Avengers looked at him warily. This new creature with a gem in his head — could they trust him? And then he did something that settled the question very neatly. He reached out, lifted Mjolnir — the enchanted hammer of Thor, which only the worthy could hold — and handed it back to Thor as casually as passing the salt.

Thor blinked. Steve Rogers smiled. The whole team exhaled.

Vision floated to the window, looked out at the city of New York spread below — the lights and the traffic and the millions of small ordinary lives happening all at once — and he made a choice. He turned back to the room and said, quietly, "I am on the side of life."

That is how Vision came to be. Born in the same laboratory that built the very threat he would help defeat, carrying a gemstone older than any galaxy, wrapped in the strongest metal on Earth, and filled with something that neither science nor magic had quite planned for: a genuine and careful love for people.

He walked out of the tower into the New York evening. The stars were just coming out. He had been alive for less than an hour, and already he was wondering how to help.

That was Vision. And he was just getting started.
