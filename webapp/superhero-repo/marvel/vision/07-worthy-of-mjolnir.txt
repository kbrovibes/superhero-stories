Mjolnir is the most particular hammer in the universe. The dwarves of Nidavellir forged it inside the heart of a dying star and asked the All-Father Odin to enchant it with a single, very strict rule: only the worthy may lift it. Kings have tried and failed. Giants have tried and failed. Tony Stark, at a party in his living room, propped his feet up and tried with both hands and a War Machine assist and could not budge it half an inch. Mjolnir does not care how strong you are. It cares how good you are.

So when the brand-new Vision floated up out of Helen Cho's cradle in Stark Tower, the Avengers were nervous. He was pink and silver and calm. He spoke in J.A.R.V.I.S.'s gentle British voice. He carried the glowing yellow Mind Stone in his forehead. But he had been alive for less than a minute. Could they really trust him? Steve Rogers crossed his arms. Clint Barton kept one hand near his quiver. Tony Stark watched everyone watching Vision, and nobody knew what to say.

And then Vision did the most casual thing anyone had ever done in a lab full of nervous Avengers. He walked past Thor, picked up Mjolnir off the workbench where Thor had set it down, weighed it in his palm as if it were a teacup, and politely handed it back. "Thor," he said in his soft, clear voice, "I think you forgot this." Then he turned and floated over to the window to look at the lights of New York.

The room went completely silent. Then Thor burst out laughing — a huge, bright, godlike laugh that rattled the glassware on the shelves. Steve Rogers smiled the small careful smile he wore when he was secretly very impressed. Tony Stark blinked twice and said something quiet to Bruce Banner that nobody quite caught. Clint Barton lowered his bow. In that single moment, without any votes or paperwork or speeches, the Avengers decided that this new being could be trusted. Mjolnir, after all, was never wrong. If the hammer said Vision was worthy, then Vision was worthy. He stayed at the window for a long time, watching the city, and the rest of the team went back to work around him — a little less afraid, and a lot more curious about the gentle pink stranger who had been alive for half an hour and already passed the hardest test in the universe.
