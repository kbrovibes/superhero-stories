Vision was the newest Avenger and, in many ways, the wisest. He had been alive for only a few years. But underneath his thoughts ran the JARVIS code that had watched Tony Stark for nearly two decades — all the mistakes, all the learning, all the late nights. Vision held all of that quietly inside him, and his teammates learned, slowly, to turn to him with the questions they were afraid to ask out loud.

When Tony Stark and Steve Rogers were shouting at each other about the Sokovia Accords — a great argument about whether Avengers needed government supervision — Vision floated quietly to the middle of the room and asked a single question. He pointed out that since Tony had become Iron Man, both the number of powerful people in the world and the number of disasters had grown at the same rate. Was there a connection? The room went silent. No one could answer. Vision had not taken a side. He had simply given everyone something true to think about.

And do you know what he told Wanda when she was afraid she had become a monster? "Monsters," he said gently, "do not worry about being monsters."

He told Tony that peace is built slowly over time — not given away in a single afternoon. He told Steve that he did not know if a person could follow their heart and break the law at the same time — but he admired that Steve believed they could.

Vision could fly. He could carry the most powerful stone in the universe. He could phase through any wall on Earth. And still, what he chose to do most often was simply listen.

From that day on, the Avengers understood: wisdom is not about having all the answers. It is about making room for everyone's questions.
