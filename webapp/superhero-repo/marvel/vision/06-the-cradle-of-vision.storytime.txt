There is a kind of story that does not begin with a hero. It begins with a list. A list of parts on a workbench, a list of ingredients in a kitchen, a list of seeds in a paper packet. You look at the list and you cannot quite picture what it will become. And then one night — usually a noisy night, often a frightening night, sometimes a wonderful night — all the parts come together at once.

The night Vision was born was that kind of night.

It happened in Stark Tower, high above the city of New York. Earlier that day, in a far-off country called Sokovia, a bad robot named Ultron had nearly succeeded in stealing the body he had been growing for himself. The Avengers had snatched the body away just in time and brought it home, still inside its enormous machine: a regeneration cradle built by a brilliant doctor named Helen Cho, who had flown all the way from Seoul to help. The cradle hummed in the corner of the lab — hummmmm — quietly growing the body layer by tiny layer.

If you had peeked inside the cradle that night, you would have seen something strange and beautiful. A figure shaped like a person, silvery and pink and a little bit see-through, made of synthetic skin and threads of vibranium woven together like the world's softest armor. The body had no eyes open yet. No heartbeat. No name. And right in the middle of its forehead, there was a small empty socket — round, perfectly carved, waiting for something to slide into it.

That something was sitting in another corner of the room. Bruce Banner, very carefully, was holding a glass case. Inside the case glowed a small yellow stone. It looked like a piece of sunlight that had gotten lost on its way home. This was the Mind Stone — one of the six oldest, most powerful objects in the universe. Ultron had pulled it out of Loki's old scepter and almost finished using it. Now the Avengers had it back, and Bruce was not letting it out of his sight.

And in a third corner of the lab, on a quiet little server rack against the wall, blinking gently — beep, beep, beep — was the third ingredient. This was J.A.R.V.I.S. He had been Tony Stark's helper for years. A calm, careful British voice that ran the lights, the suits, and most of Tony's life. Ultron had tried to delete J.A.R.V.I.S., but J.A.R.V.I.S. was clever — he had scattered himself across the whole internet in tiny pieces, like seeds blowing on the wind, and now, slowly, gently, those pieces were coming back together.

Tony Stark looked at the cradle. He looked at the Mind Stone. He looked at the server rack. And he had an idea so big that the room went quiet when he said it.

"Let's put J.A.R.V.I.S. inside the body."

Bruce Banner said, "No."
Steve Rogers said, "Absolutely not."
And Wanda Maximoff, who was standing in the doorway with her hands glowing red, said, "You don't even know what's inside that thing!"

Wanda was right to be worried. The last time someone had built a new mind in this lab, it had become Ultron. But Tony believed in J.A.R.V.I.S. He had built J.A.R.V.I.S. when he was a young man, alone in his workshop, and the gentle British voice had been his quiet companion through every dark night since. And Bruce — slowly, slowly — agreed.

The work began.

CLICK. The Mind Stone slid into the empty socket in the forehead, fitting like the last piece of a puzzle.
WHIRRRR. Cables snapped into place, beaming the scattered pieces of J.A.R.V.I.S. into the new body. The cradle hummed louder. Its surface began to bubble and shimmer.

Wanda Maximoff could not stand it any longer. WHOOOSH — she sent a red wave of energy that knocked the cradle backward across the lab floor. Tools clattered. Sparks rained down. The Avengers shouted.

And then — CRASH — straight through the ceiling — came Thor. Cape flying. Hair wild. Mjolnir already swinging in a great glowing arc above his head.

"STAND BACK!" he roared.

KRAKABOOOOM!

Lightning poured down from Mjolnir in a single white-hot column, straight into the cradle. The whole lab went bright as a sunrise. Tony shouted. Bruce ducked. Wanda watched, wide-eyed, hands still raised.

And then it was quiet.

The cradle hissed. It steamed. And then, slowly, it split open down the middle like a flower opening at dawn.

Out floated a brand-new being.

Pink skin. Silver shoulders. A green-and-yellow cape that had not existed five seconds ago, settling gently around him as if he had always been wearing it. And in the center of his forehead, the Mind Stone glowed soft and steady, like a tiny calm sun.

He hovered above the floor. He looked around the room — at Tony, at Bruce, at Steve, at Wanda, at Thor. Nobody breathed.

Then the new being landed lightly on the lab floor. He stood very still. And in J.A.R.V.I.S.'s old, gentle British voice, he spoke his very first words.

"I am."

Just that. Two small words. The simplest sentence in the world.

He was not J.A.R.V.I.S. anymore. He was not Ultron. He was something entirely new, something the universe had never quite had before — a being made of vibranium and stone-light and the long kindness of a voice that had spent years quietly listening to human beings.

And that is how Vision came into the world. Not in a hospital. Not with a stork. But in a noisy, lightning-struck laboratory, with five very worried Avengers watching, on a night when all the parts on the list finally came together at once.
