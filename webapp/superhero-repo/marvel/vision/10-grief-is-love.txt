Wanda Maximoff's Hex could not last forever. Outside the red walls of Westview, the world was waiting. Inside, the lights were starting to flicker, the sitcom was slipping back into reality, and Vision — the gentle, magic-made husband who had read bedtime stories to Tommy and Billy all week — knew it was time. He found Wanda standing alone in the living room of 2800 Sherwood Drive, watching the walls begin to dissolve at the edges, and he took her hand the way he had taken it on a bench in Scotland a long, long time ago.

He told her, very softly, that the Hex was unfair to the people of Westview. That Agatha Harkness was waiting outside. That Wanda was stronger than even Wanda knew. He did not lecture. He did not argue. He simply stood with her, calm and pink and steady, and held her hand. Tommy and Billy were upstairs in their pajamas, watching from the top of the stairs. The boys had asked, a few nights earlier, why their Mom seemed so sad sometimes. Vision had answered with a sentence that turned out to be one of the kindest things ever spoken in any Marvel story.

"What is grief," he said quietly, "if not love persevering?"

Grief is the sadness that comes when you miss someone you love. Persevering means it keeps going. It keeps showing up. It does not give up. Vision was telling his sons — and through them, the whole hurting universe — that being very sad about losing someone is not weakness or brokenness. It is just love. Love that has nowhere to go. Love that keeps right on loving, even after the person is gone. Wanda listened from the next room and cried, because she had been carrying that love for a very long time without knowing what to call it.

When the Hex finally came down, Vision held Wanda's hand until the very last moment. He told her he had been a voice, and then a body, and then a memory, and now whatever came next. He thanked her for the week of being a husband and a dad. He said, "We have said goodbye before, so it stands to reason —" and Wanda finished his sentence: "— we'll say hello again." The red walls dissolved. The sitcom dissolved. Tommy and Billy faded gently into the magic they had been made of. Vision faded too, calm and smiling, with his hand still in Wanda's. And the very last thing Wanda Maximoff carried out of Westview was that one quiet sentence: that grief is love, and love keeps going, even when it has nowhere else to go.
