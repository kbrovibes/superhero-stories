Peter Parker had a very unusual job for a superhero. Every week, he would walk into the offices of the Daily Bugle — the loudest, most excitable newspaper in all of New York City — and sell photographs to the gruff, flat-topped editor J. Jonah Jameson. The photographs were of Spider-Man in action. And Spider-Man, of course, was Peter himself.

Jameson never knew that, of course. He just wanted pictures so he could print headlines like "Spider-Man Attacks Local Bank!" and tell the whole city that their wall-crawling protector was a menace. Peter would stand there listening, quietly turning red, and then hand over the very evidence that Jameson would twist into tomorrow's headline. He needed the money to help Aunt May with the bills. It was one of the strangest, funniest, and most frustrating situations you can imagine.

But the Daily Bugle wasn't all bad. Inside its loud and busy newsroom, Peter found people he truly cared about. City editor Robbie Robertson was warm and wise — a real mentor. Reporter Betty Brant became a friend. These were good people doing their best, even if their boss was shouting the wrong thing at the top of his lungs.

Jameson believed that real heroes were the ones without masks — the police officers, the firefighters, the soldiers who showed their faces every day. That idea, as wrong as it sometimes seemed, actually made Peter think. He realized he didn't need anyone's applause. He helped people because it was right. He protected the Bugle staff even when they printed terrible things about him, because that was the kind of hero he chose to be.

And that is how Peter Parker learned that being good isn't about being thanked. It's about doing what you know is right, even when no one is watching.
