Once there was a man named Quentin Beck who had always wanted to be a star. He was a special-effects wizard in Hollywood — he could make anything look real with lights, smoke, and clever tricks. But he never got the fame he wanted. So he made a decision: if the world wouldn't notice him as a helper, they would notice him as a villain.

He put on a shimmering green suit and a great domed helmet that reflected everything like a mirror, and he called himself Mysterio. His weapon wasn't fists or fire — it was illusions. He could make you see things that weren't there. He could make real things disappear. And his first plan was to make Spider-Man look like the bad guy.

He framed Peter Parker with clever holograms, and the whole city turned on its favorite hero. Then he lured Spider-Man into an abandoned theater and filled it with towering monsters and twisting nightmares — none of it real, but all of it terrifying. He used hallucinogenic gases and hidden drones to confuse Peter's spider-sense until Peter couldn't trust his own eyes.

And do you know what Peter did? He closed his eyes. Right in the middle of the chaos, he shut them tight and listened only to the gentle tingling of his spider-sense — that deep, quiet voice inside him that never lies. It guided him straight through the illusions and right to the man hiding behind the curtain.

One firm punch shattered Mysterio's helmet, and all the grand magic crumbled away. Beck was just a lonely man with clever machines and a wounded heart.

From that day on, Peter Parker knew that no trick, no matter how dazzling, could fool a hero who trusts what is true inside himself.
