While the radioactive spider bite gave Peter Parker his powers, it was his own scientific genius that made him a truly effective hero. His most iconic tools were his web-shooters, which he wore hidden under the cuffs of his costume. The "web fluid" was a complex chemical polymer of Peter’s own invention, stored in small, high-pressure nickel-plated brass cartridges. On contact with air, the fluid instantly solidified into a silk-like thread with a tensile strength of 120 pounds per square millimeter—strong enough to stop a speeding train or web up a mechanical Goblin Glider.

Peter also wore a specialized "Utility Belt" tucked under his suit's waistline. This belt held spare web-fluid cartridges and his "Spider-Signal"—a high-intensity searchlight built into his belt buckle that projected his red mask symbol onto walls or through the city's thick fog. He used the signal to alert villains that he was arriving or to light up dark alleyways during rescue missions. "It's all about the preparation," Peter would tell himself while refilling his cartridges in the chemistry lab at Midtown High, making sure every gear and spring was perfectly oiled.

Another piece of factual "Spidey-Lore" is the design of his mask. The white eyepieces are actually specialized lenses that act like one-way mirrors; Peter can see out clearly, but no one can see in. These lenses also help him focus his vision, much like the aperture of a camera—a skill he learned from his job as a photographer. The lenses often "squint" or widen depending on Peter's expressions, helping him communicate his feelings even when his face is completely covered. It was this combination of biology and high-tech engineering that allowed a teenager from Queens to stand among the world's greatest legends.

Peter kept a secret laboratory in a corner of his bedroom, filled with microscopes, beakers, and half-finished gadgets. He knew that his webs would naturally dissolve after exactly one hour, which was a deliberate design choice to avoid cluttering the city. He constantly refined his formula, adding "acid-webbing" for melting locks or "electric-webbing" for shorting out robots. Even without his super-strength, Peter's mind was his greatest weapon. He proved that being a hero wasn't just about how hard you could punch, but how smartly you could solve a problem.
