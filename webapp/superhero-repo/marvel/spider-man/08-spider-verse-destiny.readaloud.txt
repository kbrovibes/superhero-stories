What if I told you that somewhere out there — in a different world, spinning alongside ours — there's another Spider-Man? And then another? And another after that?

Are you ready for this? There isn't just one Spider-Hero in all of Marvel's universe. There are HUNDREDS. Every time something small went differently — a different spider, a different bite, a different choice — a whole new world branched off. And in many of those worlds, someone amazing put on a spider-symbol and made the same promise: to use their power responsibly.

In 2014, the comics decided to bring them all together. It was called the Spider-Verse, and it was extraordinary.

Peter Parker, the original, found himself suddenly connected to Spider-Heroes from every corner of the multiverse. Miles Morales came from the Ultimate Universe — a young, talented kid who had been bitten by his own radioactive spider and had his own set of powers, including a stunning electric venom-strike that Peter didn't have! Miles was quick, creative, and bursting with potential.

Then there was Gwen Stacy — on her world, Earth-65, it was Gwen who had been bitten, Gwen who became the spider-hero. Seeing her alive and brave and extraordinary was almost too much for Peter to look at. Bittersweet is what the grown-ups call that feeling.

And there was a noir Spider-Man from the gloomy 1930s, who wore a trench coat and spoke in short, clipped sentences and viewed the whole multiverse with deeply suspicious eyes.

But danger had found all of them. The Inheritors were interdimensional hunters — creatures who leaped between worlds tracking down Spider-Totems, feeding on what made them special. One spider-hero alone couldn't stop them. Even Peter alone couldn't stop them.

But here's what Peter realized, standing in the middle of this extraordinary spider-army: every single one of them — from Miles to Gwen to the noir Spider-Man to Silk to Spider-Ham — had faced a moment of devastating loss. And every single one had made the same choice in the aftermath. To help. To protect. To use the power they didn't ask for in service of someone else.

THAT was the thread connecting them all. Not the web-shooters, not the spider-sense. The promise.

Peter, as the oldest and most experienced, led the charge to the Inheritors' home world. His spider-army stormed the fortress, a crackling, swinging, THWIPPING wave of heroes from every dimension, and they won.

Afterward, the worlds remained connected. A permanent web, invisible and strong, linking every Spider-Hero who had ever made that choice.

Peter Parker went home to Queens a little different than he'd left. He had always known he was Spider-Man. Now he understood something bigger: Spider-Man was an idea that the universe kept inventing over and over again — because the world always needed it.

He was never truly alone. None of them were.
