Peter Parker always thought his story was his own — one boy, one radioactive bite, one city to protect. But then he discovered something that changed everything: the Spider-Verse.

It turned out that in the great, spinning web of all possible worlds, there were many Spider-Heroes. Some wore different costumes. Some were from different times. There was Miles Morales, a young man from the Ultimate Universe who had found his own radioactive bite and his own great responsibility. There was Gwen Stacy, whose world had chosen her to be its Spider-Woman. There was even a Spider-Man from the shadowy 1930s, who spoke in clipped sentences and wore a trench coat. Peter stared at all of them and felt something he had rarely felt before: he was not alone.

But danger had found them all. The Inheritors were powerful, interdimensional hunters who fed on Spider-Totems — beings like Peter and his new friends. They traveled between worlds, relentless and hungry, and the only answer was for the spiders to stand together.

And do you know what Peter realized? Every single one of them — every Spider-Hero from every world — had faced a moment of terrible loss. And every one of them had made the same choice: to use their power to protect others. That shared promise was the web that bound them together stronger than any spider's silk.

Peter, the first and oldest of them all, led the charge to the Inheritors' home. His experience. His heart. His stubborn, refusing-to-quit spirit. Together, the army of spiders won.

From that day on, Peter Parker knew that his story was not just one thread. It was part of a tapestry that stretched across every world — and no spider ever truly swings alone.
