Far away from Queens, Peter Parker once traveled to a cosmic battlefield called the Secret Wars. There, among the strangeness of the stars, he found something that looked like a wonderful gift — a pool of shimmering black liquid that shaped itself into a brand-new costume. It clung to him like a second skin, gave him all the webbing he could ever need, and made him feel very powerful indeed.

But when he came home, he noticed something unsettling. He was sleeping less. His temper was shorter. He was rougher in his fights. The suit, it turned out, was alive — an alien creature called a symbiote, quietly whispering to his feelings and nudging him toward darkness.

Peter found out that the creature was frightened of loud sounds. So he climbed to the top of a great church tower, and when the bells rang out with a tremendous CLANG, the symbiote peeled away in pain. Peter was free. But the creature did not vanish. It found a new home in a man named Eddie Brock — a journalist whose career had been ruined, and who blamed Spider-Man for all of it. Together, they became Venom.

And do you know what made Venom truly frightening? Because the symbiote had once lived inside Peter, it knew everything. It knew his face. It knew his voice. It knew how to sneak past his spider-sense like a shadow slipping under a door.

Peter had to think carefully and scientifically to find a way to separate Eddie from the creature. Sound and fire — those were the keys. He used them both, bravely and cleverly.

From that day on, Peter knew: even the gifts we bring home from far away must be examined with care, because some things that feel powerful can slowly pull us away from who we truly are.
