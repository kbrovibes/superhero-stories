Some stories begin in alleys. Some stories begin in laboratories. This story begins among the stars.

Spider-Man was carried far away from Earth one strange day, along with many other heroes, to a place called Battleworld. They had been gathered there by a powerful being called the Beyonder, who wanted to watch heroes and villains fight in his great cosmic playground. It was called the Secret Wars.

While he was out there, Peter Parker found something that would change his life.

His old red-and-blue costume had been damaged in a battle. He needed a new one. On a strange alien world, he stepped into what looked like a clothing machine — and out poured a thick, black, shimmery substance that flowed up over his body and shaped itself into a brand new suit.

The new costume was incredible. It was sleek and pure black, with a single bold white spider on the chest. It responded to Peter's thoughts — if he wanted a hood, it grew a hood. If he wanted gloves, it became gloves. And best of all, it had its own endless supply of webbing. Peter did not even need his web-shooters anymore. The suit just made web straight from its own surface.

Peter loved it.

He brought the suit back to Earth. He wore it everywhere. He swung between the buildings of New York in his new black suit, feeling stronger and faster than ever before.

But something started to feel off.

Peter began waking up tired. Sometimes more tired than when he had gone to bed. He would find himself standing in places he did not remember walking to. He had vague hazy memories of swinging through the city in the middle of the night — but he had not gone out. Had he?

He began to feel ANGRIER than he used to. Quick to snap at Aunt May. Short with his friends. Harsh with the villains he caught — harsher than Peter Parker had ever been.

He went to the brilliant scientist Reed Richards of the Fantastic Four, and Reed examined the suit carefully under his microscopes. The truth was strange and alarming.

The suit was ALIVE.

It was not just clothing. It was a creature — an alien symbiote that had bonded to Peter and was slowly, gently, starting to change him. It needed him. And it did not want to let him go.

Peter knew, deep in his honest Spider-Man heart, that this was not who he wanted to be.

He climbed to the top of an old church one night — the Our Lady of Saints church, with its high stone bell tower — and when the bells rang out in their deep, booming chimes, the symbiote SHRIEKED. It hated the loud vibrating sound. It loosened its grip. Peter pulled it off himself in great inky strands and stuffed it into a containment tube.

He thought he was free of it.

He was wrong.

The symbiote did not die. It crept out of its tube. It went looking for a new person to bond with. And it found one. A man named Eddie Brock.

Eddie Brock had been a journalist who had ruined his own career by publishing a wrong story about Spider-Man. He blamed Peter for everything that had gone wrong in his life. When he was at his lowest, sobbing on the floor of that same Our Lady of Saints church, the symbiote slithered down from the rafters and offered him exactly what he wanted.

Power. Strength. A way to hurt Spider-Man.

Eddie said yes.

The symbiote merged with him, and the two of them became one terrifying creature. Black goo. Enormous white eyes. Huge sharp teeth that grinned too wide. A long pink tongue. A voice that sometimes said "we" instead of "I."

His name was Venom.

Venom was a dark reflection of Spider-Man in every way. He had all of Peter's powers — the strength, the wall-crawling, the leaping. He could shoot webs of black goo from his own body. He was bigger. He was scarier.

And because the symbiote had once been bonded to Peter, it KNEW his secrets. It knew his identity. It knew his spider-sense, the small tingle at the back of his head that always warned him of danger. Venom could turn that off. Venom could sneak up on Peter in a way no other villain could.

Their early fights were terrifying. Venom was a shadow on rooftops. A great toothy face appearing from a dark alley. He liked to scare Aunt May. He liked to frighten Peter's friends. He believed, in his own twisted way, that HE was the real protector — and that Peter Parker was the villain.

Peter studied the symbiote. He learned its weaknesses. He learned it hated loud high-pitched sounds. He learned it hated fire. He used his scientific mind to invent ways to peel the symbiote off Eddie, at least temporarily.

But the bond was strong. Always, Venom came back.

Over time, though, something interesting happened. Eddie Brock got tired of being a villain. He had a small streak of goodness deep in him, and the symbiote, in its own strange way, did too. Venom started to call himself a LETHAL PROTECTOR. He decided to protect innocent people from real bad guys — in his own scary, loud, toothy way.

Peter and Venom were never friends, exactly. But over time they reached a wary kind of truce. They sometimes worked together against bigger threats. Eddie even, sometimes, surprised Peter with quiet kindnesses.

The black suit had taught Peter something important.

Power, if you are not careful, can change WHO YOU ARE. It can amplify the parts of you that are angry. It can quiet the parts of you that are kind. The most dangerous villain is not always the one with the biggest fists. Sometimes it is the small whispered change inside your own heart.

Peter Parker learned to be vigilant. Not just against criminals. Against the slow drift in himself.

And on quiet nights, when the bells of an old church ring out across Manhattan, he thinks — just for a moment — about a black suit on an alien world, and about how close he came to becoming something he would not have wanted to be.

Then he swings on. In red and blue. Where he belongs.
