Of all the troubles that ever found Peter Parker, the most painful came wrapped in a friendship he trusted. Norman Osborn was the father of Harry, Peter's best friend. Norman was smart and powerful — and hungry for more of both. He created a potion he called the Goblin Formula, hoping it would make him stronger and sharper than anyone alive.

It did. But it also cracked something deep inside him. Norman became the Green Goblin — a cackling figure in a purple costume, flying over the city on a glider shaped like a terrible beast, hurling pumpkin bombs at anyone who stood in his way. And worst of all, he somehow found out who Spider-Man really was. He knew Peter Parker's face. He knew his heart. And he decided to use that knowledge in the cruelest possible way.

He took Gwen Stacy — the kind, brave girl who was the most important person in Peter's world — and flew her to the top of the George Washington Bridge. Peter swung to save her with everything he had. He reached out his web as she fell. He caught her. But the world is sometimes very unfair, and the sudden stop was too sharp and too fast. Gwen was gone. And Peter's grief was the kind that never fully leaves.

Their battles after that were long and hard. The Goblin wanted Peter to become angry and cruel — to be just as dark as he was. But Peter never gave in. He kept fighting with kindness in his heart, even when that heart was breaking.

From that day on, Peter understood that a hero's most important battle is the one to remain good — no matter how great the pain, and no matter how loudly the darkness calls.
