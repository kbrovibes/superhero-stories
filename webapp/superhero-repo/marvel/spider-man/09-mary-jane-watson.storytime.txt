Of all the people Peter Parker has ever known, there is one whose name is woven into his very heart.

Mary Jane Watson.

Their story began long before they ever spoke. Aunt May, in her gentle way, had been trying for ages to set Peter up with "the nice Watson girl from down the street." Peter had dodged it every time. He was busy. He had homework. He had Spider-Man things to do. He imagined Mary Jane was probably some boring girl who would have nothing in common with him.

He could not have been more wrong.

The day they finally met, Peter opened his front door. And standing there in a doorway full of sunlight, with bright red hair and a wide smile and dancing green eyes, was Mary Jane Watson.

She tilted her head. She took him in. And she said the line that has lived in comic-book history ever since.

"Face it, Tiger. You just hit the jackpot."

Peter's whole brain emptied of every clever thing it had ever thought of. He stood in his Aunt May's doorway with his mouth slightly open, trying to remember how words worked.

That was the beginning.

At first, Mary Jane seemed like the opposite of Peter. He was serious. She was outgoing. He was bookish. She was a wannabe actress with big dreams. He liked microscopes. She liked theater. He had the weight of being Spider-Man on his shoulders. She had the lightness of someone who had decided, very young, to laugh through the hard things.

But the deeper Peter looked, the more he saw.

Behind Mary Jane's bright smiles lay someone who had faced her own quiet struggles. Her family had not been an easy one. She had learned to keep her sadness wrapped up neatly, behind her jokes, so as not to burden anyone. She was much more perceptive than she let on. She saw things other people missed.

Including Peter's secret.

She figured it out long before he told her.

One quiet evening, sitting beside him on a rooftop with the lights of Manhattan spread out below them, she said simply, "I know, Tiger. I've known for a while. And it's okay."

Peter felt the world tilt and right itself in the same heartbeat. He had been carrying his secret for so long. To finally have someone — someone he trusted with his whole heart — who knew and still smiled at him exactly the same way... that was a kind of magic he had not known existed.

From that moment on, MJ became something rare in Peter Parker's life. She became his anchor.

The relationship was not easy. It never could have been.

Every night Peter swung out the window of their apartment, MJ had to stand at that window and watch him go, never knowing for sure if she would see him come back. She faced threats from his villains. She was followed by photographers. She had to live with the constant low hum of fear that comes with loving someone whose job is to leap off rooftops to save strangers.

But MJ was tougher than she looked. She was tougher than ANYBODY looked. She did not crumble. She did not beg him to stop. She understood, deep in her bones, who Peter was — and she knew that he would not be Peter without Spider-Man.

She also made him better.

When Peter was being too hard on himself — which he was, almost always — MJ was the one who could tease him out of it. "Tiger," she would say, throwing a pillow at him, "you saved seven lives today. You don't have to also do the dishes tonight." When the weight of the world got too heavy, she was the one who made him laugh. When he was filled with self-doubt, she was the one who reminded him who he really was.

She was, in many ways, the strongest person in Peter's life.

One of the most beautiful days in their long story was their wedding. It was not a small thing. It was a real, full, joyful wedding, with flowers and music and family and friends. Aunt May cried. Peter cried. MJ wore a white dress and her bright red hair was crowned with little white blossoms, and when she walked down the aisle, Peter Parker, who had faced gods and aliens and goblins, almost forgot how to breathe.

It was a celebration of something quietly radical. Even in a world of supervillains and cosmic threats and Green Goblins and Doctor Octopuses, it was still possible to choose a life of LOVE. A life of partnership. A normal beautiful messy life, lived together.

They faced everything together after that. Financial struggles. Career changes. The loss of people they loved. Spider-Man missions that lasted three days at a stretch. The big things. The small things. The argued-about-toothpaste-cap things.

MJ was not a damsel in distress. She was not just a "love interest" sitting at home waiting for Peter to come back. She was a hero in her own right. A working actress. A model when she chose to be. A businesswoman when she wanted. A friend to many. A woman of fierce loyalty and quick courage who, on more than one occasion, saved Peter's life right back.

Their story has had ups and downs over the years. There have been times they have been apart. There have been hard moments. Comics are like that. But the truth at the bottom of everything, the truth nobody can take away, is this.

Peter Parker and Mary Jane Watson belong to each other.

She is the heart of Spider-Man. She is the reason he always finds his way home. She is the laugh in the doorway at the end of a long bad day. She is the person who, when Peter is the loneliest hero in the world, makes him not lonely at all.

She said it first. Face it, Tiger. You just hit the jackpot.

And the truth is — they BOTH did.
