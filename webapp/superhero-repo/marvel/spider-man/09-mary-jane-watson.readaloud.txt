Seven words. That's all it took to change everything.

"Face it, Tiger — you just hit the jackpot."

Mary Jane Watson said this to Peter Parker the first time they properly met, with a smile like a flashbulb going off. She was tall, red-haired, outgoing, funny, and so comfortable in her own skin that it made everyone around her stand up a little straighter. Mary Jane was a model and an actress, the kind of person who walked into rooms and made them brighter just by being there.

Peter, who spent most of his time worrying about rent, homework, and whether his web fluid would hold up in the rain, had absolutely no idea what to make of her.

But here's a secret about Mary Jane Watson: behind the laughter was someone who had faced real hardship. Her home life had been difficult, and she'd learned to keep smiling even when things weren't easy. Her warmth wasn't an act — it was genuine, hard-won, and hers.

And she was paying very close attention to Peter Parker.

Here's the most extraordinary part: the night Uncle Ben was taken from the Parker home in Forest Hills, Queens, Mary Jane was nearby. She saw Peter run inside the house — and she saw Spider-Man climb out of the upstairs window a moment later. She pieced it together on her own, long before Peter ever told her. She knew. And she chose to stay.

That is the kind of person Mary Jane Watson was.

Their life together was not the easy kind. Every evening that Peter strapped on his web-shooters and disappeared into the Manhattan skyline, MJ sat with a quiet knot of worry behind her heart. Would he come home? What if this was the night something went truly wrong? Villains sometimes came after her directly. The newspapers ran horrible stories about Spider-Man. There were times when the weight of it all felt enormous.

Mary Jane never asked Peter to stop.

She understood the promise he'd made to Uncle Ben as clearly as if she'd been there herself. She knew that Peter couldn't be Peter without also being Spider-Man. So she chose to love the whole of him — the boy from Queens and the hero in the red-and-blue suit together.

They got married on a day full of hope. When things were hard afterward — and things were sometimes very hard — they faced them side by side.

And when Peter came home from a long, bruising night, swinging through the Queens streets toward their window, Mary Jane was always the light he was steering toward.

Mary Jane Watson knew exactly who Peter Parker was — all of him, the ordinary boy and the extraordinary hero — and she thought he was pretty spectacular.
