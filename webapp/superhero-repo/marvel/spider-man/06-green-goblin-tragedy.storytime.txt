Of all the villains Peter Parker has ever faced, only one has truly broken his heart.

His name was Norman Osborn. The Green Goblin.

Norman was not always a villain. For a long time, he was simply a wealthy industrialist — the owner of a great company called Oscorp, a man with sharp suits and sharper ambitions. He was also the father of Harry Osborn, who was Peter Parker's best friend in college. Peter used to come over to Harry's penthouse for dinner. Norman would shake his hand at the door, smile a little stiffly, and say, "Welcome, Peter. Always good to see you."

Peter liked Norman. Peter felt a little sorry for Harry, whose father was always too busy, always too distant, always working. But Peter liked him.

He did not know what Norman was building in his private laboratory.

Norman was obsessed with becoming stronger, smarter, more powerful than any man alive. He had developed something he called the Goblin Formula — a serum meant to enhance human strength and intellect. He tested it on himself, alone, late one night.

It worked. It also broke something inside him.

The Goblin Formula gave Norman incredible strength. It gave him a brilliant, twisted cleverness. But it also splintered his mind. A new personality woke up inside him — wild, cackling, hungry for chaos. Norman put on a green mask shaped like a goblin's face. He climbed onto a bright orange glider that could fly. He filled his belt with pumpkin bombs that exploded in showers of orange sparks. And he soared out into the night sky over Manhattan, laughing in a high cracked voice.

The Green Goblin was born.

What made the Green Goblin truly terrible was not his glider or his bombs. It was that he learned Peter's secret. He discovered that Peter Parker was Spider-Man.

Most villains attack a hero's mask. The Green Goblin attacked the PERSON underneath.

He targeted everyone Peter loved. He whispered threats. He showed up at places Peter thought were safe. He cackled, "I know who you are, Spider-Man," and he meant it. Peter had no safe place anymore.

The saddest moment in the whole long sad story of Norman Osborn was the night he hurt the person Peter loved most in the world at that time. Her name was Gwen Stacy.

Gwen was Peter's first true love. She had clear blue eyes and a long blonde braid and a brilliant mind for science that matched Peter's own. The two of them used to study together in the library at Empire State University, their notebooks slowly drifting closer across the table until they were touching. She knew Peter's secret. She loved him for it.

The Green Goblin took Gwen to the very top of the George Washington Bridge, one cold dark night, and he forced Spider-Man to come for her.

What happened next was the worst moment of Peter Parker's young life.

He tried so hard to save her. He fought the Goblin. He shot his web. He reached. He stretched. He gave everything he had.

But in the end, she was lost. Quietly. The way a flower closes at the end of a long day. Peter knelt on the cold bridge and held her, and he wept the kind of tears that go on for a very long time.

The Goblin had wanted exactly this. He had wanted to take away the thing Peter loved most. He believed he had won.

In a way, he had. The wound never quite closed. For the rest of Peter's life, he would carry a small private ache for Gwen — a quiet sadness that lived just below his ribs.

But the Goblin had been wrong about one thing. He thought that losing Gwen would turn Peter into a monster. He thought Peter would give in to rage and become like him. That is what the Goblin had always wanted, deep down — for Spider-Man to become a goblin too.

Peter did not.

He grieved. He grieved with everything in him. But the Goblin's evil did not break Peter's goodness. If anything, it deepened it. Peter doubled his vow to protect everyone he could. He whispered Gwen's name to himself sometimes, on the wind, between rooftops, as he swung through the city. He swore to remember her by being WORTHY of her.

The Green Goblin and Spider-Man fought many more times after that. Norman tried, again and again, to push Peter into rage. Peter, again and again, refused.

The Goblin even, eventually, "died" in their last great battle. But Norman Osborn was a stubborn ghost. He found ways to come back. He passed the green mask on to others, including, terribly, his own son Harry — who, mad with grief and the Goblin Formula in his veins, briefly became a Goblin himself.

That was the hardest fight of Peter's life. To raise his fists, even gently, against his best friend.

In the end, Harry came back to himself. The friendship was healed, though the scars remained.

The Green Goblin is the ghost who never quite leaves the rooftops of Manhattan. Late at night, when the wind whistles a certain way through the towers, Peter Parker sometimes thinks he can hear a faint, far-off cackle.

He pulls his red-and-blue mask down a little tighter.

He keeps swinging.

For Gwen. For Harry. For everyone Norman Osborn ever hurt.

And the Goblin, wherever he is, does not win.

Not while Spider-Man is still in the sky.
