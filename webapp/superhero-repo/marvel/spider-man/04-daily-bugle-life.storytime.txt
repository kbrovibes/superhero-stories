In the middle of Manhattan, on a very busy street, there was a building with big gold letters above the door that read: THE DAILY BUGLE.

The Daily Bugle was a newspaper — the kind with ink-black headlines and photographs and stories about everything happening in New York City. And the man who ran it, the man who sat at the biggest desk with the loudest voice and the most impressive flat-top haircut in all of New York, was J. Jonah Jameson.

J. Jonah Jameson did not like Spider-Man.

He really, truly, deeply did not like Spider-Man.

Every morning, he would slam his fist on his desk and bellow: "PICTURES! I want PICTURES of Spider-Man! Front page! Big! ENORMOUS! And the headline — 'Spider-Man: Menace to the City!'"

He believed — truly and sincerely believed — that anyone who wore a mask was hiding something. Real heroes, he would say, were police officers. Firefighters. Soldiers. People who showed their faces and stood in the light.

And here was Spider-Man, swinging around in the shadows, face hidden, identity secret, getting all the credit.

It drove J. Jonah Jameson absolutely bananas.

Now. Here is the delicious secret.

The boy who sold J. Jonah Jameson his Spider-Man photographs — a quiet, polite young man named Peter Parker who came in carrying a camera and looking slightly tired — was Spider-Man.

Peter would swing out at night, do something heroic, accidentally snap a photo of himself mid-swing with the camera he had rigged to a wall, and then show up at the Daily Bugle the next afternoon and sell the picture to Jameson for enough money to help Aunt May pay the electricity bill.

It was the most complicated job in the history of employment.

"Parker!" Jameson would shout, holding up a photograph. "This is the best shot you've ever gotten! Spider-Man caught in the act! Right here! BEAUTIFUL! How do you always know where he's going to be?"

"I just... have a feeling," Peter would say, very carefully.

Inside the Bugle, Peter found something he hadn't expected: a family.

There was Robbie Robertson — the wise, kind editor who always had time for Peter, who gave him advice about photography and life and how to tell a story with a single image. Robbie believed in Spider-Man, even when Jameson didn't, and he believed in Peter too.

There was Betty Brant, who worked at the front desk and smiled when Peter walked in and sometimes slipped him an extra assignment when she knew he needed the money.

And even Jameson himself — GRUMPY, bellowing, anti-Spider-Man Jameson — in his own strange way, cared about the truth. He wanted the newspaper to matter. He wanted people to be safe. He just had the completely wrong idea about who the hero was.

Peter never told him. He couldn't. Because the moment J. Jonah Jameson knew that his star photographer and his most-hated super-villain were the same person, every single person Peter loved would be in danger. The mask wasn't just a costume. It was a shield around Aunt May.

But there was something hard about it, too.

Because Spider-Man saved people. He caught falling taxis. He pulled children from burning buildings. He stopped bank robbers mid-sprint. And the very next morning, the Daily Bugle would run the headline: "SPIDER-MAN CAUSES CHAOS ON FIFTH AVENUE."

Peter would read those headlines over his cereal and sigh.

He didn't need the city to love him. He had figured that out. He did what he did because it was right, not because it made people cheer. Uncle Ben had taught him that. But it was still hard, on the quiet Tuesday mornings, to read about himself in the newspaper and feel like a stranger.

One afternoon, a group of thieves — four of them, with masks of their own — broke into the Daily Bugle building while Jameson was giving a speech downstairs. They were after the paper's files. They wanted to silence the stories the Bugle was working on about their crimes.

Peter felt his spider-sense tingle in the middle of the lobby.

He slipped away.

THWIP — up the side of the elevator shaft. THWIP — across the ceiling of the third floor. The thieves never heard him coming. They were very surprised to find themselves hanging from the ceiling in cocoons of silvery web, their tools scattered across the newsroom floor.

Spider-Man was gone before Jameson reached the third floor.

The next morning, the headline read: "SPIDER-MAN BREAKS INTO DAILY BUGLE — LEAVES THIEVES IN WEBS."

Peter read it over his cereal and laughed quietly to himself.

Aunt May looked over. "Something funny, dear?"

"No," said Peter, folding the newspaper. "Just… New York."

THWIP.
