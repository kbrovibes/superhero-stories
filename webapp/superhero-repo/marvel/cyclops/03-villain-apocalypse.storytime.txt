Once upon a time, in an ancient land of pyramids and golden sand, a child was born who would grow up to believe something terribly wrong.

His name was En Sabah Nur. It meant The First One.

He lived and grew for many, many centuries, and he came to believe that strength was the only thing that truly mattered. Not kindness. Not wisdom. Not love. Strength alone, he decided, determined who deserved to remain. By the time Cyclops was grown and leading the X-Men, En Sabah Nur had taken a new name: Apocalypse. And he had decided the time had come to remake the world his way.

He had gathered four powerful mutants and called them his Four Horsemen. He sent them ahead of him like a storm, to weaken anyone who might resist.

When the Horsemen appeared above Manhattan, Cyclops was ready.

He had spent days studying everything the X-Men knew about Apocalypse: his armor, his tactics, the way he moved. He had drawn diagrams in the Xavier School's war room and asked questions no one else thought to ask. When the alarms finally sounded, he stood and said simply: "Let's go."

The battle on the rooftops was bright and loud and very fast. The Four Horsemen came from every direction, and the sounds of the city — horns and wind off the Hudson River — mixed with the crackle of powers clashing. Cyclops moved his team like a conductor, his voice calm and certain in their earpieces.

"Wolverine — left flank. Jean — cover from above. Storm — I need weather, pull it in from the harbor."

And they trusted him. That was the thing about Cyclops. His teammates trusted him completely, because he never sent them somewhere he hadn't already thought through.

He stood at the center of the rooftop, firing his optic blasts in short precise bursts — not wild, but aimed like a careful pencil making deliberate marks. One Horseman stumbled. Then another. Then a third.

And then the sky darkened.

Apocalypse descended on a vast armored transport. He stepped to the edge and looked down, and even from below, Cyclops could feel the weight of that ancient gaze.

"You lead with your mind," Apocalypse said. His voice was deep and unhurried. "Wisdom or weakness — I intend to find out which."

He swept one massive armored arm across the rooftop. CRASH. Three X-Men went tumbling. Even Wolverine had to dig his claws into the concrete to keep from going over the edge.

But Scott Summers had fought hard battles before, and he was not the kind of person who panicked when things tilted sideways.

He raised his visor and fired — a full, sustained blast, every bit of power he could produce, aimed straight at Apocalypse's chest. It didn't stop the ancient mutant. But it made him take a step back, and a step back was enough.

"NOW," Scott said quietly into his earpiece.

Jean Grey's telekinetic wave hit Apocalypse from the side — heavy and sudden, like an invisible tide.

Storm lifted her arms, and the clouds from the harbor — dark and full of lightning she had been carefully pulling together — released their bolt directly overhead.

CRACK.

The lightning struck Apocalypse dead center.

He staggered. His transport shuddered and tilted. He looked at Scott one more time — not with fear, but with something that might, just barely, have been surprise.

And then the transport rose and swept back, and he was gone.

The rooftop went quiet. Rain began to fall — soft and cold, washing the dust from the concrete.

Scott counted his team. One, two, three, four, five. All safe. All there. He let out a long breath.

On the bus back to Westchester, nobody talked much. Jean sat beside Scott, and he watched the dark city pass, already thinking through what had worked and what he would do differently next time. That was Cyclops. He was always already planning.

When they reached the Xavier School, warm light glowed in the kitchen windows. Someone had left a note on the counter: Cocoa in the pot. Professor Xavier's handwriting.

Scott poured two mugs, handed one to Jean, and sat down at the kitchen table.

"Good work tonight," Jean said.

"Good work, team," Scott said.

Outside, the rain fell on the garden. The Xavier School was safe and warm and full of sleeping heroes.

Sleep well. The best leaders bring everyone home safe — and then they start planning for next time.
