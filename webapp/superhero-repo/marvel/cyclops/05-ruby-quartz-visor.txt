Most people never think about the thing that lets them look at the world. Scott Summers thought about it every single day. His ruby-quartz visor was not just protective gear — it was the reason he could have a normal life, sit down to breakfast, look a friend in the eye, walk down a street without leaving a path of destruction behind him. Without it, his optic blasts fired continuously. With it, they stayed exactly where they belonged.

Ruby quartz is a rare, dense crystal that absorbs concussive energy rather than transmitting it. Professor Xavier had first located a deposit in the mountains of Madripoor and arranged for a craftsman named Henry Larner to grind and shape the lenses to match the exact focal distance of Scott's eyes. Larner spent three weeks calibrating the curve of each lens. The finished visor was a seamless band of deep red crystal fitted into a polished steel frame — elegant and exact, the way Xavier liked all his tools.

The visor has a slider control on the right temple. Pushed all the way down, it narrows the lens aperture to a tight, focused beam no wider than a pencil — perfect for precision work, like snapping a lock or stopping a single falling object. Pushed all the way up, it opens the aperture wide, dispersing the energy into a broad spread that can push back a crowd or slow a large object. In the middle range, where Scott uses it most, the beam is roughly the width of a fist: powerful, controllable, aimed.

Jean Grey once asked Scott what it was like to see the world through red lenses all the time. He thought about it for a moment and said, "Warm." He had never seen the world in full color. Every sky was amber, every face was tinged with rose, every star was a shade of deep red. He sometimes wondered what the color blue looked like. Jean described it to him — cool and wide and open, like the feeling of a long breath. Scott nodded and said that sounded nice, and he meant it without any sadness, because the world through ruby quartz had always been his world, and he had learned to find it beautiful.

The visor was the first thing he touched every morning and the last thing he set aside every night. It was, in its quiet way, the truest symbol of who he was: not a weapon, but a discipline. Not a limit, but a choice. Choosing, every day, to hold the power carefully and aim it only toward good.
