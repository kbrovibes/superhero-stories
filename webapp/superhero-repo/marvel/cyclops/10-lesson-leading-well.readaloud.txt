Okay. I want to tell you about the day Scott Summers got something wrong.

Not wrong in a big dramatic villain way. Wrong in a very quiet, very important way. And what he did NEXT is the most impressive thing in this whole collection.

It started in the Danger Room. Routine training exercise — bridge rescue, four team members extracting civilians from a collapsing structure. Scott had been distracted all week because Mister Sinister had surfaced again, new signals on the Essex Industries frequencies, and Scott was running every scenario in his head constantly, trying to figure out what Sinister was planning.

He set up the exercise quickly. Assigned positions from habit, not from thought. Stepped into the Danger Room with most of his brain somewhere else entirely.

And then — CRUNCH — the simulated structure collapsed in the wrong sequence. Ororo found herself in the wrong quadrant, no weather clearance. Bobby's ice bridge landed two meters short of the extraction point. Jean used her telekinesis to cover BOTH gaps — but then a third problem appeared from a corner Scott hadn't mapped, and Jean had nothing left.

The exercise failed.

The simulated civilians were not saved.

Scott called the RESET — the room went quiet — and stood in the empty chamber.

Then he gathered the team.

And here is what he said:

"I was distracted this week. I set up that exercise without my full attention. The failure happened because of me, not because of anything any of you did. Ororo, Bobby — I'm sorry. Jean — thank you for covering the gaps I created. That was my job, not yours."

The room was quiet.

Then Ororo said, "Thank you for saying so."

Wolverine — who had been standing with his arms crossed — uncrossed them. That was a big deal.

Jean said, "What do we know about the Sinister signals?"

And just like THAT — snap — they were all working the real problem together. As a team. No blame. No awkwardness. Just the next thing.

Professor Xavier found Scott in the hallway afterward.

"Leading well," Xavier said, "isn't about never making mistakes. It's about being honest about them."

Scott said he should have been upfront at the START — told them about the distraction before running the exercise.

Xavier smiled. "Yes. And now you know you will."

The next exercise? Perfect. Not because Scott was flawless. But because he told his team what he was working with, and they adjusted together, and THAT is how a team is supposed to work.

ZZZAP! The best leaders don't pretend to be perfect. They tell the truth. And then they lead.
