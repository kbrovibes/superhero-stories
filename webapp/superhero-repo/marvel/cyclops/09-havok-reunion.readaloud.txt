This might be the best story in the whole collection. Are you ready? Because this one is about finding someone you thought was lost.

Remember when Scott was little — the parachute, the Montana forest, the orphanage — and his brother Alex was taken somewhere else?

Scott never, ever forgot.

For YEARS, he searched. Old social services records. Transfer documents. Forwarding addresses that led to OTHER forwarding addresses. Professor Xavier helped when Scott was old enough to ask. It took three years of searching. Three years.

Then one afternoon, they found him.

Alex Summers. Pasadena, California. Good family. Good life. And — yes — absolutely a mutant. Alex's power was different from Scott's: instead of a focused beam, he projected energy in big concentric rings that spread outward from his body like a shockwave. WHOOOOOM. Wide, explosive, powerful. The perfect complement to Scott's tight precision beam.

He called himself Havok.

Scott flew to California. He arrived fifteen minutes early at a coffee shop in Pasadena and stood outside. He waited.

Alex arrived exactly on time.

Two tall young men. Same jaw. Same hairline. One wearing a red visor. One squinting in the California sun.

They looked at each other.

Alex said, "You look like Dad."

Scott said, "So do you."

And then they went inside and talked for FOUR HOURS.

They filled in each other's whole stories — what did the Montana forest look like when they landed? What did the caseworkers say? What was Pasadena like? What was Omaha like? Each of them had half the story; together they had the whole thing. Like fitting two puzzle pieces together that had been kept in separate boxes for years.

When they got to talking about Christopher and Katherine — their parents — the café had long gone quiet around them. That part was gentle and sad and real, the way important things are.

Scott invited Alex to the Xavier School. Alex visited. He enrolled. He became Havok, field member of the X-Men.

In training, their powers turned out to be a BRILLIANT combination. Scott's tight beam — CRACK! — opens a precise angle. Alex's wide rings — WHOOOOOM — cover the spread. Together they can bracket an enemy position that neither one alone could handle. They had to learn the right spacing — too close and Alex's rings clip Scott's beam — but they worked it out, patient and careful, until it was clean.

They hadn't grown up together, and that gap never fully disappeared. But every day, they chose to be brothers. They showed up. They trained together. They kept filling in the story.

That, Scott decided, was more than enough.

CRACK. WHOOOOOM. Two brothers, one team.
