Once upon a time, a boy could not open his eyes.

Not because he was asleep. Not because he was frightened. But because his eyes held something enormous inside them, and the moment he opened them — even a crack — that something would burst out and break everything in its path.

Scott Summers had the most extraordinary eyes of anyone he had ever met. They could fire a beam of pure concussive force — not heat, not fire, but a great rushing wall of energy — powerful enough to move a boulder or dent a steel door. He had no off switch. His eyes were always pressing, always ready, and the only thing between him and a world of accidental destruction was keeping them shut.

Which is a very hard way to live.

Professor Charles Xavier understood this. He thought about it carefully — as he thought about everything carefully — and he remembered something he had once read about a mineral called ruby quartz. It was rare and very dense and had a remarkable property: rather than transmitting energy through itself, it absorbed concussive force and held it suspended within the crystal. A beam could press into ruby quartz all day long and the quartz would simply hold it. Not let a single bit through.

Xavier sent a trusted contact to find a deposit of ruby quartz in the mountains of Madripoor, a distant island in the South China Sea. The crystals came back in a padded case — heavy for their size, glowing like embers. Xavier brought them to a craftsman named Henry Larner, who had made optical instruments his whole life.

"Three weeks," Larner said. He spent every one of those days grinding and polishing and testing, until the lenses were perfect.

When the visor was finished, it was a beautiful thing. Deep red crystal, smooth and seamless, fitted into a frame of polished steel that curved gently around the face. It looked, in a way, like a window onto a sunset.

Xavier brought it to Scott.

Scott held it in his hands for a long moment before he put it on. He ran his fingers along the steel frame, along the smooth face of each lens. Then he closed his eyes, took a slow breath, and slid the visor on.

He opened his eyes.

The world was red. Every color was shifted — every sky was amber, every wall was rose, every face warm and reddish in the light the crystal let through.

He could see. He could look at Professor Xavier sitting across the table from him and really, truly see his face.

He was quiet for a while.

"How does it look?" Xavier asked.

"Warm," Scott said.

There is a slider on the right temple of the visor — a small metal control, easy to find by touch. Pressed to its lowest setting, the lens aperture narrows to the thinnest possible channel, and the blast comes out like a pencil line — precise enough to stop a single falling stone. Pushed to its highest, the aperture opens wide and the energy disperses into a broad spread of force. In the middle range, where Scott uses it most, the beam comes out roughly the width of a closed fist — powerful, deliberate, aimed.

He practiced with the slider for weeks. Down, up, middle. Again and again, until the adjustment was as automatic as breathing.

Jean Grey asked him once, sitting on the roof of the Xavier School in the evening, what the world looked like through the visor.

Scott thought about it properly before answering.

"Warm," he said. "Everything is amber. Like the whole world is lit by something comfortable." He paused. "I've never seen blue. Not really."

"Blue is cool and wide," Jean said. "Like taking a very long, slow breath. Like looking at something that goes on forever and feeling peaceful rather than scared."

Scott nodded slowly. "That sounds nice."

He meant it without any sadness. The red-warmed world had been his world for so long now that he had found it beautiful. He had learned to love the amber light, the rose-tinged faces, the deep red sky.

Every morning he reached for the visor first, before breakfast or boots or the day's schedule. Every night he set it down gently on the nightstand.

Not a cage. Not a burden. Not a limitation.

A discipline. A choice. The choice to hold something powerful carefully, and to aim it only toward good.

Sleep well. The things we carry carefully are often the things that help us most.
