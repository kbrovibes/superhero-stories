Wolverine arrived at the Xavier School the way a storm arrives — loudly, without much warning, and with no intention of apologizing for it. His name was Logan, and he had been alive longer than anyone knew, with a skeleton laced in unbreakable adamantium and three retractable claws on each hand. He operated on instinct. He trusted his gut over any battle plan ever written. He called Scott "Summers" with the slight emphasis that meant he thought the name was mildly ridiculous.

Scott, for his part, found Logan infuriating in the most productive way possible.

The two of them disagreed on almost everything tactical. Logan believed in reacting fast and adjusting on the move. Scott believed in mapping the terrain before setting a single foot inside it. Logan thought Scott's briefings were too long. Scott thought Logan's approach would eventually get someone hurt. They argued about this — in training rooms, in the briefing chamber, in the hallway after missions — with enough noise that the other X-Men had learned to find somewhere else to be during the first ten minutes.

But somewhere inside all the arguing was a genuine, grudging, mutual respect.

It emerged during a mission to the Weapon X facility in Alberta, where the X-Men were extracting three young mutants who had been held against their will. The extraction had gone smoothly until the building's automated defense systems — massive hydraulic doors designed to seal corridor by corridor — began activating ahead of schedule. Scott and Logan were in the deepest corridor with two of the young mutants between them, and the last door was slamming shut with thirty seconds left.

Logan looked at the door, then at Scott. "Tell me you can hit the locking mechanism."

It was a ten-centimeter target, sixty meters away, in a corridor filling with steam from a burst coolant pipe. Scott narrowed his visor to minimum aperture and fired — CRACK — and the locking pin sheared clean. The door stayed open.

Logan grabbed both young mutants, tucked one under each arm, and sprinted. Scott covered the rear, firing twice more to hold back automated sentinels. They made it out with four seconds to spare.

Outside, in the cold Alberta air, Logan set the two kids down and looked at Scott for a long moment. "Good shot," he said. It was possibly the least enthusiastic compliment Scott had ever received. It was also, from Wolverine, an enormous thing to say. Scott said, "Good run," and Logan grunted and walked toward the jet and that was the end of the conversation, but both of them understood something had been established.
