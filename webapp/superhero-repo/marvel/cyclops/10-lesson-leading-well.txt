The day Scott Summers nearly got it wrong was not a dramatic day. There was no enormous villain, no city at risk, no stakes that history would remember. It was a routine training exercise in the Danger Room, and it went badly because Scott was trying to solve two problems at once and hadn't told anyone.

He had been distracted for a week. Mister Sinister had surfaced again — a new sighting, new coded signals from Essex Industries frequencies — and Scott had been running the intelligence through every scenario he could model. Meanwhile, the Danger Room session was a bridge rescue: four team members extracting simulated civilians from a collapsing structure while a fifth managed defensive fire. Scott had set it up quickly, assigned positions from habit rather than thought, and stepped into the exercise with most of his attention somewhere else entirely.

When the simulated structure collapsed in the wrong sequence, Ororo found herself in a quadrant without weather clearance. Bobby Drake laid an ice bridge two meters short of the extraction point. Jean compensated with telekinesis to cover both gaps, which meant she had nothing left when a third problem emerged from a corner Scott hadn't mapped. The exercise failed. The simulated civilians were not saved.

Scott called the reset and stood in the empty chamber for a moment.

He gathered the team and told them what had happened. Not a debriefing with tactical language — he just told them plainly: he had been distracted, he had set up the exercise without full attention, and the failure was the direct result of leadership that wasn't fully present. He apologized to Ororo and Bobby specifically. He thanked Jean for covering the gaps he'd created.

The room was quiet. Then Ororo said, "Thank you for saying so." Wolverine, who had been standing with his arms crossed, uncrossed them. Jean said, "What do we know about the Sinister signals?" And just like that, they were working the real problem together.

Professor Xavier found Scott afterward. He said, "Leading well isn't about never making mistakes. It's about being honest about them." He paused. "You did that today." Scott said he should have done it at the start — been upfront about the distraction before running the exercise. Xavier said, "Yes. And now you know you will."

The next exercise ran perfectly. Not because Scott was flawless, but because he told his team what he was working with, and they adjusted together, and that was how it was supposed to work all along.
