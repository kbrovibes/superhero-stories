The Xavier School for Gifted Youngsters sat at the end of a long gravel drive, behind an iron gate in Westchester County, New York. From the road it looked like a grand old manor house — brick walls covered in climbing ivy, wide windows, tall chimneys. To the students inside, it was the first place many of them had ever felt completely at home.

Professor Charles Xavier had built the school with a single clear purpose: to give young mutants a place where their abilities were not a source of shame, but a subject of study. He was a telepath of extraordinary range, and he had spent years quietly locating gifted young people who had nowhere else to go. He offered them two things — education and community — and most of them discovered that the second mattered more than the first.

Scott arrived as the school's first student, and he took that responsibility seriously. He helped Xavier design the curriculum. He set the training schedules in the Danger Room — the underground simulation facility where students practiced their powers against increasingly complex scenarios. He ran the sessions himself, which meant he had to think not only about his own optic blasts but about every other student's abilities: where Jean Grey's telekinesis was strongest, where Bobby Drake's ice constructs were most stable, where Hank McCoy's agility could be best applied.

The Danger Room taught Scott something he kept for the rest of his life: a good plan is built around the people in it. Not around what a leader wants to do, but around what each person does best. He had tried the reverse once — assigning positions based on what the scenario called for, not who was best suited — and it went badly. He filed that lesson carefully and never repeated the mistake.

Outside the training room, the Xavier School was warm and noisy. Hank McCoy argued comparative literature at the breakfast table. Ororo Munroe tended the garden, which was more beautiful than any other garden in Westchester because the weather was always exactly right. Bobby Drake left ice sculptures in the hallway that melted by noon. Scott sometimes walked the grounds at dusk, listening to all of it — the voices, the laughter, the sound of young people figuring out who they were — and he understood why Xavier had built this place. Not just to make good heroes. To make good people. The heroism was a consequence of the rest.
