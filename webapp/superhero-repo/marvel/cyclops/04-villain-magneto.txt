Magneto — Erik Lehnsherr — believed he was protecting mutantkind. He had lived through great cruelty as a child, and that experience had convinced him that humans would always turn on mutants, that safety could only come from power and fear. He was not wrong about history, but he was wrong about what to do with it. When he encased the island of Genosha in a dome of solid steel and threatened to cut it off from the outside world permanently, Scott Summers flew to Genosha not to destroy him, but to talk.

That surprised Magneto. Most people came to fight.

Scott landed on the dome's outer surface, set down his communication device in the open so Magneto could see it was his only weapon, and waited. After a long silence, a seam of metal parted and Scott dropped inside. The dome's interior was vast and dim, lit only by strips of magnetic coils along the walls, crackling with blue-white energy. Magneto stood in the center, his deep-crimson helmet catching the light, his cape sweeping in the field he generated around himself.

"You came alone," Magneto said.

"I came to listen," Scott told him.

For two hours, Scott listened. He heard the history that shaped Erik Lehnsherr: the losses, the years of fighting alone, the desperate logic that had led him here. Scott did not agree with the dome. He said so plainly. But he also said something that made Magneto go still: "Professor Xavier lost his closest friend when he lost you. He has never stopped believing you could come back."

Magneto turned away. The magnetic field around him surged once, violently, then went quiet.

In the end, Magneto lowered the dome. Not because Scott threatened him, but because someone had looked at him as something other than a problem to be defeated. The people of Genosha walked out into open air, blinking in the sunlight, and Scott stood beside Magneto as they did.

The two of them did not become friends that day. The argument between them was old and complicated, and it would continue for years. But something shifted. Magneto looked at the young man in the red visor and said, "You have Xavier's stubbornness." Scott said, "I'll take that." It was, for both of them, exactly the right answer.
