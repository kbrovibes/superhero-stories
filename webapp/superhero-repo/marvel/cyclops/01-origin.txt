The story of Scott Summers began far above the earth, in a small plane falling through the night sky over Alaska. Scott was eight years old when the engines failed. His parents, Christopher and Katherine Summers, made the only choice they could. They buckled one parachute around both boys, pushed Scott and his little brother Alex out of the door, and watched the plane disappear below the clouds. Christopher and Katherine were gone. The boys tumbled toward the mountains alone.

Scott woke in an orphanage in Omaha, Nebraska. He had suffered a serious head injury during the fall, and the damage had done something strange to his eyes. Whenever he opened them without protection, beams of bright red energy — optic blasts — fired uncontrolled, shattering whatever they touched. The doctors fitted him with a visor of ruby-quartz, the only mineral capable of containing his power. With the visor in place, the blasts held back. Without it, he could barely function. He spent years in the orphanage, quiet and careful, carrying the weight of what he could do.

Everything changed when a man in a wheelchair arrived at the orphanage door. His name was Professor Charles Xavier, and he had a gift for finding young people whose bodies had been changed by a natural mutation in their genes. Professor Xavier called these people mutants, and he had built a school in Westchester, New York — the Xavier School for Gifted Youngsters — where mutants could learn to understand and control their abilities. He offered Scott a place there. For the first time since that night over Alaska, Scott felt something he had almost forgotten: hope.

At the Xavier School, Scott trained harder than anyone. He practiced with his optic blasts in the Danger Room, learning to channel them with precision — bouncing shots off metal walls, threading narrow targets, holding back when restraint was needed. Professor Xavier saw something in him that Scott had not yet seen in himself: the steady calm of a natural leader. Before long, Xavier asked Scott to lead the first X-Men, a group of young mutants who would protect a world that feared them. Scott chose the name Cyclops and accepted without hesitation.

Cyclops became the field commander the X-Men relied on. His power was enormous, but what made him irreplaceable was his discipline. He studied every battle, planned every approach, and trusted his teammates completely. When things went wrong, he was the first to stand firm and find a way through. He knew, more than most, what it meant to carry something you could not simply put down. His optic blasts were always there, waiting behind the ruby-quartz — a reminder that power without control could hurt the people you loved most.
