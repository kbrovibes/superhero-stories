Once upon a time, Scott Summers did something braver than any optic blast.

He put down his communicator, walked in alone, and listened.

The island of Genosha sat in the middle of the ocean, a small and troubled place where mutants had gone hoping to find peace. But peace did not always find them back. And one grey morning, the sky above Genosha went dark as a ceiling of solid steel rolled itself closed, dome by dome by dome, sealing the entire island inside. Magneto had done it. Magneto, whose real name was Erik Lehnsherr, who could command every piece of metal in the world as easily as you or I might pick up a spoon.

He said he was protecting the island. He said the outside world would only bring more harm.

At the Xavier School, Professor Xavier sat at his desk with his hands folded and his eyes very still. He and Erik Lehnsherr had been the closest of friends once, long ago. They had argued, and the argument had never healed, and every year since then, Xavier had wondered if there was still a path back. He was not strong enough anymore to go himself.

Scott asked to go alone.

"Magneto will not be moved by force," Xavier said. "He has resisted forces far greater than ours."

"I'm not going to use force," Scott said.

Xavier looked at him for a long time. Then he nodded.

Scott flew to Genosha in a small X-jet, alone. He landed on the outer surface of the dome. It was cold up there, the Atlantic wind cutting across the curved steel, the ocean very far below. He set his communication device down on the dome's surface, in plain sight, and he sat down beside it. He carried no other equipment.

He waited.

For a long time, nothing happened. Then a seam appeared in the steel, and a hatch opened, and Scott dropped down inside.

The interior of the dome was vast and quiet, lit by strips of magnetic coils that ran along the inner walls and threw a cold blue-white glow across everything. It smelled of metal and ocean. Magneto stood in the center of the space, his crimson helmet and deep red cape catching the light. The magnetic field he generated moved around him like invisible weather — you could feel it in the small hairs on the back of your neck.

He looked at Scott.

"You came alone," Magneto said.

"I came to listen," Scott said.

And so Erik Lehnsherr talked. He talked about a childhood full of loss and fear and cruelty — experiences so hard that they had built a wall inside him, a wall made of the certainty that the world would always, always turn on the people it feared. He talked about years of fighting — alone, then with followers, then alone again. He talked about the dome. He said it was protection. He said there was no other way.

Scott listened to all of it without moving, without arguing, without reaching for his visor.

Then, when Magneto was finished, Scott said: "I don't agree with the dome. I think it will make things worse. I think isolation hardens people and fear grows in the dark." He said this clearly and quietly, looking at Magneto without any attempt to soften it. "But I also want to tell you something else."

He paused.

"Professor Xavier lost his closest friend when he lost you. Not a colleague. Not an opponent. His closest friend. He has never stopped believing you could come back."

The words landed in the space between them like a stone dropped into still water.

Magneto said nothing. The magnetic field around him surged — once, powerfully, a great invisible shudder — and then went completely quiet. He turned away. He stood very still for a long time, looking at the glowing coils on the wall.

Then, slowly, the dome began to retract.

It folded back section by section, steel peeling away from the sky, and the sunlight poured in like something that had been waiting patiently outside. The people of Genosha — mutants who had been sealed in the dark — walked out into the open air, blinking and slow, tilting their faces up toward the light.

Scott and Magneto stood side by side and watched them go.

"You have Xavier's stubbornness," Magneto said at last.

Scott smiled, just a little. "I'll take that."

They did not become friends that day. The arguments between them were old and deep and would continue for a long time. But something had changed — something quiet and important — and on the long flight home, Scott looked out the window of the X-jet and thought about what it meant to truly see someone. Not just what they had done, but who they were underneath it.

That was Xavier's gift. And now it was his.

Sleep well. The bravest thing you can do is sometimes simply this: sit down and listen.
