Scott had been searching for Alex for years. After the parachute, after the Montana forest, after the orphanage system had separated them and moved them to different states, Scott had followed every lead he could find — old social services records, transfer documents, forwarding addresses that led to other forwarding addresses. Professor Xavier helped when Scott was old enough to ask. It took three years.

Alex Summers had ended up in California, with a good family in Pasadena. He had grown into a confident, easygoing young man with a quick smile who seemed to move through the world with none of Scott's careful deliberateness. He was also a mutant. His power was different from Scott's — Alex projected energy in a wave that spread outward from his body in concentric rings, explosive and broad, rather than a focused beam. He called himself Havok, which suited him perfectly.

They met for the first time as adults on a gray morning outside a coffee shop in Pasadena. Scott arrived fifteen minutes early and waited. Alex arrived exactly on time. They stood on the sidewalk looking at each other for a moment, two tall young men with the same jaw and the same hairline, one wearing a red visor, one squinting in the California sun.

Alex said, "You look like Dad."

Scott said, "So do you."

They went inside and talked for four hours.

The shared history came fast — each of them knew the broad outlines but not the details. What had they landed on, that night in Montana? What did the caseworker at the orphanage say? What had the years in Pasadena been like, the years in Omaha? They filled in each other's story piece by piece, carefully, like restoring something fragile. When they finally talked about their parents — about Christopher and Katherine — the café had long since gone quiet around them.

Scott invited Alex to the Xavier School. Alex visited, then enrolled, then became a field member with the X-Men designation Havok. In the field, their powers complemented each other in unexpected ways: Scott's tight beam and Alex's wide rings could bracket an enemy position, or layer coverage in a way that one power alone never could. They had to practice careful spacing — too close together and Alex's rings could clip Scott's beam — but they worked it out until it was clean and reliable.

They had not grown up together, and that gap never fully closed. But they chose, every day, to be brothers — to show up, to know each other, to keep filling in the story. That, Scott had decided, was more than enough.
