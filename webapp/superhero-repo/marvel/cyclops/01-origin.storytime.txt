Once upon a time, in the wide cold skies above Alaska, there lived a family who loved each other very much.

The father's name was Christopher Summers, and he flew planes — real ones, with engines that roared and wings that tilted into the wind. His wife Katherine had a warm laugh that filled their small house in the evenings. And they had two boys: Scott, the older one, with careful eyes and a quiet smile, and Alex, the little one, who never stopped running.

Then one night, everything changed.

The family was flying together in Christopher's plane when the engines went quiet. Not the friendly quiet of a sleeping house — the terrible quiet of something broken. The plane began to fall.

Christopher and Katherine moved fast. They had one parachute, and they knew exactly what to do. Christopher buckled it around both boys — Scott and Alex, together — and he held Scott's face in his hands for just a heartbeat.

"Hold on," he said. "Hold each other."

And then the boys were falling through the cold dark air, and the plane was gone.

Scott and Alex landed safely. But their parents could not be found. Scott was eight years old, and he learned that some goodbyes come without any warning.

The two boys were separated in the confusion that followed. Scott ended up alone at an orphanage in Omaha, Nebraska. He was quiet there. Careful. He missed Alex. He missed his parents. He missed the sound of engines and cold mountain air.

Then, when Scott was thirteen, something happened. A beam of blazing red energy erupted from his eyes — punching through a brick wall like it was paper. Scott shut his eyes tight, but he could feel the power pressing against his eyelids like a river pushing at a dam. He was frightened. Deeply frightened.

But he was also brave — braver than he knew.

The grown-ups scrambled, and they found a solution: a visor made of a rare mineral called ruby-quartz. The moment Scott put it on — CLICK — the red energy stopped. He could open his eyes again. He wore that visor from that day on, a thin line of red light always resting across his vision. Some children thought it looked strange. Scott thought it felt like something he'd been given rather than something he'd lost.

Not long after, a man came to the orphanage. He arrived in a wheelchair, with kind eyes and a calm voice, and he made Scott feel — for the first time in years — that someone was truly paying attention.

His name was Professor Charles Xavier. He told Scott that there were others like him — young people whose bodies had changed in extraordinary ways, because of a tiny change deep in their genes. Xavier called them mutants. He had built a school in a big house in Westchester, New York, where mutants could learn and train and be safe together.

"I think you belong there," Xavier said. "And I think you have the heart of a leader."

Scott packed his bag that night.

At the Xavier School for Gifted Youngsters, Scott met Jean Grey, who could move objects with her mind, and Bobby Drake, who turned hallways icy, and Warren Worthington, who had great feathered wings. They were strange and wonderful, every one.

Scott trained harder than all of them. He spent hours in the Danger Room — a special chamber that tested his aim — learning to send his optic blasts exactly where he chose. His gift was not just power. His gift was precision.

One morning, Professor Xavier called Scott to his quiet study.

"I am forming a team," Xavier said. "The X-Men. Young mutants who will protect people and show that our kind can be trusted. I would like you to lead them, Scott."

Scott was quiet for a long moment. He thought of his father's flight maps. He thought of Alex, somewhere far away. He thought of every night he'd spent in the orphanage, carrying a power he hadn't chosen, waiting for something to make sense.

He thought: this is what I was waiting for.

"Yes," he said.

He put on his ruby-quartz visor. He walked out to meet his team. And from that day on, the world knew him as Cyclops — the X-Man who aimed true, who led with steadiness and courage, and who never, not once, gave up.

And if you ever see a flash of red light cutting clean and straight across the sky, you'll know. Cyclops is on his way.

Sleep well. The bravest leaders are the ones who were brave long before anyone was watching.
