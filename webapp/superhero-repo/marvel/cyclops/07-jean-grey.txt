Jean Grey had been at the Xavier School almost as long as Scott. She was a telepath and telekinetic — she could read thoughts and move objects with her mind — and she was quick and laughing in a way that made the entire school feel lighter. Scott noticed her immediately. He also noticed that she was completely out of his league, which made him quiet around her for the first six months in a way that everyone found completely obvious.

Jean, who was a telepath, definitely noticed.

She started sitting near him at training sessions, asking questions about the scenarios he designed. Not because she needed help — Jean's instincts in the Danger Room were excellent — but because she understood that the person designing the exercise was working on a problem nobody else was thinking about. She asked good questions. Scott gave careful, thorough answers. Over time, the conversations grew longer and moved away from training entirely.

What Scott appreciated most was that Jean treated his discipline not as stiffness but as seriousness. Wolverine called him "Summers" and raised his eyebrows whenever Scott added a step to a plan. Hank McCoy called him meticulous and occasionally said it as a compliment. Jean called him exactly what he was: someone who cared enough to get things right. She understood the difference between caution and fear, between preparation and anxiety, and she never confused them.

In the field, they worked together with the kind of precision that comes only from genuine trust. Jean's telekinesis could hold a position Scott couldn't reach with a beam, and Scott's optic blasts could open angles Jean needed for a telekinetic lift. They trained combinations in the Danger Room for months until the timing was second nature — a shared rhythm, not a rehearsed sequence.

Scott kept a photograph of them on his desk at the Xavier School. It was taken one afternoon at the edge of the school's lake by Bobby Drake, who had tripped the shutter by accident and captured something real: Scott laughing — actually, completely laughing — at something Jean had said, the visor pushed up slightly in the way it went when his face moved unexpectedly. Jean was looking at him instead of the camera. It was not the most technically good photograph Bobby had ever taken. It was, Scott thought, the best thing in the room.
