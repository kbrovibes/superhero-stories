Once upon a time, Scott Summers made a mistake.

It was not a spectacular mistake. There was no enormous villain, no city under threat. It happened in the Danger Room on a Tuesday morning, during a training exercise that should have been straightforward, and it happened because Scott was trying to be in two places at once — which no one, not even the most precise field leader the X-Men have ever had, can actually do.

For a week, Scott had been tracking new signals. Mister Sinister had appeared again — in small ways: coded transmissions on old Essex Industries frequencies, a pattern Scott's careful mind had been assembling into something worrying. He ran scenarios in his head at breakfast. He ran them while walking the grounds. He ran them when he was supposed to be designing the week's Danger Room session.

He designed the session anyway. Quickly. From habit. Assigning positions based on how he'd done similar exercises before, not on who was best placed right now, not with the full attention he normally brought to every decision.

It was a bridge rescue: Jean, Ororo, Bobby, and Hank extracting simulated civilians from a collapsing structure. A standard exercise. Scott stepped into the chamber with most of his brain somewhere else.

The simulation began. CLICK. The bridge structure appeared around them.

Then the structure collapsed in the wrong sequence.

He had loaded the wrong parameters — a three-second difference in timing — but in an exercise, small errors have large consequences. Ororo found herself in a quadrant where the ceiling was too low for her weather effects. Bobby's ice bridge landed two meters short because the gap had shifted. Jean covered both gaps with her telekinesis. And then a third problem appeared from a corner Scott hadn't mapped.

SIMULATION FAILED, said the display above the floor.

Scott called the reset. The chamber went quiet and white. They were standing on the clean grey floor, all five of them, in the cool light of the empty room.

He thought about what he could say. He could call it a simulation error. He could move past it quickly and schedule a rematch for the afternoon.

He did none of those things.

He gathered them together and said, plainly and simply: "I've been distracted this week, and I set up that exercise without the attention it deserved. The failure was mine. I assigned positions without thinking carefully, and I loaded the wrong parameters, and the gaps that appeared were because I wasn't fully here." He paused. "Ororo, I put you in an impossible quadrant. I'm sorry. Bobby — your bridge was exactly right for the position I gave you; the position was wrong. I'm sorry." He looked at Jean. "You covered two problems that I created. Thank you. That was my job, not yours."

The room was very quiet.

Then Ororo Munroe said: "Thank you for saying so."

She said it simply, without drama. But what those five words contained was enormous: the acknowledgment that honesty, even when uncomfortable, is a gift. That a leader who tells the truth about their failures is more trustworthy than one who never admits to any.

Wolverine, standing at the edge of the group with his arms folded, slowly uncrossed his arms. He didn't say anything. But he uncrossed his arms. From Wolverine, that was a great deal.

Jean Grey said, "What do we know about the Sinister signals?"

And just like that, the whole team shifted — from the exercise to the real problem, together, without any of the awkwardness that lingers when things are unsaid. Scott shared everything he had been carrying in his head for a week, and Hank went to the technical analysis, and Ororo asked the strategic question that opened a new angle, and Bobby said something that turned out to be the key connection.

By lunchtime they had a theory. It was better than anything Scott had come up with alone.

Professor Xavier found him in the hallway afterward.

"That was well done," Xavier said.

Scott shook his head. "The exercise failing wasn't well done."

"No," Xavier agreed. "And yet you told the truth about why, and your team trusted you and worked with you. That is leading well. It's not about never making mistakes, Scott. It's about being honest about them when you do."

Scott looked at his hands. "I should have said something at the start. Before the exercise."

"Yes," Xavier said. "And now you know you will."

The next session ran perfectly. Not because Scott was flawless — he was not, he never would be — but because at the start he stood in front of his team and said, quietly: "I have been working a difficult problem this week and my focus has been divided. I want to name that before we start." And they adjusted. Together.

That was how it was supposed to work all along.

Sleep well. Even the steadiest, most capable people you know need to ask for help sometimes. And saying so is the first step toward getting it right.
