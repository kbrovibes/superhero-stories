Sam Wilson had a habit of collecting stray pilots. The first one had been a young Air Force lieutenant named Joaquin Torres, stationed at a small base in Tunisia, who had spent a month chasing a tip about the Flag Smashers when Sam first found him. Joaquin was sharp, funny, and could fly anything with a control stick — including a borrowed helicopter, which he had once landed on a runway with only one working rotor. Sam liked him from the first conversation. He liked him even more after Joaquin took a beating from a Flag Smasher and walked into a Tunisian hospital room asking, calmly, "So when are we going back out?"

When Sam became Captain America, he kept Joaquin close. They built a working partnership the same way Sam had built one with Bucky — slowly, with arguments, with shared takeout containers, with Joaquin learning to read Sam's silences before they became orders. Sam took Joaquin to Wakanda and introduced him to the Royal Engineering Corps. He took him to Delacroix to meet Sarah. He took him into the air, again and again, and showed him exactly how the new vibranium wings caught a crosswind. Joaquin was a good student, and Sam was a patient teacher.

The day came, finally, when Sam handed the wings over. Sam was Captain America now. The shield needed both his hands. Joaquin had been training in his own pair of Wakandan-tech wings for almost a year, and they fit him as if they had been built for him — because, in fact, they had been. The new suit was sleeker than Sam's old one. The chest plate was deep green and gold. There was a small Air Force pin tucked into the collar, because Sam knew Joaquin would not feel right flying without it. Joaquin pulled the helmet on for the first time as the official Falcon, looked at himself in a polished hangar wall, and said, very quietly, "Oh, this is gonna be a problem."

It was, in fact, a problem — but only for the people Joaquin Torres was now chasing through the sky. He took to the role the way Sam had once taken to it, with skill and humor and a complete inability to leave a friend behind. He worked alongside Sam as a partner now, not a student. Sam flew with the shield on his arm; Joaquin flew with the Redwing drone at his shoulder. The two of them moved through the sky together over Washington and Wakanda and Delacroix, and Sam Wilson, watching his young friend bank into a clean dive at three hundred miles an hour, finally understood what Steve must have felt the day he passed the shield to him. Some gifts, given at the right time, fly even higher than they did before.
