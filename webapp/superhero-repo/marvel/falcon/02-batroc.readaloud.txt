Before Sam Wilson could deal with the larger problem he was chasing, he had to handle a very theatrical smaller one. A French mercenary named Georges Batroc had taken a hostage onto a turboprop plane flying at twenty thousand feet above the desert. Batroc was famous for his fighting style — a French form of kickboxing called savate — and for having once gone toe-to-toe with Captain America on the deck of a cargo ship. He had lost that fight. He had not forgotten it.

Now he was airborne above the border between Libya and Tunisia, and Sam Wilson was coming for him.

Sam's new wings were a slimmer Wakandan design, faster and more agile than the old EXO-7. He banked between the plane's engines and the desert cliffs below, moving so tightly that the air shook behind him. Batroc's men jumped out on parachutes and tried to shoot him in mid-flight. Sam dropped his Redwing drone — a small mechanical bird of prey — to handle the jumpers, while he himself dove into the open back of the plane.

He found the hostage. He grabbed them. And then he jumped out into the desert sky with only one set of wings between the two of them.

Sam pulled up six feet above the sand dunes. The ground exploded around them in a great cloud. The hostage — a Tunisian intelligence officer — looked at him as if he had just been pulled out of a dream.

And do you know what Sam felt in that moment? Not like Captain America. Not yet. He felt like himself: a pararescue specialist from Harlem who always flew toward the trouble.

From that day on, he knew that was exactly enough.
