After the Flag Smashers had been stopped and the world's cameras had finally moved on, Sam Wilson and Bucky Barnes still had a long ride ahead of them. They drove south together, in a borrowed pickup truck loaded with tools, all the way from a New York rooftop down to a fishing dock in Delacroix, Louisiana. Sam drove with one elbow out the window. Bucky sat in the passenger seat with his vibranium arm resting on the door and a baseball cap pulled low over his eyes. They did not talk for the first three hundred miles. They did not need to. They were finally a team.

Sarah Wilson met them on the porch of the Wilson family home with her two boys on either side of her. She looked at the rusty pickup. She looked at Bucky. She looked at Sam. Then she handed Bucky a glass of sweet tea and said, "There's a list on the fridge. Start at the top." Bucky read the list. The top item was a leaky bilge pump. The second item was a broken winch. The third item was a kitchen door that did not close. Bucky nodded once, gravely, and got to work like a man who had finally been told what to do with his hands.

The next three weeks were quiet in a way that Bucky Barnes had not been quiet in seventy years. He scraped barnacles off the hull of the Paul and Darlene with Sam's nephew Cass leaning over his shoulder asking endless questions about his metal arm. He sat at the dinner table and ate gumbo and corn bread. He helped Sarah re-hang a screen door. He walked down to the bayou at sunset with Sam and watched the herons. He did not check his phone. He did not write any new names in his notebook. He just was there, in his body, in that town, in that family, for the first time in his entire long life.

On the last morning, Bucky carried his bag out to the porch and shook each of the boys' hands like they were grown men. He looked at Sarah. He could not find the words. Sarah, who had raised two boys mostly alone and could read a man's face like a weather report, just hugged him. "Come back whenever," she said. "There's always a list." Bucky climbed into the pickup. Sam slid in beside him. As they rolled out of Delacroix together with the Mississippi Delta sliding past the windows, Sam glanced over and saw Bucky asleep against the door, finally, peacefully, for the first time in years.
