Here is a question that takes a while to answer: what happens when someone is RIGHT about a problem but WRONG about how to fix it?

That's what Sam Wilson had to figure out when he met Karli Morgenthau and the Flag Smashers.

When Thanos was defeated and the snap was reversed, half the world came back — but the world had changed in five years! New people lived in old apartments. Countries had moved their borders. People who lost everything had spent five years rebuilding new lives — and overnight, those new lives got swept away.

Karli Morgenthau had seen all of it. She'd grown up in a global support shelter during those five years, watching refugees build real communities. Real homes. Real belonging. Then watched it all vanish. So Karli and her friends took a powerful serum — as strong as the Super Soldier Serum — and formed the Flag Smashers. They wanted a single world with no borders at all. And they would fight ANYONE who stood in their way.

Sam met Karli in a warehouse in Munich. She was fast, super-strong, and absolutely certain she was right. She stopped before she finished the fight and looked at Sam hard. She asked him: do you even understand what it feels like to be invisible to the people in power?

Sam looked right back at her. "I understand that better than you know," he said quietly. Then: "How does hurting people fix it?"

Karli didn't have a good answer. And the look on her face told Sam she'd been thinking about that question too.

The confrontation built across countries and weeks until Sam put on a brand-new suit that Shuri in Wakanda had built for him — red, white, and blue, with vibranium wings — FWOOOOSH! — and found Karli on a New York rooftop. He didn't throw the shield at her. He TALKED to her. Told her she was right about the problem. Told her violence was the wrong door.

After everything was over, Sam stood before the cameras and named Karli — not as a villain, but as a young person the world had failed. Over and over again.

And that is how Sam Wilson carried the shield the way it was always meant to be carried: for everyone, especially the ones nobody else was fighting for.
