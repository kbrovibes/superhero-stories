Okay, picture this. Sam Wilson and Bucky Barnes — two guys who used to argue about EVERYTHING — climbing into a beat-up pickup truck together, loaded down with tools, and driving fifteen hundred miles south. From New York City all the way down to Delacroix, Louisiana. VROOOOOM!

What were they driving toward? A fishing boat called the Paul and Darlene. A family. A chore list on a fridge.

But first, the drive. Sam was at the wheel with his elbow out the window. Bucky sat in the passenger seat with his vibranium arm resting on the door and a baseball cap pulled low over his eyes. And do you know how long they went without speaking?

THREE HUNDRED MILES.

Not because they were mad. Because for the very first time, they didn't NEED to say anything. That's what real partners feel like. The quiet is easy.

When they pulled up to the Wilson house, Sam's sister Sarah was waiting on the porch with her two boys, Cass and AJ. Sarah took one look at the rusty pickup. She looked at Bucky. She looked at Sam. Then she did something amazing — she handed Bucky a glass of sweet tea and pointed at the chore list on the fridge.

"There's a list," Sarah said. "Start at the top."

Bucky walked over and read it. Top of the list: a leaky bilge pump on the family fishing boat. Bucky — a man who had survived seventy years of war and loss — nodded VERY seriously, like he had just been given a sacred mission. Then he picked up a wrench and got to work.

For three whole weeks, Bucky stayed in Louisiana. He did all kinds of things he had never really done before.

He scraped barnacles off the boat. SCRAPE SCRAPE SCRAPE!

Cass — Sam's nephew — followed him around all day asking questions. "Is your arm heavy?" SCRAPE. "Can you feel it?" SCRAPE. "Can you pick up a truck?" Bucky answered every single question. Every one!

He sat at the dinner table and ate gumbo with the family. SLURP! He ate corn bread. CRUMB CRUMB. He helped Sarah re-hang the screen door that had been broken for three years. CREAK CLICK CLANG!

He walked down to the bayou at sunset with Sam and watched the herons fly overhead. WHOOSH. Big slow wings on a pink sky.

And do you know what he didn't do?

He didn't check his phone. He didn't write any new names in his notebook of regrets. He didn't have nightmares. He just was THERE. Really there. In his body. In that family. For the first time in seventy years.

On the very last morning, Bucky came out to the porch with his bag. He shook each of the boys' hands like they were grown men. Then he turned to Sarah Wilson, and he tried to say something — anything — and the words just wouldn't come.

Sarah, who had raised two boys mostly by herself and could read a man's face like a weather report, just hugged him.

"Come back whenever," she said. "There's always a list."

Bucky climbed into the pickup. Sam slid in next to him. They rolled out of Delacroix together with the Mississippi Delta sliding past the windows.

After a while, Sam glanced over.

Bucky was ASLEEP.

Head against the door. Cap over his eyes. Sound asleep. For the first time in years.

Sam smiled and kept driving. Sometimes the most heroic thing two friends can do is just let each other rest.
