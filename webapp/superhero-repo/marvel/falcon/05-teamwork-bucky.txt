Sam Wilson and Bucky Barnes did not like each other. They tolerated each other for Steve’s sake, which is not the same thing. Sam had read Bucky’s file as the Winter Soldier and remembered every name on the list of people Bucky had hurt while HYDRA was controlling his mind. Bucky had read Sam’s file as the Falcon and decided he was a smiling Air Force kid who liked his wings too much. The two of them spent the first year of their unlikely partnership rolling their eyes at each other across whatever quinjet, sedan, or motorbike they were trapped on.

Their first real mission together after Steve was gone took them across three countries chasing the Flag Smashers. They sat side by side in a cargo plane and argued for an hour about whether Bucky had to apologize to the families of the people the Winter Soldier had hurt. Sam said he did. Bucky said it would not undo anything. Sam said that was not the point. Bucky stared out the open cargo door for a long time and then admitted, quietly, that he had not slept a full night in three years. Sam did not say anything to that. He just slid a notebook across the bench so Bucky could write the names down properly.

The closer they got to Karli Morgenthau, the more they trusted each other. Bucky, in his quiet way, became the one who could stop Sam from second-guessing himself in front of a mirror. Sam, in his open way, became the one who could pull Bucky out of his memories before he stayed stuck inside them. In a courtyard in Sokovia, the two of them sat across from Helmut Zemo, the man who had once broken the Avengers in half, and asked him for help. Zemo gave it to them. They walked out of his castle with new information and the same wary friendship.

By the time the final battle ended in New York, the two of them had a single, unspoken rhythm. Bucky covered the ground. Sam covered the sky. Bucky threw the shield. Sam caught the shield. They finished each other’s plans without speaking, and they argued through every meal they shared. At the end of it all, Bucky walked into Sam’s sister Sarah’s yard in Louisiana, helped her fix her boat, and accepted a plate of food from Sam’s nephews. He did not say thank you. He just sat at the table and let himself, for one quiet afternoon, be a person again. Sam looked at him over the rim of his glass and finally, finally, smiled.
