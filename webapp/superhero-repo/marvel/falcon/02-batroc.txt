After the snap was reversed and half the world came home from the dust, Sam Wilson took on a mission with a long, complicated name: stop a group of enhanced anti-government fighters called the Flag Smashers from destroying every border in the world. The group’s leader was a young woman named Karli Morgenthau, and she was clever, fast, and dangerously certain that she was right. But before Sam ever met Karli, he had to deal with a far more theatrical problem — a French mercenary named Georges Batroc.

Batroc was the kind of man who introduced himself in three languages and then kicked you in the face. He fought with savate, an old French style of kickboxing, and he had once fought Captain America hand-to-hand on the deck of a S.H.I.E.L.D. cargo ship. He had lost that fight, but he had remembered it. Now Batroc had been hired by a stranger to deliver a hostage across the border between Libya and Tunisia, and he was airborne in a turboprop plane when Sam Wilson found him at twenty thousand feet.

The chase was the most thrilling thing Sam had ever flown. His new wings, a slimmer Wakandan-tech design, banked tightly between the plane’s engines and the cliffs of the desert below. Batroc’s men jumped out of the plane on parachutes and tried to shoot Sam in mid-air. Sam dropped his Redwing drone behind him to handle the jumpers, while he himself dove into the open back of the plane, freed the hostage, and tossed both of them out into the sky on a single set of wings. He pulled up six feet above the dunes. Sand sprayed twenty meters in every direction. The hostage, a Tunisian intelligence officer, looked at him as if he had just been pulled out of a dream.

Batroc lived to be a problem again, but Sam had proved something to himself that day. He had spent months unsure whether he could be Captain America without the shield, and even more unsure whether he wanted to be. Batroc’s aerial heist reminded him of the thing he had always been first — a pararescue specialist who got people home. Whether the shield ended up on his arm or not, Sam Wilson would always be a man who flew toward the trouble, not away from it. He landed on the desert floor and started walking the hostage to safety, the Redwing drone circling overhead like a small mechanical bird of prey.
