Here is something nobody tells you about wearing a Captain America shield: you cannot also fly the wings.

The shield needs both hands. It is round, it is heavy, it is vibranium, and when it spins back to you across a battlefield you have to be ready to catch it. The wings are different. The wings need both hands free for the controls and both arms unburdened for the turbulence. You can be Falcon, or you can be Captain America, but you cannot, in any single afternoon, be both.

When Sam Wilson finally accepted the shield, he had to make a decision. Someone, somewhere, was going to have to take the wings.

That someone turned out to be a young Air Force lieutenant named Joaquin Torres.

Sam had met Joaquin years earlier, on a dusty base in Tunisia. Joaquin was twenty-five years old, sharp, funny, and a little bit reckless. He had been stationed at the base for a year. He had been chasing tips about the Flag Smashers entirely on his own time for a month. He had a wall full of news clippings and a notebook full of theories, and when Sam first walked into his office to talk to him, Joaquin pushed a folder across the desk and said, "I think I know where they'll be tomorrow."

He was right.

Joaquin could fly anything. Anything. He had been an Air Force pilot since he was twenty, and there was a story — possibly exaggerated, possibly not — that he had once landed a damaged helicopter on a Tunisian runway with only one working rotor. When asked about it later, Joaquin smiled in the way he always smiled and said, "I had options."

The day after Sam met him, Joaquin got into a fight with a Flag Smasher and lost. Badly. He woke up in a Tunisian hospital with a sore jaw and two cracked ribs. Sam went to visit him, expecting to find a sad, bandaged lieutenant.

Instead, Joaquin was sitting up in bed, eating crackers, and grinning.

"So," Joaquin said, "when are we going back out?"

Sam knew, in that exact moment, that this kid was going to be a problem. The good kind.

When Sam became Captain America, he kept Joaquin close. They built a partnership slowly, the way Sam built every partnership that mattered — with arguments, with shared takeout containers on long flights, with Joaquin gradually learning to read Sam's silences before they became orders, with Sam gradually learning to trust that Joaquin's instincts were as good as his own.

Sam introduced Joaquin to Bucky, who narrowed his eyes at the kid for exactly three seconds and then decided he was acceptable. Sam took Joaquin to Wakanda — yes, Wakanda — and Joaquin's eyes were as wide as dinner plates the entire trip. The Royal Engineering Corps met him, measured him, and built him a custom pair of Wakandan-tech wings — sleeker than Sam's old EXO-7, lighter than Sam's first vibranium pair, with controls calibrated to Joaquin's exact wingspan and reaction speed.

Sam took Joaquin to Delacroix, Louisiana, to meet Sarah Wilson and her two boys. Cass, who had spent three weeks pestering Bucky Barnes with questions about vibranium arms, now had a fresh victim to interrogate. Joaquin answered every single question Cass had about jet propulsion, wing aerodynamics, drone control, and what the inside of a fighter plane smelled like. (Hot metal and old coffee, Joaquin said. Cass wrote it down.)

For almost a year, Sam trained Joaquin in those new wings. He taught him how the vibranium caught a crosswind. He taught him how to use the Redwing drone — Sam's little mechanical companion — as a partner in the air. He taught him how to hold a hostage steady on a single set of wings during a dive. He taught him how to land softly enough that the people you were rescuing did not stagger sideways when you set them down.

Sam was a patient teacher. Joaquin was a fast learner.

And then, one bright morning at a hangar in Washington, D.C., the day finally came.

Sam stood in front of Joaquin with the new suit laid out on a workbench. It was a beautiful piece of work. The chest plate was deep green and gold — Joaquin's colors, not Sam's. The wings folded neatly into the back. At the collar, in a tiny gold clip, there was an Air Force pin. Sam had put it there himself. He had known, without asking, that Joaquin would not feel right flying without it.

"They're yours," Sam said simply.

Joaquin stared at the suit for a long time. He did not say anything. Then he picked it up, like a person picks up something they have been waiting for their whole life. He went into a side room. A few minutes later he came back out, fully suited, the helmet under one arm.

He walked over to a polished hangar wall and looked at his reflection.

He pulled the helmet on. He stood up a little straighter.

Then he said, very quietly: "Oh, this is gonna be a problem."

Sam laughed — really laughed, the kind of laugh that came from deep in his chest. He clapped Joaquin on the shoulder. He nodded toward the open hangar doors.

"Let's go fly," Sam said.

And they did.

From that day on, Sam and Joaquin flew together as partners. Not as teacher and student. Not as mentor and apprentice. Two heroes, side by side, with very different gear and very different jobs and the same exact reason for showing up. Sam flew with the shield on his arm — heavy, gleaming, ready. Joaquin flew with the Redwing drone darting at his shoulder, the way it had once darted at Sam's. The two of them moved through the sky together over Washington and Wakanda and Delacroix and a dozen other places, and the world got used to seeing them as a pair.

One afternoon, over the Mississippi Delta, Joaquin banked into a clean three-hundred-mile-an-hour dive to test a new wing configuration. He came out of it smoothly, leveled off ten feet above the water, scared a flock of pelicans into the sky, and pulled up laughing.

Sam, watching from a thousand feet above, felt something he had not quite felt before. It was something close to what Steve must have felt the day Steve handed Sam the shield by the lake.

Pride, yes. But more than pride. Wonder.

Because here is what Sam realized, in that exact moment, with the river shining below and Joaquin Torres turning a victory loop over the cypress trees:

Some gifts, given at the right time, to the right person, fly even higher than they did before.

That is the story of Joaquin Torres. The new Falcon. The one who took the wings and made them his own. Watch the sky for him. He'll be the green-and-gold streak with a grin behind the visor, banking into a dive nobody else would dare try, with a Redwing drone darting at his shoulder and an old Air Force pin glinting at his collar.

He's a problem.

The good kind.
