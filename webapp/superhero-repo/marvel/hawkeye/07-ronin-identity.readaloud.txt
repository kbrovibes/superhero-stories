There is a chapter of Clint Barton's story that he does not like to talk about. But it matters, because it is the chapter that nearly swallowed him whole.

When Thanos snapped his fingers and half of all life vanished, Clint was standing in a field with his family. And then they were gone. Just gone. His wife. His children. In a single, silent moment, everything he had built his life around disappeared.

Clint put down his bow. He picked up a blade. He put on a mask and dark armor and called himself Ronin. He traveled the world — Tokyo, cartel hideouts, cities draped in shadows — going after criminal organizations that had survived while good people had not. He was not looking for justice. He was looking for somewhere to put his grief.

For five years, the name Ronin meant something cold and frightening in the underworld. But Clint was not better for it. He was hollowed out.

And then Natasha came. His oldest friend. She had tracked him all the way to Tokyo, and she stood in front of him and told him there might be a way to bring everyone back.

He almost didn't believe her. But something in him remembered who he used to be. He put the blade down. He came home.

When everything was restored, the Ronin mask went back into the dark. But Clint carried the memory of those five years with him — not as shame to hide, but as something honest that reminded him why the light is worth fighting for.

From that day on, Hawkeye returned. And he fought harder than ever, because now he knew exactly what he had to lose.
