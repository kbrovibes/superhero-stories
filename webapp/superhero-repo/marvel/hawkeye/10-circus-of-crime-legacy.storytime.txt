There is a smell that Clint Barton carries in his memory that no other Avenger has: the smell of a circus.

Sawdust and lanolin and canvas and the particular sweetness of spun sugar and the cold air that comes in under the tent walls in the evening. Distant music from a tinny speaker. The sound of animal sounds from the menagerie. The roar of a crowd that appears every night and disappears again, leaving the performers in the sudden quiet of a field that belongs to no city and no time in particular.

He grew up in that smell. He became who he is inside it.

The Circus of Crime, when Hawkeye first encountered them, was a shock of the specific kind that only nostalgia can produce — the shock of something familiar wearing the wrong face. A traveling show that performed genuine acts of circus craft — acrobatics, strength feats, the particular theater of spectacle that circuses had perfected over two centuries — and used the performance as cover for something else entirely.

The Ringmaster sat at the center of it. A man named Maynard Tiboldt, who had found that crowds gathered for entertainment were crowds whose attention could be controlled, and that a crowd whose attention was controlled was a crowd that could be robbed at scale. His primary tool was his hat: a remarkable device with a hypnotic function that worked on anyone looking at it, which, during a circus act, was everyone. The audience sat in their seats, pleasantly dazzled, and then the Great Gambonnos moved through the aisles — twin acrobats, identical in their red-and-gold costumes, extraordinary in their aerial work — and Princess Python and the other performers managed the logistics of relieving several hundred people of their valuables simultaneously.

It was, Clint would reluctantly admit, extremely well organized.

He encountered them multiple times across his career. Each time, the Ringmaster arrived with some new elaboration on the basic scheme — a different city, a different cover story, new members who brought new capabilities. Each time, the fundamental approach was the same: distraction, misdirection, the confident use of people's willingness to look where the performer directs their gaze.

Fighting the Circus of Crime was uniquely complicated because Clint knew what he was looking at. He knew the rhythm of a performance. He knew where the setups were, what the misdirection was trying to pull attention away from, which acts were genuine and which were positioning. This knowledge gave him significant advantages in predicting their moves.

But it also cost him something. Each confrontation was a trip down a road he had mostly made peace with, and mostly is not completely. The smell of sawdust, in a confrontation with the Ringmaster, carried everything: the boy who had run away to find something, the mentor who had turned out to be the wrong kind of complicated, the years of learning to be the person who steps up when things are wrong even when stepping up is expensive.

He always dismantled the scheme. He always found the point in the performance where the real work was happening and interrupted it. The hypnotic hat was, on balance, a solved problem once you knew about it — you simply didn't look at it, which was easier for someone who had learned from circus performers how to not look at the thing they wanted you to look at.

They came back, the Circus of Crime. New members, new plans, the same fundamental confidence that people could be made to look the wrong direction. Hawkeye was always there to look the right one.

The wonder of circus performance — the human body doing things it should not be able to do, the coordination and skill and training that produced spectacle from years of unglamorous daily practice — this was real and he honored it. The Ringmaster had taken something genuinely extraordinary and made it a tool for theft.

Clint had taken the same background and made it into Hawkeye.

Heroism begins in unexpected places. It is not the beginning that determines the outcome.

It is the choice you make, every day, about what to build with where you've been.
