Among all the performers in the circus, there was one who spotted something special in Clint Barton the very first day. His name was Trick Shot, and he was the finest archer anyone had ever seen. Targets blindfolded. Arrows fired backward over his shoulder. Shots from impossible distances in rain and wind. The crowd went wild every night.

Trick Shot saw that Clint had that same gift — raw, unpolished, like a diamond hidden in rough stone. He took the boy under his wing and taught him everything. How to read the air. How to breathe before the release. How to feel the arrow in flight before it even leaves the string. His training was harsh. He pushed Clint to positions that seemed ridiculous and distances that seemed impossible. But Clint learned.

The trouble was, Trick Shot used his brilliance for robbery and worse. And as Clint grew and earned respect with the Avengers, Trick Shot grew bitter. He had made Clint into what he was — and now that student had turned his back on the life they shared.

They faced each other more than once. Each encounter was painful in its own way. Trick Shot fired with cold resentment. Clint fired with a heart full of something more complicated — gratitude for what he'd been given and sorrow for how it had been used.

What Clint kept was the discipline, the technique, the extraordinary precision that Trick Shot poured into him. What he left behind was the selfishness and the crime.

From that day on, every arrow Hawkeye fired — every impossible shot that saved a life — was Trick Shot's teaching turned toward the light.
