Clint Barton had dealt with alien armies. He had stood on the roof of a building in New York City while things fell from the sky. He had fought alongside the most powerful collection of beings ever assembled on Earth, against enemies whose names took several words to describe.

The Tracksuit Mafia, he would admit, was not in that category.

They called themselves Bro. A lot. They called each other Bro. They called Clint Bro, despite being his adversaries. Their tracksuits came in a variety of colors, all of them enthusiastic. Their willingness to take a hit and keep coming was, if not precisely impressive, at least persistent. He would give them that.

The conflict began at an auction. Someone had gotten hold of the Ronin suit — the dark armor from Clint's five years of bad decisions — and was selling it to the highest bidder. Clint went to the auction in civilian clothes with the very specific intention of disappearing before anyone made the connection between the suit and him. He was unsuccessful. Kate Bishop, who had arrived at the same auction for her own set of complicated reasons, was also unsuccessful at disappearing, and the evening turned into the kind of evening where you find yourself running from a large group of men in colorful athletic wear through the streets of New York City.

He didn't have his hearing aids in during the auction. This was a problem. Clint Barton's hearing had taken significant damage over years of working in conditions where large sounds happened frequently. He wore hearing aids that made ordinary function possible. Without them, the world went muffled and directional in the specific way that hearing loss creates. He managed this. He always had. But the chaos of that particular evening was harder to manage without full situational audio.

He and Kate — who had done quite well in a fight she hadn't been prepared for, which he noted — ended up in her apartment, then his. The Tracksuits kept finding them, which suggested a level of coordination backed by resources that exceeded the obvious.

Maya Lopez led them. She was fierce and deeply motivated, and her connection to what Clint had done during his Ronin years was specific and personal in a way that he couldn't dismiss or fight around. She was also Deaf — a detail Clint only learned partway through the conflict, and one that landed differently than most information, for reasons he didn't immediately articulate.

The conflict built toward Rockefeller Center at Christmas: all the lights and the crowd and the rink, and two archers working in tight partnership against opponents who kept coming. Kate's arrows. Clint's arrows. Their timing developing through the pressure of real engagement in the way timing can only develop — imperfect and then better and then very good, very fast.

Lucky found them somewhere in the middle of all of this. A one-eyed golden dog of medium age and enormous goodwill, operating without any clear organizational affiliation, who looked at Clint and Kate and decided they were his people. This was, Clint thought, good judgment on the dog's part.

The Tracksuit conflict pulled in complications that went well beyond tracksuit range. Yelena Belova, one of the most capable operatives alive, arrived with an assignment regarding Clint that was based on information she had been given and that was incorrect, and the confrontation between two people who both knew Natasha Romanoff and were grieving her differently was not simple.

Beyond Yelena: the Kingpin. The name behind the organization, the architecture of influence that made the Tracksuits more than neighborhood trouble. Wilson Fisk — enormous, controlled, absolutely certain of his own authority in the spaces where he operated.

Every level of this conflict forced Clint to confront the consequences of his Ronin years in real, specific, unavoidable terms. Not as abstract guilt but as a chain of actual harm that had moved through the world and kept moving.

He dealt with each link he could reach. He helped where helping was honest. He chose peace over violence when the choice was available.

Street-level heroism — protecting neighborhoods, ordinary people, the communities where ordinary life happened — was the same work as saving the world. It had the same values underneath. It deserved the same seriousness.

He'd learned that from a rooftop in New York, a long time ago.

He hadn't forgotten it.
