When a great battle begins, Thor calls lightning from the sky and the Hulk leaps into the middle of everything with a tremendous roar. And somewhere above it all, standing on a rooftop or a fire escape or the wing of a jet, Clint Barton is watching. Counting. Calculating.

He sees the robot moving to flank Captain America before Captain America knows it is there. He sees the sniper on a distant building lining up a shot at someone below. He sees exactly which cable, if cut, will bring the whole enemy formation tumbling down. And then, very quietly, he draws his bow.

Being the Avengers' marksman is not the loudest job. But it might be the most important. Clint protects the people on his team who cannot protect themselves in the middle of a firefight. He times his shots so precisely that he can buy his teammates a crucial extra second — just enough to get up, to turn, to survive.

He has no superpowers. Every shot has to be perfect. There is no margin for error, and Clint knows it, and so he never stops practicing. He never stops caring.

He is also the one who asks how everyone is doing when the battle is over. Who remembers birthdays. Who talks about family. In a group of extraordinary beings, Clint Barton is the most human, and that is his greatest strength. He reminds the Avengers what they are actually fighting for: ordinary people, going about ordinary lives, safe because Hawkeye was watching from the rooftop.

From that day on, the Avengers knew: the archer who never missed was also the heartbeat who kept them all together.
