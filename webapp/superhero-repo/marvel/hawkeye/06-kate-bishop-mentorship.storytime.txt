Kate Bishop had been watching Hawkeye for years before she met him.

Not in the way that sounds — she wasn't haunting rooftops or following his missions. She had watched the way anyone watches a person who changes them: from a distance, the way a news broadcast reaches through a screen, the way a moment of witnessed courage plants something in a person that takes years to fully grow. She had seen Hawkeye once, in action, during the Battle of New York — fighting without powers, without armor, with a bow and what seemed like pure determination against impossible odds — and something in her had decided that this was possible. That being this was possible.

She took up archery the next week.

By the time they actually met — through a series of events involving a criminal auction, the Ronin suit, and the specific bad luck of being the wrong person at the wrong moment in the wrong New York City building — Kate Bishop had spent years becoming extremely good. Better than extremely good. She was the kind of archer who turned heads at tournaments, who had gotten quiet, professional evaluations from instructors that were difficult to reconcile with her age, who could look at a problem and identify the arrow-shaped solution faster than most people could identify the problem.

Clint recognized her immediately. Not her face — he hadn't seen her before. Her talent. The way she moved, the set of her feet, the draw that came from years rather than months of practice. He recognized it because he had been it, a long time ago, in a circus tent when a mentor saw something in a young boy and decided to say so.

He also recognized that she had no idea what she had walked into.

Teaching her was not straightforward. It never is, with the genuinely talented ones — they resist the slower pace that fundamentals require, they want to test themselves before the tests are appropriate, they push toward the hardest applications before the foundations are fully set. Kate pushed. Clint pushed back. Their arguments about this were specific and frequent and ultimately productive, because Kate listened, eventually, to the parts that were actually instruction rather than the parts that were Clint being cautious.

He taught her things he had learned the hard way. About the physical toll of this work — the shoulders, the hands, the cumulative wear of years of high-draw weight bows. About the responsibility that comes with skill: not just the skill itself but the decisions about when and how to apply it. About what he called the boring parts of being a hero, by which he meant the days when nothing happened but everything required vigilance, and vigilance was its own exhausting discipline.

He also told her about Ronin. Not easily, and not all at once, but truthfully. He had promised himself he would be honest with her, because he wanted her to be a better person than he had been at his worst — not a better archer, which she might already be or would soon be, but a better person. The Ronin chapter was not something he was proud of. He told her anyway.

She absorbed it. She asked questions that were uncomfortably good. She made him think through things he had been carrying in a corner he didn't often examine.

In their work together against the Tracksuit Mafia — whose name inspired a comedy of reactions from both of them, though the threat itself was entirely real — they synchronized in ways that surprised him. She moved differently than he did, her intuitions shaped by different training, and those different intuitions found angles he missed. He found angles she missed. Together the coverage was nearly complete.

He saw his best qualities in her. He also saw potential that might one day exceed his own, and this gave him uncomplicated satisfaction rather than the complicated feeling it might have given a different person.

The Hawkeye identity, he understood, was never just a name for himself. It was a symbol for what the work meant: skill, heart, and standing up for ordinary people.

He passed it to someone worthy.

That is the greatest act of heroism available to a person who has gotten older. Not fighting the last battle. Ensuring that the values you fought for will be carried forward.

By someone who will carry them well.
