Every family has secrets. Some are small, like who finished the last cookie. And some are big — so big that the people keeping them think they are protecting everyone. This is a story about a big secret. The biggest secret in Loki's whole life.

Loki was a grown-up prince by now. He was tall and lean, dressed in green and gold, with a silver tongue that could talk anyone in or out of trouble. His brother Thor was loud and golden, the favored son, the one Odin had clearly chosen to be the next king of Asgard. Loki had spent his whole life slightly in Thor's shadow, and he had grown skilled at hiding how much that hurt.

One day, the two brothers were walking through the vault of Asgard — the great underground room where Odin kept treasures and trophies from the wars of long ago. The vault was full of glittering objects. The Tablet of Life and Time. The Eternal Flame. The Casket of Ancient Winters.

The Casket. They had known it since they were small. It was a smooth blue box, glowing faintly from within, ice-cold to anyone who got too close. Frigga had told them stories about it. Long ago, the Asgardians had taken it from the Frost Giants of Jotunheim, ending a terrible war. The Casket held the heart of winter itself.

This time, alone in the vault for just a moment, Loki reached out his hand.

He had always wondered.

He laid his palm flat against the cold blue surface.

And nothing hurt.

That was the first strange thing.

The second strange thing was happening to his skin. Where his hand touched the Casket, his skin was turning blue. A deep, beautiful blue, like the sky just after sunset. Small raised ridges appeared on the back of his hand. He looked down at himself in the polished mirror-floor and saw, for the first time, that his eyes were glowing red.

Loki stood very still.

He had grown up listening to stories about Frost Giants. Big, monstrous, scary creatures from the cold realm of Jotunheim. Asgardian children were told to behave or the Frost Giants would come. And here he was, in the middle of the vault, with his hand on the Casket — and he was one of them.

He pulled his hand away. Slowly, slowly, the blue faded. His skin turned pink again. His eyes returned to their familiar green. But Loki knew. He could not un-know.

He ran.

He ran through the long marble corridors of Asgard, his cape streaming behind him, his heart pounding so loud he could barely hear his own boots. He ran all the way to the throne room, where his father Odin sat. Odin was old by then. Very old. He was about to fall into the Odinsleep — the deep magical rest that sometimes overtook the Allfather. His one good eye was already drooping.

Loki shook him gently and asked the question.

"Am I a Frost Giant? Tell me. The truth."

And Odin, with the heavy kindness of a father who has been hiding something for too long, told him the story.

A long time ago, there had been a great battle on the freezing realm of Jotunheim. The Frost Giants were ruled by their king, Laufey, and they had grown bold and dangerous. The Asgardians had marched in to stop them, and they had won. The Casket of Ancient Winters had been carried home as a trophy.

After the battle, walking through a quiet, abandoned temple in the snow, Odin had heard a small sound. Crying. A baby's cry.

The baby was tucked into a corner, wrapped in dark cloth. He was so small — so much smaller than other Frost Giant babies that Odin guessed his own family had left him behind, thinking he was too weak to ever become a warrior. The baby's skin was blue. His eyes glowed red.

Odin had picked him up.

The baby's skin had slowly turned pink in Odin's warm hands. His eyes had darkened from red to green. The magic of Odin's touch had softened the Jotun blood, making the child look, for all the world, like an Asgardian baby.

Odin had carried him home. He had given him a name. He had raised him as a prince.

That baby was Loki.

Loki sat down on the steps of the throne. The marble was cold against the back of his legs. He felt like all the stories of his whole life had just been rewritten. The brave second prince of Asgard? Born to a Frost Giant king. The boy who had been told scary tales about Jotunheim? Was, himself, Jotun.

"So I was a tool," he said, very quietly. "A bargaining chip. Something you could use later."

"No," Odin said. "You were a baby. You needed a family. I took you because of that."

But Loki could not hear it. Not that day. The hurt was too sharp, and the surprise was too big, and the words were the wrong shape to fit into the heart-broken cracks of him. He stood up and he left the throne room without bowing.

He went down the long corridor to his old bedroom. He shut the door. He sat on the floor with his back against the bed.

And his mother Frigga came in.

She had heard. Word travels fast in a royal house. She did not knock. She did not say anything at first. She just sat down next to him on the floor and put her arm around him, exactly the way she had when he was small and scared of a thunderstorm.

"You are my son," she said.

That was all. Just those four words.

Loki did not believe them. Not right then. The grown-up Loki, the clever one, the suspicious one, the one who had just learned that his whole life had a secret folded inside it — that Loki could not believe a hug could be that simple.

But somewhere deep underneath, in the much smaller part of him that had been a baby once, a baby who needed a family — somewhere down there, the words landed.

He did not know it yet, but he had just been given the most important gift of his life. Not the throne. Not the kingdom. Not the magic.

A mother who said, "You are mine," when she could just as easily have said, "You are theirs."

It would take Loki many, many years — and many adventures, and a lot of mistakes — to understand what Frigga had given him in that small moment on the floor of his bedroom.

But the heart of Loki, blue or pink, Frost Giant or Asgardian, was still beating.

And that, for now, was enough.
