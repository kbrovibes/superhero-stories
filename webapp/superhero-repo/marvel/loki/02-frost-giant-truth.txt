It happened on a day that seemed, at first, completely ordinary. Loki was a grown prince by then, walking through the vault of Asgard with his brother Thor. There among the trophies of old wars stood the Casket of Ancient Winters, the same blue artifact the boys had once dared each other to touch. This time, Loki reached out and laid his hand upon it.

The cold did not hurt him. That was the first strange thing. The second was that his skin, where it met the casket, began to turn a deep, beautiful blue. Ridges he had never seen rose along his hands. His eyes, when he caught his reflection in the vault's polished floor, glowed a soft and steady red. He was looking at himself — and he was looking at a Frost Giant.

Loki ran to find Odin. The Allfather was old then, tired, lying in the long sleep that sometimes took him. Loki shook him gently and asked the question. And Odin, with that terrible kindness that fathers can have when they have been hiding something for a very long time, told him the truth. There had been a great battle on the icy realm of Jotunheim against King Laufey. After the battle, walking through the cold ruins, Odin had heard a small cry. A baby, abandoned because he was too small to be a Frost Giant warrior. A baby with blue skin and red eyes. Odin had carried him home.

"You took me for a purpose," Loki whispered. "A bargaining chip. A tool."

"No," Odin said. "I took you because you needed a family."

But Loki was not ready to hear that. The world he had built — the prince of Asgard, the second son of Odin, the brother of Thor — had cracked open. Everything he thought he knew about himself was painted a different color now, the color of frost. And underneath the hurt was something much harder to admit. Loki had grown up hearing scary bedtime stories about Frost Giants. He had been raised, without even meaning to, to fear what he actually was.

It would take a long time, and many adventures, for Loki to understand that his Jotun blood was not a curse. It was simply part of him. Frigga, when she heard the news, hugged him exactly the same as before. "You are my son," she said. And in that one quiet sentence was a truth that even Loki, who was usually the cleverest person in any room, would need years to truly believe.
