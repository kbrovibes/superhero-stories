There is a special kind of moment in some stories. It only comes once. It is the moment when the hero finally — after all the wrong turns and the lost battles and the bad decisions and the long road home — becomes the person they were always meant to be.

Tonight is that moment for Loki.

Pay attention. This one matters.

The multiverse was breaking. Sylvie had killed He Who Remains. The branches of the multiverse had been growing wild ever since — sprouting in every direction, blossoming like a tree in spring. Some of those branches were wonderful new worlds. Some of them were terrifying ones. New versions of He Who Remains were rising in some of them — versions calling themselves Kang the Conqueror, and worse names — and they were starting to look for the doors back to the timeline.

The TVA was in chaos. The old officers were confused. The old systems were not working. Nobody knew what to do.

Loki, who used to love chaos more than anything, walked into the TVA's machine rooms looking for someone with answers.

He found OUROBOROS.

Ouroboros — or "O.B.," as Mobius called him — was a kind, dusty, very old man with bright eyes and white hair. He had been the inventor at the TVA for as long as anyone could remember. He had built most of the machines in the place, including the TemPads and the temporal collars and the time doors. He worked in a cluttered, cozy little workshop full of bits and bobs and half-finished projects.

Ouroboros peered at Loki over the rims of his glasses and said, "Oh dear, oh dear. You'd better come down to the basement. I'll show you what's really keeping this place together."

He took Loki and Mobius down a service elevator, through a long hallway, past a sliding metal door, to a place none of them had ever been.

It was the room of the Temporal Loom.

The Loom was enormous. It filled a room the size of a stadium. It glowed with golden light. Thousands of strings of pure energy flowed through it, all moving in the same direction, all being woven together by spinning wheels and humming machinery. Each string, Ouroboros explained, was a branch of the multiverse. The Loom kept them all flowing safely along one shared path.

But the Loom was overheating.

Now that there were too many new branches — branches growing because He Who Remains was no longer pruning them — the Loom was running at twice its capacity. Sparks were flying off the spinning wheels. The room felt hotter than it should. Ouroboros pointed to a dial that was nearly all the way in the red.

"If that dial reaches the end," Ouroboros said quietly, "the Loom will burn out. And if the Loom burns out, every branch goes with it. Every Variant. Every person on every timeline. Gone."

Loki stood very still.

Every person on every timeline.

Mobius. Hunter B-15. Casey. Thor. Sylvie. Frigga, in some other branch where she was still alive. Every kid eating cereal. Every grandmother in every kitchen. Every dog asleep on every rug.

Gone.

He had to fix it.

He tried.

He tried so many things.

He learned, through hard practice, how to TIME SLIP. That meant slipping through time without any machine — using only his magic, only his will, only his mind. He could blink from one moment to another, BWOOSH, like a stone skipping across a pond. He used it to save Mobius from being pruned. He used it to save Hunter B-15. He used it to skip backward and forward through the moments of the breaking Loom, trying to find one thing he could do to stop the overheating.

He stepped out onto the Loom itself — out among the wild glowing strings — in a special suit Ouroboros built to protect him. The energy whipped around him like a storm. The strings of the multiverse were like fire under his fingers. He tried to weave them by hand. He tried to slow the overheating. He tried to add more strings to the Loom. He tried to take strings away.

Nothing worked.

He time-slipped back. He tried again. Nothing.

He went to find Sylvie.

She had vanished back in the timeline somewhere. Loki searched. He time-slipped. He looked. He finally found her in a small diner in the year 1982, in a small town in Oklahoma. She had taken a normal name. She had a job there serving pancakes. She lived in a small apartment. She watched movies at night. She had — for the first time in her whole life — a normal life. Quiet. Safe. Hers.

Loki sat across from her in a red diner booth. The fluorescent light was buzzing softly overhead.

"The Loom is failing," he said. "I need you. We have to fix it together."

Sylvie looked at her plate of pancakes. She looked out the window at the empty Oklahoma highway.

"Loki," she said. "I have spent my whole life running. This is the first place I've ever stopped. I can't go back. I can't."

He understood. He really did. He had spent his life trying to make a home for himself, and here she was, finally in one. He could not ask her to give it up.

They sat together in the booth for a long quiet moment. He reached across and held her hand. She held his. They did not say goodbye exactly. They just both understood.

Loki went back to the TVA.

And here, in the quiet, alone with everything he had tried and failed, Loki finally figured out the truth.

The Loom could not be saved.

Not from the inside. Not by a clever trick. Not by a quick repair. The math of it did not work. The branches were growing faster than the machine could weave them. The fix the Loom needed was a person. Somebody to step out onto the Loom itself, grab every branch with their own hands, and hold them.

Forever.

Alone.

At the end of time.

Somebody had to do it. The math did not care who. But somebody had to.

Loki looked at his friends. Mobius, with his soft tie and his magazine collection. Hunter B-15, who was tough and good and had become a friend. Casey, who had finally — after centuries — learned what fish were and could not stop talking about them. Sylvie, eating pancakes in her diner. Thor, somewhere out there, holding Stormbreaker. Every single person on every single branch of the wild beautiful multiverse, all of them living their stories.

The trickster god, the boy from Asgard who used to perform little tricks to get his father's attention, the boy who had spent so much of his life trying to grab some kind of throne — he understood, in one quiet moment, that the throne was not the point.

The PEOPLE were the point.

Loki put on his finest green-and-gold coat. He brushed it off. He stood up straight. He gave Mobius and Ouroboros a small bow — not a sneer, not a smirk, but a real bow, the kind a king gives his subjects in respect — and he walked toward the door of the Loom chamber.

"Where are you going?" Mobius asked.

Loki smiled.

"Out," he said. "To hold the door open. For everyone."

Mobius understood, slowly, what that meant.

He started to argue. Loki gently shook his head. "You can't talk me out of this," he said. "I have spent three thousand years not knowing what I was for. I know now."

He opened the door.

He walked out onto the burning Loom.

The wild energy of the multiverse whipped around him. The strings of the timeline whipped past, each one a whole universe of people. He kept walking. He held out his hands. He reached for the strings. He grabbed them — every single one — and he HELD them.

The dying Loom, the failing machine, the overheating room — it began to transform.

The walls of the chamber faded. The mechanical wheels became leaves. The strings of energy turned into branches. The Loom became a great glowing tree. The Yggdrasil of all stories. Every branch was a universe. Every leaf was a moment. Every twig was a person, living their life.

Loki sat at the center of the tree.

Not on a fancy throne. Just a quiet chair. At the root of the whole great living thing. Holding every branch in his hands. Holding every story safe.

He had spent his life trying to be a king of one place.

Now he was the keeper of every place.

He was the new center of the multiverse. The new replacement for He Who Remains. But unlike He Who Remains, who had ruled by force and by pruning, Loki ruled by holding on. By caring. By keeping every wild branch alive instead of cutting it away.

The branches no one would ever prune again.

The multiverse, finally, was free.

He sat there alone.

He would always sit there alone.

His friends would go on with their lives. Mobius would visit a son he had never gotten to know on the sacred timeline, riding his jet ski. Sylvie would stay in her diner, finally at peace. Casey would learn about more fish. Hunter B-15 would continue protecting the timeline in her new, gentler way. Thor would adventure on, never knowing that his brother was — somewhere, somehow — holding the whole sky up for him.

And every now and then, in some moment of his eternal quiet keeping, Loki would close his eyes and remember.

Frigga's library. The candles that never burned out. The bowl of apples turning into snakes. His mother's voice saying, "You have a real gift, my son. Be gentle with it."

He thought she would have been proud.

The trickster god, holding the multiverse safe, alone at the end of time.

Not because he had to.

Because he chose to.

Because, after all his long twisty centuries, after every wrong turn and every failed scheme and every lost battle — he had finally figured out what he wanted to be.

Not the king of Asgard.

Not the conqueror of Earth.

The keeper of stories.

And he was good at it.

Sleep well, little listener. Tomorrow's story is the last one. It is about all the small ways Loki became, in the end, a real and quiet hero.

Goodnight.
