Okay, listener. This is the last Loki story. We've come a long way together. From a quiet little boy reading books in Frigga's library, all the way to the God of Stories holding the multiverse safe. So let's talk about something important. Let's talk about what makes Loki — Loki.

Some heroes change completely. They start out scared and they become brave. They start out unkind and they become kind. They become DIFFERENT versions of themselves.

But Loki? Loki didn't actually change completely.

He was a trickster as a kid. He was a trickster as a villain. He was a trickster when he was Mobius's friend. He was a trickster when he became the God of Stories.

He just changed what he used the tricks FOR.

Let me show you.

Remember when little Loki used to make Thor's drinking horn turn into a snake? SSSSS! Thor would shriek and throw the horn. Loki would giggle. He did it to embarrass Thor. He did it because being clever felt like winning.

By the end of his life, Loki was STILL doing tricks like that. POOF! He still turned sandwiches into snakes when nobody was looking. He still made illusions of himself stand in doorways just to make Mobius jump and yell "AGH! LOKI! NOT FUNNY!" He still shape-shifted into the most inconvenient person possible in a serious meeting at the TVA. Once he became Ravonna Renslayer halfway through a speech, just to see what would happen.

The MISCHIEF didn't go away. He was a trickster forever.

But here is what changed.

When Mobius was about to be pruned by some bad TVA officers — ZAP! Loki created a SHIMMERING double of himself across the room, drawing the bad officers away. WHOOSH! They ran after the fake Loki. The real Mobius was saved.

When Sylvie was being chased across the timeline, Loki planted a clever rumor in just the right ear. "Oh, did you hear? They saw her in NINETEEN-SIXTY-EIGHT BUDAPEST!" The TVA went looking for her in Budapest. She was actually in Oklahoma, eating pancakes. SAVED.

When Casey, the friendly TVA clerk who had finally figured out what fish were, was struggling to open a stuck door, Loki — POOF! — shape-shifted into a WRENCH so Casey could grab him and use him to twist the bolt loose. Casey almost dropped him. Loki yelped from inside the wrench. They both laughed.

The tricks got used for KINDNESS instead of pride.

Even his oldest, weirdest habit — pretending to be dead — turned out to have something gentle hiding in it.

You know how Loki had faked his own death about a hundred times? So many times that Thor had stopped believing it? Well, looking back, you can see what that was. It was always a way to ESCAPE the people who wanted him to be someone he wasn't. Odin wanted a different kind of son. Asgard wanted a different kind of prince. Loki kept faking death so he could disappear, change his shape, become someone he liked better.

But by the end of his story? Sitting at the root of the great tree of stories? He didn't need to escape anymore. He didn't need a costume. He didn't need a false name. He didn't need to fake anything. He could just sit there and BE Loki, alone in the quiet, holding every branch of the multiverse safe.

For the first time in his entire long life, the trickster god was finally, truly himself. ALL the time.

So here's the truth, dear listener.

There's no neat little lesson at the end of Loki's story. He was a lot of things. A brother. A Frost Giant. A villain. A fake Odin. A "get help" bowling ball. A brave little dagger in front of Thanos. An orange jumpsuit at the TVA. A friend to Mobius. Somebody Sylvie kissed once and walked away from. The keeper of all stories.

He grew up. But he never stopped being a trickster.

He just figured out, slowly, painfully, beautifully, that being CLEVER wasn't the same as being GOOD.

But being clever — when you used it for the people you loved — could be a small kind of love.

Frigga would have been proud.

And somewhere, in some branch of the wild beautiful timeline, Loki is still her son.

The end.

Goodnight, brave little listener. And remember — your tricks can be used for kindness too.
