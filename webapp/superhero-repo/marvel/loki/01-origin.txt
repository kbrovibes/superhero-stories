Long before he became the God of Stories, before the Tesseract and the TVA and the orange jumpsuit, Loki was simply the second prince of Asgard. He grew up in the golden halls beside his older brother Thor, raced him across the Rainbow Bridge, and learned from their mother Frigga in the quiet library where she kept her oldest spellbooks. Odin sat on the throne. The Warriors Three laughed in the courtyards. To anyone watching, it looked like the most perfect childhood in the Nine Realms.

But Loki always felt a little out of step. Thor was loud and golden, the kind of son who threw an axe further than anyone and laughed when it came back. Loki was the quieter one — the reader, the watcher, the boy who preferred a clever trick to a hard punch. While Thor wrestled with Volstagg, Loki sat with Frigga and learned to bend light. A bowl of apples would become a bowl of snakes. A statue would suddenly walk. Frigga would smile and say, "You have a gift, my son."

The trouble was, gifts that other people did not understand often felt like flaws. Asgardians respected thunder and broadswords. They had a harder time respecting illusions and whispers. So Loki began trying — too hard, sometimes — to prove he belonged. He told stories that made him sound braver than he was. He pulled pranks that made Thor look foolish in front of Sif. He wanted very badly for Odin to look at him the way Odin looked at Thor.

What Loki did not know yet was that the look he was waiting for would never come the way he wanted it to. Not because Odin did not love him — he did, in his complicated Allfather way — but because Loki had been measuring himself with the wrong ruler. He kept trying to be Thor, when the universe had something stranger and more interesting in mind for him.

Still, in those early days, the brothers were brothers. They sparred. They argued. They sneaked into the vault to look at the Casket of Ancient Winters when no one was watching. And on quiet nights, Frigga would tuck them both into bed and tell them old stories about gods and tricksters — never guessing that one of her sons would grow up to become both.
