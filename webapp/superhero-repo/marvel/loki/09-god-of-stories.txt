The multiverse was breaking. Every time Loki tried to fix one branch, another one started to unravel. He learned, with help from Mobius and Ouroboros (the kind old TVA inventor who built the agency's machines), that the heart of the whole system was an enormous device called the Temporal Loom. The Loom wove all the branches of the multiverse into one safe, manageable thread. But now that He Who Remains was gone, branches were growing faster than the Loom could weave. The Loom was overheating. Soon it would burn out. And if it burned out, every branch would collapse, and every Variant — every person in every universe — would be lost forever.

Loki tried everything. He used his magic to slip through time. He learned to "time slip" with no machinery at all, blinking from one moment to another by sheer will. He saved Mobius. He saved Hunter B-15. He even reasoned with Sylvie. He found her in a small Oklahoma diner where she had hidden, finally living a normal life, and he tried to convince her to come back and help. They argued. They cared. They could not agree.

In the end, Loki understood. The Loom could not be saved. Not from the inside. Anyone who tried to walk out onto the Loom to repair it would be torn apart by the wild energy of every branch passing through. To save the multiverse, someone would have to step out there, grab all the branches themselves, and HOLD them. Forever. Alone. At the end of time.

Loki looked at his friends — Mobius, with his soft tie and his magazines; Sylvie, fierce and free; Hunter B-15; Casey, who had finally figured out what a fish was. He looked at every person on every branch of the wild beautiful multiverse, all of them living, all of them real, all of them safe in their own version of the story.

And the trickster who had spent so much of his life trying to claim a throne walked out onto the burning Loom, in his green-and-gold finest, calm as a long bow at the end of a play. He took every branch in his hands. The Loom transformed. The dying machine became a beautiful living tree of light. Loki sat at its center, on a chair that was not so much a throne as a quiet seat at the root of all stories. He was the new keeper. Not by force. Not by trickery. By choice. He had become the God of Stories — the one who held everyone else's tale safe, alone in the dark, by holding on.
