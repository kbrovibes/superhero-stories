High above the Nine Realms, in a golden city that shimmered like sunrise on water, there lived a family of four. The father was Odin, the Allfather, the king of Asgard, with his long silver beard and his one watching eye. The mother was Frigga, a sorceress, kind and wise, who kept the oldest spellbooks in a library where the candles never burned out.

And then there were the two boys. Thor was the older one. Loki was the younger.

If you had walked into the royal nursery on any given morning, you would have known right away which boy was which.

Thor was loud. Thor was golden. Thor liked to throw things — apples, sticks, his wooden practice axe, anything that could be flung. He laughed with his whole body. He wanted to be a warrior the moment he could walk, and Odin smiled down at him and said, "That one will sit on the throne someday."

Loki was quiet. Loki was watchful. Loki liked to read, and to sit very still in corners, and to figure out exactly how things worked. He learned to write in three different alphabets by the time he was seven. He could tell when someone was lying just by listening to the small pauses in their voice.

And he loved magic.

His mother Frigga taught him. In the library, with the candles burning low, she would show him how to bend light — just a little, just enough that a thrown ball seemed to curve in the air. She would show him how to make an illusion of himself stand in the doorway, perfectly still, while the real Loki crept out the window. She would smile and say, "You have a real gift, my son. Be gentle with it."

Loki tried.

But Asgard was not always a gentle place for clever boys.

Asgardians loved thunder. They loved bright golden armor and giant swinging hammers. The Warriors Three — Volstagg, Hogun, and Fandral — they were Thor's friends, big and bold and ready to brawl at the drop of a horn. Even Sif, the only girl in the warrior crowd, was a fierce swordfighter who could outduel most of the boys in the realm.

Loki did not fit easily in that crowd.

So he started, very slowly, to do something that would shape the rest of his life. He started performing. He started showing off. He started telling stories where HE was the bravest one. He started playing pranks on Thor — making Thor's drinking horn turn into a hissing snake, making Thor's bedroom door open onto the wrong room. SSSSS! "AHHH!" THUMP! He thought, if he could just be clever ENOUGH, surprising ENOUGH, dramatic ENOUGH, then maybe Odin would look at him the way Odin looked at Thor.

But Odin was a very hard man to impress. And the harder Loki tried, the more it felt like he was reaching for something just out of his fingertips.

Here is the secret Loki did not know yet.

Loki had been measuring himself with the wrong ruler. He kept holding himself up next to Thor and saying, "Why am I not LIKE that?" But the universe had not made him to be a Thor. The universe had made him to be a Loki. And being a Loki was going to mean something the boy could not yet imagine — something far stranger and more wonderful than just being a louder, hammer-swinging warrior.

Still, in those early days, before any of the big tangled chapters of his life began, there were quiet moments too.

The brothers played together. They sparred in the courtyard. They snuck into Odin's vault late at night, holding their breath, and stood in front of the Casket of Ancient Winters — a blue, glowing artifact captured from the Frost Giants long ago. The Casket gave off a cold so deep it made their fingers ache. Thor would dare Loki to touch it. Loki would dare Thor right back. Neither of them ever did. They would run, laughing, back to their beds before the guards noticed.

And on the quietest nights, Frigga would come into their bedroom — Thor's bed on the left, Loki's bed on the right, with the velvet curtains pulled back — and she would sit between them and tell them stories. Old, old stories. Stories from before Asgard was even built. Stories about gods who could turn into salmon, and tricksters who could outsmart entire kingdoms with a single clever sentence.

Loki would lie very still and listen. He would look up at his mother's face in the candlelight and think — without even knowing he was thinking it — "I want to be in a story like that someday."

Frigga would smooth his hair and kiss his forehead.

She had no idea, in those soft golden bedtime moments, that one of her two sons would grow up to be BOTH. A god AND a trickster. A villain AND a hero. The most surprising, most twisting, most unexpected of all the heroes of the Nine Realms.

But that is a story for another night.
