When the Dark Aster fell toward the surface of Xandar, there was a problem that none of the Guardians could solve with a weapon or a clever plan. The ship was going down. They were inside it. The impact would be devastating — the kind that leaves nothing recognizable. Groot looked at his friends. He looked at the cargo bay floor. And then he did something that was, in the context of his own quiet nature, entirely ordinary.

He grew.

He spread his arms outward and upward, and his branches multiplied and interlaced, arching over the Guardians in a lattice of living wood — bark and twig and root weaving together into a dense, rounded shell that enclosed all of them inside it. The branches locked together. The whole structure hardened. Groot poured every bit of himself into it, his bark thickening to absorb the impact, his roots anchoring to the ship's floor so the cocoon would hold its position.

Rocket was inside the shell. He understood immediately what was happening. He grabbed Groot's arm and said, "No — Groot — you'll —"

"I am Groot," Groot said.

Just the three words. Quiet and certain and warm, the way a forest is warm even in winter. He was looking at Rocket.

Rocket understood. He had always understood.

The Dark Aster hit the surface of Xandar.

The impact was tremendous — a compression wave that would have killed everyone inside if not for the shell of living wood surrounding them. The bark absorbed the force. Branches cracked and splintered. The outer layers of the cocoon broke apart under the pressure and the heat. But the innermost layers held. The Guardians held.

When Star-Lord and the others pushed through the wreckage to the surface of Xandar, they were alive. The sky above was dust and light. Around them lay the remains of the Dark Aster and, scattered in the broken earth, the fragments of what had been Groot — broken branches, bark chips, pieces of a creature who had given everything he had to make sure his friends survived.

Rocket sat in the wreckage for a while. He didn't move. Then he reached into the debris and began to gather the pieces — carefully, the way you gather something important that has fallen. Every twig. Every fragment.

He found a small pot.

And he planted them.

Groot grew back — he always grows back. That is the nature of trees and of Groot: the season ends, and then it begins again. But the courage of that moment in the Dark Aster, the absolute certainty in those three quiet words, never left anyone who was there to hear them.
