Once upon a time, a small green shoot uncurled from the soil in a metal pot in the cargo bay of the Milano.

It was barely an inch tall, pale and new and slightly uncertain about the world, the way all new things are uncertain. But it was reaching toward the light — the light from the porthole, the stars beyond it — the way all growing things reach toward the light.

Rocket watched it from across the room.

He had been watching it every day for months.

It grew. Very slowly at first, then faster. It became a small plant, then a larger one, then a thing with a face and arms and new pale bark that was unmistakably, completely, only ever going to be one thing.

Star-Lord came and stood next to Rocket and looked at the little plant, and asked the question he had been working up to:

"Is it really him? Is it the same Groot?"

Rocket did not have to think about it.

"He's Groot," he said. "That's enough."

And he was right. Because here is the truth about who a person is: they are not only their memories. They are their character. The choices they make when no one is watching. The things they reach toward, like a green shoot reaching for light on a new morning.

This Groot — small and new, learning the Milano and the galaxy and the people around him for what was technically the second time — reached toward all the same things. He was patient when things were frustrating. He was kind to every small creature, from the birds that drifted in through the ventilation to the smallest beetle found on the landing ramp. He held steady when the ship rattled or when five very different people with very strong opinions argued in a very small space, which happened quite often. He put himself between the people he loved and anything that wanted to harm them, every time, without being asked and without expecting thanks.

He was Groot.

He had taught the Guardians things they hadn't known they needed to learn.

He taught Star-Lord that steadiness is its own kind of courage. Star-Lord watched Groot be quietly sure, day after day, and became a better captain.

He taught Gamora that protection can be given without a price — that kindness doesn't have to be a negotiation. She began to trust more, by degrees.

He taught Drax that strength and gentleness are not enemies. That the most powerful tree in the old forest is also where the birds build their nests. Drax began to laugh more. He began to be tender, in his loud dramatic way.

And Rocket — Rocket he had taught from the very first day on Xandar. From the moment a large tree-creature walked alongside him and asked nothing and gave everything.

Rocket knew. He had always known.

The Guardians called themselves a team. In the Nova Corps records and the various old bounty notices that had not yet been officially rescinded, they were listed as a team. But what they were — what they had truly become, through everything they had shared and lost and found again — was a family. Not born into. Chosen. Kept by decision and renewed every day by loyalty that no one had ever asked for and no one had ever stopped giving.

And at the centre of it — never the loudest, but always the most constant — was Groot. The oldest tree in their forest. The roots holding everything in place. The canopy where the others sheltered.

The morning the new Groot said his three words for the first time, Rocket crossed the cargo bay in four strides and stood in front of him.

"I am Groot," said Groot.

Rocket looked up at him.

"Yeah," he said. "I know." He put his hand briefly against Groot's bark arm. "Good to have you back."

The Milano hummed around them. The stars slid past the porthole. The galaxy went on — immense and cold and full of both danger and wonder — and in a small warm ship traveling through it, a family was whole.

We are all Groot, when we want to be. When we are patient where patience is hard. When we choose kindness. When we hold on for the people we love.

You already know how to be Groot.

Goodnight, little one. Goodnight.
