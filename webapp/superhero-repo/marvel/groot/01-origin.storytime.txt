Once upon a time, in a galaxy very far from ours, there was a planet called Planet X.

Planet X was nothing like Earth. There were no cities of glass and steel. Instead, there were forests — enormous, ancient forests that stretched as far as the light could reach. And the people of Planet X were not made of skin and bone. They were made of wood, and bark, and living branches. They were called the Flora Colossi, and they had lived on Planet X since before anyone could remember.

The greatest of them — in the ways that truly matter — was a young Flora Colossi named Groot.

Groot had bark the colour of old oak and arms as wide as branches in a summer storm. His eyes glowed with soft violet light, like the last embers of a fire just before sleep. And Groot carried something the other Flora Colossi seemed to have misplaced: a very large, very warm heart.

The others were proud and stiff. But Groot was gentle. He let birds rest on his shoulders. He stood still in the rain so tiny creatures could shelter underneath his canopy. The elders shook their branches and said Groot was too soft, too easily swayed.

Then one morning, a small human child wandered into the sacred groves of Planet X — lost and frightened and very far from home. The child sat at Groot's roots and began to cry. Every other Flora Colossi turned away. Strangers were not permitted in the sacred groves.

But Groot folded his great arms around the child and said, quietly, the only three words he had ever been able to say:

"I am Groot."

The child stopped crying.

The elders called a meeting. Groot had broken the old laws. A creature so soft had no place among the proud Flora Colossi. And so Groot — who had only wanted to be kind — was sent away into the wide, unknowing universe.

He stepped into the stars without a plan.

For a long time, Groot drifted. He worked on cargo ships, loading crates at space stations, watching the stars wheel slowly past porthole windows. He saw planets with two suns and moons that sang. He was never truly alone — some small creature always found him and curled up against his bark — but he missed belonging somewhere.

Then, on a busy planet called Xandar, Groot sat down in a cantina to rest.

A voice came from beside him — fast and clever and rather screechy.

"You're big," it said. "That could be useful."

The voice belonged to Rocket — a small, sharp-witted creature who looked a bit like a raccoon but moved like a coiled spring, with mechanical parts fitted here and there and a plan for absolutely everything. Rocket talked without stopping. Groot listened without interrupting. Within a single week, they were best friends.

Rocket understood every single thing Groot said. "I am Groot" — just three words — and yet Rocket always knew whether Groot meant "I'm worried" or "that looks delicious" or "please, let us not do that particular thing." The two of them fit together like a river fits its banks: completely, naturally, as if it had always been so.

Together they found their way to a team: Star-Lord, who flew a ship called the Milano and played music from a cassette player; Gamora, the best fighter any of them had ever seen; and Drax, who was very strong and very serious and had the loudest laugh in any room. They were a ragged crew — loud, argumentative, not very good at following rules.

They were the Guardians of the Galaxy.

In every battle, Groot's gentle strength was what held them together. WHOMP — he grew walls of bark to shield his friends. CREAK — he stretched his arms into bridges across deep gaps. THUD — he scooped the hurt ones up and carried them to safety, his arms warm and steady.

And through every battle, the same three words drifted through the noise of the galaxy:

"I am Groot."

The elders of Planet X had once said Groot was too gentle for the universe.

But the universe, it turned out, needed exactly that.

Shhh now. Somewhere out there — past the double moons of Xandar, past the ribbon nebula — a very large, very kind tree-creature is keeping watch over his friends.

I am Groot. Goodnight.
