Among all the remarkable things about the Guardians of the Galaxy, none was more remarkable than the friendship between Groot and Rocket. They were mismatched in every measurable way: one enormous and slow-spoken, the other small and ceaselessly loud. One made of living wood and ancient patience, the other built from mechanical augmentations and fast-twitch instinct. And yet they understood each other with the completeness that takes most friendships a lifetime to build.

Rocket had come from a place he did not discuss. He had been taken apart and put back together against his will, and the experience had left him with technical genius, considerable anger, and a deep suspicion of anyone who seemed to want something from him. He trusted Groot before he trusted anyone else, and it had happened very fast — the way certain rare things happen fast, because they were always supposed to happen.

He was the only being in the galaxy who understood every shade of Groot's three words. "I am Groot" could mean a hundred different things — curiosity, warning, hunger, comfort, urgency, quiet affection — and Rocket heard each one precisely. He knew the difference between the low steady "I am Groot" that meant danger and the bright quick "I am Groot" that meant he had spotted something interesting. He knew the particular softness in Groot's voice when he said it after a hard fight, which meant: are you okay?

They had never fought a battle without the other nearby. In every tight situation, their coordination was wordless and fluid: Groot would create a barrier while Rocket climbed to a firing position on his shoulder; Rocket would identify a target and point without speaking while Groot's roots moved silently across the floor toward it. They had no need to discuss it. They had been doing it long enough that it was reflex.

When Groot had been reduced to nearly nothing after the battle over Xandar — just a handful of twigs that Rocket had gathered from the wreckage and pressed into a small pot of soil — Rocket had sat with him every day. He had talked to the pot. He had argued with it. He had, at least once, apologized to it. It took months for the first shoot to appear, and when it did, Rocket had called everyone on the Milano in to see it with an urgency that brooked no questions.

The relationship between Groot and Rocket was the kind that children understand instinctively and some adults forget: total loyalty, freely given, expected and returned. The galaxy was large and often unkind. Having someone in it who always understood you, and who you always understood, was everything.
