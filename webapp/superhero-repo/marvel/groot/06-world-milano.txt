The Milano was Star-Lord's ship — an M-class quadrant vessel with a hull patched in fourteen different places, a cassette deck hardwired into the flight controls, and a persistent engine fault that Rocket claimed to have fixed six times. It was named after Alyssa Milano, an Earth actress Peter Quill had admired as a boy on the planet he had left behind. The ship was loud, cramped, and temperamental. To the Guardians of the Galaxy, it was home.

Groot had a particular relationship with the Milano. He was too large for most of the interior corridors, which he navigated by tilting sideways and folding in his taller branches. He slept in the cargo bay, where the ceiling was high enough and the deck was bare metal that he could root into slightly during deep rest, drawing trace minerals from the hull's alloy in the slow way trees drink through their roots. Rocket had complained about this for a while, until he noticed that the hull in Groot's corner was actually denser and more corrosion-resistant than anywhere else on the ship, reinforced by Groot's own biological processes.

From the cargo bay's porthole, Groot could watch the galaxy go by. He had seen Xandar's double sunset — two suns setting at different speeds until the sky turned every shade from amber to deep violet. He had watched a pulsar from safe distance, its regular beam sweeping the dark like a lighthouse above an endless sea. He had seen the ribbon nebula of the Kyln region, where the Guardians had once been imprisoned in the galaxy's worst space station, and had watched the light of a thousand stars filter through it in colors that had no names in any language he knew.

The galaxy was immense in ways that even Groot — who had come from a world that measured time in centuries — found hard to hold entirely in mind. The Nova Corps policed a fraction of it from Xandar. The Kree Empire claimed another. The Sovereign civilization occupied their golden planet, endlessly offended by everyone else. Between all these territories floated thousands of independent worlds and stations — including Knowhere, the skull-colony where you could buy anything if you knew who to ask.

Groot had been cast out from Planet X for caring too much. He had wandered this enormous galaxy and, somehow, improbably, found the place where he belonged: a battered ship, a loud small friend, and whatever came next.

"I am Groot," he said one evening, to no one in particular, as the nebula light fell through the porthole and turned his bark every shade of purple and gold.

Rocket, who was working on the engine four meters away, didn't look up. "Yeah, buddy," he said. "I know."
