Once upon a time, two teams of heroes met on a ruined moon, and for a moment — just a moment — they pointed their weapons at each other.

Because that is sometimes how it goes, the first time.

The Guardians of the Galaxy had been crossing a distant sector when a distress signal reached them. They followed it and found a half-destroyed ship carrying two Asgardian survivors with urgent information: Thanos was coming. The six Infinity Stones were his target. All six. And when he had them all, the galaxy would be at the mercy of one man who had decided he knew better than everyone else what was best for the universe.

A chain of fast events brought them through a shimmering portal to a place called Titan.

Titan was a sad place. Once a world of cities and people, now ruin — crumbled towers, broken archways, stone columns lying in yellow dust, a dead sky above and a silence that had been going on for years. It had been Thanos's home. Looking at it, Groot thought he understood something about why Thanos had become who he was.

Waiting in the ruins were two strangers.

One wore armor — red and gold, complex and beautiful. His name was Tony Stark. He was from Earth. The other wore robes and had a quality of stillness that felt like readiness, not patience. A sorcerer named Stephen Strange. Around his neck hung the Time Stone — one of the six.

The Guardians and the Earth heroes stared at each other. Weapons came up.

Groot sat down on a broken column and waited. He knew this shape. The energy was wrong for a real fight. These people were angry at the situation, not at each other. They would figure it out.

And they did.

After Star-Lord and Tony Stark argued about who was in charge of what — both of them leaders, neither of them particularly practiced at sharing — they found a way. After Rocket explained the six Infinity Stones to a young hero in a red-and-blue suit named Peter Parker, who went by Spider-Man and asked good questions but had never encountered a living tree before and kept looking at Groot with politely concealed astonishment. After Doctor Strange described what he had seen: fourteen million possible futures. One outcome. One path through all of it, barely visible, fragile as a new shoot.

Groot turned this over in his mind like a seed pressed into winter soil. He did not say anything. He sat on his broken column in the ruins of Titan and felt the stone beneath him — cold, old, dead — and thought about what it meant to keep growing on rock that had forgotten what growing felt like.

Then Thanos came.

He arrived the way he always arrived — slowly, without hurrying, as if the ruins were a place he owned and had simply been away from. The golden Gauntlet gleamed. He moved with the certainty of someone who has already decided.

And the two teams moved together.

Groot did what he did best. He grew barriers — WHOMP — dense walls of bark rising from the ground to block attack lines. He grew vines — CREAK — wrapping around an ankle, a wrist, anything to slow the enormous force. He grew a bridge of living branches across a twenty-foot gap in the ruins when three heroes needed to cross it at once and there was no other way.

And when Thanos turned those heavy, certain eyes directly on Groot, Groot drove his roots into the cracked stone of Titan. As deep as they would go. And held.

The battle did not go the way they hoped. Thanos was too powerful that day. The heroes of Earth and the Guardians stood in the yellow dust of Titan and understood the war was not yet over.

Groot stood still for a while.

He opened his palm. From it — slowly, quietly — grew a single small flower, pale gold, the colour of afternoon light. He hadn't planned it. It just happened.

He looked at it for a moment.

Then he closed his fingers gently around it and kept it safe.

Some things you keep for later. For the day when the hard season ends and the new one begins.

Groot looked around at the ruins of Titan — the crumbled towers, the dust, the broken archways — and thought about how everything that had died here had once been alive, once been full of people and ordinary mornings and the specific warmth of being home somewhere. Things end. But things also begin.

That day was coming.

He put the flower in his bark pocket — a small crease in the wood near his chest — and walked back toward the others.

Shh. Hard seasons always end. Brave hearts always find each other in the ruins. And small pale flowers grow, sometimes, in the most unlikely places.

Goodnight.
