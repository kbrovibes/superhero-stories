Of all the remarkable things about Groot, the most extraordinary was not his strength or his size, though both were astonishing. It was the fact that he could not be permanently stopped. Snap a branch and he would grow it back. Burn his bark and new layers would form within days. Cut him to the roots — and from a single twig, in enough time, a new Groot would grow. His biology defied every weapon the galaxy could throw at it. He was, in the deepest sense, a being made of renewal.

His roots allowed him to draw nutrients from nearly any surface. He had walked across barren asteroid rock and space station metal floors and still maintained the quiet process of growth that kept him alive. On worlds with rich soil — like the forested moons of the Pama system — he grew noticeably taller within hours, his bark thickening, his fingers extending, the faint bioluminescence in his chest brightening like fireflies in an old wood.

His strength was something different from brute force. He could press the limits of any structure he entered, his arms capable of bending steel beams, his fists punching through ship hulls rated for heavy bombardment. But he was also precise: Groot could thread a single vine through a locked door mechanism and feel the tumblers turn, could grow a tendril fine as thread to retrieve something dropped into a narrow gap. His control over his own body was remarkable — the difference between a storm and a surgeon's hand, depending on what the moment required.

What puzzled xenobiologists who had briefly studied Groot's biology was the distributed nature of his consciousness. His mind did not seem to live in one place. It was spread through his entire body — root to crown — which was why blows to the head troubled him far less than they would any other being. It was also, Rocket suspected, why Groot felt things so completely. Joy moved through him like sap in spring. Sadness settled in him the way autumn settles in a forest.

Groot had once grown an entire shell of bark around an injured bird that had fallen onto the Milano's deck during a turbulent landing. The shell was porous — air came through, light came through — and it held the bird still and warm until it recovered. He was sitting next to it the whole night, one finger resting lightly against the shell, feeling the tiny heartbeat inside.

A creature built for both enormous power and extraordinary gentleness. In the galaxy's long history of remarkable beings, very few had managed both at once.
