Once upon a time, in the middle of the most frightening moment the Guardians had ever shared, something extraordinary happened.

But first — the frightening moment.

The Dark Aster was falling.

It had been a tremendous battle. Ronan the Accuser and his Cosmi-Rod and the blazing Power Stone, and all five Guardians fighting with everything they had in the corridors and on the bridge of a warship the size of a city. They had done the impossible. The Power Stone was free. Ronan was defeated. The planet Xandar, below, was safe.

But the Dark Aster itself — powerless now, its engines burned out, its hull cracked along a dozen seams — was falling. Falling fast. And the Guardians were still inside it.

Ronan's great black warship, the one shaped like a blade, had been defeated — the Power Stone was out of his hands at last. But the ship itself, powerless and burning, was falling out of the sky above Xandar. Fast. And the Guardians were still inside.

There was nothing to shoot. Nothing to out-think. The ground was coming.

Groot looked at his friends.

He looked at Star-Lord, who never gave up on anything. He looked at Gamora, who had fought so hard to find people she could trust. He looked at Drax, who laughed more than he used to. He looked at Rocket — his best friend, small and clever and the only person in the universe who understood every word he said.

Then Groot spread his arms.

It started slowly. His arms moved outward and upward. His fingers spread. And from his fingers came branches — growing fast now, faster than Groot had ever grown anything — arching over the Guardians' heads, weaving together in a dome of interlocked living wood. He poured himself into it, hardening the layers, driving his roots into the ship's floor to hold the cocoon in place.

A shell. Made of Groot. Around all of them.

Rocket had been watching from the first moment. He reached out and grabbed Groot's arm.

"No — Groot — you'll —" he started.

Groot looked down at him.

"I am Groot," he said. Very quietly. Very warmly. Three words that meant: I know. It's alright. I'll grow back. And I need you to be here.

Rocket's eyes were bright.

The last branch wove into place. It was dark and warm inside the cocoon. The sound of the falling ship was muffled by all that living wood.

BOOM.

The cocoon shuddered. The outer bark cracked. Branches on the outside splintered and broke away — each layer giving before the next one, absorbing the blow. Like the rings of a great tree that grow through hard years and stay standing.

The innermost layers held.

When Star-Lord pushed through the wreckage to the surface of Xandar, the first thing he did was reach back and help the others through. One by one they emerged into the dim, dusty sky.

Scattered in the broken earth were the pieces of Groot — bark and branch and fragment, the remains of a gentle giant who had given everything to make sure his friends were safe.

Rocket did not move for a long time. He stood very still among the wreckage, looking at the pieces, and said nothing.

Then he began to search. Very carefully. Methodically. The way Rocket does everything when it truly matters — completely, leaving nothing behind. Every twig. Every chip of bark. Every fragment he could find in the dust and the debris of the crashed warship and the broken field around it. He gathered them all.

He found a small metal pot in his salvage kit — the kind he used for spare parts — and filled it with a handful of rich soil from the fields of Xandar.

He planted everything.

He carried the pot back to the Milano and set it in the corner of the cargo bay where the light came through the porthole in the mornings. And then he sat down next to it.

Groot grows back. This is the truth at the very heart of who Groot is — he is made of renewal, and the season always turns, and what is planted always grows. He came back slowly, and then faster, and then one day he said his three words again and the Milano felt whole in a way it had not felt for months.

But every Guardian who had been inside that cocoon carried those three words with them always. Three words said very quietly in the dark of a falling ship, by a gentle giant who had decided — without hesitation, without weighing it, without a plan — that his friends would be safe.

That is courage. Quiet and certain and as warm as the heartwood of the oldest tree.

Shh. Someone loves you exactly that much. And you will always grow back, too.

Goodnight.
