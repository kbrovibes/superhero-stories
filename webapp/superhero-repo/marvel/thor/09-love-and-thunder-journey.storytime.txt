After the great battle against Thanos, after Asgard was destroyed and rebuilt, after Thor passed his crown to Valkyrie, the God of Thunder did something he had never really done before.

He went looking for himself.

He boarded a strange purple-and-orange spaceship called the Benatar, and he joined a wild loud loving group of misfits called the Guardians of the Galaxy. There was Star-Lord — a cocky human raised among aliens — and Rocket Raccoon, who was a very angry talking raccoon with too many guns, and Groot, a young walking tree who said only one phrase ("I am Groot") but said it with great expressiveness. There was Drax, who was very literal about everything, and Mantis, who could feel people's feelings just by touching them, and Nebula, who carried her own quiet sorrows.

They were chaos. They were family. They were exactly what Thor needed.

Thor traveled with them across the galaxy. He helped them on adventures. He laughed. He ate strange alien food. He stopped feeling sorry for himself, slowly, one small adventure at a time. He got back into shape — for real this time. He shed the heaviness of his grief, not by ignoring it, but by carrying it lighter.

He became, in this new chapter of his life, a god in search of a heart.

This was the time the storytellers came to call Love and Thunder.

In the middle of his wandering, two important things happened.

The first was the rise of a new and terrible villain. His name was Gorr, and they called him the God Butcher.

Gorr had not always been a villain. Long ago, on a hot dusty world, he had been a humble man with a family. He had prayed and prayed to his gods to help his people during a great drought. The gods had not come. His family had been lost.

That grief had broken something inside Gorr. When he later found a strange ancient weapon called the Necrosword — a black blade made by darkness itself — he picked it up. The sword whispered to him. And Gorr swore an oath. He would hunt every god in the universe and end them all. Because the gods, he believed, did not deserve to live while mortals suffered.

He began to keep his oath. Across galaxies, gods began to fall to his black sword. Thor heard the news with a heavy heart.

The second important thing was Jane.

Jane Foster, his long-ago love, returned to his life — as the Mighty Thor, carrying a reforged Mjolnir, fighting beside him for the first time in years. (Her own brave sad story is told elsewhere.)

Thor saw her, and his whole heart turned over in his chest. They had been apart for so long. Now here she was, lifting his hammer, standing beside him.

They went together to face Gorr.

Their journey took them across the strangest places in the universe. To Omnipotence City, a glittering decadent town in the clouds where the bored gods of a thousand species lived in lazy luxury. (Thor was disappointed in those gods. He saw, with new clarity, what Gorr had hated about them — the way they ignored the suffering of mortals, the way they cared more about parties than people.) To the dark shadowed realm of Eternity, the cosmic entity at the center of the universe who could grant any wish.

Along the way, Thor and Jane fought side by side and remembered, slowly, all the reasons they had loved each other.

The final confrontation with Gorr was unlike any battle Thor had ever fought.

Thor had expected a great clash. Lightning. Thunder. The God of Thunder against the God Butcher. He arrived ready to swing Stormbreaker.

What he saw instead was a broken man.

Gorr stood before the entity Eternity. The Necrosword was darkening his soul a little more with every passing moment. He looked up at Thor with the eyes of someone who was very, very tired.

And Thor — older now, wiser now, softer in the right ways — chose something different.

Instead of more rage, he chose empathy.

He spoke to Gorr. He told him he understood his grief. He told him about losing his own family. About losing his home. About the dark years after the Snap. He did not pretend to know exactly what Gorr had felt. But he did know, deeply, what loss could do to a heart.

"You have a wish, Gorr," Thor said gently. "You can have any wish. You stand before Eternity. Do not waste it on hate."

Gorr trembled. The Necrosword whispered angrily to him. But Gorr was already weakening, dying from the corruption of the sword.

He looked up. He thought for a long moment.

Then he made his wish. Not for the destruction of the gods.

He wished for his daughter back. Her name was Love.

And Eternity, in its strange cosmic way, granted his wish.

A small girl appeared, blinking, alive and whole. She looked around in confusion. She saw her father.

"Papa?"

Gorr smiled the first real smile he had smiled in a very long time. He gathered Love into his arms. He looked at Thor.

"Will you... will you watch over her?" he whispered. "When I am gone? I don't have long. The sword has taken too much."

Thor knelt beside them. "I will," he promised. "On my life. I will."

Gorr passed peacefully then, with his daughter in his arms, the dark sword crumbling to harmless dust. The God Butcher's long sad story ended not in violence, but in mercy.

Thor took Love by the hand.

She was scared. He was scared too. He had never been a father.

But he had become, somehow, exactly the right person at exactly the right moment.

Today, Thor travels the stars with Love at his side. He has been teaching her about Asgard. About lightning. About kindness. He has built her a small version of Stormbreaker, just her size. She calls him "Uncle Thor," which is what he asked her to call him, because he knew her real father had loved her best.

They protect worlds together. The God of Thunder and the little girl named Love.

It is, Thor sometimes thinks, the most surprising and most beautiful chapter of his long life.

The lightning still crackles.

But now it is tempered with the gentleness of a child's hand in his.

The God of Thunder. And Love.
