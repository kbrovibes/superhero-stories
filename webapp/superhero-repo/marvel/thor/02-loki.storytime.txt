In the palace of Asgard, there were two princes.

One was loud, the other quiet. One swung a hammer, the other turned the pages of ancient books. One trained with warriors in the practice yard, the other sat in the library studying spells that had not been cast since before either of them was born. One burned bright like the sun, the other moved through shadows like a shadow himself.

Their names were Thor and Loki. And they were brothers.

They had grown up together in the golden halls of Asgard, racing each other through the palace corridors, stealing honey cakes from the kitchen, sitting together in the great throne room while Odin taught them the history of the Nine Realms. Thor was always bigger, always louder, always the one who got the most cheers. But Loki was always cleverer. He could make himself look like anyone. He could make you see things that were not there. He could slip through a conversation like water through fingers and leave you wondering, afterward, exactly what had just happened.

For a long time, they balanced each other. The hammer and the mind.

And then Loki found a secret.

It was written in an old room of the palace, in an old record that had not been opened in many years. He was not the son of Odin. He was not Asgardian. He was the son of Laufey, king of the Frost Giants of Jotunheim — born during a battle, left behind to freeze on the ice, found by Odin and brought home and raised as a prince.

All his life, he had been the spare. Thor was the heir. Thor was the one who would be king. And now Loki did not even know which world he belonged to.

The bitterness that had always been there — small and cold and quiet — grew enormous.

He schemed. He arranged for Thor to be exiled to Midgard. He arranged for himself to sit on the throne of Asgard while Odin slept in his long healing rest. He invited the Frost Giants in. He sent the Destroyer to finish Thor off. He pulled the strings of everyone around him like a puppeteer working a dozen marionettes at once, and he was so very good at it that nobody saw.

Except it did not work. Thor came back. Worthy, transformed, better. And Loki — falling from the edge of the universe after a confrontation at the Bifrost, falling into the darkness between worlds — was gone.

When Loki came back, he came back with an army.

He landed in New York with the Chitauri — an alien force, thousands strong — pouring through a glowing blue portal in the sky above Manhattan. He wanted to rule. He wanted the Tesseract, the great cosmic power source, and a throne, and all the things he had told himself he deserved. He wanted to feel like he was more than the spare, more than the shadow, more than the brother who stood always one step to the left.

The Avengers stopped him. Thor, among them, faced his brother in the ruins of Stark Tower and said the same thing he always said, in his deep voice, steady and without anger:

"Brother. Come home."

Loki looked at him with those green eyes that had always been calculating and unreadable and said: "I have no home."

He did not believe it entirely. That was the tragedy.

Because there were moments — you had to look carefully, but they were there — when the mask slipped. When Loki fought beside Thor against a common enemy, the way they had when they were children, and something old and real came through. The Asgardian who loved his brother and was loved. The boy who had raced through the palace corridors and stolen honey cakes.

When Loki faced Thanos — the most terrible force in the universe — he did not hide. He did not scheme. He stepped forward with a small knife and the most honest thing he had ever said: "I am Loki of Asgard."

And he was gone.

Thor carried that moment forever. In the thunder. In the storms over every world he flew across. In the nights when the grief sat heavy.

Because that was the truth about Loki: he was both things at once. The villain who schemed and the brother who loved. The god of mischief and the son of Odin. The shadow that could never quite stay in the dark, no matter how hard he tried.

Two princes in a golden palace. Thunder and mischief, always together, always inseparable, even across the spaces between worlds.

Even after everything.

Especially after everything.
