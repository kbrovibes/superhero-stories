Guess what happens when you put a genius inventor, a super soldier, a giant green scientist, a couple of super spies, and an actual honest-to-goodness god from another realm on the same team? The answer is: the most interesting team meetings you have ever seen.

Thor Odinson arrived among the Avengers and was completely, cheerfully baffling. He called everyone "friend" in a big booming voice. He used words like "verily" and "thee" and "dost" with total confidence. One day he bought himself a coffee in a paper cup, drank it with enormous satisfaction, and then — because in Asgard a good cup means you want another — smashed the cup on the floor and announced: "ANOTHER!" Everyone stared. Thor beamed. This was going great.

But the moment trouble started? Nobody stared at Thor anymore. Everyone followed him.

Because Thor could call lightning from the sky. Real lightning — KRAKOOM! — the kind that splits the air and makes your hair stand up. During the Battle of New York, when Loki's Chitauri army came pouring through a portal in the sky, Thor climbed to the top of the Chrysler Building and used it like a giant lightning rod, charging up the biggest bolt you have ever seen and blasting it straight through the portal into the oncoming army. ZAP! The sky went white. The Chitauri scattered.

He also helped create one of the Avengers' most powerful members — when his lightning struck the Infinity Stone in Vision's forehead and brought the kind, calm robot to life. Can you imagine? Thor's lightning literally made a new hero.

Thor brought something else the team couldn't get anywhere else: knowledge. He knew about the Infinity Stones — ancient, terrifying crystals of cosmic power. He knew about the creatures that lived beyond the stars. He knew the old stories that turned out to be very real warnings. Steve Rogers and Tony Stark were brilliant, but Thor was their window into a universe bigger and stranger than they had ever dreamed.

And he loved them for it. He called them his new Warriors Three and meant it with his whole enormous heart.

The hardest part for Thor was knowing he would probably outlive every single one of his mortal friends. Gods live a very, very long time. Humans do not. So Thor was always the one who threw himself between his teammates and the worst of the danger. Every. Single. Time.

He didn't do it because he was the strongest. He did it because he was their friend — and friends take the hit when they can.

That is what a god from Asgard learned from a team of humans: that friendship is its own kind of thunder.
