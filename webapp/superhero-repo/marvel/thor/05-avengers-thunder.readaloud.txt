When the Avengers first assembled, they had a scientist, a soldier, a spy, a man who turned green when he was angry, and a billionaire in a metal suit. And then there was Thor — an actual god, who had traveled from another realm entirely, and who spoke as if every sentence deserved a little ceremony.

Thor was not what any of them expected. He used words like "thee" and "dost" and "verily" while the others drank coffee and talked about traffic. He once bought a mug of coffee, enjoyed it immensely, and then smashed the cup on the ground for emphasis. "Another!" he announced. His teammates stared.

But in battle, no one questioned him for even a second.

Thor could call the lightning from the sky. He could fly on the wind. He could feel the storm the way you feel your own heartbeat. He was the one who knew about the Chitauri army, and the Infinity Stones, and the terrible things moving in the dark corners of the universe. He was the team's bridge between the world they knew and the cosmos they hadn't imagined yet.

He formed a deep friendship with Steve Rogers and Tony Stark — the soldier and the inventor who both reminded him, in different ways, of the best kind of warrior. He called them his new Warriors Three and meant it with his whole heart.

And do you know what happened as the years went on? Thor began to understand something that made him more careful than any battle had. His teammates were mortal. They aged. They could be hurt in ways he could not. And so he threw himself between them and the worst of the danger, every time.

That is how a god became one of Earth's truest friends.
