Long before Thor became a king, long before he lost Asgard, he had fallen in love with a brilliant scientist from Earth.

Her name was Jane Foster.

Jane was an astrophysicist — that is a scientist who studies the stars. She had wild curly hair that she was always pushing out of her eyes. She wore practical sweaters and beat-up sneakers and carried a notebook covered in equations no one else could read. She had spent her whole life looking up at the night sky, trying to understand it.

And then, one strange day, a god FELL out of that sky and landed in front of her car.

She did not run away. She did not scream. She got out, helped him up, and asked him a hundred quick scientific questions about the inter-dimensional phenomenon she had just witnessed.

That was the moment Thor first met Jane Foster.

He loved her almost immediately. She was unlike anyone he had ever met. She was small. She was mortal. She was, in many ways, fragile compared to him. But she had a mind like a sword, a heart like a furnace, and a courage that made even an Asgardian prince stand up a little straighter.

Their love was beautiful. It was also complicated. Thor was a god of thunder with duties across the Nine Realms. Jane was a scientist with a life on Earth. They drifted apart, eventually, the way long-distance loves sometimes do. But the love itself never disappeared. It simply moved into a different room of the heart.

Years passed.

Then one terrible day, Jane Foster received news she did not want to hear. She had been diagnosed with cancer. Despite her brilliance and her best fight, the doctors said the illness was serious. She began chemotherapy — the long hard medicine that would help her fight — and she carried on with her work and her life with the quiet brave grace she had always had.

But she also began, late at night, to dream.

She dreamed of a hammer. The shattered pieces of Mjolnir, which had broken when Hela came. They had been left behind in the ruins of Asgard, then scattered across the cosmos. In Jane's dreams, the pieces called to her.

She told no one. She thought she was just having strange dreams.

The dreams kept coming.

One night, weak from chemotherapy, Jane drove to New Asgard — the small Norwegian fishing village where the surviving Asgardians had made their new home. She walked, in her wool coat and her beat-up sneakers, to a quiet spot on the shore where the broken pieces of Mjolnir lay scattered on a stone pedestal as a memorial.

She reached out. She brushed her fingers across the pieces.

The hammer responded.

The shattered Uru metal rose up into the air. The pieces spun and flew and assembled themselves, glowing white-hot. Mjolnir was whole again. Thunder rolled across the Norwegian sky. Lightning lanced down from the clouds — and where Jane Foster had been standing in her wool coat, there now stood a tall, golden-armored warrior with a flowing red cape and bright wonder in her eyes.

The Mighty Thor.

Jane laughed out loud, astonished. She lifted the hammer over her head. The clouds boomed.

She had become a god.

In her time as the Mighty Thor, Jane Foster did extraordinary things. She fought cosmic threats. She protected the Nine Realms. She did not just IMITATE Thor — she brought her own scientific mind to the hammer, using it in ways no one had ever imagined. She used it to read the energies of distant suns. She used it to send signals across galaxies. She fought with a creativity that surprised even the Asgardians.

But the power had a terrible cost.

Every time Jane transformed into the Mighty Thor, the magic of Mjolnir purged her body of the chemotherapy that was keeping her cancer at bay. Every transformation made her weaker as Jane, even as it made her stronger as Thor.

She knew. The doctors warned her. Her friends begged her not to use the hammer. Jane listened. Then she looked at the world — at the people who needed protecting, at the threats no one else could face — and she chose to keep going.

"The world needs a Thor more than I need a life," she told a friend quietly.

Thor — the original Thor — discovered her transformation one day in the middle of a battle. He was shocked. Then he was speechless. Then, watching her fight, he was filled with the deepest respect he had ever felt for anyone.

She was, he realized, more worthy of the hammer than HE had ever been.

She was reminding him of why he had fallen in love with humanity in the first place. Their stubborn courage. Their refusal to give up. Their capacity to do extraordinary things even when they themselves were small.

They fought side by side after that. Two Thors. A god and a woman who had become one. Lightning and lightning, working together.

The Mighty Thor's story reached its bravest chapter when a terrible enemy called Gorr the God Butcher rose up, seeking to destroy every god in the universe. Jane was already weakening. But she fought anyway. She fought beside Thor. She fought beside every god still standing.

In the very final moments, Jane used the last of her strength to do something extraordinary. She shattered Gorr's terrible weapon, the Necrosword, breaking his power forever. She knew it would be her last transformation. She knew the cost.

She did it anyway.

When the battle was over, Jane lay in Thor's arms on the broken battlefield. She was small again. She was Jane.

She smiled at him, very gently, and whispered something that made him weep. And then her spirit lifted, with the Valkyries who had come to escort her, up and up and up to Valhalla — the hall of heroes — where the bravest warriors of all the ages are welcomed.

Thor knelt where she had lain. The thunder above him rolled softly, almost like a lullaby. He wept the way a god weeps, which is the way any of us weep when we lose someone we love.

But he carried Jane with him after that. In his choices. In his kindness. In the way he held his own crown.

She had taught a god what it meant to be worthy.

Her thunder still rolls. Her lightning still flashes. Somewhere in Valhalla, the Mighty Thor is laughing with the warriors of old, and the heavens are a little brighter for her being there.
