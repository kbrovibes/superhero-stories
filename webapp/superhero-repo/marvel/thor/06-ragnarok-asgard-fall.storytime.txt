In the highest realm of the Nine Worlds, far above Midgard, sat the kingdom of Asgard.

Asgard was made of gold. Its towers rose like sunbeams. Its great rainbow bridge — the Bifrost — arched across the cosmos like a path painted by a giant. For thousands of years, Asgard had been ruled by Odin the All-Father, with his long white beard and his wise single eye, and the kingdom had been the wonder of the universe.

But in the ancient prophecies of the Asgardians, there was one word that everyone whispered with fear.

Ragnarok.

Ragnarok was the foretold end of Asgard. The day the golden realm would burn. The day the gods would fall.

For all his long centuries, Odin had worked to delay it. But Ragnarok was patient. And one terrible day, it came.

It began when Odin passed peacefully away on a quiet cliff on the coast of Norway. He had grown very old, and his time had simply come. But his passing released something the All-Father had been holding back with all his strength. Something he had hidden away from his children for their own protection.

Her name was Hela. The Goddess of Death.

Hela was Thor and Loki's older sister, though they had never known about her. Long ago, before Thor was born, she had been Odin's executioner, fierce and terrible. When she grew too cruel, Odin had locked her away in a faraway realm — and there, slowly, she had grown stronger and angrier through the long centuries.

The moment Odin's spirit released its hold, Hela burst free.

She came back to Asgard wearing a great spiked black crown and a furious smile. Thor tried to stop her with Mjolnir — his beloved hammer, forged in the heart of a dying star — but Hela was older than the hammer. With one quick crack of her palm, she SHATTERED Mjolnir into a hundred pieces.

Thor stood with empty hands.

In the chaos that followed, Thor was hurled across the universe and landed on a strange junk planet called Sakaar — yes, the same Sakaar where the Hulk once ruled. There he met an old surly drinking warrior who turned out to be a Valkyrie, the last of the legendary winged warriors of Asgard. He met Korg and Miek, who had been the Hulk's gladiator friends. He found, eventually, the Hulk himself.

And little by little, Thor gathered an unlikely team to bring back home.

But while Thor was building his strange new family, things in Asgard were terrible. Hela had taken the throne. She had raised an army of undead warriors. She had crushed the Warriors Three and any Asgardian who tried to stand against her. The kingdom was dying.

Thor came home in a stolen ship with his strange new friends. He charged into the final battle without his hammer, terrified that without Mjolnir he was nothing.

His father appeared to him then, in spirit, gentle and smiling.

"Are you Thor, the God of Hammers?" Odin asked.

And Thor, in a moment that would change him forever, understood.

He had never been the hammer. The hammer had been his TOOL. The lightning was his BIRTHRIGHT. The strength had been HIM all along.

Thor closed his eyes. He breathed in. And electricity began to crackle in his hair, in his palms, around his bare hands.

KRA-BOOM.

Thor became a living thunderstorm. He flew at Hela in a great arc of blue lightning, and her undead army scattered before him like leaves before a wind.

But even with all his new power, Thor realized he could not defeat Hela. As long as she stood on Asgard, she was unstoppable. Her strength came from the realm itself.

The only way to stop her was to do something no king should ever have to do.

Thor had to destroy his own home.

He turned to Loki — his brother, his constant trickster, the one who had betrayed him many times but who had also, somehow, always come back. "Loki," Thor said. "I need you to release Surtur."

Surtur was the great fire demon of prophecy, the one whose flaming sword would burn Asgard to ash. He had been imprisoned for millennia. Releasing him would fulfill the prophecy. Releasing him would end the kingdom.

But it would also end Hela.

Loki, for once in his life, did not argue. He nodded. He went.

Surtur rose. His great horned crown brushed the sky. His massive sword swung down through the rainbow palaces of Asgard, and the city Thor had grown up in began to burn.

Thor stood on the bridge of a refugee ship, his friends and his people gathered behind him, watching the golden realm vanish in a great flash of light. His face was wet. His shoulders were heavy.

But he was not broken.

He had finally understood the deepest truth of his crown. "Asgard," he said quietly to his people, "is not a place. Asgard is its PEOPLE."

The kingdom of stone and gold was gone. The kingdom of Asgardian hearts and hands — that was still here, gathered around him on the refugee ship.

Thor set a course for Earth.

He was no longer a prince of a golden city. He was now a king of a wandering people. He had lost his father. He had lost his hammer. He had lost his home. But he had gained something too.

A clearer eye. A simpler purpose. A people who needed him.

The twilight of the gods was over.

The dawn of a new kind of king was just beginning.
