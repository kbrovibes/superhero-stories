Long ago, in the golden realm of Asgard — a magnificent place at the heart of the Nine Realms — a prince was born. His name was Thor, son of Odin the All-Father and the wise Queen Frigga. He grew up tall and strong and very, very sure of himself.

Thor loved adventure more than anything. He would set out with his warrior friends — the fierce Sif and the brave Warriors Three — on bold journeys across the realms. He thought that being powerful meant being the best, and he spent his early years proving it in every way he could.

Then he made a serious mistake. Without his father's permission, he led an attack on Jotunheim — the frozen home of the Frost Giants — and nearly started a war that Odin had worked for centuries to prevent. Odin was not just disappointed. He was heartbroken. He stripped the armor from Thor's back and the power from his hands, and cast him down to Earth — to a dusty corner of New Mexico called Midgard — as an ordinary mortal.

And with Thor's enchanted hammer, Mjolnir, Odin placed a great spell: "Whosoever holds this hammer, if he be worthy, shall possess the power of Thor." The hammer fell into the desert dirt. Thor reached for it. It did not move.

A kind scientist named Jane Foster found the confused and powerless prince and helped him get his bearings. Thor had never needed to ask for help before. It was uncomfortable. It was also exactly what he needed.

When a terrible machine called the Destroyer arrived to finish Thor off, he did something he had never done before. He offered his own life to save the people of the town around him.

That act of selflessness was enough. Mjolnir flew back to his hand in a crack of lightning.

From that day on, Thor knew that true power begins with humility, and that the greatest kings are the ones who choose to serve.
