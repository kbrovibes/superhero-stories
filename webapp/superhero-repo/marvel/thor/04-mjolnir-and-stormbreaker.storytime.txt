Let us talk about hammers.

Not ordinary hammers — the kind you find in garages and toolboxes. The kind that have been forged in the hearts of dying stars, cooled in the cold between galaxies, shaped by hands that have made weapons since before the first kingdoms of the Nine Realms were built.

Mjolnir was made long ago in Nidavellir, the realm of the dwarves — the greatest craftspeople in all of existence. It was hammered from Uru metal, dark and ancient, and its handle was wrapped in leather the color of night. It was compact, shorter than you would expect for something so legendary, but dense and heavy with a power that went beyond weight.

The dwarves poured everything they knew into it. And Odin took it and laid on it the enchantment that would make it more than a weapon: "Whosoever holds this hammer, if he be worthy, shall possess the power of Thor."

Worthy. That word again. Worth is not about strength. Worth is about what you carry inside you — how you treat the frightened, what you do when no one is watching, whether you choose the harder right thing over the easier wrong one.

Mjolnir chose Thor, eventually. But it also chose Steve Rogers, on a terrible night when it was needed, which made Thor say "I knew it!" with the most enormous smile.

Hela destroyed Mjolnir with her bare hand.

And so Thor had to go looking for something new.

He traveled with two companions: Rocket Raccoon — small, sharp, technically brilliant, a raccoon in a way that he would strongly prefer you not comment on — and Groot, young and wooden and gentle, who could say only three words but meant a great deal with them.

They traveled to Nidavellir.

It should have been a place of fire and song and the endless ringing of hammers. Instead, it was cold and dark and silent. The forge fires had been out for a long time. Thanos — the great tyrant, the one who wanted to hold all six Infinity Stones and use their power to reshape the universe — had visited Nidavellir. He had wanted weapons. And when the dwarves had refused to help him, he had taken care that they would never help anyone again.

Only one dwarf remained: Eitri. The dwarf king. He was enormous — enormous for a dwarf, which means he was enormous in any terms — and he sat in the ruins of his forge in the silence, and when Thor found him, Eitri's hands were encased in solid metal. Thanos's way of making certain he could not build again.

But you cannot take the knowledge out of a craftsman. It lives in the mind, not just the hands.

"A king's weapon," Thor told him. "One worthy of stopping Thanos. Can you forge it?"

Eitri looked at the cold forge for a long time.

He said yes.

To forge Stormbreaker, they needed to restart the fires of Nidavellir — which meant opening the iris of a dying star. A giant shutter, locked shut. The star's energy was the furnace that could melt Uru metal. But the iris had been sealed by Thanos to prevent exactly this from happening.

Rocket flew the ship into the mechanism and began working frantically at the gears and hydraulics. Thor saw the only other option: hold the iris open with his own body.

He grabbed the mechanism with his bare hands and pulled.

The energy of a dying star — not sunlight, not warmth, but raw stellar radiation, the kind that had forged the original Mjolnir — poured over Thor like a river. It was like holding a lightning bolt from inside. CRAAAACK. BOOOOOM. His body was the door. His strength was the hinge. He burned white with the effort.

"It's killing him!" Rocket said.

Groot looked at the ax blade that had taken shape in the mold, at the finished weapon that had no handle, at the molten Uru still cooling. And Groot made a decision.

He reached his arm into the mold.

The handle of Stormbreaker — the greatest weapon in Asgardian history, mightier even than Mjolnir — was made from the arm of Groot. It grew from his wooden body and bound the axe blade and weight together, and Groot severed his own arm to complete the binding, his face calm with a kind of quiet courage that few creatures in any realm ever manage.

Thor grabbed Stormbreaker.

He was barely breathing. Burned, exhausted, his body shaking with stellar radiation. But his hand closed around the handle — the handle made from Groot's sacrifice — and the power of the storm filled him from inside out.

He opened his eyes.

He breathed.

He rose.

WHOOOOOM. Stormbreaker could summon the Bifrost all on its own — no Heimdall, no rainbow bridge needed. Just the axe and the intention, and suddenly you were anywhere in the universe.

Thor arrived at the Battle of Wakanda from the sky, lightning wrapped around him like a cape, Stormbreaker in his hand, and he landed with a sound that shook the ground and turned the tide of a battle that had been going very badly.

It was the hammer of a different era. Not a symbol of worthiness — a tool of purpose. Made in fire. Sealed in sacrifice. Carried by a god who had learned, at tremendous cost, exactly what was worth fighting for.

He held it steady.

He did not let go.
