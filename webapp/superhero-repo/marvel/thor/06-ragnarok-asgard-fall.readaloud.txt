Some stories don't have happy endings. They have brave ones instead. This is one of those stories.

For as long as anyone could remember, the word "Ragnarok" made Asgardians go quiet. It was a prophecy — a vision of the future written in the oldest books in the universe. Ragnarok meant the end of Asgard, the end of the age, the burning of everything. And the one who would bring it? A fire giant named Surtur, whose blade blazed like a sun.

Thor spent years trying to stop it. He traveled the realms, chased clues, searched for answers. He believed — the way you believe something with your whole chest — that Ragnarok could be prevented.

He was wrong. And being wrong turned out to be the best thing that ever happened to his people.

When Hela shattered Mjolnir and threw Thor from the Bifrost to the distant junkyard planet Sakaar, she thought she had won. She marched into Asgard. Her undead army poured out from the vaults beneath the palace. STOMP! STOMP! STOMP! Her great wolf Fenris, resurrected from ancient times, prowled the golden streets. Nobody could stop her. While she stood on Asgardian soil, she drew power from the land itself.

In a battle on Sakaar's fighting arena, Thor felt lightning rip out of his own body — not from Mjolnir, which was in pieces — just from him. KRAKOOM! It was the most powerful he had ever felt. His father's voice came back to him: "Are you the God of Hammers?" He wasn't. He never was. He was the God of Thunder, and the thunder lived inside him.

He fought Hela. He lost one eye in the battle. She was too strong, too connected to Asgard, too fueled by the land he loved.

That is when the idea came to him. An idea so painful it nearly knocked the breath out of him.

"Asgard is not a place," he told his friends. "It's a people."

So he did the unthinkable. He found Surtur's crown — the source of the fire giant's power — and placed it in the Eternal Flame. BOOM! Surtur grew and grew and GREW until he was taller than the palace, taller than the mountains, enormous beyond imagining. His flaming sword came down.

KABOOOOM! Asgard burned. Every tower. Every hall. Every beautiful golden thing.

Hela fell with it. The prophecy that had haunted Thor his whole life came true — and it saved everyone.

Thor stood at the window of a refugee ship, watching his home disappear into fire and ash. His one remaining eye filled with tears. All around him, thousands of Asgardian survivors breathed and lived and would live again.

He had traded a place for a people. And a people is always worth more.
