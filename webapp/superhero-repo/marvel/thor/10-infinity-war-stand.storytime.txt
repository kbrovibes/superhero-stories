The darkest chapter in Thor's long life began on a refugee ship in deep space.

He was a king now. He had just saved his people from Ragnarok. New Asgard was nothing more than a hope yet — the survivors were packed onto a great vessel, traveling through the stars in search of a new home. His brother Loki was at his side, finally, truly at his side, after a thousand years of mischief. His old friend Heimdall, the all-seeing guardian of the Bifrost, stood with them. The Hulk was aboard. So were the Valkyrie and Korg and Miek and the rest. It was a tired, happy, hopeful ship.

Then a much bigger ship appeared. A great dark vessel that blocked the stars.

It belonged to Thanos. The Mad Titan.

Thanos had begun his long terrible quest to gather the six Infinity Stones — the Mind Stone, the Time Stone, the Space Stone, the Power Stone, the Reality Stone, and the Soul Stone — the six gems of pure cosmic power, each a different color, each unimaginably old. With all six of them in his great Gauntlet, Thanos believed he could solve the problems of the universe with a single snap of his fingers.

He believed those problems would be solved by ENDING half of all living things.

He was wrong, of course. But he was very strong, and he was on his way.

He came aboard the Asgardian ship with his terrible Black Order. He had come for the Space Stone, the Tesseract — the glowing blue cube — which Loki happened to be carrying.

The battle that followed was the saddest the Asgardians had ever seen. Heimdall fell defending Thor. Loki, in a final act of love for his brother, gave his life standing up to Thanos. Thor was left battered and bleeding, drifting in the vacuum of space, when the great dark ship vanished into hyperspace with the Stone aboard.

The Guardians of the Galaxy found him drifting. They pulled him into their ship, the Benatar. They patched him up.

Thor sat up slowly. He looked at the strange new faces around him — Star-Lord, Rocket, Groot, Drax, Mantis, Gamora. Then he stood, his eyes burning with a cold focused fire.

"I am going to kill Thanos," he said simply.

Rocket the raccoon raised a hand. "Cool, cool. With what?"

Thor had a plan.

Mjolnir was gone, shattered by Hela in Asgard's last days. He needed a new weapon. A weapon that could match Thanos. So he set out, with Rocket and young Groot, on a desperate journey to the dwarven forge of Nidavellir — a great ring of metal that orbited a dying sun, where the universe's greatest weapons had been forged for a thousand thousand years.

The forge was cold when they arrived. The dwarves had been hurt by Thanos already. Their fires had gone out. Only one dwarf was left — Eitri, a quiet giant with terrible scars and steady hands.

To forge a new weapon, Eitri said, they needed the heat of the dying star itself. And someone — someone strong enough — would have to OPEN the great metal shutters of the forge and hold them open while the star's heat poured in.

Thor stepped forward.

The star burned him. The light alone would have killed an ordinary man. Thor held on. His arms shook. His face turned to red and gold. He poured every ounce of his will into the handles of the great shutters. The star's heat poured into Eitri's forge. The fires roared back to life.

Eitri worked quickly. A great axe-and-hammer took shape under his hands — a weapon of pure Uru, fitted to a handle of young Groot's own arm, which the little tree gave willingly.

Stormbreaker was born.

Thor lifted it. The forge trembled. The lightning of his birthright crackled along the great axe blade.

He was ready.

Meanwhile, Thanos had come to Earth. The Avengers were already fighting him in Wakanda. They were holding, barely. The Mad Titan had five of the six Stones. He needed only the Mind Stone, which lived in the forehead of the gentle android Vision.

The battle on the open plains of Wakanda was the largest the Earth had ever seen. The Dora Milaje chanted YIBAMBE in deep waves of thunder. The Avengers stood shoulder to shoulder with King T'Challa and his armies. Outnumbered. Almost overrun.

Then the sky split open.

A great pillar of multicolored Bifrost light crashed down into the middle of the battlefield. Out of it stepped Thor — bigger, stronger, with Stormbreaker crackling in his fist and Rocket and Groot at his side.

"BRING ME THANOS!" he roared.

The shift in the battle was immediate. Thor cut through the enemy armies like a thunderstorm. He flew. He struck. He turned the tide.

Eventually, he came face to face with Thanos himself in the middle of the battlefield. The Mad Titan was huge, his purple skin gleaming, the Gauntlet glowing with five Stones already.

Thor reared back Stormbreaker. He hurled himself across the battlefield. The axe plunged deep into Thanos's chest.

For a heartbeat, the universe held its breath. Thanos staggered. His eyes flickered.

But Thanos did not fall.

He looked up at Thor with terrible weary sadness. "You should have gone for the head," he whispered.

And he snapped his fingers.

The Snap. The Blip. Half of all living things blew quietly away into golden dust on the wind.

Thor sat in the dust afterwards, alone, looking at the spot where Thanos had been a moment before. He could feel everyone who was gone — Asgardians, Avengers, friends, strangers. He could feel half the universe missing.

The grief that followed lasted five long years.

Thor blamed himself. He went home to New Asgard. He stopped being Thor. He grew his hair long and his beard wild. He played video games and drank too much. He hid.

But his friends did not let him stay hidden forever.

When the Avengers figured out a plan to bring everyone back — the Time Heist — they came for Thor too. Rocket and Hulk found him in his little cottage. They took him by the arm. They told him he was needed.

Thor went.

His piece of the Time Heist took him back to Asgard before Ragnarok, before any of the loss. And there, by chance, he met his mother Frigga in the gardens of the old palace, on the day of her own quiet passing.

Frigga did not know he was the older Thor, at first. Then she did. She always did. She had always seen him.

She held his bearded face in her gentle hands.

"Eat a salad," she teased him quietly. Then, more seriously: "Everyone fails at being who they are SUPPOSED to be, Thor. The measure of a person — of a hero — is how well they succeed at being who they ARE."

He cried. He told her he had failed.

"You haven't," she said. "You are HERE."

He left her with a kiss. He came back to his own time carrying his mother's love and a new clarity in his heart.

The final battle, when it came, was beautiful and terrible. Thor stood beside Captain America and Iron Man, the original three Avengers reunited at last on a smoking battlefield. They faced Thanos one more time. They held the line.

In the end, it was Tony Stark who made the final sacrifice. Thor knelt with the others at his side. He wept again, openly, the way a god weeps.

But the Snap was undone. The lost came home. The universe survived.

Thor's stand against Thanos was not perfect. He failed. He grieved. He came back. And in coming back, he became more than he had been before — not a god of unbroken strength, but a god who knew what failure felt like, and who chose to rise anyway.

That, in the end, is the truest strength.

The thunder still rolls.

And when next the universe needs a god to stand against the storm, Thor will be there.
