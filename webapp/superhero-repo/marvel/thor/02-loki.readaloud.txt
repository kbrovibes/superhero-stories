Every great story has two brothers. This one has Thor, the God of Thunder, and Loki, the God of Mischief — and their story is one of the most complicated and most heartbreaking in all the Nine Realms.

They grew up together in the golden halls of Asgard. Thor was loud and brave and strong. Loki was quiet and clever and surprising. They were very different, but they were brothers, and for a long time, that was enough.

Then Loki discovered a secret. He was not Asgardian at all. He was the son of Laufey, the king of the Frost Giants — taken by Odin as a baby and raised as a prince without ever being told the truth. This discovery hit Loki like a hammer. He had always felt slightly out of place. Now he knew why. And the hurt that he had been carrying quietly for years turned into something darker.

He became the God of Mischief — scheming and plotting, always a few steps ahead, always wearing a different face. He tricked Thor into exile. He tried to take Odin's throne. He once sent an army of Chitauri against the great city of New York. He wanted to prove, more than anything else, that he was worthy of belonging.

But here is what made Thor different from any other hero: he never stopped believing in his brother. Even when Loki betrayed him. Even when it hurt. Thor saw the loneliness behind the tricks and the smiles, and he kept his hand held out.

In the end, when Thanos arrived and everything was darkest, Loki made one brave choice — and it cost him everything.

And that is why Thor carries his brother's memory everywhere he goes. Because even the most mischievous star still shines.
