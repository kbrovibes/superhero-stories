After the fire of Ragnarok and the terrible snap of Thanos, the Asgardians needed a home. They chose a quiet coastal village in Norway called Tønsberg, where ancient Norse people had told stories about gods for thousands of years. It seemed like the right place. They called it New Asgard.

It was quite different from the golden towers they had left behind. The houses were small and modest. The sea air was cold and a little salty. The neighbors were very surprised to discover that their new town had actual Norse gods in it. But the Asgardians were nothing if not adaptable, and slowly, carefully, New Asgard began to feel like home.

Valkyrie — fierce, brave Valkyrie — took on the responsibility of running things. She made sure her people were fed and housed and heard. She negotiated with the outside world on their behalf. She did everything a good leader does, every single day.

Thor, meanwhile, was not doing very well at all. He was grieving. He had lost his brother, his mother, his father, his home, and half his people. The weight of all those losses pressed down on him until he hardly left his house. A god who had once called lightning from the sky now spent his days being very still and very quiet.

But New Asgard grew around him. The people rebuilt. Children laughed in the streets. Tourists arrived to see the most unusual village on Earth. And slowly, Thor began to hear something in all that life — a sound like hope.

He returned to his calling. And when he did, he looked at Valkyrie and understood: she was the king this new world needed. Not him.

And that is how New Asgard taught its oldest resident the one thing he had never quite learned — that every ending is someone else's beginning.
