Of all the weapons carried by members of the Guardians of the Galaxy, none were simpler or more effective than Drax's daggers. While Star-Lord had his Element Blasters that fired discs of pressurized gas, and Gamora carried the sword called Godslayer, and Rocket Raccoon designed elaborate cannons that were bigger than he was, Drax carried two plain blades. No energy cells. No gadgets. No buttons to press. Just polished metal, wickedly sharp, balanced for his hands alone.

The daggers were forged on Titan during Drax's years of training there. A Titan metalsmith named Peliali worked the blades from a compound metal alloy that could cut through materials most ordinary steel only scratched. The hilts were wrapped in dark cord that Drax had re-wrapped himself a dozen times, worn smooth in the exact spots where his thumbs rested. He could throw them with an accuracy that made the other Guardians stop and stare whenever they saw him practice. From thirty meters he could split a single bolt into two pieces. Rocket once set up a row of tin cups just to watch.

What made the daggers more than just sharp metal was the intention behind them. Each dagger was named — a tradition Drax observed privately, never explaining it to the others. He called them Sorrow and Purpose. Sorrow reminded him of what he had lost. Purpose reminded him of what he had chosen to do about it. When he held them, he was not only holding weapons — he was holding the shape of his whole life, the before and the after, the grief and the determination. After many years, those feelings were not sharp and painful anymore. They were quiet and steady, like the weight of the daggers themselves.

There was a third thing the daggers represented: restraint. Drax was strong enough to fight with his fists and leave enormous dents in metal walls. He had stopped charging warships once by gripping both sides of a doorframe and refusing to move. But he chose to fight with blades because blades required precision. You could not throw a dagger and let your emotions do the aiming. You had to be still inside, just for a moment, to make the blade go true. Every time Drax threw Sorrow and Purpose, he was practicing stillness. Not a lesson he had been born knowing, but one the daggers had taught him, throw by careful throw.
