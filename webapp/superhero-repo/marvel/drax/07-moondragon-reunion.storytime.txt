Once upon a time, on the moon called Titan, a little girl opened her eyes.

Her name was Heather Douglas. She was very young and she did not understand what had happened to her family, and the stars outside the window were not the stars she knew. A man with kind eyes sat beside her. His name was Isaac, and he was one of the Eternals — a very old and very wise people who had lived on Titan for centuries. He had found Heather on that terrible night, small and alone in the Nevada dark, and he had brought her somewhere safe and warm and full of light.

"You are safe," Isaac told her. And she was. And she would be.

Isaac had a gift for recognizing extraordinary things in small packages. He had seen it before, in the long centuries of his life. And Heather Douglas — this quiet, serious, watchful girl who asked more questions than any child he had ever met — was extraordinary in ways that would take years to fully understand.

Isaac raised Heather with care and rigor. Because Heather's mind was extraordinary — by the time she was grown, she could reach into the thoughts of others and read them like pages in a book. She could lift objects with focused concentration alone. She trained at a place called the Monastery of the Dragon of the Moon, mastering the hardest things the mind can do.

She chose a hero name: Moondragon.

She did not think about Drax very often. She had been too small when they were separated to carry more than an impression — warmth, a big gentle voice. She had built herself from scratch, and she was proud of it. She was also, sometimes, very quietly lonely in a way she could not name.

And then one day she walked around a corner on Titan and almost walked directly into an enormous green man covered in tattoos.

She stopped. He stopped.

He was taller than the doorframe. His face was serious. His eyes were taking in every detail of her with an intensity she recognized, because she had inherited it.

"You are Heather," he said.

His voice was what she almost remembered.

"Yes," she said. "You are Drax."

"Yes."

They stood in the corridor for a moment that stretched and stretched, full of every unsaid thing in the world.

Drax looked at his daughter — at the careful, extraordinary person she had become — and felt the two marks above his heart press warm against his chest.

"I am glad," he said, "that you are well."

It was not much. It was everything he had.

Heather, who could read minds, did not need him to say more. She could feel what he meant as clearly as if he had shouted it from the tallest tower on Titan — warmth, relief, fierce pride, the kind of love that is also just a wall between someone and everything dangerous in the world.

"I am glad too," she said.

Their reunion happened in pieces after that — small and sideways and mostly during other things. In battle they were an extraordinary pair. She could feel what Drax was about to do just before he did it, and she moved to cover him before he even took the first step. WHAM, ZING — together they moved like something that had practiced for years. Which, in a way, they had.

They argued too. Heather spoke in abstractions that Drax found incomprehensible. He once asked her to explain a plan more clearly. She said it was "a calculated gamble." He asked what was being gambled and what the calculation was. She sighed.

But Drax never called her Moondragon.

Not once.

She was Heather. Always Heather. On missions, in arguments, in battle, even when she introduced herself to others as Moondragon and gave him a pointed look afterward.

"You are Heather," he would say simply, and go back to what he was doing.

The other Guardians learned to watch her face when he did this: a very small, very controlled, very private warmth — the face of someone receiving a gift they were pretending not to want.

Once upon a time, a family was broken. Two people became extraordinary in entirely different ways — one a warrior of the body, one a warrior of the mind. And then, by the long strange kindness of the universe, they found each other again.

Not in the way of stories with big embraces. In the way of real things — slowly, through arguments and battles and one name, said simply, again and again.

Heather.

Close your eyes. Sometimes the things that matter most don't announce themselves. They just stay.

Sleep well.
