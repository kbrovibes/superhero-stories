Once upon a time, before Drax the Destroyer learned to throw a single dagger, before he trained for a single morning on Titan, before he fought a single battle in the long cold between the stars — he sat still, in a quiet room, and received his marks.

The room was small and warm and smelled of something herbal. There was a low table and a carefully laid set of instruments, and a practitioner named Sera, who had very precise hands and had been doing this work for many years. Mentor stood in the doorway and watched.

Drax sat with his arms out and did not flinch.

Sera worked from the shoulders downward, in the traditional order of the Kylosian warrior rites. She explained each mark as she made it — not because Drax would remember the words, but because the words were part of the ritual and the ritual was the point.

These first marks, she said, are called the Marks of Becoming.

Her hands moved with the certainty of someone painting a picture they had long imagined. The lines curved over Drax's wide shoulders in a pattern that looked, from a distance, like a pair of wings spreading outward toward the world.

These marks say: you came from somewhere. You were someone before this. That person is not lost — he is the foundation of everything that stands here now.

Drax looked at the lines on his shoulder. He did not remember Arthur Douglas. He did not remember Nevada or the car or Heather's small face against the window. Mentor had arranged it that way — not cruelty, but necessity. The memory would have made grief too heavy to carry. But something in Drax's chest went quiet and warm when the Marks of Becoming settled on his skin, the way a room goes quiet when someone lights a lamp.

He sat still.

Sera moved to his forearms.

These, she said, are the Oaths of Protection.

Long, careful lines running from elbow to wrist — purposeful, like a road drawn on a map. This mark is a promise, she explained. A permanent one. You are saying: I will put myself between those who cannot protect themselves and whatever threatens them. In a language that does not wash off.

Drax felt something lock into place in him, quiet and certain as a key in a lock.

Last came the marks above his heart.

Sera looked at Mentor. Mentor looked at Drax. There was a pause.

"Where do you want these?" Sera asked.

Drax put one large finger on his chest, above his left side, and tapped twice.

"Here," he said.

He did not know their names. He knew only that there was a hollow in him shaped like love, and that it had a warmth to it, which meant whatever had been there had been good. That warmth deserved marks — deserved to be written on the outside where it could not be taken from him.

Sera made two matching marks, small and precise, directly above his heart.

Drax looked down at them for a long time.

"Good," he said finally.

— — —

Years later, on an ice planet called Berhert, Gamora stood in the doorway of a supply depot watching snow spiral down in great grey sheets, and Drax walked out into it wearing nothing above the waist.

"Drax," she said. "It is very cold."

"I do not feel the cold," he said, and disappeared into the blizzard.

Gamora stood in the doorway. She could still see the lines of his tattoos moving through the white — the Marks of Becoming on his shoulders, the Oaths on his forearms, the two small marks above his heart. Seen through snow, they looked more like what they were: permanent, written in a language that did not change.

She pulled her jacket tighter and watched until he was out of sight.

There were many strange and wonderful things about Drax. His strength, which could shake a room. His honesty, which could also shake a room. The way he looked at small, growing things — baby Groot in a pot of soil, a seedling he had quietly watched over — with a gentleness that seemed to come from somewhere very deep.

But the tattoos were the thing Gamora thought about on the long quiet nights between the stars. In a body that was not the one he had started with, in a life handed to him by someone else, Drax had found a way to keep his own history. Written on the outside. Carried everywhere. Permanent as stone.

You cannot make him forget what is written on him. You cannot take it away.

Close your eyes and think of the things you carry that are yours — deep and permanent, written in you, that no one can reach. Those are worth honoring.

Sleep well.
