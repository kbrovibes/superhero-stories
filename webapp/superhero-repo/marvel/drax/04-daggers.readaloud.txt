Time for a very important question. Are you paying attention? Good.

If you were a space warrior with enough strength to bend metal beams and stop a charging warship with your bare hands — what weapon would YOU choose?

A giant cannon? A laser sword? Some kind of enormous exploding thing?

Drax the Destroyer chose two daggers.

That is IT. Two blades. No buttons. No energy cells. No remote control. No instructions. Two polished daggers, balanced perfectly for his hands, sharpened to a point that could cut through things ordinary metal only scratched.

Rocket Raccoon, who loves enormous weapons, once asked Drax about this.

"You can bench-press a spaceship," Rocket said. "You could carry the biggest gun I have ever built. Why two tiny knives?"

Drax looked at his daggers. Then at Rocket. Then back at the daggers.

"They are not tiny," he said.

The daggers were forged on Titan by a metalsmith named Peliali, using a special alloy — a very strong metal mix — that Drax had tested until it met his standards. The hilts were wrapped in dark cord, worn smooth in exactly the spots where Drax's thumbs rested. He could throw them from thirty meters away — that is longer than three school buses parked end to end — and hit a target the size of a coat button.

One day on the Milano, Rocket set up a row of tin cups on a shelf. Just to watch.

WHOOSH. WHOOSH. WHOOSH.

Every single one. Without Drax even looking at the last two.

"Okay," Rocket muttered. "Fine. FINE."

But here is the most important part. Each dagger had a name. Drax never told the others — it was his private thing, carried quietly the way he carried most important things. He called them Sorrow and Purpose.

Sorrow. For what he had lost — his wife Yvette, his ordinary life, that long quiet Nevada road.

Purpose. For what he had chosen to do about it.

And when he threw a dagger, something interesting happened. He had to be absolutely still inside — just for one breath — to make the blade fly true. You cannot let your feelings aim for you. You have to breathe, and go quiet, and then — WHOOSH — let it go.

That quiet moment before the throw? It was the hardest thing for Drax to learn. He practiced it every single morning before the rest of the Guardians woke up. Alone in the training bay. Still as a stone. Then — CRACK. Hit.

Star-Lord once asked him the secret.

"You must be still," Drax said, "before you can be precise."

Peter Quill thought about that for a very long time.

Two daggers. Two names. One warrior who chose precision over power, every single day.

WHOOSH. Perfect aim. Every time.
