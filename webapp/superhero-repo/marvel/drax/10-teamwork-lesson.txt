There came a time when the Guardians of the Galaxy took on a mission to defend a small mining settlement called Vesper Station, which orbited a planet whose name no one could pronounce and everyone on board the Milano agreed to call Gravel Planet. The settlement was being threatened by a crime syndicate called the Sovereignty, and the miners — forty-three of them, including twelve children — had nowhere to turn. Vesper Station sent one distress call into the galaxy and hoped. The Guardians picked it up.

The mission did not go cleanly. The Sovereignty had sent more agents than expected. Gamora and Rocket disabled a weapons array on the station's underside while Star-Lord and Nebula ran interference from the Milano. That left Drax on the station itself with the forty-three miners, twelve children, and the task of keeping them all in one place and safe.

He had held lines before. He had never held a line that included twelve frightened children asking questions.

A child named Mirava, about six years old, was crying and could not be comforted. He sat on the floor beside her — which put his eyes at roughly her height — and told her plainly that she was safe. He named every Guardian and what each one was doing right now to protect this station. He said: Gamora is the best warrior you will ever meet. He said: Rocket is a genius and the weapons array is handled. He said: I am here and I will not move and nothing will get past me.

Mirava stopped crying. She asked if the raccoon was really as smart as everyone said. Drax said yes, without qualification or pause.

When the mission was over, Rocket came to find Drax and stopped in the doorway. Drax was sitting on the floor with Mirava asleep against his arm, her small head against his tattoos, the other children nearby in various states of tired calm. "You've got to be kidding me," Rocket said. Drax looked at him with quiet dignity. "I was protecting them," he said.

He was right. He had been doing exactly that. And Mirava would grow up to be a mining engineer, and she would name her first deep-core drill the Destroyer, and she would never in her life be afraid of the dark.
