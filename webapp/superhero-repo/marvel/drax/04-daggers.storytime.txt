Once upon a time, in a training yard on the planet Titan, a newly made warrior stood alone at dawn and threw a dagger at a wall.

It hit exactly where he intended. He walked to the wall, pulled it free, walked back to the same spot, and threw it again.

Hit.

Again.

Hit.

He did this every morning, before anyone else was awake, before the light changed from grey to gold. The other warriors on Titan slept. The metalsmith named Peliali was still in his forge. The whole world was quiet. And Drax the Destroyer stood alone and threw his daggers at the wall until his arms felt warm and his mind felt clear.

Now. Drax was stronger than almost any being in the galaxy. His body had been built by a cosmic entity for the express purpose of being strong — strong enough to challenge the most dangerous warlord alive. He could grip a doorframe and stop a warship from moving. He could punch through a wall of solid rock and leave a crater in the shape of his fist. He had done these things. They were not impressive to him. They were simply what he was.

But the daggers were something he chose.

He had gone to Peliali early in his training and asked for two blades. Peliali was an old man with careful eyes, and he did not ask why a warrior who could fight with his hands would want knives. He simply asked: what weight, what edge, what balance? And Drax thought about it for a long time — longer than you might expect — and described blades that would be heavy enough to feel certain in his hand and light enough to fly straight and fast. Peliali nodded and went to his forge.

Two weeks later the daggers were ready. They were beautiful in a plain way — no decoration, no inlay, just clean lines of a very strong metal alloy, hilts wrapped in dark cord. Drax held them and felt something click into place inside him, the way a door settles properly on its hinges.

He named them privately, because naming things felt important to him and he did not know how to explain that to anyone else.

He called the first one Sorrow.

He called the second one Purpose.

Sorrow, because he carried it — the quiet sadness of a man who had lost everything, the knowledge of a family on a desert road, the terrible finality of one bad night. He did not push it away. He held it, like the blade, and knew its weight.

Purpose, because he had chosen something. He had not collapsed under the sorrow. He had stood up, and trained, and found direction, and gone out into the stars. Purpose was the answer to Sorrow. Not a cure. An answer.

The other Guardians knew about the daggers, of course — it was hard to miss them. Rocket once asked about the names, tilting his head.

"Do your knives have names?"

"Yes," said Drax.

A pause. "What are they?"

Drax looked at the blades for a moment. "My daggers," he said.

Rocket waited for more. More did not come.

What the Guardians did notice was that Drax threw the daggers unlike anyone they had seen throw anything. He would go completely still first — one breath, one beat of quiet — and then the dagger would leave his hand and fly with the focused sureness of a thought clearly finished. No hesitation in the throw itself. All the hesitation came before it, and was resolved in that single breath.

Star-Lord once asked him how he got so accurate.

"You must be still," Drax said, "before you can be precise."

Peter Quill thought about that for quite a while.

The daggers grew worn and smooth in the places Drax's hands held them every day. The cord wrapping was replaced many times, always the same dark color, always wound the same way. The blades were sharpened on the same whetstone Peliali had given him. There were small nicks and marks on the metal now from years of hard use — from battles across a dozen worlds, from ice planets and desert moons and the broken ruins of Titan.

But the balance was exactly the same. The weight was exactly the same.

Sorrow and Purpose.

Still there. Still steady.

In the dark of the Guardian's ship, when everyone else was asleep, Drax sometimes held both daggers and turned them slowly in the starlight coming through the porthole. He was not sad when he did this. He was something quieter than sad — something that felt like understanding, or acceptance, or maybe just the peace of a man who knows exactly what he is carrying and why.

He set the daggers down gently. He closed his eyes.

In the morning he would throw them again. Hit. Hit. Hit.

Every morning. Every single one.

Close your eyes now. It is all right to carry something heavy, as long as you also carry something that knows where to go.

Sleep well.
