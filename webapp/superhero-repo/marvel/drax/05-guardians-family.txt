The Guardians of the Galaxy came together in the least dignified way possible for a group of heroes. They met in a prison. Star-Lord, whose real name was Peter Quill, had stolen a silver orb containing the Power Stone and been immediately captured by the Nova Corps. Gamora had come to take the orb for her own reasons. Rocket and Groot had come to collect a bounty on Quill's head. And Drax had been sitting in the Kyln prison long before any of them arrived, waiting for anyone connected to Thanos to walk through the door.

Drax did not immediately consider them friends. He considered Gamora an enemy — she was Thanos's adopted daughter, and he had to be persuaded that she was fighting against Thanos, not for him. Star-Lord he found baffling. Rocket annoyed him. Groot he respected from the start, without knowing exactly why, which was unusual for Drax, who usually needed reasons. But they escaped the Kyln together, and then they survived the chaos on Knowhere together, and then they made a plan to save Xandar together, and somewhere in the middle of all that — between the arguments and the disasters and the moments when Rocket said something that everyone pretended not to find funny except that they all found it funny — the group became something Drax had not expected.

A family.

Not because they shared blood or the same planet or the same history. Because they chose each other. Repeatedly. In situations where no one would have blamed them for choosing something else. When Groot wrapped his wooden arms around the whole group and said the only three words he ever said — "I am Groot" — and sacrificed himself to protect them as their ship fell through Xandar's sky, Drax held on and understood exactly what was happening and felt something in his chest that was not grief but was adjacent to it. Groot came back, slowly, from a single twig. And Drax looked after that twig with the kind of attention he had once given only to Heather.

The Guardians made each other better in specific ways. Gamora taught Drax when to pause. Star-Lord taught him when to laugh, though Drax almost never agreed that the joke had been funny. Rocket, who was small and prickly and sometimes sad in a way he never admitted, taught Drax that strength does not always look like muscles. And Drax, in turn, gave all of them something that only he could give: the absolute certainty that when things got very bad, he would still be standing in front of them, daggers out, absolutely unmovable.

The galaxy was dangerous. The Guardians made it a little less so for everyone in it.
