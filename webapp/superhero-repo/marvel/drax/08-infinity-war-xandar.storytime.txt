Once upon a time, above a golden city, a shadow fell.

The city was Xandar — home to the Nova Corps and their great domed headquarters and the wide avenues where children chased each other through the parks. A city that had never asked for any of this. A city that was simply there, ordinary and alive, on an afternoon that had been unremarkable until the sky changed.

The Dark Aster came through the clouds like a continent of iron.

Ronan the Accuser stood at its bow, Universal Weapon in hand, Power Stone sealed into the weapon's head and blazing with purple light so intense it threw shadows across the deck below his feet. He had everything he needed. One strike was all it would take.

Below, the people of Xandar looked up.

And then, from the west, came one battered, patched, asymmetrical ship that had clearly been repaired using parts from four different vessels and the personal ingenuity of one very annoyed raccoon. The Milano. Flying straight at the Dark Aster and Ronan's entire fleet.

The Guardians had looked at the situation on approach and each had said some version of the same thing, in their own individual voices, and then they had taken their positions.

Rocket went first. He broke away from the Milano in a single-engine fighter and went directly at the warships — unreasonable for one small raccoon, and he did it anyway. BLAM — one warship listing. KABOOM — another engine cut out. Rocket flew between them shouting things no one could hear over the engine noise, which was probably fine.

Gamora cut forward with Godslayer, moving toward the source of the Power Stone's signal with the focus of someone who has exactly one goal and will not be stopped.

Groot unfurled in a corridor on the Dark Aster, his branches spreading to the walls on either side, holding the structure firm while the Guardians pressed forward. He looked at his friends with calm, ancient kindness. "I am Groot," he said softly — which meant: I am here, keep going, I have you.

And Drax held the forward position on the Dark Aster.

He did not charge ahead alone this time. He had learned something on Knowhere about the difference between bravery and impatience — the lesson of a battle lost, a challenge made too soon, a name called into the dark when he was not yet ready. That lesson had stayed with him in the place where lessons that matter actually stay. He held his position. He fought what came to him. He protected the corridor behind him the way a door is meant to protect a room.

When Ronan himself appeared, Drax moved. But not alone.

Gamora was there. Star-Lord was circling. Rocket had come back for another pass. And Drax drove forward with the full collected force of everything he was — the long training on Titan, the years crossing the galaxy, the warmth of those two marks above his heart pressing forward — and CRACK, the Universal Weapon's staff fractured under the blow, and Ronan stumbled back across the deck.

And then Peter Quill began to dance.

It was so entirely, bewilderingly unexpected that every armored Kree soldier on the Dark Aster stopped what they were doing. Ronan stared. Even Drax stared. Peter Quill was in front of the most powerful being in the room, pointing his fingers like guns and shuffling his feet to music from a small cassette player that had no business being in a space battle.

"What is he doing?" Drax asked.

"I have no idea," said Gamora. But she was already moving.

In that single distracted pause, the Power Stone changed hands. And then all five Guardians were around it, holding it together, the purple energy screaming through their hands and arms. It was painful. It was overwhelming. The kind of force that should have torn them apart individually.

Together they held it. And together they turned it on Ronan.

The Dark Aster fell. The sky above Xandar went quiet. The afternoon light came back through the dust, golden and ordinary — the kind of light that belongs to days when nothing terrible is happening.

Drax stood on the surface of Xandar and looked up at that sky. His whole body ached. His hands felt the weight of the empty air where his daggers rested. The city around him was still making sense of itself — sirens, voices, the Nova Corps organizing the aftermath.

He breathed in. He breathed out.

Ronan was finished. Thanos was still out there somewhere. There was more road ahead. He had known this since Titan, and he knew it now.

But this was done. He had stood with his friends on the day Xandar needed them, and they had held together, and the city below those golden towers was still standing.

For now, that was exactly enough.

Close your eyes. Sometimes the bravest thing is not charging ahead alone. It is standing firm with the people beside you and not letting go.

Sleep well.
