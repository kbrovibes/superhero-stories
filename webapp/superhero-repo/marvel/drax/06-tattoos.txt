The first thing most people noticed about Drax was his size. The second thing was the tattoos. They covered his arms from wrist to shoulder, traced lines across his chest and back, and wound in careful patterns up his neck. On a human body they would have been remarkable. On Drax's green skin they seemed like something older — marks that had always been there, records carved into living rock.

The tattoos were ritual markings applied on Titan before Drax began his training. The custom came from the Kylosian warrior tradition — a ceremonial practice where each mark represented a test completed, an oath taken, or a loss honored. Mentor had ensured that Drax received the opening marks before he was trained to fight, because Mentor believed that a warrior who fought without honoring what they were fighting for was only an animal with weapons. The marks came first. The training followed.

Drax could read every line of his tattoos. The first set, curved across his shoulders, were called the Marks of Becoming — symbols that acknowledged the life he had lived before, the ordinary human existence of Arthur Douglas, without which none of the rest made sense. The marks running down his forearms were Oaths of Protection, the formal declaration that he would defend any who could not defend themselves. Most personal were the two matching marks on his chest — direct above his heart — which honored Yvette and Heather. He had asked for those specifically. No one had to tell him where to put them.

The other Guardians noticed that Drax never wore a shirt, while simultaneously never being self-conscious about it in the way that someone showing off would be. He simply did not seem to notice or care about clothing in general, which led to a memorable conversation when Gamora suggested he might want a jacket on an ice planet called Berhert. "I do not feel cold," Drax told her. Gamora explained that she knew he did not feel cold, but that the other inhabitants of Berhert might find it unexpected to see someone dressed that way. Drax considered this. "They will adapt," he said, and walked out into the snow.

The tattoos were the most permanent thing about Drax — his history written on the outside where he could never lose it, even if he forgot everything else. In a body that was not the one he was born in, in a life that had been handed to him by a cosmic stranger, the marks were the thread that kept him connected to who he had always been.
