Heather Douglas had not died on that Nevada road. She had been found by the elder of a group called the Eternals who lived on Titan, a man known as Isaac. He raised her with discipline and care, training her mind as rigorously as others trained their bodies. She developed extraordinary telepathic and telekinetic abilities — the power to reach into minds and move objects with thought alone. She shaved her head and wrapped herself in dark clothing and trained for years in the monastery of the Dragon of the Moon on Titan until she became a hero known as Moondragon.

Drax and Moondragon were reunited on Titan in a meeting that went very differently from anything the stories you normally hear about reunions. There was no long embrace. There were no tears. Heather was a woman who had cultivated enormous control over her own mind, and Drax was a man who expressed his feelings mostly through action. They stood facing each other in a corridor of Titan's great compound, and Heather looked at this enormous green warrior covered in tattoos, and Drax looked at the calm and powerful woman she had become, and neither of them quite knew what to do with the other.

What they found, slowly and imperfectly, was a working relationship built on mutual respect. They did not always agree. Moondragon's telepathic abilities made her impatient with people whose minds moved more slowly, which Drax found rude. Drax's complete literal-mindedness made him resistant to Heather's more philosophical arguments, which she found maddening. They argued about strategy in ways that sometimes made the other Guardians quietly leave the room. But when the fighting started, there was something between them that worked — she could anticipate what he was about to do before he did it, which made them extraordinarily effective together, and he trusted her completely in a way that had nothing to do with strategy and everything to do with the fact that she was Heather.

He never called her by her hero name. She was always just Heather to him. She pretended to find this annoying. She did not find this annoying.

The reunion they had eventually, in pieces over weeks and months, was not a storybook moment. It was smaller and truer than that — two people from the same broken beginning, finding that they had both, separately and in entirely different ways, become remarkable. Drax could not have planned it. He could not have foreseen it. He would not have changed a single moment of it.
