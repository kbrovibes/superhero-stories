Once upon a time, on a long and quiet desert road that stretched across the state of Nevada, there was a man named Arthur Douglas.

Arthur was not a warrior. He was an ordinary man. He liked long drives and the sound of the radio in the evening, and most of all he liked the people beside him — his wife Yvette and his little daughter Heather, who had her nose pressed against the car window watching the stars.

The stars were very bright that night. Very, very bright.

And then one of them got brighter.

And bigger.

And then the sky above them split open with a flash of purple light, and the ship of a warlord named Thanos descended like a thundercloud, and in one terrible moment that was quiet and sudden as a blown-out candle — Arthur Douglas was gone.

The ship flew away. The desert went still.

But the universe was watching.

High above in the cold between the stars, a wise and ancient being named Mentor watched the darkness where the Douglas family car had been. Mentor was old and he was powerful, and he understood something most people do not: that the smallest, most ordinary life can become something extraordinary if given the chance to keep going. And Mentor felt something else — a kind of aching sorrow — because the terrible ship that had just passed over Nevada belonged to his own son. Thanos. His own son, who had wandered so far from kindness that even his father could not reach him anymore.

Mentor reached down through the darkness and found Arthur Douglas's spirit, still glowing, still warm.

And he made something new.

He built a body that was strong as a mountain, with arms like the roots of an ancient tree and hands that could catch a falling boulder without flinching. He pressed Arthur's spirit into that body and wrapped it in ritual tattoos that swirled like river currents across the wide green skin — each one a mark of purpose, each one a promise.

When the new being opened his eyes, he was lying on the surface of a planet called Titan, looking up at a sky full of stars. He did not remember Arthur Douglas. He did not remember the car or the desert road or the radio. But deep in his chest, like an ember that would never go out, he felt one thing clearly.

A name. Thanos.

He sat up, and the ground shook a little.

He stood, and the shadows around him leaned away.

His name, from that moment on, was Drax. Drax the Destroyer.

He trained on Titan until the other warriors there would not spar with him anymore because he was simply too strong. He practiced throwing his daggers until each one flew straight and silent as starlight. He did not stop. He did not tire. He was patient the way a river is patient — quiet and steady and going only one direction.

And then he found out something that changed the fire in his chest to something warmer.

Heather had survived.

His daughter — small Heather, who had watched the stars from the car window — had been found alive that terrible night by a kind man named Isaac. Isaac had raised her gently, and Heather had grown up brave and strong and full of light, until she became a hero herself, one they called Moondragon. She was out there somewhere in the galaxy, walking her own path, following her own stars.

Drax sat with that knowledge for a very long time, very still.

He was not good with feelings that were soft and quiet. He was better with feelings that were big and loud. But this one — the feeling that Heather was alive and well and brave — this one he held carefully, like something he did not want to drop.

There was one more thing about Drax that everyone who met him quickly discovered: he took every single word completely literally. Once, Rocket told him their ship was "dead in the water." Drax looked at the floor for the water for a full minute.

"We are in space," he announced. "There is no water."

Rocket put his head on the control panel and groaned.

The Guardians learned — slowly — that Drax was not confused or slow. He just lived in a world where every word meant exactly what it said. But they also learned that when trouble came, when darkness pressed in from all sides, there was no one in the galaxy they would rather have standing beside them.

Drax never told a lie. Drax never left a friend behind. And Drax, the Destroyer who had started as an ordinary man on a quiet desert road, turned out to be one of the bravest hearts in the whole wide universe.

The stars he looked up at now were the same stars Heather watched. That was enough. That was everything.
