Erik Lehnsherr chose the name Magneto the day he decided the world would never willingly accept mutants. He built the Brotherhood of Mutants — a fierce group including Mystique, Sabretooth, and Toad — and declared that mutants were the next step of evolution, born to lead rather than to hide. Professor X understood exactly how Erik had arrived at that conclusion. He had stood beside Erik in those long Oxford debates, had seen the pain behind his old friend's iron-grey eyes. That understanding made opposing Magneto the hardest thing Charles Xavier ever had to do.

The conflict began in earnest when Magneto targeted a United Nations assembly in New York City, planning to use a radiation device to turn the world's leaders into mutants — forcing acceptance by transformation rather than earning it through trust. The X-Men deployed immediately: Cyclops fired his optic blasts from the roof, Jean Grey held the panicked delegates safely in place with her telekinesis, and Beast moved through the building with terrifying grace. Professor X did not fight with fists. He reached out through Cerebro, amplifying his mind until he could feel Magneto's precise location in the facility's electromagnetic grid.

When the two men finally stood face to face in the assembly chamber, Erik raised a gauntlet of steel beams and bent them toward Charles without a word. Charles held up one hand. Not to block the metal — he could not do that — but as a signal: stop. Look at me. I am your friend. Magneto hesitated. One second. Two. In that pause, Jean Grey shielded Professor X, and Cyclops knocked the device from Erik's control with a single precise beam. The Brotherhood retreated.

Afterward, sitting alone in the ruined chamber, Charles held the Brotherhood's deactivated device in both hands and felt the full weight of the day. Magneto believed that fear was the only language the world understood. Charles believed that fear only produced more fear. Neither of them was entirely wrong about the world's cruelty. But Charles was certain — as certain as he was of anything — that building walls between people only made the distance greater.

He sent a private message, mind to mind, that evening. No words were spoken aloud. Just two old friends, one in a mansion in Westchester and one somewhere out in the world, sharing the same memory of late-night tea in Oxford and the same stubborn, unresolved argument they had been having ever since. Charles held no anger. He held only hope that one day Erik would find a path back to something gentler. That hope was also, quietly, a kind of love.
