Once upon a time, a teacher set out to find his students.

He went down to the Cerebro chamber, put on the chrome helmet, closed his eyes, and listened to the world. And the world told him: here is a boy in Nebraska who has worn a ruby-red visor every day since he was nine. Here is a girl on the Hudson River whose thoughts arrive in other people's heads whether she means to send them or not. Here is a young man in Ohio who can bench-press a car and recite the periodic table in the time most people take to lace their shoes.

One by one, Professor X found them. One by one, he knocked on their doors — respectfully, with patience, with the quiet message: I see what you can become, and I am in no hurry.

Scott Summers came from an orphanage in Omaha, Nebraska. Quiet, serious, careful his whole life, because the beams of energy from his eyes could cut through walls if he was not. He wore a ruby-quartz visor every waking moment and had learned to measure every movement twice. Professor X saw in Scott the soul of a leader: someone who thought before acting and took responsibility naturally.

Jean Grey came from Annandale-on-Hudson. She was telepathic — she could hear the feelings of people nearby the way you hear music from the next room — and also telekinetic, meaning she could move objects with her thoughts. Both powers at once, like two rivers sharing the same bed. She was working very hard to keep them both in order, and she was exhausted from working quite so hard at simply existing. When Professor X's message arrived, she felt an enormous, quiet relief.

Hank McCoy came from Dunfaith, Ohio. His mind was a marvel — genetics, biophysics, literature, music, languages — and his body was equally extraordinary: fast, flexible, strong, graceful in a way that surprised people who did not expect it. Hank had spent time being self-conscious about both things at once. It was Professor X who said, simply: "Hank, your intellect and your body are not two gifts. They are the same gift, described twice. Once you see that, stop apologizing for either one."

Hank thought about that for three days. Then he decided it was the truest thing anyone had said to him.

With Bobby Drake — Iceman — and Warren Worthington III — Angel — the first class of the Xavier School was complete.

The Danger Room was the center of their training: a chamber below the school that could simulate any environment — a glacier, a collapsing building, a crowded city street. Professor X watched every session from the observation deck, took careful notes, and never praised carelessly. When he said "good," his students knew he meant it.

Cyclops learned to make decisions when there was no clearly right answer. The Danger Room gave him situations where every option had a cost and the clock was running. Over and over, until Scott Summers understood: the best decision, made clearly and committed to fully, was almost always better than the perfect decision made too late.

Jean Grey learned to hold both powers open simultaneously — not switching between them but running them in parallel, like a musician playing melody and harmony at once. When she finally held both channels steady for a full minute, Professor X said one word from the observation deck: "There." She looked up, slightly breathless, and smiled.

But the most important thing Professor X taught them was not in the Danger Room.

It was in the classroom, over tea, in the evenings.

He taught them the history of mutation. The scientific version: every extraordinary adaptation that ever made a living thing stronger was, once, just one organism doing something that had never been done before. Mutation is not an accident. It is what life does when it becomes more itself.

Many of his students had spent years believing their mutations were mistakes. Things to hide. Charles replaced that story with a different one — factual, careful, consistent — not pushing the truth at them but setting it down clearly enough that they eventually picked it up themselves.

His bond with Jean Grey ran deepest. As her abilities grew beyond what he had anticipated, he worked with her privately. Long, quiet sessions talking about what the edges of her power felt like. He trusted her completely. She felt that trust the way you feel sunlight through a window in winter.

The X-Men went on mission after mission. And every time they protected a city — mutants and humans together — Professor X felt the argument he had been making his whole life become more visible, more real.

Humans and mutants. Not separate. Not in conflict. Together.

Sleep well. Tomorrow there is more to learn, and we are not done yet — not by a long way.
