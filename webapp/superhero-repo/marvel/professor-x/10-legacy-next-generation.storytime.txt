Once upon a time, on a Tuesday in November when the Westchester leaves had all gone down and the sky was the colour of old pewter, a girl named Kitty Pryde arrived at the Xavier School.

She was thirteen — small, quick-eyed, the kind of person who noticed things without seeming to be watching. Her parents walked beside her up the long driveway, trying to look calm and not quite managing it. They loved Kitty very much and were frightened of the thing that had begun to happen to her, not because it was wrong but because they did not understand it, and things we do not understand feel dangerous until we learn them better.

Three weeks earlier, Kitty Pryde had walked through the back wall of her school bus.

Not on purpose. She had been moving toward the rear door and the wall had simply not stopped her. Her molecules passed through the solid metal the way water moves through a cloth, and she had found herself standing on the road behind the departing bus with twenty-two classmates staring at her through the rear window.

She had been very embarrassed. Since then she had phased through the kitchen counter, two bedroom walls, the garden gate three times, and her bicycle, which her father had helped her out of.

She stood in Professor X's study and tried to be brave.

Charles Xavier smiled at her. "Hello, Kitty. Sit down — would you like some tea?"

For the next hour, he did not ask her to demonstrate her ability. He asked what she liked to read — science fiction, especially stories about first contact across large distances. What sport she played — ice hockey, she was a very good skater. What she thought about when she could not sleep — computer programming; she had been teaching herself for two years and was, she admitted, already quite good.

He listened with complete attention. The real kind. By the end, Kitty had entirely forgotten to be frightened. She was just talking.

He enrolled her that afternoon. In his private notes, he wrote: remarkable focus, remarkable precision, instinctive ethical awareness — asking "should I?" before "can I?" She will teach the others as much as they teach her.

He was right.

In her first year, Kitty wrote a computer program that improved Cerebro's search algorithms — she found an inefficiency that had been there since Forge originally built it, fixed it on a Saturday as a kind of puzzle, and then realized she had substantially improved the most important machine at the school. She became the Xavier School's unofficial ice-hockey instructor. And her phasing ability developed to the point where she could pass through walls, floors, and once an entire building, controlled and intentional. The older X-Men, who were not easily impressed, said things like "exceptional control" in the thoughtful tone that meant they were genuinely impressed.

But the best thing about Kitty Pryde was her questions.

Not "what can my power do?" — though she asked that too. Mainly: "When should I use it? And when should I not?"

Those were the right questions. The difference between someone with a power and someone with wisdom.

Professor X answered each one carefully, then asked her back: "What do you think?" Kitty would think seriously and give her honest answer. Her answers were almost always good. Sometimes they were better than his.

The Xavier School was not meant to produce a single type of person. It was a community where people learned to understand themselves and take responsibility for what they could do. Some graduates became X-Men. Others became doctors, engineers, teachers — all carrying the same understanding: being different was not a deficit, power required care, and other people's wellbeing was connected to their own.

On the last afternoon of Kitty's first year, she sat in his office and asked: "What is the most important thing this school teaches?"

Charles was quiet for a moment. He took it seriously.

"The choice to help others costs you something," he said. "Time, energy, comfort, sometimes safety. And it is worth it. Every single time."

Kitty thought about it. "Even when it's genuinely hard?"

Charles Xavier smiled — the smile of a man who had waited his whole life for exactly the right questions.

"Especially then," he said. "That is precisely when it counts."

Kitty stood up. She considered the wall. She walked through it — cleanly, deliberately, on purpose — and from the other side said: "Thanks, Professor X."

Charles laughed. A real laugh, warm and delighted.

The lights of the Xavier School were on all over the building. Students at dinner, at homework, at the ordinary noise of a place where people lived and learned together.

Close your eyes.

The dream is real. The lights are on. There is a place for you.

Goodnight.
