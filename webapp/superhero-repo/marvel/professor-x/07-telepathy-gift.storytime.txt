Once upon a time, a boy was born who could hear what everyone was thinking.

Not always clearly. More like sounds from a neighboring room — the general rise and fall of emotion, the shape of a feeling, the warmth or worry that moved through a person like water. The boy's name was Charles, and from the time he was very small, he knew whether the cook was having a difficult morning or the postman was in a hurry.

It was always there. Always on. Like a fire in the fireplace — you do not have to look at it to feel its warmth.

Over thirty years, Charles Xavier learned to manage it.

He built, inside his own mind, something like a set of curtains — careful, practiced curtains he could draw as close or as far as the situation called for. In a crowd, he drew them almost all the way closed. Alone with a student or a friend, he opened them a little further, because being truly present with another person required paying attention, and attention was something Charles considered a form of kindness.

His most precise skill — the one he was most careful about — was something he called a telepathic bridge.

A private channel between two minds, opened only with both people's full and willing consent. Not just words, but the full experience of an idea: the texture of a thought, the color of a memory, the exact feeling of a moment from years past, as vivid as the first time. Two people, understanding each other completely.

Charles used it very, very carefully.

Because the other side of his gift — the part he felt most seriously about — was this: entering another person's mind without permission was the most serious thing he could imagine. A violation of the most private place any person had. He had an absolute rule: he would not do it. Not for convenience, not for efficiency, not unless lives were genuinely in danger and there was truly no other way.

One Tuesday afternoon — unassuming sort of day for a very serious event — an assassin arrived in Midtown Manhattan with a device he intended to use to cause great harm. The police could not reach him. The X-Men were too far away. Thousands of people were in the streets below.

Professor X sat very still.

He found the man's mind in the great buzz of the city and moved through it, as gently as he could, until he found the place where the decision lived. The certainty. He changed it — just slightly, just barely — replacing certainty with doubt. The gentle, pressing kind that makes a person stop and wonder: am I sure? Is this right?

The man paused. He looked around. He walked away.

The city was safe. No one in those streets ever knew what had happened.

That evening, Professor X told the X-Men exactly what he had done. He did not soften it. He described it precisely and waited.

Jean Grey asked, quietly: "Do you feel different? After doing it that way?"

Charles was quiet for a long moment. The question deserved a real answer.

"Yes," he said. "Which is why I will not do it again unless there is truly no other way. Having the power to do something does not make it the right thing to do."

The room was quiet. Jean nodded. That was enough.

Here is what thirty years of reading minds had done to Charles Xavier: it had made him almost incapable of genuine hatred.

Because he had felt — from the inside — what fear looks like in the people who fear him. What anger looks like in people who oppose him — the anger that came from pain, that had been something softer once before the world hardened it. What loneliness looks like in every child who arrived at his school's door hoping this place was real.

Understanding someone is not the same as agreeing with them. You can understand a person completely and still believe they are wrong.

But understanding makes cruelty very hard to sustain. And it makes patience very easy to find.

Professor X had more patience than almost anyone on Earth. He had spent his whole life inside the most interesting, complicated, surprising thing in the universe.

The human mind.

He never quite got over how extraordinary it was.

Goodnight. Think good thoughts tonight.
