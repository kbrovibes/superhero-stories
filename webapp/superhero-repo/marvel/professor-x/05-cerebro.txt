Deep beneath the Xavier School, past the gymnasium and the med bay and the corridor lined with student photographs, there was a sealed circular door made of reinforced titanium. Behind it sat the most remarkable machine on Earth: Cerebro. Professor X and the mutant inventor Forge had built it together over eighteen months, soldering circuit boards and aligning quantum amplifiers in the predawn hours when the school was asleep. The machine looked like a helmet at its center — sleek chrome, connected by hundreds of wires to a spherical chamber forty feet across — and when Charles put it on, it changed the scale of his perception entirely.

Without Cerebro, Professor X's telepathy reached roughly a mile in every direction — enough to fill a room with awareness, to sense nearby minds, to communicate in real time. With Cerebro, his range expanded to cover the entire planet. Every mutant mind on Earth appeared to him at once, like a field of stars each carrying its own particular light. He could feel a teenager in São Paulo whose hands had suddenly become magnetic. He could hear the frightened thoughts of a child in Cape Town whose skin was turning blue. He could locate them, understand their situation, and plan how to reach them before they faced the world alone.

Using Cerebro required enormous concentration and care. Charles described it as standing in a river made of thought — the pressure of a billion human minds flowing past at once. He had trained for years to work inside that pressure without losing himself, to remain Charles Xavier, professor and teacher, while holding the awareness of a world. Sessions never lasted more than forty minutes. Longer risked exhaustion so deep it could last for days.

The machine's most critical use came during a crisis when the mutant-hunting Hellfire Club attempted to use a telepathic disruptor to scramble Cerebro's signal and prevent Professor X from locating a group of young mutants trapped in a government facility in Nevada. Charles sat in the chamber with the helmet on and felt the disruption hit him like a wave of static — cold, disorienting, like trying to read in a spinning room. He held on. He narrowed his focus from the whole world down to a single state, a single county, a single building, and he found them. One by one, mind by mind, he relayed their precise location to Cyclops, who led a rescue team in within the hour.

Afterward, Forge offered to add extra shielding to Cerebro's neural interface to prevent future disruption. Charles shook his head. More shielding would narrow the range. He would rather take the risk himself than reduce the machine's ability to find one more child who needed to be found.
