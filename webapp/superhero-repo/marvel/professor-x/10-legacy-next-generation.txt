Kitty Pryde arrived at the Xavier School on a Tuesday afternoon in November, escorted by her parents who were polite and nervous. She was thirteen years old, small and quick-eyed, and she had discovered three weeks earlier that she could walk through walls. Not metaphorically — literally through walls, her molecules passing cleanly through solid matter as if matter had simply agreed to step aside. Her parents' plaster, the kitchen counter, the garden gate, the back wall of the school bus she had accidentally walked through in front of her entire class. She stood in Professor X's study and answered his questions in a voice that was trying very hard to sound unafraid.

Charles talked with her for an hour before he talked with her parents. He asked about her interests — science fiction novels, ice hockey, computer programming — and listened with complete attention, not to assess her powers but to understand who she was. By the end of the hour, Kitty had stopped trying to sound brave and was simply talking. He enrolled her that afternoon. In his private notes, he wrote: remarkable focus, remarkable precision, instinctive ethical awareness. She will teach the others as much as they teach her.

He was right. Within her first year, Kitty Pryde had written a computer program that significantly improved Cerebro's search algorithms, become the school's unofficial ice-hockey instructor, and developed her phasing ability to a level of control that made some of the older X-Men quietly impressed. She also asked better questions than almost anyone Charles had taught. Not "what can my power do?" but "when should I use it, and when shouldn't I?"

What Professor X built at the Xavier School was not a factory for heroes. It was a community of people learning to be responsible. Every student who graduated carried the school's purpose into the world in their own way: some as X-Men in the field, some as doctors and scientists and teachers and engineers who happened to be mutants and who chose to use their understanding of difference to make their corners of the world a little more generous. The school itself was the proof that the dream was real — not a theory, not a wish, but a building with lights on and children learning inside it.

He sat with Kitty in his office on the day she completed her first year, and she asked him what he thought the school's most important lesson was. He considered it carefully, the way he considered everything. Then he said: "That the choice to help others costs you something, and is worth it. Every time." Kitty thought about that. "Even when it's hard?" she asked. Charles smiled. "Especially then. That's precisely when it counts."
