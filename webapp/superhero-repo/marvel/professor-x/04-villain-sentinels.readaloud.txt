Picture this: robots the size of buildings. Purple steel. Eyes that glow like hot coals. And they have ONE job — find mutants and stop them.

These are the Sentinels. And they are a BIG problem.

A scientist named Bolivar Trask built them, convinced that mutants were dangerous. He put his fear into a blueprint and his blueprint into fifty-foot-tall robots, and the government launched the first twelve from a facility in Washington, D.C. They rose into the sky like a wall of purple iron, and every alarm in Cerebro went off at once.

BLIP. BLIP. BLIP. BLIP. BLIP. BLIP. BLIP. BLIP. BLIP. BLIP. BLIP. BLIP.

Professor X sat very still.

Then: "X-Men," he said, in his calm, steady, absolutely-no-panicking voice, "we have work to do."

The Blackbird — the X-Men's sleek supersonic jet — launched in sixty seconds. Storm went to the sky first. She raised her arms and pulled lightning from a perfectly CLEAR blue sky — KRAKKABOOOM — blinding the sensors of the first Sentinel. Cyclops aimed and fired, two precise optic blasts at the joint where neck met chestplate — KRAKOOM, KRAKOOM — and two Sentinels went DOWN. Wolverine sliced through a third's control panel with his adamantium claws — SNIKT SNIKT — sparks flying everywhere.

Three down. Nine still coming.

Professor X made a decision that surprised everyone.

He reached out with Cerebro and found a specific mind — iron-grey, familiar, very far away — and sent one clear thought: I need you. Now.

Magneto arrived above Central Park, standing on a platform of levitated metal, his red cape moving in the wind. He did not say hello. Professor X did not waste time on pleasantries either. Forty seconds. Situation explained. Nine Sentinels on approach. What do you say?

Magneto looked at the approaching wall of purple robots.

"Obviously," he said.

He raised both hands.

FWHHRRRRRRRRR — every scrap of metal in nine Sentinels twisted, bent, pulled, and CRUSHED toward him at once. Fifty-foot robots folded like paper. Their programming screamed and failed. Their enormous forms shrank into small, crumpled, harmless balls of steel that fell gently into the park far below.

The sky was clear.

Professor X immediately sat down and wrote a message to the United States government. Calm. Precise. Three paragraphs explaining that building weapons to target an entire category of human beings based on their genetics alone was not a solution to anything. He signed it: Charles Xavier, Xavier School for Gifted Youngsters, Westchester County, New York.

Then he closed his eyes and sent three words directly to Magneto's mind.

Thank you, Erik.

He waited. And then — very quiet, very brief — he felt something warm come back.

Charles smiled.

Not everything about today was easy. But that tiny warm feeling? That was worth a lot.
