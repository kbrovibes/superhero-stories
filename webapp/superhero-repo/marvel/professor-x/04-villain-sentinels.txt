Bolivar Trask was a scientist who believed that mutants were dangerous, and he built his fear into steel. He designed the Sentinels — towering purple robots, each one fifty feet tall, programmed to detect mutant DNA and neutralize any mutant they found. The first fleet launched from a government facility in Washington, D.C., rising into the sky like a wall of iron clouds. When Cerebro lit up with the readings, Professor X sat very still for a long moment. Then he looked at Cyclops. "We have work to do," he said, quietly.

The Sentinels operated on a targeting algorithm that could identify mutant genetic signatures from three miles away. They moved fast for their size, crashing through city blocks with single-minded purpose. The X-Men intercepted three of them over the skies above Manhattan: Storm called lightning from a clear sky to blind their sensors, Cyclops disabled two with precise optic blasts aimed at the joints between their neck and chest plates, and Wolverine cut through the control panels of a third with his adamantium claws. But the fleet had twelve units, and twelve was more than a small team could handle alone.

Professor X made a decision that surprised even his most experienced students: he called Magneto.

The two old rivals met in the air above Central Park — Professor X in the X-Men's Blackbird jet, Magneto standing on a platform of his own making, his cape moving in the wind. They exchanged no pleasantries. Charles explained the tactical situation in forty seconds. Magneto listened, nodded once, and said: "Obviously." Then he turned toward the approaching Sentinel fleet and pulled.

Every scrap of metal in nine remaining Sentinels twisted toward his outstretched hand at once. They crumpled like paper cups, their programming screaming and failing, their enormous forms folding into compressed spheres of useless steel. The sky over Manhattan went quiet.

Afterward, in the Blackbird, Professor X drafted a formal message to the United States government, calm and precise, explaining that weapons designed to target an entire category of human beings based on genetics alone were no solution to anything — and that the X-Men would continue to stand against that kind of fear, always. He also sent Magneto a private telepathic note. Three words: Thank you, Erik. Somewhere in the clouds, he felt a brief, grudging warmth come back the other direction. Charles allowed himself a small smile.
