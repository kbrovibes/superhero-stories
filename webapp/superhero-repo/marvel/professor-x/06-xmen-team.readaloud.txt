How do you build the greatest team of heroes in the world?

One student at a time.

He used Cerebro. He searched. And one by one, he found them.

First came Scott Summers — from an orphanage in Nebraska. Quiet. Careful. Deeply serious. Scott's eyes fired beams of powerful energy, and he had worn a special visor every single day since he was old enough to understand what he could do. He would become Cyclops — the X-Men's field leader.

Then came Jean Grey — from Annandale-on-Hudson, New York. Already telepathic. Already telekinetic. Already working very hard to keep both those powers in balance at the same time. She would become one of the most gifted mutants Professor X had ever encountered.

Then came Hank McCoy — from Dunfaith, Ohio. Genius-level brilliant. Incredibly athletic. Already a little embarrassed by how easily he could do most physical things. He would become the Beast.

Add Iceman. Add Angel. And you have the first class of the Xavier School.

Now here is what Professor X taught them in the Danger Room — the school's programmable combat training chamber:

He taught Cyclops to make decisions when there was no perfect answer. DECIDE. Lead.

He taught Jean Grey to keep TWO channels of her power open simultaneously. Telepathy AND telekinesis, both under control. That is very hard. She did it.

He taught Beast that his amazing brain and his amazing body were not TWO gifts — they were the SAME gift, expressed twice. Once you see that, you can use them together in ways that surprise even yourself.

But here is the thing Professor X gave them that mattered most of all: vocabulary.

Most of his students had spent years thinking their mutations were mistakes. Accidents. Embarrassments. Things to hide.

Professor X sat them down — calm, factual, precise — and explained: every adaptation that ever made a species stronger was, once, just one organism doing something unusual. Mutation is not a mistake. It is evolution. It is what life does when it is becoming something remarkable.

That knowledge changed how they thought about themselves. That is the most powerful thing a teacher can give.

His bond with Jean Grey ran deepest of all. She was gifted beyond what he had expected, and careful beyond what most gifted people manage. He worked with her privately on techniques for staying centered as her abilities grew. He trusted her completely and she felt it — the way you feel warm sunlight coming through a window on a cold morning.

The X-Men went on mission after mission. And every time they protected a city — mutants and humans together — they wrote one more sentence in Professor X's oldest argument:

We can coexist.

We can do more than coexist.

We can THRIVE.
