Here is a question for you: what do you do when your best friend becomes your greatest rival?

That is exactly the problem Charles Xavier faced — because his old friend Erik Lehnsherr had decided to call himself Magneto, and Magneto had a plan that Professor X could not allow.

Erik built the Brotherhood of Mutants — a fierce crew that included Mystique, who could transform into ANY person she liked, and Sabretooth, enormous and snarling, and quick, acrobatic Toad. Magneto believed mutants were the next step in human evolution. He believed that if the world would not accept mutants willingly — then he would MAKE it accept them by force.

And his first target? The United Nations in New York City!

WHOOOMP. The X-Men deployed the moment they got word. Cyclops lit up the rooftop with his optic blasts — KRAKOOM! Jean Grey used her telekinesis to hold the terrified delegates safely in place — they floated inches off the ground, perfectly unharmed. Beast bounded through the hallways with incredible speed, cutting off the Brotherhood's escape route.

And Professor X? He sat perfectly still. He closed his eyes. He reached through Cerebro and felt the whole building in his mind — every heartbeat, every footstep — until he found the one he was looking for.

Erik. There. Third floor. Moving fast.

When they came face to face in the great assembly chamber, Magneto raised a dozen steel beams and aimed them at Charles. Professor X did not flinch. He raised one hand — just one hand — slowly and steadily.

Stop. Look at me. I am still your friend.

Magneto hesitated. One second. Two.

That was all Jean Grey needed. WHOOOOSH — she shielded Charles. KRAKOOM — Cyclops knocked the device clear across the room. The Brotherhood retreated into the grey New York sky.

Later, alone in the quiet, Charles held the broken device and thought about his friend. Magneto was not wrong that the world could be cruel — he had seen things that gave him real reasons to be angry. But building walls between people only made the gap wider. That was the part Erik could not yet see.

That evening, Professor X closed his eyes — and sent a quiet, invisible message across the miles. No anger. No lectures. No "I told you so." Just the memory of two young men at Oxford, drinking tea, arguing about everything, and laughing anyway.

He still believed in his friend. He always would.

Come back, Erik. The door is still open.

It always will be.
