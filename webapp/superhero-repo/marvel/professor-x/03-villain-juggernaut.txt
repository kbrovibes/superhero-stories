Cain Marko had been Charles Xavier's stepbrother long before either understood what that word truly meant. They had shared a home, a dinner table, and a great deal of unhappiness, and when they grew up, Cain made it clear that he resented everything about Charles — his brilliance, his calm, his effortless success. So when Cain discovered the mystical Crimson Gem of Cyttorak during a military mission in Korea and touched it, the transformation that followed felt almost inevitable. The gem's ancient power poured into him, making him unstoppable: a living juggernaut of armored muscle and mystical force who could not be slowed by any physical barrier on Earth. He took that power and pointed it directly at the Xavier mansion.

The attack came on a Thursday morning, as the students were in the middle of a mathematics lesson. BOOM. BOOM. BOOM. The footsteps were audible two miles away, and by the time Juggernaut reached the mansion's outer wall, every student had been evacuated to the sublevels by Cyclops and Colossus. The outer wall went down like paper. Professor X kept his voice steady over the intercom, giving calm instructions, watching the security feeds on his monitors. He already knew that his telepathy had no effect on the Crimson Gem's helmet — Cain had learned long ago that the metal dome sealed his mind against any psychic intrusion.

Physical force alone could not stop Juggernaut. Jean Grey managed to slow him with telekinesis for ninety seconds before the mystical power overwhelmed her. Beast tried to knock the helmet free while Cyclops created a barrier of rubble. The strategy was precise and brave and it still fell short. Professor X watched from the war room, thinking through every option, calm in a way that looked like stillness but was actually deep concentration.

Then he spoke quietly to Cyclops over the communicator. He had noticed something: Juggernaut was following a single path, a straight line toward the main building. He was relying on the helm to protect his mind. He was not thinking tactically at all — he was just charging. Charles had known Cain his whole life. He knew exactly what that meant: Cain's anger had made him careless.

The X-Men lured him into the underground tunnel system, where his size worked against him. Beast dropped the ceiling section at precisely the right moment, burying Juggernaut under thirty tons of reinforced concrete. When Cain's helmet cracked loose in the impact, Professor X reached in — just for a moment — and gently suggested that rest was a good idea. The enormous figure went quiet under the rubble.

Charles sat in the silence afterward and thought about the boy Cain had been before all this. It was a complicated kind of sadness.
