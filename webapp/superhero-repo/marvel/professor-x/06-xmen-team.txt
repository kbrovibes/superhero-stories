Professor X did not recruit his first X-Men with advertisements or speeches. He found them through Cerebro, one mind at a time, and knocked on their doors quietly, with respect, and with the patience that said: I see what you can be, and I am in no hurry. Cyclops — Scott Summers — arrived from an orphanage in Nebraska, controlled and carrying a terrible responsibility behind his visor. Jean Grey came from Annandale-on-Hudson, her telepathy and telekinesis already straining against her careful control. Beast, whose real name was Hank McCoy, came from Dunfaith, Ohio — extraordinary intelligence combined with athletic grace that embarrassed his own modesty. Together with Iceman and Angel, they became the first class of the Xavier School.

Charles trained them not just in combat but in themselves. Every session in the Danger Room — the school's programmable training chamber — was designed to push a specific student's particular limitations. Cyclops learned to lead under pressure by making decisions when there was no right answer, only the least wrong one. Jean Grey learned to hold two channels of her power open at once without losing either. Beast discovered that his mind and his body were not separate gifts but the same gift expressed twice. When they fought together on real missions, the fluency they had built in those sessions showed in every movement.

What Professor X gave them most was vocabulary. Many of his students had spent years believing that their mutations were accidents, mistakes, things to be ashamed of. Charles taught them the history of mutation in biology — that every adaptation that ever made a species stronger had once been just a single organism doing something unusual. He was not sentimental about it. He was factual, precise, and consistent, and the precision was itself a kind of care.

The bond between Charles and Jean Grey ran deepest. She was his most gifted student, and the most careful one, and he recognized in her the same combination of power and responsibility that made great teachers take extra care. When her abilities grew beyond what he had anticipated, he worked with her privately on new techniques to maintain her focus. He never told her what he saw in the furthest edge of her potential — not because he was hiding it, but because some knowledge was better earned than given. He trusted her completely. She felt that trust the way you feel warm sunlight through a window.

The X-Men became more than a team. They became a demonstration. Every time they protected a city — mutant and human together — they wrote one more sentence in the argument Professor X had been making his entire adult life: that the two could coexist, and more than that, could thrive.
