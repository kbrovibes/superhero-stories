Here is how you know a school is great: the students start teaching the teachers.

That is exactly what happened with Kitty Pryde.

She arrived on a Tuesday in November, thirteen years old, quick-eyed and trying very hard to be brave. Three weeks earlier she had walked through the back wall of her school bus in front of her ENTIRE class. Not on purpose! She could phase through solid matter — her molecules passing right through walls — and she had not figured out how to stop doing it at surprising moments.

She stood in Professor X's study and answered his questions in a voice that was trying hard to sound unafraid.

Professor X did not pull out a clipboard. He asked: what do you like to read? What sport do you play? What do you think about when you can't sleep?

Kitty blinked. Then she answered: science fiction. Ice hockey. Computer programming — she was, she admitted, already pretty good.

They talked for an HOUR. By the end, Kitty had stopped trying to sound brave and was just talking. Like a normal conversation with someone genuinely interested in who she was.

She enrolled that afternoon. Professor X wrote in his notes: remarkable focus. Instinctive ethical awareness. She will teach the others as much as they teach her.

He was right.

In her first year — ONE YEAR — Kitty wrote a computer program that improved Cerebro's search algorithms. She became the unofficial hockey coach. And her phasing ability reached a level of control that made veteran X-Men quietly say "impressive" in the understated way of people who do not say that lightly.

But the BEST thing about Kitty Pryde was her questions.

Not "what can my power do?" — she asked that too. But mainly: "When should I use it? And when should I NOT?"

That is the question Professor X had been waiting his whole life for every student to ask.

On the last day of Kitty's first year, she asked him: "What is the most important lesson this school teaches?"

He thought carefully.

"The choice to help others costs you something," he said. "And it is worth it. Every time."

Kitty: "Even when it's hard?"

Charles smiled. "ESPECIALLY then. That is precisely when it counts."

Kitty stood up. She considered the wall. She walked through it — on PURPOSE this time — and from the other side said: "Thanks, Professor X."

Charles laughed. A real, warm, completely delighted laugh.

That is what a great school feels like.
