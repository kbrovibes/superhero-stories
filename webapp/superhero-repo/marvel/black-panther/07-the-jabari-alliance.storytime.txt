High above Wakanda, where the jungle gives way to raw stone and the air turns sharp and cold, the mountains begin. Not gentle slopes but jagged, snow-covered peaks that lean against the sky like old giants asleep. No magnetic trains come here. No Talon Fighters circle overhead. Up in these mountains, the Jabari people live exactly as their ancestors lived — and they prefer it that way.

They do not want your Vibranium.

The Jabari are proud and strong and have been both for a very, very long time. While the rest of Wakanda built towers and hovercrafts and neuro-link bracelets, the Jabari built their homes from the living rock of the mountain itself, carving great halls and winding corridors into the stone. Their weapons are hardwood — dense, ancient, heavy — and their warriors train by carrying enormous logs up the mountain in the snow. Just for practice.

Their leader is M'Baku, and M'Baku is exactly as large and formidable as you would expect from a man who rules a tribe of mountain warriors. He is tall as a doorframe and wide as two doors. His laugh shakes the walls. His battle cry — a low roar that the Jabari warriors echo back in a thundering chorus — sounds like an avalanche deciding to have an opinion.

M'Baku worships Hanuman, the gorilla deity, and the sacred animals of the mountain. He does not like city visitors. He does not care about diplomacy. He does not want to shake hands.

For many years, the Jabari and the lowland kingdom watched each other from a distance, neither fully trusting the other. Mutual suspicion built up over generations like frost on a stone wall.

Then came the day Killmonger seized the throne.

T'Challa had been defeated at Warrior Falls — pushed over the great waterfall by his cousin's battle-fury, lost in the thundering white water. His friends searched. The Dora Milaje searched. And eventually, they came to the one place left to try: the Gorilla Mountains.

They were not warmly received.

M'Baku stood in his great carved hall, arms folded, flanked by warriors who looked like they had been hewn from the mountain itself. He listened to the plea — bring T'Challa back to take his throne — with a face that gave nothing away.

Then he showed them something. T'Challa, barely alive, preserved in a bed of snow and mountain herbs, still breathing. Still fighting.

M'Baku had found him at the base of the waterfall. M'Baku had brought him here. M'Baku had kept him alive. He said nothing about why. He just looked at the gathered company with those deep, watchful eyes.

The Jabari came down from the mountains.

Their warriors joined the fight to reclaim Wakanda's throne, crashing into Killmonger's forces like a landslide — hardwood weapons THWACK-ing against armor, huge men moving with terrifying coordination. The Dora Milaje fought with Vibranium spears and technical precision. The Jabari fought with raw power and mountain ferocity. Together they were overwhelming — two entirely different kinds of strength woven into one unstoppable force.

After it was over, T'Challa did not simply thank M'Baku and send him back to his mountains.

He offered him a seat on Wakanda's tribal council. A permanent voice at the table. Not a lesser voice — an equal one. M'Baku sat down in that council room, took up a great deal of space, and immediately began providing exactly the kind of plain-spoken, no-nonsense counsel that royal councils desperately need.

Now T'Challa sometimes travels to the mountains to train with M'Baku directly — no suits, no technology, just old techniques and hard ground and two warriors pushing each other to be better. Can you picture it? The king of the world's most advanced nation and the chief of its most traditional people, side by side in the snow, laughing when someone lands face-first in a drift?

That is what Wakanda is becoming. Not just a golden city. Not just a mountain tribe. Something both — and something more.

Wakanda is strongest, T'Challa has come to understand, when all its people stand together.

Mountain and city. Hardwood and Vibranium. Ancient ways and new ones.

All of it, together, making something stronger than any piece could be alone.
