When Natasha Romanoff left the Red Room and joined S.H.I.E.L.D., she arrived with extraordinary skills — skills that had been drilled into her over years of training until they were as natural as breathing. Her hand-to-hand combat. Her languages. Her ability to read a room and a person at the same time. She brought all of that.

What S.H.I.E.L.D. gave her in return were the bracelets.

They didn't look like much at first glance — two sleek cuffs worn at the wrists, integrated seamlessly into the black of her combat suit so they barely showed at all. Engineers at S.H.I.E.L.D. had been working on them for years, refining the technology with each iteration. The current version was elegant, versatile, and absolutely devastating when it needed to be.

Natasha called them the Widow's Bite. The name suited them.

The primary function was electricity. The bracelets could emit a high-frequency electrostatic discharge — adjustable from a mild stun that would drop a human briefly without lasting harm, all the way up to thirty thousand volts, which was enough to stop someone with strength well beyond ordinary human capability. One careful twist of her wrist, a flick of the output setting with a motion so small it was invisible in the heat of a fight, and the choice was made. She could be precise about it. She usually chose to be.

But that was only the beginning of what the bracelets could do.

The grappling hook was built into the left-hand unit. A line fired with a sharp CLICK — catching on a rafter, a pipe, an architectural ledge — and Natasha was in the air, swinging across warehouse spaces and factory floors and the vertical faces of city buildings without breaking the fluid momentum of a fight. She had been trained in the Red Room to move through complex environments, and the grappling line extended that training upward and outward. She could change the level of a fight entirely, going from floor to rafter to suspended catwalk in moments, leaving opponents who had been certain of her position suddenly looking at empty space.

The right-hand unit carried the tear gas pellets and the sonic blast function. The tear gas was for crowds that needed to be cleared nonlethally. The sonic emitter produced a tight-beam high-frequency sound that disrupted the balance and concentration of anyone it hit directly — useful when someone needed a few disorienting seconds she could use.

One battle in particular stayed in her memory. An industrial warehouse on the outskirts of a city that will remain unnamed — a mission to shut down a black-market weapons network. The building was large, poorly lit, full of stacked crates and overhead gantries and a considerable number of men in very heavy armor, the kind with its own power systems and enough plating to stop conventional ammunition cold.

She assessed the situation in about two seconds.

Then she held her arms slightly out from her sides — a casual gesture, almost — and released a wide-pattern electrostatic burst that arced across the space between her and the armored group. ZAP-ZAP-ZAP-ZAP — the power units in each suit sputtered and failed in rapid succession, emergency systems fighting the surge and losing. The suits went dark. The men inside them were unharmed, but the armor that had made them confident was now just heavy, unresponsive metal.

She was in the rafters before they had fully processed what had happened.

Recent upgrades had enhanced the energy storage, allowing longer and more powerful discharges between recharges. Engineers had also added a direct computer interface function — she could press the bracelet's edge against a device's port and run a bypass without stopping to open a case or find a keyboard. Information at the speed of touch.

The Widow's Bite was not just a weapon. It was a statement about what Natasha Romanoff was and was not. She had been made into something controlled, something used. The bracelets she now wore were her own, built for her, chosen by her — tools of a protector who made her own decisions about when and how and how much.

She was not a weapon anymore.

She was the one holding it.
