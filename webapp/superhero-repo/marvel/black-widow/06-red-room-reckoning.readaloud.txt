Natasha Romanoff had believed, for a long time, that the Red Room was gone. That the programme that had stolen her childhood had finally been shut down for good. But some shadows, it turns out, are very good at hiding.

The Red Room had survived. Its architect, a cold and cruel man named General Dreykov, had rebuilt it — and this time, his new Widows had no free will at all. A chemical agent controlled their every action. They were not spies. They were prisoners.

When Natasha discovered the truth, she knew there was only one thing to do. She reached out to the people who understood her past better than anyone — her surrogate family. Yelena Belova, her Red Room sister. Melina Vostokoff and Alexei Shostakov, the two agents who had once played her parents in a long-ago mission. Years had separated them. Complicated feelings stood between them. But the shared memory of what the Red Room had taken from all of them bound them together in the end.

Together, they located Dreykov's secret airborne facility — a fortress floating in the sky — and they went inside. Not to destroy, but to free. When Natasha came face to face with the new Widows, she felt the weight of her own history in every one of their blank, controlled expressions. She deployed the chemical antidote carefully, gently, breaking the hold Dreykov had over each of them.

And then she dismantled everything he had built.

From that day on, Natasha worked to give every freed Widow a safe place to begin again — proof that the darkest possible beginning does not have to be the whole story.
