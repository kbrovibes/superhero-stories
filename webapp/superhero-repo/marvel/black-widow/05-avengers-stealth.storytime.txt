The front doors of the Hydra compound opened at exactly three in the morning, when the guards changed shift and the brief window of transition opened — the thirty-seven seconds when the second team was in place but not yet fully alert, when the cameras cycled between automated sweeps, when a person who knew what she was doing could slip through a gap that wasn't quite a gap and be gone before anyone counted the footsteps.

Natasha Romanoff was very good at these windows.

While Thor and the Hulk created the distraction at the front gates — which was, admittedly, quite a distraction; the lights of the compound turned on all at once and stayed on — Natasha was already in the ventilation system. Can you picture her? Moving through the ductwork with absolute silence, each hand and knee placed with total precision, the sound of her movements lost completely in the ambient noise of the building's heating system. Below her, guards ran toward the front in response to the extraordinary amount of noise a thunder god and a green person of significant size can generate when they try.

She counted the footsteps below. She noted the pattern. She moved.

The Red Room had given her something that no amount of technology could replicate: awareness. Total, constant, effortless awareness of her environment. The temperature of a room told her where the heat vents were. The slight vibration through a floor told her how many people were in the room below. The angle of a shadow in a corridor, caught at the edge of her vision as she approached an intersection, told her a guard was around the corner thirty feet away, moving away from her position.

She never triggered a single alarm.

The motion sensors in Sector Three were the first serious test — they were newer than the building's others, installed recently by someone who had the right instinct that the older systems weren't adequate. They swept in a pattern she identified in four seconds of watching. She moved through in the spaces between, timing each step to the gap in the sweep, body low and unhurried, nothing rushed. Rushing was what got people caught.

The elite guards she encountered were neutralized quickly, with the quiet efficiency she had perfected over years of this exact kind of work — nonlethal, precisely calibrated, positioned carefully so they would be found in places that implied patrol failures rather than intrusion. No blood. No alarms. Just a guard who would wake up with a headache and a confused recollection of the last few minutes.

At the center of the compound: the server room. Banks of encrypted drives, humming in the cold air, holding the Hydra sleeper agent protocols — names, locations, triggers — that the Avengers needed. She pulled the hacking tool from the sealed pocket in her thigh, slotted it against the access port, and began the breach while simultaneously monitoring the corridor through the small camera she had dropped at the entrance.

Seven minutes. She needed seven minutes.

She had six when the patrol pattern shifted. Someone had changed the schedule — a small change, the kind that most infiltrators would not have accounted for. She had accounted for it. She had three contingency plans for exactly this scenario, and she moved to the second one without pausing in her typing.

The data was hers at six minutes and forty seconds. She was out through the same ventilation route before the patrol completed its new cycle.

Outside, in the cold air, she slipped her earpiece back in and spoke two words to the Avengers: "I have it."

Thor's voice came back, slightly surprised. "Already?"

The intelligence she brought back from that server room changed the shape of the next six months of Avengers operations. Three sleeper networks were dismantled. Dozens of people who had been at risk were protected. None of them ever knew it. None of them ever knew her name.

She was the link between raw power and precision — the quiet thread that held the strategy together. The one who went where no one else could go and came back with what no one else could find.

She didn't need a hammer or a shield or a suit of flying armor.

She needed skill, nerve, and the training to use them both perfectly.

She had all three.
