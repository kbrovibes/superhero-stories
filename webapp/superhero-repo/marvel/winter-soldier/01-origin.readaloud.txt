Before there was a Winter Soldier, there was a kid from Brooklyn named Bucky Barnes. He was tall, easygoing, and funny, and he had spent most of his childhood looking after a small, scrappy, stubborn friend named Steve Rogers. When World War II came, Bucky shipped out first to fight — while Steve, still thin and small, was turned away from every recruitment office in the country.

Bucky fought through the frozen forests of Italy until HYDRA agents captured him. In a cold steel room, a scientist named Arnim Zola injected him with an early version of the Super Soldier Serum. He should not have survived. But he did. And then a blond stranger in a star-spangled suit walked through the door. The stranger was Steve Rogers — finally chosen, finally transformed, now Captain America. The two friends walked out of that HYDRA factory side by side.

But the fall came months later. On a train high above the Alps, Bucky was knocked from the open side car. Steve lunged for his hand and missed. Bucky fell into the snow far below. The world believed he was gone.

He was not gone. HYDRA found him. They replaced his missing left arm with a heavy metal one. They erased his memory with a chair full of electrical current. And they planted ten trigger words inside his mind — words in Russian that, spoken in the right order, turned him into something else: their secret weapon, the Winter Soldier.

For seventy years they froze him, woke him for a mission, then froze him again before he could remember his own name.

But somewhere underneath all of it, very quietly, a boy from Brooklyn was still waiting.
