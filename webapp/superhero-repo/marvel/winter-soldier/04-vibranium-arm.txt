The arm was the easiest thing about Bucky Barnes to notice and the hardest thing about him to understand. HYDRA’s original prosthetic was a heavy slab of dark gray Soviet metal, riveted to his shoulder with bolts the size of bottle caps. It was strong enough to stop a moving truck, dense enough to deflect a bullet, and cold enough that it ached in the winter. It was a weapon shaped like a limb. Every time Bucky raised it, even decades after HYDRA fell, he half-expected someone to give him an order.

After the Civil War, T’Challa offered Bucky a quiet place to recover in Wakanda. Shuri took one look at the HYDRA arm and laughed out loud at how primitive it was. She removed it gently, put it in a glass box, and let Bucky live for nearly two years without a prosthetic at all. He worked on a goat farm at the edge of the Wakandan jungle, ate proper meals for the first time in seventy years, and slept under a mosquito net while Shuri quietly worked on something better in her lab. She called him "White Wolf," because the village kids said he looked like one when he smiled.

The new arm Shuri built was made of pure Wakandan vibranium, the same metal that lined Steve’s shield and Black Panther’s suit. It was matte black with a small white star painted on the shoulder. It weighed less than the original and could absorb kinetic energy instead of just delivering it. Bucky could catch a thrown shield with the new arm and send it back without ever feeling the impact. Better still, the arm did not pull at his shoulder the way the old one had. For the first time in his adult life, he could sleep on his left side. He cried, very quietly, the first night he did.

The arm changed how Bucky fought, but more importantly, it changed how he felt. The HYDRA arm had been built to remind him of what they had done to him. The Wakandan arm was a gift, given by people who had decided he was worth helping. He learned, slowly, to use it for things other than violence. He caught a falling kid in a courtyard in Latvia. He held a glass of water steady when his other hand was shaking. He patted Steve Rogers on the back at the end of a battle and felt the warmth of his friend through the cold metal. The arm did not stop being a weapon. It just became, in his hands, the kind of weapon that could also be a comfort.
