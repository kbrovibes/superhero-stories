Picture this: Brooklyn, New York, almost a hundred years ago. Brownstone houses lined up in a neat row. Clotheslines stretched between the windows. Corner shops that smelled like fresh bread. RING-RING! Goes the bell on the bakery door. CLOMP-CLOMP! Go the boots of the dockworkers heading home for dinner.

And in the middle of all of it — two boys. One tall, dark-haired, with a half-grin that made grown-ups trust him on sight. That was James Buchanan Barnes. Bucky, for short. The other one was small and thin and got sick almost every winter. That was Steve Rogers.

Now, the very first time these two met? Steve was getting picked on. A kid twice his size had backed him up against a wall behind the Brooklyn Heights library. Steve was so small. But you know what he was doing? FISTS UP! Ready to fight!

That's when Bucky came around the corner. He took ONE look at the situation, marched right over, and said, "Pick on someone else." And just like that — friends for life.

Here's what their world was like. Bucky's dad worked long, long hours down at the docks. His mom kept a tidy little kitchen and ALWAYS had a spot at the table for Steve. Steve's mom, Sarah Rogers, was a nurse who worked nights at the hospital — so Bucky's family looked after Steve when Sarah was gone. CLINK! Went the spoons on Mrs. Barnes's bowls of soup. PSSSST! Hissed the radiator in winter.

They walked to school together down Flatbush Avenue. They cheered for the Brooklyn Dodgers — that was their baseball team. Steve could never afford a ticket to the game, so Bucky would say, "I won these in a coin flip!" Was that true? NO! Bucky had been saving his nickels for weeks. Don't tell Steve.

Summers at Coney Island! Hot dogs! The boardwalk! The CYCLONE roller coaster — CLICK CLICK CLICK to the top, WHOOOSH back down! Steve threw up after, just a tiny bit, and you know what? Bucky never told ANYONE. Not when Steve was a kid, not when Steve was Captain America, not ever. That's a real friend.

Friday nights they went to the movies. Adventure serials. Gangster pictures. Anything with a hero in a hat doing the right thing. Bucky always sat on the aisle so he could stretch his legs. Steve sat right next to him, head tipped back, eyes WIDE.

But here is the most important moment.

Steve's mother, Sarah, became very sick. And after a long time, she passed away. Steve sat on the funeral home steps afterward, all alone, not knowing where to go.

Bucky walked up. Didn't say much. Just sat down next to him. And then he said — gentle and steady — "I'm with you to the end of the line, pal."

Listen carefully to those words.

"I'm with you to the end of the line."

Because those words? Those words would travel with both of them through World War II. Through ice. Through seventy lost years. And ALL the way home again.

But right then, on a quiet Brooklyn stoop, they were just one boy promising another boy: you are not alone. You will never be alone. Not while I'm around.

And he meant it.
