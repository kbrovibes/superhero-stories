Sam Wilson and Bucky Barnes did not become partners overnight. They became partners the way a stubborn screw turns — a quarter-twist at a time, with a lot of grumbling. They had been thrown together by Steve Rogers, who loved them both, and they had been told by Steve, in essence, to figure it out. Sam was a former Air Force pararescue who had become the Falcon and now, finally, the new Captain America. Bucky was a hundred and six years old, fresh out of seventy years of HYDRA programming, and not exactly a people person. The first time Sam suggested they grab dinner together, Bucky said, "No," and walked away. Sam laughed all the way back to Louisiana.

The thing that finally bound them was a mission against a group called the Flag Smashers, led by a young woman named Karli Morgenthau. The two heroes argued constantly while they worked. Sam wanted to talk to Karli; Bucky wanted to take her down. Sam ate balanced meals; Bucky ate plums standing over the sink. Sam liked jazz; Bucky liked the Brooklyn Dodgers, who had not played in Brooklyn since 1957. They drove from Munich to Riga together in a tiny rental car arguing about strategy, and by the end of the trip they had figured out something neither of them had expected. They actually liked each other.

The partnership got real on a boat in Louisiana. Sam's sister, Sarah Wilson, ran the family fishing business out of Delacroix. The boat was falling apart and the bank wanted to take it. Bucky showed up one weekend, sleeves rolled up, vibranium arm gleaming, and helped Sam patch the hull. Sarah's two boys, Cass and AJ, climbed all over Bucky and asked detailed questions about his arm. Bucky answered every one of them seriously. He fixed a stuck cleat. He carried a heavy outboard motor across the dock like it was a loaf of bread. He stayed for dinner. He did the dishes. By Sunday night, the Wilson family had decided he was part of the family, and nobody had bothered to ask Bucky's opinion.

The new Captain America fought differently from the old one. Sam refused to put on the original outfit. Instead, with Wakandan vibranium provided by the king himself, Sam built a new winged suit with the shield mounted on his back — Falcon and Captain America in one. Bucky walked beside him. The new partnership had a rhythm. Sam in the air, Bucky on the ground. Sam talking the enemy down; Bucky standing just close enough that they did not try anything. Sam smiling at cameras; Bucky standing slightly behind him with his arms crossed. They argued about jazz. They argued about diet. They argued about whether wings counted as a superpower. They did not argue, ever, about who had each other's back. And on quiet Louisiana evenings, when Sarah served gumbo and Cass and AJ were finally asleep, the old Brooklyn boy and the new Captain America would sit on the dock with their feet in the water, and Bucky would think: I am, after everything, in good company. I am, after everything, home.
