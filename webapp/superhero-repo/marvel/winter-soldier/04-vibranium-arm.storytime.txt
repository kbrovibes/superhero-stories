Let me tell you about two arms.

The first arm was made of dark Soviet metal — gray and heavy, bolted to Bucky Barnes's shoulder by HYDRA engineers who did not care how much it hurt. It was not built for comfort. It was built for strength and for obedience: strong enough to stop a truck, cold enough that in winter it ached all the way through, heavy enough that he could feel its weight every time he raised it. Every time Bucky lifted that arm, some part of him expected to hear a voice giving him an order. That was what it had been made for.

After the Civil War — when Tony Stark and Steve Rogers and Bucky had destroyed each other in a Siberian bunker and the Avengers had fallen apart — Bucky didn't know where to go. He had no orders. He had half his memories back and all of his guilt and a metal arm that reminded him of everything wrong about the past seventy years.

And then T'Challa arrived.

King T'Challa of Wakanda — the Black Panther, who had chased Bucky across Berlin believing he was a terrorist and then discovered the truth — offered him sanctuary. No conditions. No requirements. Just a place to rest and to heal.

Bucky accepted. Because he was tired in a way that goes deeper than the body.

Shuri was the one who met him in the laboratory in Birnin Zana, in the gleaming city at the heart of Wakanda. She was younger than he expected and sharper than anyone he had ever met, and she looked at the HYDRA arm with the expression of a scientist looking at something very old and very broken and already thinking about what comes next. She removed it carefully. She put it in a glass case.

Then she said: you don't have to have an arm right now. You can rest.

Bucky Barnes had not rested in seventy years. Not truly. He had been frozen, which is not the same thing. He had been on missions, which is the opposite of rest. He moved to a hut at the edge of the Wakandan jungle, where the mornings were warm and the food was good and there were goats to look after. He slept under a mosquito net and ate proper meals and began, cautiously, to remember who he was when no one was telling him who to be.

The children of the village discovered him. They were completely unimpressed by his history and extremely interested in whether he wanted to chase goats with them. They gave him a name in their language that translated as the White Wolf — because of the way he smiled, eventually, when he'd been in the sun long enough. Not a soldier's name. A person's name.

After nearly two years, Shuri called him back to the lab. She had built something new.

The Wakandan arm was made of pure vibranium — matte black, a white star painted on the shoulder, designed by the most brilliant engineer in the world with one specific intention: to be a gift, not a weapon. It weighed less than the HYDRA arm by half. Where the old arm had added force to every impact, this one absorbed kinetic energy instead — steadied him, balanced him, worked with him instead of pulling him into old patterns. It attached to his shoulder without pain. It moved as naturally as thought.

He tested it in the lab, raising it and lowering it, opening and closing the fingers. Shuri watched with her arms crossed and her head tilted, checking her work.

The first night he slept on his left side, Bucky woke with tears on his face and did not entirely know why. But they were not unhappy tears. They were the kind that come when something you stopped believing in turns out to still be possible.

He looked at the arm in the morning light — the vibranium gleaming, the white star quiet on his shoulder — and he understood the difference between the two. The HYDRA arm had been built to remind him of what had been done to him. The Wakandan arm was built by people who had chosen, freely and without requirement, to help him become someone new.

That is what a gift is. Not something given in exchange. Something given because the giver decided you deserved it. Because they saw you as a person, not as a weapon, even when you weren't sure yet that they were right.

Bucky Barnes had a new arm. And for the first time in a very long time, he felt like the arm belonged to him.
