Before he was the Winter Soldier, James Buchanan Barnes was a kid from Brooklyn who liked baseball and looked after a sickly, scrappy friend named Steve Rogers. Bucky was tall, easygoing, and funny. He was the older-feeling one in the friendship, even though they were the same age. When World War II came, Bucky shipped out first, joining the 107th Infantry Regiment of the United States Army. He fought through the hedgerows of France and into the snowy forests of Italy, while back home Steve was rejected from every recruitment office in the country for being too small.

Bucky was captured by HYDRA agents during a battle near Azzano, Italy, and taken to a hidden laboratory run by a scientist named Arnim Zola. There, in a cold steel room, Zola injected him with an early version of the Super Soldier Serum to see if it would work. Bucky should have died on that table. Instead, his arm twitched, his eyes opened, and he stared up at the ceiling lights, alive but changed. A blond stranger in a strange star-spangled suit walked in moments later and pulled him off the slab. The stranger turned out to be Steve Rogers, now Captain America, who had finally been chosen for the same serum back in Brooklyn. The two friends walked out of the HYDRA factory side by side, and the war began to turn.

The fall came months later. On a train high above the Alps, Bucky was knocked off the open side car by a HYDRA agent named Heinrich Zemo. Steve Rogers, six feet away, lunged for his hand and missed. Bucky fell into the snowy mountains below and disappeared. The world believed he had died that day. He had not. HYDRA recovered him from a crevasse, replaced his missing left arm with a heavy metal one, and erased his memory with a chair full of electrical currents. They gave him a string of trigger words in Russian — wishing, furnace, daybreak, seventeen, benign, nine, homecoming, one, freight car. Anyone who said the words in the right order made him stop being Bucky and start being something else.

For the next seventy years, HYDRA used him as their secret weapon. They unfroze him for a single mission, sent him into the world in a black mask and a black coat, and froze him again before he could remember his own name. He had no idea Steve Rogers was alive. He had no idea who he had been before the Soldier. The world called him a ghost. They called him the Winter Soldier. And somewhere underneath the trigger words and the metal arm, a Brooklyn boy named Bucky Barnes was waiting, very quietly, to be remembered.
