Have you ever lost something important? A favorite toy? A library book? A glove on the playground? You know that awful feeling when you reach for the thing and it's just — gone?

Now imagine if the thing that got lost… was YOU.

This is the story of how Bucky Barnes was lost. And someday, the story of how he was found.

It started on a train. A HYDRA cargo train, rocketing through the Alps in the middle of World War II. Bucky was on it, fighting alongside Steve Rogers — Captain America now — and a brave team called the Howling Commandos. The mountain wind whistled. WHOOOO! The train rattled across a high bridge over a snowy gorge. CLACK-CLACK-CLACK-CLACK!

And then — RIP! — the side panel of the train tore away. Bucky was holding on. Suddenly he wasn't. The wind pulled him out into the cold open sky.

Steve LUNGED. He stretched out so far his feet almost left the train. Their fingers brushed. JUST brushed. And then…

Bucky fell.

Down. Down. Down. Into the snow far, far below.

Now here is the truth: Bucky should not have lived. The fall was very long. But remember, Arnim Zola had injected him with an early version of the Super Soldier Serum back in Italy. That serum was inside his body, helping him heal. So when he hit the snow, he didn't die. He was hurt — VERY hurt — but he was alive.

His arm, though? His left arm? That couldn't be saved.

Bucky blacked out. When he opened his eyes, men in heavy gray coats were carrying him on a stretcher through the snow. They were speaking in a language he didn't understand. He tried to ask for Steve. His mouth wouldn't work. Everything kept fading in and out, like a wobbly radio signal. ZZZRT. ZZZRT.

These men were HYDRA. The same Arnim Zola from before — that same sneaky scientist — recognized Bucky and ordered him taken to a secret facility deep in the wilderness. The doctors built him a new arm. Not a real arm. A heavy slab of dark gray metal, riveted onto his shoulder with bolts the size of bottle caps. CLANK! CLANK!

But here is the saddest part. Are you ready?

HYDRA didn't just take his arm. They took his MEMORIES.

They put him in a special chair. The chair gave off soft buzzing electrical currents — BZZZZZZT — and slowly, gently, pushed all of Bucky's real memories down into a deep dark place where he couldn't reach them anymore. He forgot Steve. He forgot Brooklyn. He forgot his mother, Winifred, and his three little sisters. He forgot Coney Island. He forgot the Brooklyn Dodgers. He forgot Friday night movies and stickball in the alley.

He even forgot his OWN NAME.

They told him stories about himself that weren't true. They gave him a different name in a different language. And whenever he started to remember anything real, they put him back in the chair, and the memories went away again.

Far away, across the ocean, Bucky's mother got a telegram saying her son had gone missing in the war. She kept hoping it was a mistake. It was. It really was. He wasn't gone. He was just LOST.

Lost like a coat dropped behind a couch. Lost like a toy that slipped under the bed. Lost like a library book that fell into the wrong shelf. Still here. Still real. Just waiting — VERY patiently — for someone to come find him.

How long would it take?

Seventy years. SEVEN ZERO. That's a long, long time to wait.

But somebody was going to come.
