When Bucky Barnes returned to Brooklyn after the Snap and the Blip, he was a man with a new arm, a clean mind, and a very long list of things to make right. His court-appointed therapist, Dr. Christina Raynor, had given him a small spiral notebook and a simple rule: write down every person the Winter Soldier hurt while HYDRA was driving — and then, one name at a time, do something to set the wrong right. Not to be forgiven. That was not the point. The point was to be honest.

Bucky bought a black pen at the corner bodega and started the list on a rainy Tuesday afternoon. He sat at the small table in his apartment and pulled out memories he had been afraid to look at since Wakanda. He wrote them in plain block letters. Yori Nakajima. Leah Behrens. Aaron Cleary. A scientist in Stuttgart. A janitor in Odessa. A senator's aide in Washington. By the time he stopped writing, the page had names in three columns, both sides, his hand shaking. Dr. Raynor read it the next morning and said only, "Good. Now we work."

The work was harder than any battle Bucky had ever fought. Each name was its own slow project. Some people on the list could be helped directly — paying off a debt the Winter Soldier had caused, finding a lost daughter, returning an item that had been taken. Some could only be helped in the background, quietly, in ways the person would never know. A few had to be told the truth, which was the very hardest kind of amends. Bucky carried the notebook in the inside pocket of his jacket everywhere he went. He crossed names off only when the work was really done — not when he had merely done something. There is a difference.

Dr. Raynor caught him once trying to cross out a name without telling the person what had happened. She tapped the page with one finger and said, "You don't get to skip the honest part because it's the scary part. That's the whole work." Bucky put the name back on the list. He went back the next day and told the truth. The person threw him out. He stood on the sidewalk afterward and felt, against all expectation, lighter. The notebook was working.

One name at a time, the list got shorter. Some names had a line through them and a quiet little note beside them. Some names had a line through them and nothing — those were the people who had told him to leave forever, and Bucky respected the silence. He was not making himself feel better. He was making the world a little bit truer. That, Dr. Raynor told him, was the actual job. Not absolution. Not redemption. Just truth, told kindly, one name at a time, until the notebook ran out of pages and Bucky Barnes had walked all the way through his own past, and out the other side.
