Bucky Barnes spent most of his time after the snap making amends, one phone call at a time. He had a small notebook with a long list of names — the people the Winter Soldier had hurt while HYDRA was driving — and he had promised his court-appointed therapist, Dr. Raynor, that he would close out every line. Some of the people on the list were not ready to forgive him. Some of them were. A few of them did not know who he was, and Bucky had to decide, very carefully, whether telling them the truth would help them or hurt them. He chose, almost always, the harder option.

One of the names was Yori Nakajima, an older man who ran a small sushi restaurant in Brooklyn. The Winter Soldier had ended the life of Yori’s son, RJ, decades earlier. Bucky began eating dinner at the restaurant a few times a week. He listened to Yori talk about his son. He did not say who he was. He simply made sure Yori was not alone on the holidays. He told his therapist that this counted as helping, and his therapist told him, gently, that it didn’t. Bucky finally walked Yori to a quiet bench one evening and told him the truth. Yori cried. He did not forgive Bucky. He told Bucky to leave. Bucky did. The work of making amends, Bucky understood, was not about being forgiven. It was about being honest enough to risk not being forgiven.

When Sam Wilson finally asked Bucky to be his partner, the new shield ringing on his arm, Bucky was surprised by how much he wanted to say yes. The two of them argued constantly — about strategy, about diet, about jazz, about whether wings counted as superpowers — but underneath the bickering was a quiet, steady trust. Sam reminded Bucky that he was a person, not a weapon. Bucky reminded Sam that not every fight could be won with a smile. They worked the way Steve and Bucky had once worked, except now Bucky was the older one, and Sam was the leader.

The best part of being Sam’s partner was that Bucky finally had a routine that did not involve revenge. He woke up in Brooklyn. He ran on the riverfront. He ate dinner at Yori’s, when Yori would let him. He helped Sarah Wilson fix her family’s boat in Louisiana on weekends he was not on a mission. And every once in a while, in the middle of a long sunset over the Hudson, he would look up at the sky and think about a small Steve Rogers, decades ago, telling him, "I’m with you to the end of the line." Bucky was past the line now. He was, against every odd, beginning again.
