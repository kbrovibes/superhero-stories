Imagine if you had done some things you weren't proud of. Not because you wanted to — but because somebody had tricked you, or made you forget what you were doing, or pushed you when you couldn't push back. And now, finally, you were free. You had your own thoughts again. Your own choices.

What would you DO with that freedom?

Here is what Bucky Barnes did.

He got himself a notebook.

Not a fancy one. Just a small spiral notebook from the corner store, with a plain cardboard cover. He bought a black pen at the bodega down the street. He went back to his little Brooklyn apartment. He sat at the kitchen table. And on a rainy Tuesday afternoon — TIP-TAP, TIP-TAP went the rain on the window — he opened to the first blank page.

Now, this part is important. Bucky had a therapist. Her name was Doctor Christina Raynor. She was court-appointed, which means a judge had said, "You HAVE to go talk to her, every week." And Dr. Raynor had given Bucky one simple rule:

Write down EVERY name. Every person the Winter Soldier hurt while HYDRA was driving the body. And then, one name at a time, DO SOMETHING to set the wrong right.

NOT to be forgiven. Listen to that part again. NOT to be forgiven.

The point was to be HONEST.

So Bucky started writing. SCRATCH SCRATCH SCRATCH went the pen.

Yori Nakajima. A man in Brooklyn who ran a sushi restaurant.

Leah Behrens. A woman in Berlin.

Aaron Cleary. A boy in Detroit.

A scientist in Stuttgart.

A janitor in Odessa.

A senator's aide in Washington, D.C.

Name after name after name. Both sides of the page. Three columns. His hand started to shake. He kept writing.

The next morning, Dr. Raynor read the list. She didn't gasp. She didn't lecture. She just looked at him and said, "Good. Now we work."

And oh, was it work! Harder than any battle Bucky had ever fought. Each name was its own little project. Some people he could help directly — paying off a debt the Winter Soldier had caused, finding someone's lost relative, returning a stolen thing. Some people had to be helped in secret, quietly, in ways they would never even know.

But some? Some had to be told the truth. THAT was the hardest part of all.

One day, Dr. Raynor caught Bucky cheating. She saw him cross out a name without telling that person what had happened. She tapped the page with ONE finger — TAP — and said, very calmly:

"You don't get to skip the honest part just because it's the scary part. THAT is the whole work."

OOF. Right? That's a tough one.

So Bucky put the name back on the list. He went back the next day. He told the person the truth. The person yelled at him. Threw him out. Said never come back. And Bucky stood on the sidewalk afterward —

— and against all expectation, felt LIGHTER. Just a little. Like he had set down a stone he'd been carrying for seventy years.

That's how he knew the notebook was working.

He carried it everywhere. Inside his jacket pocket. Through the rain. Through the snow. To diners, to airports, to therapy sessions. SHUFFLE-SHUFFLE went the pages every night when he checked the list. SCRATCH went the pen when another name finally got crossed off.

Some lines on the page had little notes next to them — "called his sister, gave her the photo" — quiet good endings. Some lines had no note at all — those were the people who had told Bucky to leave forever. And Bucky respected the silence. He did not push. He did not chase them.

Here is the truth Dr. Raynor taught him:

The job was never to make himself feel BETTER.

The job was to make the world a little bit TRUER.

One name at a time. One honest conversation at a time. Until the notebook ran out of pages, and Bucky Barnes had walked all the way through his own difficult past — and out the other side.
