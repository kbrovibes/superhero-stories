The fall from the train happened so fast that Bucky barely had time to be afraid. One moment he was clipped into the open side car of a HYDRA cargo train rocketing through the Alps, fighting beside Steve and the Howling Commandos. The next, the side panel ripped away under his hand, and the wind pulled him out into the open sky. Steve lunged. Their fingertips brushed. And then Bucky was falling toward the snow far, far below.

He should not have lived. The drop was hundreds of feet into a deep ravine packed with snow and broken pine. His left arm was shattered against the rocks. He blacked out before he hit bottom, which was a quiet mercy. When he opened his eyes, he was strapped to a stretcher being carried by men in heavy gray coats. They were speaking in a language he did not understand. He tried to ask for Steve. His mouth would not work. The world kept fading in and out, like a flickering radio signal.

The men who found him were HYDRA. Arnim Zola — the same scientist who had once injected Bucky with the early Super Soldier Serum back in Azzano — recognized his face from old files and ordered him taken to a hidden facility deep inside the Soviet wilderness. The serum in Bucky's blood had kept him alive against impossible odds. It had not, however, kept his arm. The doctors fitted him with a heavy slab of dark gray metal, riveted to his shoulder. When he woke up properly for the first time, he stared at the strange new limb and could not understand whose body he was in.

The hardest part was not the arm. The hardest part was the not-knowing. HYDRA had decided very early that the simplest way to control him was to keep him from remembering. They told him stories about himself that were not true. They gave him a new name in a language that was not his. They put him in a special chair and used its electrical currents to gently push his old memories down into the dark, where he could not reach them. He had no idea, in those first months, that anyone named Steve Rogers had ever existed. He had no idea that he had grown up in Brooklyn. He had no idea that he had a mother named Winifred who was, at that very moment, half a world away, still hoping the telegram had been a mistake.

He was lost. Not gone — lost. Like a coat dropped behind a couch, waiting for someone to come find him. It would take seventy years. But somebody would.
