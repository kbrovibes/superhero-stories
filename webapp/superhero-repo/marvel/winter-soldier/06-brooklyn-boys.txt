Long before there was a Winter Soldier, there were two boys in Brooklyn. James Buchanan Barnes lived a few blocks from Steve Rogers in a neighborhood of brownstones and clotheslines and corner shops that smelled like fresh bread. Bucky was taller, with dark hair and a half-grin that made grown-ups trust him on sight. Steve was small and thin and got sick almost every winter. The first time they met, Steve was getting pushed around behind the Brooklyn Heights library by a kid twice his size, and Bucky was the boy who stepped in, fists up, and said, "Pick on someone else." From that afternoon on, they were a pair.

Bucky's father worked long hours at the docks, and his mother kept a tidy little kitchen where Steve was always welcome for dinner. Steve's mother, Sarah Rogers, was a nurse who worked nights at a tuberculosis ward, so Bucky's family looked out for Steve whenever she was at the hospital. The boys went to the same school, walked the same routes home along Flatbush Avenue, and shared a love of the Brooklyn Dodgers, even though Steve could never afford a ticket and Bucky always pretended he had won theirs in a coin flip. He had not. He had saved his nickels.

They went to Coney Island together every summer. Steve once threw up on the Cyclone roller coaster and Bucky never told anyone, not even when Steve was a foot taller and bulletproof. They went to the movies on Friday nights — adventure serials, gangster pictures, anything with a man in a hat doing the right thing. Bucky always sat on the aisle so he could stretch his legs. Steve always sat right next to him, head tipped back, eyes wide.

The day Sarah Rogers passed away from her tuberculosis, Steve sat on the steps of the funeral home with nowhere to go. Bucky sat down next to him without a word and said, very simply, "I'm with you to the end of the line, pal." He meant it more than he knew. Those words would travel with both of them through a world war, through ice, through seventy lost years, and back again — but in that moment, on a quiet Brooklyn stoop, they were just one boy promising another boy that he was not alone. And he never was.
