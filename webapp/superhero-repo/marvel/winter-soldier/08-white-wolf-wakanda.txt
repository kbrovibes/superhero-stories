After the fight at the airport in Leipzig and the harder fight in a snowy bunker in Siberia, Bucky Barnes was a man with a metal arm, a head full of trigger words, and nowhere safe to go. The world's governments wanted him in a cage. Steve Rogers refused to put him there. The one person who offered him another path was a king. T'Challa, the Black Panther of Wakanda, looked at Bucky after the dust had settled and said quietly, "I can help you. Come home with me."

Wakanda was unlike anywhere Bucky had ever been. It was a green, mountain-rimmed country hidden behind the world's most advanced cloaking technology. Inside, it had silver cities and grassland villages and herds of long-horned cattle that wandered between them. T'Challa brought Bucky to a small thatched-roof hut at the edge of a quiet village, near a stream and a grove of acacia trees. There were no electric lights. There was a bed, a wooden table, and a window that opened to the morning sun. For the first time in seven decades, no one was about to put him in a chair.

T'Challa's sister, Princess Shuri, took charge of his mind. Shuri was sixteen, a genius, and not at all impressed by Steve Rogers's letter of introduction. She studied Bucky's brain scans for months. She built a system that could enter the part of his mind where HYDRA had buried the trigger words and gently — very gently — turn the wires off, one at a time. While she worked, Bucky lived in the village. He helped the local farmers tend a small herd of goats. He learned a little of the Wakandan language, called Xhosa. He woke at dawn. He went to bed at sunset. The village children called him Ingcuka Emhlophe, which means White Wolf, because of his pale skin and the calm steady way he watched over them.

The day Shuri finished was an ordinary morning. She brought him into her lab, sat him in a soft chair (not a HYDRA chair — just a chair), and said, "Try the words." She read each Russian trigger word in order. Wishing. Furnace. Daybreak. Seventeen. Benign. Nine. Homecoming. One. Freight car. Bucky listened all the way to the end. Nothing happened. He blinked. He looked at his hands. He smiled — a real Brooklyn smile, the kind he had not used in seventy years. "It's gone," he said. Shuri grinned, fist-bumped him, and called him White Wolf. And outside the lab, in a country half a world from Brooklyn, a kid who had been lost for a very long time was, for the first time, found.
