Once upon a time, two girls lived aboard a great dark warship called the Sanctuary, and they called each other sisters, even though neither one was quite sure, for a very long time, what that word really meant.

One was Gamora. She had green skin the color of new leaves and golden-brown eyes that saw everything. She had come from a world called Zen-Whoberi, a warm and busy planet, before Thanos took her away and brought her here. Gamora was fast and brilliant, and she had a way of moving in a fight that was like watching water flow — she found every opening, took every advantage, and almost never lost.

The other was Nebula. She had blue skin and dark, serious eyes, and she had come from a world called Luphom. She was fierce and stubborn and absolutely relentless, the kind of person who would get knocked flat and immediately start calculating how to get up.

Every morning in the Sanctuary's training rooms, they sparred.

CLANG — swords.
THWACK — kicks.
CRASH — one of them down on the mat.

More often than not, that one was Nebula.

And every time she lost, Thanos's surgeons came with their quiet instruments. A new arm. A new eye. A reinforced spine. Each replacement a message in metal: not good enough, try again, be better.

Nebula lay awake many nights staring at the cold ceiling, her metal arm resting beside her, and she made herself feel angry. Anger was useful. Anger got you up in the morning. Anger made you strong.

But underneath the anger, if you could have looked very closely, was something much softer and much more honest.

She wanted Gamora to see her.

Not as a rival. Not as someone to defeat. Just — her sister. Someone who believed that Nebula was brave and real and worth knowing, even when she lost.

When Gamora finally broke free from Thanos's orbit — when she found her way to the Guardians of the Galaxy, to Star-Lord and Rocket and Groot and Drax and the warm, rumbling ship called the Milano — Nebula felt something crack open in her chest.

Not a system failure. Something much worse and much more human.

She felt left behind.

So she chased. She fought the Guardians in the floating outpost of Knowhere, where a DJ played music in a giant skull. She pursued Gamora to the shining city-planet of Xandar. She crossed the vast, pulsing surface of Ego's Living Planet, slashing and firing and insisting, loudly and furiously, that she did not care at all about any of this.

She cared about all of it.

The fights between them were extraordinary. Gamora moved like water. Nebula moved like iron. They had trained together so long, they knew each other's rhythms — every feint, every dodge, every tell. Fighting Gamora was, Nebula sometimes thought, the most precisely she had ever felt known.

Which was, she did not say out loud, a very sad thought.

Years passed. Hard, extraordinary years. The snap. The silence. The long road back.

And at the end of that road, they sat together for the first time without an audience and without a battle pending.

Just two sisters.

"I never wanted to defeat you," Nebula said.

Gamora looked at her across the narrow space between them — the space that had held so many years of anger and distance and things unsaid.

"I know," Gamora said.

"I wanted you to — " Nebula stopped. Her processors were fast, but some things were slow to form. "I wanted you to think I was worth it."

Gamora was quiet for a moment. The Milano hummed gently around them, carrying them through the dark between stars.

"You were always worth it," she said. "You were braver than me, Nebula. Every time you got back up after losing — that was harder than anything I ever did."

Something in Nebula's chest, deep below all the metal and the circuits, went very still.

Then very warm.

She did not cry. She was not the crying kind. But she reached over and placed her cybernetic hand over Gamora's green one, and she held it there for a moment.

Just two sisters. Equal in every way that mattered.

Different paths, different colors, different scars — and the exact same stubborn, burning, beautiful determination to make the galaxy a better place.

That, it turned out, was what a sister was.

That was what it had always been.

They flew on together through the wide, quiet dark, blue and green side by side, and for the first time in as long as either of them could remember, neither one felt alone at all.
