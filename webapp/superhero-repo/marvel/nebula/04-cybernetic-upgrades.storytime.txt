Once upon a time, there was a girl who was slowly, painfully, piece by piece, rebuilt.

Not rebuilt because she was broken. Rebuilt because a tyrant named Thanos wanted her to be better than she was — or at least better than he believed she was. Every time Nebula lost a training match against her sister Gamora in the cold sparring rooms of the warship Sanctuary, Thanos would gesture to his surgeons and say one quiet word.

"Again."

And they would replace something.

Her left arm went first. CLICK. Where warm flesh had been, cool metal appeared: a cybernetic limb with three times the grip strength, cables that could extend and retract, a tiny energy emitter in the palm that could fire a shot like a stun blast. The arm was extraordinary. Nebula did not thank him for it.

Her right eye came next. A sensor array, small and round, set neatly into the socket and connected to her optical nerve. BZZT. Now she could see in total darkness. She could track twelve objects at once across a wide field. She could detect heat signatures through solid walls. She could see the exact trajectory of an object thrown toward her half a second before it arrived.

Her spine was reinforced with a titanium lattice, threaded carefully between each vertebra. Her neural processors were upgraded to run faster than any ordinary organic brain — quick enough to run combat calculations, map environments, and analyze enemies while still having plenty of processing space left over for remembering, feeling, and deciding.

Thanos meant it as discipline. He meant it as a reminder that she was not good enough.

But something happened that he did not plan for.

Nebula learned.

Not in the way he intended — not just how to be a better fighter, though she did become that, fearsome and precise. She learned her own architecture. She spent long, quiet nights in her quarters on the Sanctuary, cross-legged on her bunk, running her fingers along the seams of her cybernetic arm, learning every joint, every cable routing, every fail-safe switch. She learned which circuits governed which systems. She learned where the weaknesses were and how to protect them.

And she learned how to fix herself.

In the field, this was invaluable. A loose cable in her forearm? She could splice it in twelve seconds without looking, her organic hand moving from memory. A jammed energy emitter? Three taps in the right sequence and it cleared. She could jack a short cable from her wrist port directly into an electronic lock and override it in moments — which meant that no door in the galaxy could hold her.

She could also fight.

Thanos had drilled combat into her from the beginning, and now she had the processing speed and the body to match. She carried retractable blades in her cybernetic forearm — SCHING, out they came — and fought with a precision that came from knowing exactly how her own body moved. She had been knocked down more times than she could count, blasted backward across training rooms, thrown into walls, struck by foes twice her weight.

And every time, she got back up.

This, perhaps, was the thing that Thanos had never managed to upgrade away.

He had made her arm stronger. He had made her eye sharper. He had made her spine straighter and her brain faster. But the part of Nebula that simply refused to stay down — that looked at every obstacle and calculated not whether to continue, but how — that was hers alone. It had been hers on Luphom, before the Sanctuary, before any of this. It would always be hers.

Gamora said once, quietly, on the deck of the Milano: "You are the most determined person I have ever met."

Nebula looked at her cybernetic arm. The light of a nearby star — a small, orange sun they were passing — caught in the joints of her knuckles and glinted there, steady and warm.

She thought about answering with something cool and unbothered, the way she might have done years ago.

Instead she said, "Thank you."

It was a small thing. But in Nebula's long story, it was the beginning of something large.

She was not what Thanos had made her.

She was what she had built herself into, one quiet choice at a time, using every tool she had — the metal, the circuits, the determination, and the heart.

Especially the heart.

That one had never needed an upgrade.
