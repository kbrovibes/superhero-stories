There is no relationship more complicated, more painful, or more ultimately beautiful in Nebula's life than the one she shares with her sister Gamora. They grew up side by side on Thanos's warship Sanctuary, two girls from different worlds turned into warriors against their will. Gamora was raised on a planet called Zen-Whoberi before Thanos took half its population and called her as his own. She was fast and brilliant, and from the moment they first sparred in the Sanctuary's training rooms, she was usually the one left standing. For Nebula, losing to Gamora meant another piece of herself replaced with metal.

It would have been easy — and understandable — for that to become pure hatred. For years it came close. Nebula told herself she despised Gamora, that every loss and every replacement was her sister's fault. She challenged her, ambushed her, and swore revenge. When Gamora turned away from Thanos and found the Guardians of the Galaxy, Nebula chased after her, determined to prove that she was the stronger one. Their battles were ferocious: across the skies of Xandar, in the dark corridors of the Kyln prison, in the atmosphere above Ego's Living Planet.

But beneath the fury, something else was always there, buried deep in Nebula's circuitry like a message she refused to read. The truth was that she had never stopped wanting a sister. She had never stopped wanting Gamora to turn around and say, "You were always good enough. I never needed Thanos to pick a winner." The anger was just the shape that longing took when it had nowhere else to go.

The turning point came quietly. After the devastation of Thanos's snap and all the long, hard years that followed, Nebula and Gamora sat together without an audience, without a battle to fight, and they talked. They admitted things that had been locked away for years. Gamora said she had always known Nebula was the braver one — that it was harder to keep going after every replacement than it ever was to win. Nebula said she had never wanted to beat Gamora. She had just wanted to be equal.

They were equal. They always had been. Different strengths, different paths, but equally brave and equally real. Gamora the green-skinned warrior who found her heart among the stars. Nebula the blue cyborg who had to fight through everything to find hers. Together, in their matching stubbornness and bottomless determination, they were the best possible version of each other. And that, finally, was what a sister was.
