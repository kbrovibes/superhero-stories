Among all the ruthless figures Nebula encountered while still under Thanos's command, none was more terrifying — or more self-righteous — than Ronan the Accuser. Ronan was a Kree warrior, tall and armored in black, who carried an enormous war hammer called the Universal Weapon. He believed that the Kree Empire deserved to rule the galaxy, and he would crush anything that stood in his way. Thanos had made a deal with Ronan: retrieve the Power Stone, hidden inside a silver orb on the abandoned planet Morag, and Ronan would destroy an entire world for him.

Nebula was assigned to work alongside Ronan as an enforcer. She stood at his side on his warship, the Dark Aster, watching him command his fleet with cold fury. Ronan did not ask questions. He did not listen to reason. He simply decided, and then acted, and anyone who disagreed became his next target. Nebula recognized that pattern — it was exactly how Thanos operated — and something about watching it from the outside made her stomach turn in a way it never had before. She was not afraid of Ronan. But she was starting to understand, very quietly, that following men like Ronan and Thanos was not strength. It was surrender.

The Guardians of the Galaxy intercepted Ronan's plans on the planet Xandar. Star-Lord, Gamora, Rocket, Drax, and Groot staged a desperate defense of the Nova Corps and the millions of people living there. Nebula was deployed to stop them, and she and Gamora fought an airborne battle above the city — crashing through buildings, trading blows in mid-air, the golden spires of Xandar flashing past them. The fight was fierce, and neither sister held back.

But the Guardians won. Ronan had seized the Power Stone and nearly wielded its full, shattering force — enough to destroy a planet. It was Peter Quill, known as Star-Lord, who did the impossible: he grabbed the Stone with his bare hands, and the five Guardians together contained its power, directing it straight into Ronan. The Dark Aster crashed. Ronan was gone.

Nebula escaped in the wreckage, clinging to a fragment of the falling warship. She landed, dusted herself off, and looked back at Xandar, still standing, still shining. She had fought on the wrong side that day, and she knew it. The knowledge sat in her chest like a stone — but stones can become the foundation of something new, if you are willing to build.

She walked away, alone, into the stars. But for the first time, she was walking toward something instead of away.
