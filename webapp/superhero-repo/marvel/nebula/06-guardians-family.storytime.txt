Once upon a time, at the very edge of the galaxy, there was a place called Knowhere.

It was not the kind of place you would find on a tourist map. It was a space station built inside the skull of an ancient Celestial — a creature so enormous that its head was the size of a small moon. The skull drifted through space on no particular schedule, and inside it there was a mining colony, and a market that sold almost anything, and always, tied to the docking rings, a ship called the Milano.

The Milano was scratched and patched and repaired many times over. But it flew fast and true, and it carried five of the most extraordinary people Nebula had ever met.

Peter Quill — Star-Lord — was technically the captain. He was from a small town in Missouri on the planet Earth, which meant he was confused by roughly half the things the galaxy did, but made up for it with enthusiasm and a music player full of old Earth songs. He almost always had something playing — bright, bouncing melodies that made even tense moments feel, somehow, manageable.

Drax was large and tattooed and completely, magnificently literal. Metaphors simply did not land on Drax. Once, Nebula told him that a plan was "going over his head," and he spent several minutes squinting at the ceiling.

Rocket was small and furry and built like a raccoon, which was not a coincidence. He was also the most mechanically gifted person Nebula had ever encountered. He could look at any device for thirty seconds and explain exactly how it worked and why whoever built it had made three unnecessary mistakes.

And Groot — dear Groot — was a tree. A walking, gentle, luminous tree with bark like ancient wood and eyes like warm amber. He said three words: "I am Groot." But he said them in so many tones that the team had learned every nuance. A low "I am Groot" was concern. A bright "I am Groot!" was delight. A very soft, slow "I… am Groot" meant sit down and rest, I will be here.

Groot sometimes grew tiny flowers from his fingers and left them in places people would find them — on a control panel, beside a sleeping bag, tucked into a door hinge. He never made a production of it. The flowers just appeared, small and perfect.

Nebula had arrived at Knowhere first as an enemy. She had stormed through its corridors with fire in her eyes and a blade in her hand. She had not been a villain for the joy of it — she had been following orders, because that was all she had known. But she had been on the wrong side, and the memory of it sat in her chest like a splinter.

She returned, later, differently. Not with a ceremony or a vote. It was gradual, the way trust always is — one honest moment at a time.

She answered a comm when Rocket needed someone to watch his back on a hostile station. She sat next to Drax in silence for two hours when he was having a hard night, neither of them speaking, and found out afterward that it had helped. She learned that Peter's music was not, objectively, the greatest ever recorded — but it was clearly very important to him, and so she stopped saying so.

She learned that Groot noticed things.

He noticed when she was quiet in the tense way, not the calm way, and he would come stand near her without making it a production. He noticed when she had been awake too long and would sit heavily nearby as a gentle suggestion. He left her a flower once — a small blue thing the color of her skin — in the crook of her cybernetic arm while she slept.

She woke to find it there. She looked at it for a long time.

She did not throw it away.

Flying with the Guardians was nothing like following Thanos. There were no punishments for questions. There were arguments — loud, sometimes exhausting arguments — but they ended and the team was still the team. Nobody expected perfection. They expected effort, and honesty, and showing up.

Nebula was very good at showing up.

One night, far out in the deep dark between star systems, Nebula stood at the rear viewport and looked at the galaxy stretched behind them — every solar system, every planet, every unlikely creature living its unlikely life.

Gamora came and stood beside her.

"Good team," Nebula said.

Gamora glanced at her. The corner of her mouth moved. "Yeah," she said. "Really good team."

The Milano hummed. Peter's music drifted back from the cockpit — something sweet and old, an Earth song about being far from home and finding home anyway.

Nebula listened.

She thought she might, possibly, be starting to like it.

That was, she decided, probably fine.
