Not every hero gets their power from a magic hammer or a super-soldier serum. Nebula's powers came from something harder to celebrate: piece by piece, year by year, Thanos had replaced parts of her body with extraordinary technology. Her left arm was entirely cybernetic, capable of extending cables, firing energy blasts, and gripping with the strength of a hydraulic press. Her right eye was a sensor array that could track movement across a wide battlefield and see in total darkness. Her spine had been reinforced with a titanium lattice. Her neural processors ran faster than almost any organic brain.

These upgrades were not a gift. They were a punishment — each replacement representing a defeat Thanos decided was unacceptable. But over time, Nebula learned to use them as something entirely different: tools of her own. She could rewire her own arm in the field, splicing cables with her fingers in seconds. She could use her eye to detect heat signatures through walls on ships like the Eclector or the Sanctuary II. She could override electronic locks and hack simple computer systems by jacking a cable directly into the hardware. Her body was, in a very real sense, a one-person starship.

There was also the matter of her blades. Nebula carried a retractable blade built into her cybernetic forearm, and she fought with swords and knives with the precision of someone who had trained every single day since childhood. On Thanos's warships she had sparred against the deadliest warriors in the galaxy, losing often enough that she knew exactly where her own limits were — and she had been pushing past those limits ever since.

Perhaps the most remarkable feature was also the most invisible: resilience. Nebula could be knocked down, blasted, thrown through a hull, and still get back up. Not because she felt no pain — she felt it clearly, her sensors registering every impact — but because she had decided long ago that pain was information, not a reason to stop. Gamora once said that Nebula was the most determined person she had ever known. That was not a mechanical trait. That came from something no upgrade could build or remove.

Nebula has been asked, occasionally, whether she would choose to change back — to give up the metal and the circuits and have the body she was born with. She does not always answer. But sometimes she looks at her cybernetic arm, the way light catches the joints, and she simply says: this is what carried me through. It is part of who I am now. And who I am is something worth keeping.
