Nebula came to the Guardians of the Galaxy the long way — through battles against them, through grudging alliances, and through one slow, stubborn decision at a time. The team she eventually called family was the most unlikely collection of people in the galaxy: Peter Quill, known as Star-Lord, a half-human from Missouri who loved 1980s Earth music and somehow always found the right thing to say; Gamora, her sister; Drax, whose magnificent literal-mindedness made every conversation an adventure; Rocket, a talking raccoon of breathtaking mechanical genius; and Groot, a tree-creature whose three-word vocabulary contained more warmth than most full novels.

Their home base, more often than not, was a bizarre and fascinating outpost called Knowhere — a space station carved from the severed head of an ancient Celestial being, drifting at the edge of the galaxy. Knowhere had a mining colony, a black market, a bar called the Boot of Jemiah, and exactly the kind of character that only develops in a place where nobody else wants to live. Nebula had first visited Knowhere as an enemy, chasing the Guardians. She would later visit as something better.

The Milano — the Guardians' sleek but battered ship — became a kind of moving home for Nebula. She learned that Peter Quill put music on the cockpit speakers during long hauls, always something from his beloved Awesome Mix tapes, and that Rocket would tinker with the engines until they hummed at frequencies no standard diagnostic could measure. She learned that Groot sometimes grew tiny flowers just to leave them somewhere someone would find them. She learned that Drax cooked, badly but enthusiastically. She learned that trust, among people like these, was built slowly and tested constantly and was worth every second of the effort.

Being a Guardian meant showing up. It meant answering the comms when Rocket needed backup on a hostile station, or sitting next to Drax at a difficult moment when words were not quite working, or simply flying shoulder to shoulder through a debris field with Gamora on her left and Peter on her right and the whole brilliant mess of the galaxy spread out in front of them.

Nebula had been made to serve Thanos. She had been engineered for obedience. But the Guardians had never wanted obedience from her. They wanted Nebula — stubborn, precise, funny in her most deadpan moments, ferociously loyal once she had decided someone was worth it. That turned out to be an entirely different thing, and it suited her far better than she ever expected.
