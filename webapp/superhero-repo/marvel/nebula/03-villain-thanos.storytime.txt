Once upon a time, there was a golden gauntlet.

It was the most dangerous object in the universe. Six gems were set into its knuckles: one purple, one blue, one red, one orange, one green, one yellow. Each one contained a power beyond imagining — power over the mind, over time, over space, over reality, over the soul, over all living things. Together they were the Infinity Stones, and the golden glove that held them was the Infinity Gauntlet.

And the hand inside the Gauntlet belonged to Thanos.

Nebula knew exactly how this felt from the inside. She had been raised on the Sanctuary, had watched Thanos assemble his terrible plans piece by piece the way she had been assembled herself — one replacement at a time. Now she sat in chains aboard his new warship, the Sanctuary II, while the Gauntlet glowed gold and terrible in the darkness around her.

He did something that she had not expected, even from him.

He reached into her cybernetic memory — her own stored files, the recordings of her past — and he pulled them out like threads from a tapestry. He used her memories as a trap, as a message to the Guardians, turning her own mind into a weapon against the people she had begun to love.

Nebula sat very still. She let the anger find its proper shape.

It was not a wild rage anymore. It was something precise. Something clear. Like the edge of a blade before it strikes.

On the moon of Titan — pocked with craters, its surface broken as old stone — a desperate plan was unfolding. Tony Stark burned brilliant in his red-and-gold armor. Stephen Strange held the Eye of Agamotto in one careful hand, calculating every possible future. Peter Parker swung between columns of alien rock, his spider-sense firing fast as lightning. Star-Lord. Drax. Mantis. All of them, together, in one terrible and glorious last stand.

They came so close.

With Mantis's hands on Thanos's temple and Drax and Iron Man holding his arm and Strange weaving the air itself into chains, they nearly had the Gauntlet off. Finger by finger. WRENCH. Almost. Almost.

And then it was over.

Thanos snapped his fingers.

The sound was very small for something so large.

The sky above Titan went still. The Sanctuary II's light pulsed. And then, one by one, beings across the entire universe — half of all who breathed and lived and moved — simply stopped.

Nebula did not.

She was still there when the smoke cleared. Still standing on the grey rubble of Titan, alone in a silence that felt like the largest thing she had ever heard. She looked at the empty sky. She looked at her metal hand, her cybernetic arm, her rebuilt self.

Thanos had made her to serve. To fight. To follow.

She decided, standing alone on that broken moon, that she would do something he had never prepared her for.

She would fix it.

The years that followed were not simple. Nebula worked alongside the remaining Avengers — Steve Rogers and Natasha Romanoff and the others who had survived, who carried their grief quietly and kept going anyway. She learned what it meant to work with people who chose each other not because they were ordered to, but because they wanted to. She learned what it felt like to be trusted.

And then a strange, impossible plan took shape: travel back through time, recover the Stones before Thanos ever had them, use them to bring everyone back.

Nebula traveled into her own past. She saw herself as she had been — closed off, angry, hungry for approval from a father who saw her only as a tool. It was the hardest mirror she had ever looked into.

But she looked. And she kept going.

On the day the Avengers snapped everyone back — when Hulk raised the Gauntlet and the whole bright world breathed in again — Thanos attacked. His fleet fell from the sky. The earth shook.

And Nebula was there on the battlefield.

Not in chains. Not following orders. Not trying to earn a father's praise.

She stood with the Avengers. She stood with Gamora beside her. She stood with every circuit humming and every joint locked and every piece of her — metal and flesh and sheer, unstoppable will — pointed in the same direction.

Forward.

Later, when the battle was done and the sky was quiet again, Nebula stood at the edge of the field and let the autumn wind move past her.

Thanos had built her to be a weapon.

She had become something he never planned for.

She was a hero. And no one — not even the most powerful being in the universe — could take that away from her.
