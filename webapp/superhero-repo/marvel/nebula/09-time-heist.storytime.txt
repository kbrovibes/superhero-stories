Once upon a time, a man named Tony Stark had a wild, impossible, brilliant idea.

"What if," he said, holding a holographic interface and staring at a problem that had broken the world, "we went back?"

Back in time. Through a place called the Quantum Realm — a space between spaces, where time behaved differently, where a very precise navigational algorithm could deliver a person to any point in history and bring them home again. Tony had worked out the mathematics. The mathematics said yes. The mathematics said possible.

The plan had a name, because everything needs a name. They called it the Time Heist.

And Nebula was part of the team.

She was assigned to the planet Morag in the year 2014, alongside James Rhodes — War Machine — a former military pilot in a suit of powerful armor. Morag was a barren, tide-swept world, mostly underwater except for brief windows when the tides retreated and ancient structures rose from the sea. One of those structures held the Power Stone, sealed in a silver orb, waiting for someone to pick it up.

Simple mission. In, stone, out, home.

But Nebula's cybernetics were complicated things.

Thanos had built them. Wired them. Updated them many times over many years, across many battles and many worlds. And even now, on a mission to undo everything he had done, a dormant link in her neural network remained connected to his systems. She had not known this. No one had known.

The 2014 version of Thanos — younger but no less dangerous, not yet defeated — detected the quantum signal. His systems reached across time into Nebula's memory files, and he read everything.

The Time Heist. The Avengers. The plan. The future.

He captured present-Nebula and replaced her with his past-Nebula — the version who had not yet chosen, not yet turned, still completely in his service. Past-Nebula completed the mission. She brought Thanos's entire 2014 fleet forward through time to the present.

Present-Nebula sat in a cell on the Sanctuary II and thought.

She had come so far. She had fought so hard. She had chosen, again and again, to be something different from what Thanos had made her. And now her own architecture had been used against her.

She thought: I am not done.

She waited for exactly the right moment.

In the Avengers Compound, Bruce Banner — the Hulk — closed his eyes over a brilliantly engineered gauntlet.

SNAP.

Everywhere — on Earth, in space, across the galaxy — people who had been gone for five years simply reappeared. Mid-sentence. Mid-step. Back in the middle of their lives.

The world had half a second to feel that.

Then the Compound's roof came off.

Thanos came through the wreckage like weather, like inevitability, and behind him poured soldiers from across history and the galaxy, filling the sky.

Nebula escaped in the chaos. She found a weapon. She found a position. She found herself on a battlefield on Earth, defending it alongside people she was still learning to call her friends.

The hardest part of that day was not Thanos. It was the other her.

Past-Nebula moved through the battle, face closed, eye scanning with the same calculating efficiency. They had the same processors. The same reflexes. The same memories, up to a certain point.

But not the same choices.

They faced each other in the smoke and noise. Present-Nebula did not try to deliver a speech. She simply made her move — precise, committed, clear — and moved on. That was who she was now.

Then the portals opened.

Every hero who had been restored came through blazing — from Wakanda and Asgard and the far reaches of space — pouring through golden rings of light into the smoke of the battlefield.

Nebula watched the sky fill with allies.

She was already there. Already standing. Already on the right side — not because a portal had delivered her, but because she had fought her way there herself through years of hard choices.

When it was over, when the sky quieted and the world was still, Nebula stood in the fields of the ruined Compound and looked at the people around her — helping each other up, tending to each other, breathing.

Gamora appeared beside her. The 2014 version, uncertain and newly arrived, still finding her bearings.

"What happens now?" Gamora asked.

Nebula looked at the clearing sky, the light coming through the smoke, everything battered but still standing.

"Something good," she said.

For the first time in as long as she could remember, she was absolutely certain.
