Henry Philip McCoy was born in Dunfermline, Illinois, to a perfectly ordinary family — except that his father, Norton McCoy, had once worked at a nuclear power plant and absorbed a dose of radiation that changed his family's future forever. Hank arrived in the world with hands and feet far larger than any baby's should be, and a smile so wide it filled the delivery room. The doctors called it an anomaly. His parents called him their miracle.

Growing up, Hank discovered that his enormous hands and feet gave him astonishing agility. He could swing from a barn rafter, somersault over the school fence, and hang upside down from a tree branch while reading a library book — which he often did, because he also loved books more than almost anything. In the same year he broke every athletic record at Waukegan High School, he also won the state science fair. His classmates called him a show-off. His parents called him extraordinary.

The day his life changed again came on the football field. Hank was making a play that no human athlete should ever have been able to make — twisting, spinning, leaping fifteen feet through the air — when a group of men called the Conquistador arrived to kidnap his parents and force Hank into their service. He fought them off using nothing but speed, strength, and the acrobatic gifts he had always thought of as merely odd. Standing in the wreckage of the play, Hank finally understood that he was not merely a gifted athlete. He was a mutant.

Professor Charles Xavier had already been watching. Xavier was the founder of a remarkable school in Westchester, New York — a place called the Xavier Institute — where young mutants could learn to master their abilities and use them to protect a world that feared them. Xavier invited Hank to join the team he was assembling: the X-Men. Alongside Scott Summers, Jean Grey, Bobby Drake, and Warren Worthington III, Hank McCoy became Beast, wearing a blue-and-yellow uniform and bringing both tremendous power and genuine scholarship to every mission.

Hank was not the hero who shouted the loudest or flew the highest. He was the hero who memorized the villain's chemistry notes, designed a better antidote, and then did a double back-flip off a Sentinel's knee to deliver it. He understood early on that being a mutant was not a curse — it was a calling. He became the heart of the X-Men: brave in battle, kind in the quiet moments, and always, always ready with a book recommendation for anyone who needed one.
