Nathaniel Essex — the villain who called himself Mister Sinister — had been collecting genetic data on mutants for decades, and he had saved the most complex files for the X-Men. His underground laboratory beneath the streets of New York City was filled with stolen samples and incomplete experiments, and the machine he had built there — the Genetic Sequencer — could, if it finished its work, permanently rewrite mutant DNA. He planned to use it to create an army of modified mutants under his complete control. When the X-Men discovered his plan, Hank felt a cold precision click into place inside him, the particular focus that came when something truly serious needed to be stopped.

They went in fast. Cyclops, Jean Grey, Storm, Gambit, and Beast — through the tunnels beneath Manhattan, guided by a partial map Hank had reconstructed from stolen engineering schematics. Sinister's laboratory was defended by his Marauders, a group of mercenary mutants who hit hard and fast. Hank found himself facing Sabretooth — enormous, feral, and absolutely furious — in a corridor barely wide enough to swing properly.

The fight was technical and brutal. Sabretooth's claws were longer. Hank's agility was better. He used the walls, the ceiling, the narrow space itself — bouncing off surfaces, changing direction faster than Sabretooth could track, keeping just out of reach while directing his teammates' positions by radio. He was never simply fighting. He was always simultaneously mapping the room, assessing threats, managing the bigger picture.

Jean Grey reached the Genetic Sequencer first. She could feel the machine's power humming against her own telepathic sensitivity. Sinister himself appeared — pale-skinned, red diamonds on his forehead, a cold intelligence in his expression — and hit her with a focused psychic counter-blast. Jean staggered.

Hank crossed the laboratory in three enormous bounds, shoulder-blocked Sinister off his feet, and gave Jean the six seconds she needed to rip the Genetic Sequencer's power core free with her telekinesis. The machine went dark. Sinister fled through one of his escape hatches, as he always did.

The laboratory was destroyed. The data was secured. Back at the Xavier Institute, Hank spent two days analyzing the partial genetic data Sinister had collected and then ensured it was permanently erased. He wrote a brief note in his journal: the cost of this work is vigilance, always. Mister Sinister would build another laboratory. The X-Men would find it. That was the agreement they had made, unspoken, the day they first put on their uniforms.
