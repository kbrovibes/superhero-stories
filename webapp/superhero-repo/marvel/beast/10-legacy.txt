Every year at the Xavier Institute, on the last day before summer break, Hank McCoy taught the same lesson. It was not on the official curriculum. Professor Xavier had never assigned it. Hank had simply started doing it the second year he was back at the Institute, and by the time the students arrived in the classroom and found him sitting on the desk — not behind it, on it, his enormous feet hanging off the edge — they already knew what was coming.

He would ask them to tell him the hardest thing about being a mutant.

Not about their powers. Not about the missions. The hardest thing. And he would listen, really listen, without fixing or reassuring, until each person in the room had spoken. The answers varied every year. Being stared at in public. Not knowing how to control it. Feeling different from your family. Missing the life you might have had. Not knowing who you could trust.

Then Hank would tell them about the three days he spent alone in a Brand Corporation laboratory after his mutation became permanent. Not the version with a tidy resolution. The real version — the silence, the calculations that gave the same answer no matter how many times he reran them, the phone call to Xavier that took him four days to make.

"I'm not going to tell you it stops being hard," he said every year. "It doesn't, entirely. But it gets lighter. It gets lighter because you build things that hold it: people who know you, work that matters, the knowledge of your own capability. You've already started building. Look around this room."

They would look. The faces that had come in nervous and uncertain would be looking back at each other differently — recognizing something.

Hank had been an original X-Man, an Avenger, a doctor of two sciences, and the author of important discoveries that had helped mutants and humans alike. He had stood before the Senate and argued for the rights of people who could not argue for themselves. He had sat up all night with a frightened fifteen-year-old who could not stop accidentally making earthquakes, and he had been there in the morning when the earthquakes finally, gently, stopped.

The X-Men had given him everything: the chance to be fully himself, in a world that was still learning what that meant. All he had ever wanted to do was pass it on.

He climbed down from the desk, straightened his glasses, and sent his students out into the summer. "Go read something," he told them every year. They always groaned. He was always smiling when he said it.
