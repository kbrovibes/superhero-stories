When Hank McCoy was eighteen years old, he walked into Professor Charles Xavier's study at the Xavier Institute in Westchester and changed his whole life in about four hours.

Not because Xavier convinced him of anything. Because Hank met someone whose ideas he could ACTUALLY check — and when he checked them, they held up.

Xavier had a dream. It was a simple dream, really. He believed that mutants and regular humans could live together in peace, if enough people on both sides were willing to build that peace. He had founded the Xavier Institute to give young mutants a school, a home, and a place to discover what they could do. He had assembled the X-Men to protect the world — even when the world wasn't sure it wanted protecting.

Hank thought about all of this very carefully. He tested it. He poked at it from every angle. And he decided: yes. This is right. This is worth everything.

He put on the blue-and-yellow uniform. He became Beast. And he never looked back.

But being an X-Man wasn't only about battles. Sometimes the most important fights happened in rooms with chairs and microphones and people in suits.

One day, the government proposed something called the Mutant Registration Act. It would require every single mutant to register their name, their address, and their abilities with the government — as though being born different was something you needed to put on a list.

Hank McCoy read the proposal. Then he drove to Washington, D.C.

He walked into a Senate subcommittee hearing. He was blue. He was enormous. He had a briefcase full of notes. He stood at the microphone and gave a twelve-minute speech about civil rights, constitutional law, and what history has always told us about what happens when governments require people to register just for being who they are.

He cited twelve different legal cases. He quoted three philosophers. He looked every single senator in the eye.

Three of them changed their votes. The Mutant Registration Act failed to pass.

Hank drove home.

That evening, Professor Xavier came quietly into the laboratory where Hank was working. He didn't say much — Xavier was a man who knew when words were not required. He just put a hand on Hank's shoulder.

Hank nodded.

They had understood each other from the very first cup of tea in that oak-paneled study. They would understand each other as long as they lived.

The Xavier Institute was the first place Hank McCoy had ever been completely, openly, unapologetically himself.

He spent the rest of his life making sure others could say the same.
