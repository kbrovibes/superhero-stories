Tell me — have you ever tried to read a book upside down?

Hank McCoy has. In fact, Hank McCoy prefers it. On a quiet Tuesday at the Xavier Institute, you might walk past his laboratory, peer through the door, and find him hanging from the ceiling BY HIS FEET — completely comfortable — reading a large textbook on molecular genetics.

He does it because it helps him think. He cannot explain why. He has simply decided it is one of the interesting facts about being Hank McCoy.

He has two doctoral degrees. TWO! One in biophysics. One in biochemistry. He earned them BOTH before he was twenty-three years old. He has published scientific papers in important journals all over the world. He can walk into a room full of Nobel Prize winners and hold a conversation that makes every single one of them take notes.

And THEN — right after the Nobel Prize conversation — he will quote you a poem. Maybe Percy Bysshe Shelley. Maybe Walt Whitman. Maybe a long passage from Shakespeare that he has memorized so completely he could recite it while doing a backflip. He might do exactly that.

Because Hank's mind and Hank's body work together in a way most people can barely imagine. In the middle of an enormous battle — LEAP — bouncing off a Sentinel's arm — CRACK — sending one flying backward — he is simultaneously reading the factory markings on the machine's armor and calling out its weak point to Cyclops across the battlefield.

That is what it's like to be Beast.

He is also, I should tell you, an incredible cook. The Xavier Institute dining room has a special warmth on the nights Hank makes dinner. He once made a Moroccan lamb dish so extraordinarily good that Bobby Drake — Iceman, who has never in his life been at a loss for words — went completely quiet for nearly ten minutes.

Ten whole minutes of silence from Bobby Drake. Hank considers this one of his greatest achievements.

But the most important thing about Hank McCoy is none of those things. It is that when a new student arrives at the Xavier Institute — scared, not sure if their mutation is a gift or a mistake — Hank is the one who sits down with them. He asks what they love. He listens. He tells them, from real experience, that what makes them different is exactly what makes them remarkable.

He knows this because he learned it the long way.

And he passes it on to everyone he meets.
