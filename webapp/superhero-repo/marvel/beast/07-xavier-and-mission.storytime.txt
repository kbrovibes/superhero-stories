Once upon a time, a boy of eighteen with very large hands and a very large bag of science textbooks knocked on the door of Professor Charles Xavier's oak-paneled study.

"Come in," said Professor Xavier.

Hank McCoy came in. He looked at the bookshelves lining every wall, floor to ceiling, packed full of volumes on genetics and philosophy and history and mathematics. He looked at the warm fireplace. He looked at the Professor, seated behind a broad desk with a pot of tea already steeping and two cups already set out.

"Sit down, Mr. McCoy," Xavier said warmly. "Would you like some tea?"

"Yes, thank you," said Hank.

And he sat.

They talked for four hours.

They talked about the science of mutation — why it happened, what it meant for the future of the human species, whether the genome was naturally moving toward something new. They talked about the responsibilities that came with extraordinary gifts. They talked about whether Aristotle or Walt Whitman made a better authority to cite in the opening paragraph of a scientific paper. Xavier, gently, suggested that context mattered. Hank said context always mattered. They agreed completely, and they had been friends ever since.

Xavier's dream was a simple one. Simple dreams are always the most difficult kind to actually build.

He believed that mutants and regular humans could live together. That fear and difference, given patience and genuine goodwill, could be worked through. That the best answer to a world that feared you was not to retreat from it or try to conquer it, but to show — again and again, in small ways and large ones — that you were worthy of trust. He had built the Xavier Institute to give young mutants a school and a home: a place that said you belong here, exactly as you are.

Hank went home that evening and thought about Xavier's dream very carefully.

He tested every part of it — the way he tested every idea that truly mattered — poking at the edges, asking hard questions, looking for where it might fail under pressure. Some ideas fall apart when you examine them honestly. Some grow stronger.

Xavier's dream grew stronger.

Hank packed his books. He returned to the Xavier Institute. He put on the blue-and-yellow uniform of the X-Men, and he became Beast, and he did not look back.

For years he fought alongside Scott Summers and Jean Grey and Bobby Drake and Warren Worthington. He fought Sentinels in the New Mexico desert and the Brotherhood in the streets of New York. He worked in the laboratory. He taught in the classroom. He sat with frightened new students and helped them understand that what made them different was also what made them extraordinary.

He believed Xavier's dream. He worked for it every day.

And then one day, a new kind of fight arrived.

The government proposed the Mutant Registration Act: a law that would require every mutant to register their true name, their address, and their specific abilities with the federal government. Officially. On a list. Because they had been born different.

Hank read the proposal from beginning to end. He sat with it for a long, quiet evening.

Then he drove to Washington, D.C.

He walked into the hearing room of a Senate subcommittee. He was large and blue and carrying a briefcase, and every person in the room turned to watch him approach the microphone.

He stood there for one quiet moment, feeling the full weight of what he was about to do.

Then he opened his notes and began to speak.

Twelve minutes. He cited twelve legal cases and three philosophers and the full text of the Fourteenth Amendment. He spoke about what history tells us happens when governments require people to register simply for being who they were born to be. He spoke clearly and simply, the way he always did when something truly mattered.

When he finished, the room was very quiet.

Three senators changed their votes. The Mutant Registration Act did not pass.

Hank drove home through the dark.

That evening, the laboratory door opened softly. Professor Xavier came quietly to stand beside Hank's bench.

He did not speak. He put his hand on Hank's shoulder.

Hank looked up. He nodded.

There was nothing else that needed saying. They had understood each other from that very first cup of tea — the eighteen-year-old with enormous hands and the Professor with an enormous dream, sitting together in a warm room, discovering that they wanted the same world.

The Xavier Institute was the first place Hank McCoy had ever been completely himself. Books and poetry and biochemistry and a heart bigger than his considerable hands.

He spent his whole life trying to build more places like it.

Because everyone — without exception — deserves a place like that.

And Hank McCoy, Professor Xavier would tell you if you asked, was the very best at making it so.
