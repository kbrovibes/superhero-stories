The Apocalypse virus had spread to six cities before anyone understood what it was — a synthetic pathogen engineered by the ancient mutant warlord En Sabah Nur, who called himself Apocalypse. He believed only the strongest deserved to survive, and he had released his virus to test the world. The virus did not kill — it amplified whatever ability a person already carried, human or mutant, past any safe limit. It turned ordinary people into raging, uncontrollable forces of nature. Stopping it required both a cure and a delivery system powerful enough to reach every infected person simultaneously.

It required, in other words, both the X-Men and the Avengers working together.

Hank and Tony Stark shared a laboratory at Avengers Tower for seventy-two hours without sleeping. It was the most productive three days of Hank's scientific life and also the loudest, because Tony liked to play music at high volume while working and Hank preferred silence. They compromised on moderate classical music, which neither of them loved, and produced an aerosol cure of extraordinary precision in the time allotted.

The delivery problem was Jean Grey's and Thor's to solve. Jean could broadcast a psychic signal across a wide area, but the cure needed to be physically present. Thor, wielding Mjolnir, could generate a magnetic weather event — a controlled electromagnetic storm — that would carry the aerosol cure in its air currents across entire regions simultaneously. They had tested it twice in controlled conditions. The third test would be the real one.

Hank stood at the top of Avengers Tower with the first canister, watching Thor take his position on the roof. Captain America was coordinating ground teams in all six cities. Cyclops and Storm were airborne, clearing infected zones. Jean Grey sat in the center of the building, hands folded, concentrating with an intensity that made the air hum.

Thor raised Mjolnir. The sky changed.

In every infected city, the air carried something new — invisible, precise, drifting through open windows and crowded streets. Within four hours, the virus was contained. Within eight, it was gone. The people affected were disoriented but unharmed.

Afterward, Tony Stark walked into the empty laboratory and looked at the boards still covered in Hank's handwriting and said, simply, "Good work, McCoy." Hank nodded. They shook hands. Tony went to find something to eat. Hank sat in the quiet laboratory for a while, feeling the particular warmth of a problem that had been very large and was now completely solved.

Then he called the Xavier Institute to check on his students. Of course.
