The first time Hank McCoy sat in Professor Charles Xavier's oak-paneled study, he was eighteen years old and carrying every science textbook he owned in a bag over one enormous shoulder. Xavier had offered him tea. Hank had accepted. They talked for four hours about the genetics of mutation, about the moral responsibilities of extraordinary people, and about whether a quotation from Aristotle was technically better for opening a research paper than a quotation from Walt Whitman. Xavier, gently, suggested that context mattered. Hank said context always mattered. They agreed on this completely, and they had been friends ever since.

Xavier's belief was simple: mutants and humans could live together in peace, if enough people on both sides chose to build that peace. He had founded the Xavier Institute in Westchester, New York, to give young mutants a place to learn, belong, and become. He had assembled the X-Men to protect a world that did not always know it needed them. He did not want mutants to rule humanity, as Magneto argued. He wanted mutants and humans to understand each other.

Hank believed this too — not because Xavier had told him to, but because he had thought it through himself and arrived at the same answer. That was the thing about Hank: he did not follow ideas. He tested them. Xavier's dream passed every test he applied.

When the Xavier Institute faced one of its darkest moments — the threat of a new government program called the Mutant Registration Act, which would require all mutants to register their identities and abilities — Hank lobbied the United States Congress himself. He stood before a Senate subcommittee, blue-furred and yellow-eyed, and made a twelve-minute argument from first principles about civil rights, constitutional law, and the history of how societies had treated people who were simply different. Three senators changed their positions. The act failed to pass.

He never told Xavier about the speech. Xavier found out anyway — he had a way of knowing things — and came to find Hank in the laboratory that evening. He didn't say much. He put a hand on Hank's shoulder. Hank nodded. They understood each other completely.

The Xavier Institute was not just Hank's workplace. It was the first place he had ever been exactly who he was, out loud, with no apology. He owed Xavier that. He spent his life making sure as many others as possible could say the same.
