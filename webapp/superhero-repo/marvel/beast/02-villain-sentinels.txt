The Sentinels were built to be unstoppable. Standing thirty feet tall, clad in purple-and-pink armor plating, they rolled out of the Bolivar Trask research complex in New Mexico with one mission: find mutants and contain them. Trask had convinced the government that mutants were a danger to humanity — that people like Hank McCoy, with their extraordinary abilities, were a threat that had to be controlled. The first time the X-Men saw a Sentinel rising above the desert horizon, its eyes glowing orange and a scanning beam sweeping across the sand, Beast felt something cold settle in his chest.

It was not fear of the machine's power. It was sadness. Someone had built these things specifically to hunt people like him.

The X-Men split into pairs and moved fast. Cyclops fired optic blasts at the first Sentinel's knee joint — a precision shot that made it stagger. Iceman threw up a wall of ice to slow its advance. But there were three Sentinels, and they adapted quickly, learning the X-Men's patterns within minutes. Hank watched the second Sentinel track Jean Grey's telekinetic energy signature and pivot toward her with terrifying accuracy.

He acted without hesitation. Hank launched himself off a rock formation, vaulted the Sentinel's scanning arm, and landed on its massive shoulder housing. From there he could see the faint seam where the neck armor connected to the cranial module — the one gap that might give them a chance. He wedged his fingers into the joint and tore the sensor array free with a sound like a car crash. The Sentinel lurched, its targeting system scrambled, and for thirty crucial seconds it could not lock onto any mutant signature at all.

That was the opening Jean Grey needed. She lifted every loose stone and metal fragment within a hundred yards and drove them into the Sentinel's chest cavity. It went down hard, leaving a crater in the New Mexico sand.

The third Sentinel retreated, transmitting data back to Trask's laboratory. As the X-Men gathered in the desert dust, Hank picked up a piece of the destroyed Sentinel's armor and examined it carefully. He studied the manufacturing marks, the metal alloy, the energy cell design. He was already writing notes in his head.

"They'll rebuild them," Scott said quietly.

"They will," Hank agreed. "So we'd better understand them better than their builders do." He tucked the fragment into his pack and looked out at the long horizon. The Sentinels were not the last problem the world would create for mutants. But Hank McCoy would be ready.
