If you walked into the Beast's laboratory at the Xavier Institute on any quiet Tuesday, you would find three things: a complex biochemical experiment running on the center bench, a stack of seven or eight books in multiple languages, and Hank McCoy himself — upside down on the ceiling, reading. He had discovered early in life that hanging by his feet while reading helped him concentrate. He had never found a good scientific explanation for this. He simply accepted it as one of the more charming facts about himself.

Hank held two doctoral degrees — one in biophysics, one in biochemistry — both earned before the age of twenty-three. He had published papers in the journals of leading universities. He could discuss genetics with a Nobel laureate and then, in the same breath, recite a sonnet by Percy Bysshe Shelley or a verse from Walt Whitman. He kept his shelves organized by subject and then by emotional weight: serious science on the left, poetry in the middle, detective novels on the right for Fridays.

But Hank's intelligence was not separate from his heroism — it was the source of it. When the X-Men faced a foe they could not simply outpunch, it was Beast who built the antidote, designed the trap, or found the weakness in the enemy's technology by reading their engineering patents at three in the morning. He could hold Cyclops steady in battle by calling out a Sentinel's blind spot while simultaneously landing a flying kick on a second Sentinel fifteen feet away.

He was also, famously, an excellent cook. The Xavier Institute dining room was at its best on nights when Hank volunteered to make dinner — elaborate, careful meals that he researched with the same seriousness he brought to everything else. He once made a Moroccan lamb dish that reduced Bobby Drake to complete silence for almost ten minutes, which was considered a significant achievement.

And he was kind. Not in a simple, easy way — in the deep, attentive way of someone who has thought carefully about what kindness actually requires. When new students arrived at the Xavier Institute, frightened and uncertain about their mutations, it was often Hank who sat with them and asked them questions about what they loved. He believed that a person's gifts, however strange they looked at first, were worth celebrating. He had learned this the long way. He knew it all the way through.
