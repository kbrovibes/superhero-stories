For a time after leaving the X-Men, Hank McCoy worked as a biochemist at a place called the Brand Corporation in New York, trying to understand the genetic science behind mutation. He was good at the research — brilliant, in fact — and he enjoyed the quiet rhythm of laboratory work: the hum of centrifuges, the clean smell of reagents, the precise pleasure of a formula that finally balanced. Then came the night he discovered that the Brand Corporation was secretly developing a mutation-amplifying serum, and someone was about to steal it.

Hank did what he always did in a crisis: he improvised. He synthesized a temporary extract of the serum himself, intending to use it to disguise his own mutant DNA signature and catch the thief. The plan worked — he found the spy and stopped the theft. But when he tried to reverse the effect, he discovered that he had waited too long. The serum had become permanent. When Hank looked into the laboratory mirror, he saw grey fur covering his body. Within days it had deepened to blue-black.

His hands were the same enormous hands. His mind was the same sharp, curious mind. But now there was no pretending he looked ordinary. He was, unmistakably, a large blue creature with pointed ears, bright yellow eyes, and fangs where his regular teeth had been.

He spent three days alone in the laboratory. He reran his calculations. He double-checked every variable. The answer did not change.

Then he washed his face, straightened his glasses — which he still wore, out of habit and mild irony — and called Professor Xavier.

"I need to come back to the Institute," he said.

"The door has always been open," Xavier replied.

The X-Men welcomed him home without a single strange look. Scott nodded. Jean smiled. Bobby made an ice sculpture of a large blue bear in the common room, which Hank found extremely funny. He pinned a quote from William Shakespeare above his laboratory bench — "We know what we are, but know not what we may be" — and went back to work.

It took time to make peace with his reflection. But Hank McCoy had always believed that the measure of a person was not in their appearance, and that the words in a good book were the same whether a perfectly ordinary human hand or a large blue furred one turned the pages. He looked different now. He was still entirely, completely himself.
