Every single year, on the very last day before summer break at the Xavier Institute, Hank McCoy teaches the same lesson.

It's not in any textbook. Professor Xavier never put it on the official schedule. Hank just started doing it one year, and now the students arrive on that last day, find him sitting ON the desk — not behind it, his enormous blue-furred feet dangling off the edge, his glasses slightly crooked — and they all know exactly what's coming.

He asks: "Tell me the hardest thing about being a mutant."

Not the coolest power. Not the best mission moment. The HARDEST thing.

And one by one, the students speak. Being stared at in public, even when you're just getting orange juice at the market. Not being able to shake someone's hand without worrying what will happen. Feeling different from your whole family — separated from them by something you didn't choose. Not knowing who in the world you can actually trust with the truth about yourself.

Hank listens. Really listens. He doesn't rush. He doesn't fix. He doesn't reassure with easy words. He just lets every single person be heard.

Then he tells them HIS hardest thing.

The Brand Corporation laboratory. The three days alone after his mutation became permanent. Standing at the mirror. The calculations that gave the same answer no matter how many times he ran them. The four days it took him to pick up the phone and call Professor Xavier.

Not the tidy version. The real one — with the silence in it.

"I'm not going to tell you it stops being hard," he says every year. "It doesn't, entirely. But it gets lighter. It gets lighter as you build people who truly know you, work that matters, and the knowledge of what you are actually capable of — which is always more than you think. You've already started building all of those things. Look around this room."

The students look at each other.

Something moves through the room then. Those faces — scared and uncertain when they first arrived — look at each other and recognize something.

We are in this together.

Hank McCoy had been an X-Man from the very beginning. An Avenger. A doctor of two sciences. He had argued before the United States Senate for the rights of every mutant in America. He had sat all night with a frightened fifteen-year-old who couldn't stop accidentally making earthquakes — just sitting quietly, patiently — and was there in the morning when the ground finally went still.

All of it mattered.

He slides off the desk. He straightens his glasses.

"Go read something," he says.

Every year: groaning. Every year: he is already smiling.

That's our Beast. Scientist. Avenger. X-Man. The best teacher at the Xavier Institute, every single year, without question.
