Here is a story about the bravest thing you can do — and it is NOT punching a robot.

After some time away from the X-Men, Hank McCoy was working as a biochemist at a company called the Brand Corporation in New York City. He had a white laboratory coat and a whole shelf of very complicated chemistry equipment and a stack of books taller than a first-grader. He loved it.

But then he found out a secret. The Brand Corporation was working on a serum that could amplify mutant powers — and a spy was about to steal the formula.

Hank thought fast. He mixed up a temporary dose of the serum for himself, planning to disguise his own mutant energy signature, catch the spy, and then reverse the effect. Like a scientist-detective. Very clever!

The plan worked! He caught the spy. He stopped the theft. Mission accomplished!

But then he went to reverse the serum — and it didn't work. He had waited just a few minutes too long, and the chemical had locked into his cells permanently.

Hank looked in the laboratory mirror.

Grey fur. All over his body.

He looked again the next day.

Blue-black fur. Pointed ears. Bright yellow eyes. Fangs.

He sat very still for a moment.

Then he reran ALL of his calculations — every formula, every step — three times. Because Hank McCoy never accepted an answer until he was absolutely certain it was correct. The answer did not change.

He took a slow breath. He cleaned his glasses. He picked up the phone and called Professor Xavier.

"I need to come home," Hank said.

"I know," said Xavier. "Come."

When Hank walked through the door of the Xavier Institute, Scott Summers nodded at him like nothing was different. Jean Grey smiled and said, "Welcome back." And Bobby Drake — Iceman — went to the common room and made an enormous ice sculpture of a big blue bear.

Hank laughed until he had to sit down.

He put up a quote from William Shakespeare above his laboratory bench: "We know what we are, but know not what we may be." He rolled up his sleeves — fur and all — and got back to work.

Was it easy, seeing a new face in the mirror? No. It took time.

But here is what Hank McCoy knew, all the way down to his very large, very furry bones: what mattered was not what you looked like. What mattered was what you did. How you treated people. What you chose.

Blue fur and all — he was still Hank McCoy. Entirely and completely.

And Hank McCoy was, and always would be, extraordinary.
