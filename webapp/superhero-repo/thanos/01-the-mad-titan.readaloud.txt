Way out in the farthest reaches of space, there is — or rather, there WAS — a beautiful moon called Titan. It had cities that shimmered in the purple light. Gardens that stretched for miles. And way, way too many people crowded into it, eating up every last crumb of food, using every drop of water, until the whole world trembled on the edge of disaster.

A young boy could see it coming. His name was Thanos. And he had a plan.

"Let us choose half the people — at random, fairly — to go," he said. "So the other half can live."

The leaders of Titan called him mad. They called his plan cruel. They sent him away.

They were wrong. Titan collapsed anyway — famine, fighting, emptiness. Thanos stood on the dusty ruins of his beautiful home and felt something cold and certain settle in his chest. He had been right. Nobody had listened. And now everyone was gone.

Did that make him sad? Yes. Did it also make him MORE convinced that he was right? Also yes.

Thanos built himself a great starship and gathered the Black Order — a team of fierce warriors who would follow him anywhere. His second-in-command was Corvus Glaive, sharp and loyal. Proxima Midnight, who fought like a comet. Ebony Maw, who could talk his way through any door. Together they traveled from world to world — to Zen-Whoberi, to Praxius IX, to the icy moons of Aakon — and on each world, Thanos did what he believed was mercy.

On Zen-Whoberi, in the middle of the noise and smoke of his arrival, he noticed a small green-skinned girl standing alone, parents gone. He reached down and lifted her. He called her Gamora. He would raise her to be the most skilled fighter in the galaxy, and he would love her in the only way he knew how — which was not a very warm kind of love, but it was real.

He also took a blue girl, who he called Nebula, and tried to train her the same way. It hurt her more than it helped, though Thanos never seemed to understand that.

Thanos did not sleep in a palace. He slept in a wooden hut on a barge drifting at the edge of the galaxy. He was not interested in gold or power or thrones. He was interested in one thing: the six ancient Infinity Stones, scattered across the universe, each one controlling a law of reality itself.

He was patient. He was absolutely, terrifyingly certain he was right. And he was coming.
