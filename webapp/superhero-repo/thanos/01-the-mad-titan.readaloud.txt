A long, long time ago, on a purple moon called Titan, a child was born who saw things differently from everyone else. His name was Thanos, and even as a boy he could see the trouble coming. Titan was beautiful — full of cities and gardens — but too many people lived there, and not enough food grew to feed them all. Thanos had an idea: choose half the people at random to go, so the other half could live and be happy. The leaders of Titan called his idea cruel and impossible and sent him away.

They were wrong. Titan died anyway. The famine and the sickness and the fighting swallowed it whole. Thanos stood on the empty, dust-blown ruins of his home and felt a cold certainty settle inside him: he had been right. And nobody had listened.

He left Titan in a great starship, and with him came the Black Order — his loyal soldiers. World by world, they traveled — to Zen-Whoberi, to Praxius IX, to the icy moons of Aakon — and on each world Thanos did what no leader had done on Titan. He chose half to go, and half to remain. He did not do it with joy. He believed it was mercy.

On Zen-Whoberi, he found a small green girl named Gamora, whose parents were gone. He lifted her gently and called her his daughter. He taught her to fight, gave her blades that could cut starlight, and tried to give her a home. He did the same with a blue girl named Nebula, rebuilding her body and calling it love. Neither girl was sure what to call it.

Thanos did not live in a palace. He built a small wooden hut on a barge at the edge of space, and he looked at the stars the way a farmer looks at a field that needs tending. Out there in the dark, six ancient Infinity Stones waited. And Thanos, the Mad Titan of dead Titan, was already planning to find every one of them.
