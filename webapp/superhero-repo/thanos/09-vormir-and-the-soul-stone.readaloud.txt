Of all six Infinity Stones, the Soul Stone was the most mysterious. It did not sit in a space museum or get stored in a vault. It waited. Quietly. Secretly. On a world called Vormir.

Vormir is far — past the edge of the settled galaxy, under a sky that burns orange. Barren and silent and cold. And on its highest cliff stands a hooded figure who never leaves.

That figure was Johann Schmidt. The Red Skull. A villain who had tried to use the Tesseract for more power than he was meant to hold. The Tesseract deposited him here — unable to leave, unable to hold the stone, only able to guide seekers. A ghost with one purpose: tell them the price.

The Soul Stone has a price. It does not sell itself for power or cleverness or effort. Only one thing releases it:

A soul. For a soul.

To take the Soul Stone, you give up the soul of someone you truly love. Not someone useful. Someone genuinely, completely, really loved.

Thanos had brought Gamora to Vormir.

Take a breath. This part is hard to tell.

Gamora had always known where the Soul Stone was — buried that knowledge so deep inside herself she thought no one could ever reach it. But Nebula was part-machine, and memories saved automatically in her systems. Thanos found it there.

So they stood on the cliff together, Gamora and Thanos, with the Red Skull watching from his hood and the orange sky overhead.

Gamora understood the price before Thanos spoke. And she thought — she truly believed — that she was safe. Because she did not think Thanos truly loved her. Not enough. Not the way that counts.

Here is the hardest part of this story:

He did.

It was not a warm love, or a gentle one. It was shaped all wrong by all the wrong things. But it was real. There were tears on his face — real tears, on the cliff of Vormir, under the orange sky.

And Thanos — who had spent his whole life choosing his mission over everything — made the same choice one more time.

Gamora faded like a quiet light going out.

He walked down from Vormir with the Soul Stone — small and orange, warm in his palm — and pressed it into the fourth setting of the gauntlet.

Four stones. Two to go.

Something small and quiet had cracked inside him. He did not show it. He was very good at not showing things.

But it was there. And the Avengers racing to stop him across all of space had no idea the hardest thing Thanos would ever do was already behind him.

He thought the rest was just finishing.

He was wrong about that too.
