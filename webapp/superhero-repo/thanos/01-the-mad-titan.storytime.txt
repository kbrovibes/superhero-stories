Close your eyes and think about a moon. Not our moon — a different one, far away in a distant part of the universe, where the sky is the color of an old bruise and the mountains are tall as skyscrapers made of copper and ash.

That moon was called Titan.

It was beautiful once. Its cities rose high into the purple sky. Its rivers ran clear. Its people were clever and strong and proud. And then — slowly, terribly — it ran out of enough food. Too many people, not enough to eat. The cities became hungry places. The streets became crowded and sad.

A child was born on Titan, and even as a child, he was different. His skin was purple — the deep, weathered purple of a mountain at dusk. He was enormous — taller and wider than other children, with a chin like a cliff edge and eyes like embers. His name was Thanos.

Thanos thought about the problem. He studied it. He worked out the math. He was only young, but he was already the kind of thinker who could see things from very far away.

His idea was this: cut the population in half. Not cruelly — by chance, randomly, so that no one was chosen and no one was spared on purpose. Simply reduce the number of people until the food and water could sustain those who remained. A sad solution. A hard solution. But, Thanos believed with his whole heart, a necessary one.

He brought his idea to the council of Titan. They looked at him. They called him mad. They sent him away.

Titan fell to ruin. Exactly as Thanos had warned.

And Thanos left on a starship into the dark universe, carrying his terrible certainty like a stone in his chest.

He gathered followers. People who believed in his purpose, or feared him, or both. Together they became the Black Order — a band of powerful warriors who traveled from world to world, doing what Thanos believed must be done: culling half of every population they visited, so the other half could thrive.

He went to a world called Zen-Whoberi. He went to Praxius IX. He went to the icy moons of Aakon, which were frozen and silent. World after world, galaxy after galaxy, Thanos arrived and did what no one else would do, and believed with all of himself that he was helping.

On Zen-Whoberi he saw a small girl in the crowd. She was green-skinned and bright-eyed and she was crying, looking for her family. She was about to be chosen. Thanos reached down and picked her up.

He named her Gamora.

He raised her. He taught her to fight — and she became the deadliest warrior in the galaxy, which is no small thing when the galaxy contains Thor and Black Widow and all the others. He taught her discipline and strength and never quite managed to teach her to believe what he believed, which she always kept quietly, fiercely to herself.

He also took another girl — blue-skinned and silver-armed, part machine and part child — named Nebula. He rebuilt her piece by piece, training her, replacing flesh with metal when she lost to Gamora in practice, making her harder and colder with every year. He thought he was making her stronger. He did not understand — because understanding was not always his gift — how much this cost her.

Thanos did not live in a palace. He did not wear a crown. He slept in a simple wooden place on a great barge at the edge of the galaxy, looking at the stars, thinking about the math.

Somewhere out there, six ancient objects waited in the dark.

Six stones — each one controlling a different law of reality itself. Space. Mind. Reality. Power. Time. Soul. Six colors. Six powers. Six keys to a door that no being had ever been strong enough to open.

Until now.

Thanos had worked out the math on that too.

If he could hold all six stones at once — in a single golden gauntlet, forged strong enough to contain them — he could do what his plan for Titan had meant to do, but larger. So much larger. Not one world. Not one moon. Every world, every moon, every galaxy in the universe. All at once. In a single snap.

Half of all living things, gone. Painlessly. Instantly. By chance, not by malice.

And the other half — they would have room to breathe. Room to grow. Room to thrive.

He would be saving the universe. He was sure of it.

"I call it mercy," he told the stars.

The stars did not answer. They never do.

And Thanos set out across the dark, enormous universe, carrying his golden plan and his purple hands and his perfect, terrible certainty — toward the most powerful objects that had ever existed.

The story of Thanos is not a comfortable one. But it is important. Because understanding what someone believes — even when that belief is wrong — is the beginning of knowing how to stop them.

And the universe, as it turned out, had a few ideas about that.
