Before a single Infinity Stone had ever touched his hand, Thanos knew he needed more than strength to hold them. The stones were older than planets, older than stars — raw fragments of the universe's first instant. No ordinary material could contain them all at once. He needed something forged at the edge of creation, by hands clever enough to shape the impossible. He needed the dwarves of Nidavellir.

Nidavellir was a forge unlike any other in the nine realms. It was built around a dying star — a neutron star so dense that a spoonful of it weighed more than a mountain. The dwarves, led by their king Eitri, used that star's heat and gravity to smelt Uru metal, the most durable material in any universe. Mjolnir was Eitri's work. Stormbreaker would be, too. But when Thanos arrived at Nidavellir, he did not come as a customer. He came as a conqueror, surrounded by the Black Order, his massive form filling the great stone corridor of the forge hall.

Eitri was enormous by any measure — as dwarves of Nidavellir were — but even he looked small beside the Mad Titan. Thanos told him what he wanted: a gauntlet of Uru metal, sized for his own left hand, with six settings shaped to hold each of the Infinity Stones. He told Eitri he would spare the other two hundred dwarves of Nidavellir in exchange. Eitri had no choice. He opened the star's ring, letting its terrible heat flood the forge, and his smiths worked for three days and nights without sleep. The golden gauntlet that emerged was the finest thing Eitri had ever made — a masterwork of craft and sorrow, every joint precise, every groove shaped to channel a power it was never meant to hold.

When the gauntlet was finished, Thanos took it, closed his fingers around the intricate gold, and held it up against the light of Nidavellir's great star. Then he kept his word in the worst possible way: he spared the dwarves of Nidavellir, but destroyed the forge's mechanism so that no weapon could ever be made to challenge him. He left Eitri alone on the dead ring, the great star cooling behind him, two hundred workers gone, their king standing with burned, empty hands.

Thanos left Nidavellir wearing the Infinity Gauntlet, its six empty settings gleaming like open eyes. The quest that would change the universe was beginning in earnest, and in his heart was the same quiet conviction he had carried since the day Titan fell: that he was right, that no one else would do what had to be done, and that the gauntlet now on his hand was the instrument of a mercy no one else understood.
