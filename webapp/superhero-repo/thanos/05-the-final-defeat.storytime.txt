Five years is a long time to grieve. Long enough for grass to grow over empty parking lots. Long enough for children to become teenagers. Long enough for the world to grow quieter, and sadder, and somehow still beautiful, in the way that the world always insists on being even when you wish it would just stop.

But five years is not forever.

The remaining Avengers had not stopped moving. Captain America ran support groups in borrowed community centers. Natasha Romanoff coordinated the scattered heroes from a compound that felt too large now. Bruce Banner, who had found a way to be both his gentle self and his enormous green self at the same time, smiled in photographs and quietly worked the problem. Thor ate fish and stared at the sea in New Asgard, carrying his regret like a stone in his chest.

Tony Stark lived by a lake with Pepper and a small girl named Morgan who asked for stories at bedtime and called him Daddy.

And then Scott Lang fell out of a storage van in San Francisco — he had been inside the Quantum Realm when everything happened, and for him it had only been five hours — and he drove to the Avengers Compound with an impossible idea burning a hole in his pocket.

What if the Quantum Realm was a road through time?

Tony said no. He went home. He put Morgan to bed. He looked at the lake.

Then he went back to his workshop and solved it. He sat down with a mug of coffee and a holo-interface and worked the math that everyone said couldn't be worked, and somewhere around three in the morning, he figured it out, and he drove to Steve Rogers' door in the grey dawn and said: "I figured it out. I found a way."

The Time Heist.

Teams traveled back through the Quantum Realm to borrow the Infinity Stones from points in the past — before Thanos had them — with one clear rule: be invisible, take nothing else, leave no ripple.

It worked. It hurt, and it cost, and Natasha Romanoff made a choice on the cliffs of Vormir that meant Clint Barton walked out of a portal alone with an orange stone clenched in his fist and tears drying on his face.

But they brought the stones home. All six.

Bruce Banner — Smart Hulk, the gentlest of giants — wore the new gauntlet Tony had built, red and gold like Iron Man's armor, and he snapped his fingers with every stone blazing. The energy traveled up his arm like wildfire and he held on, because there was no other choice.

Around the world, people came back. In their beds. On their streets. Confused and blinking and alive.

And then the sky fell in.

Past-Thanos — a version of him from years earlier, who had learned through Nebula's shared memories what the Avengers had done — had sent his warship, Sanctuary II, and it rained fire on the Avengers Compound until the building was nothing but rubble and smoke.

Tony crawled out of the dust. Steve crawled out. Thor crawled out. Three men — a genius in a damaged suit, a soldier with a broken shield, a god with a heavy axe — standing in the wreckage while an enormous warship blocked out the sun.

Past-Thanos walked toward them.

This Thanos had not yet collected the stones. He did not have the gauntlet. He did not need it. He was going to destroy everything and build a new universe from scratch — one that would not remember what had been lost, and would not think to fight him.

He was so certain. He was always so certain.

Tony and Thor and Steve fought him — three against one, every trick they had, every gram of courage — and they held on. And then Steve picked up Mjolnir — Thor's hammer, the one that only the worthy can lift — and lightning crackled down his arm and through his vibranium shield and Steve Rogers fought like a storm made of stars.

But Thanos called his army. The full might of the Sanctuary II, tens of thousands of Chitauri and Outriders and Black Order, pouring from the sky. Even the three greatest fighters on Earth began to fall back.

Then the sky turned gold.

A hundred rings of sorcerer-light opened above the battlefield. Through them came everyone who had been gone. Wakanda's armies. The Guardians of the Galaxy. Ravagers on their ships. Wanda Maximoff with her hands blazing red. Sam Wilson on crimson wings. Peter Parker on webs, saying "Oh wow, oh wow, oh wow" to himself. Bucky Barnes. Falcon. Groot. Captain Marvel, arriving like a comet, punching through the Sanctuary II's hull until it fell.

Steve Rogers pressed his comms button.

"Avengers," he said, very quietly. And then the word that made the whole universe hold its breath. "Assemble."

Two armies ran toward each other.

Wanda Maximoff cornered Thanos alone and the earth broke around them. Spider-Man swung the gauntlet above the battle, webbing it from hand to hand. Pepper Potts flew in Rescue armor. Ant-Man became Giant-Man and waded through the army like a man stepping through tall grass.

And Thanos caught the gauntlet.

He raised it above his head. He was going to snap. A new universe. Empty. Quiet. His.

Tony Stark moved.

The nanobots in his suit reached out across space in half a heartbeat, touching the gauntlet, touching each stone, pulling them free one by one and drawing them across the air onto Tony's own armored hand.

Six stones. The weight of everything. The heat of every sun.

Tony Stark looked at Thanos across the smoke and the ruin of the battlefield, and Thanos looked back, and there was a long moment between them — two people who had spent their whole lives certain they were saving the world, one of them right and one of them wrong, and one of them about to prove it.

"I am Iron Man," said Tony Stark.

And he snapped his fingers.

Thanos — enormous, purple, certain, terrible — looked at his hands. And he began to dissolve. The Black Order faded. The Chitauri went dark. The Outriders stilled. The ships crumbled. Wave after wave of army turned to ash and drifted away on the wind, until there was nothing left of Thanos's great terrible plan but settling dust and a Wakandan field full of flowers.

Tony Stark lay down.

The hardest, bravest thing anyone had ever done — he had done it. And now he was still. All around him were the people he had fought for: Pepper kneeling beside him, Morgan's sunflower in her hands, heroes standing in a ring of reverence and grief.

He had done it.

Thanos had believed — with his whole enormous heart — that his math was right. That the universe needed him to make that choice. That no one else could, or would.

He was wrong. Not because the universe did not have problems. But because the answer to hard problems is not to take away half of everything. The answer is to be brave enough to build something better.

Tony Stark knew that. He had spent his whole life building better things.

And in the end, he built the most important thing of all: a universe that got to keep going.

So close your eyes now. Let the battle quiet. Feel the warmth of a world that survived.

Somewhere, a small girl with her father's eyes is planting sunflowers by a lake.

Somewhere, the stars are shining.

And somewhere out there, the next story is just beginning.
