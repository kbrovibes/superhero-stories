Thanos did not travel the universe alone. At his side, always, were the Black Order — a family he had assembled the same way he had assembled everything: by conquest, by will, and by something he was convinced was love. They were fierce and devoted, and between them they could crack open almost any world.

Corvus Glaive was the most loyal of them, thin and sharp as the blade he carried. His weapon was legendary: a glaive that could cut through any material, even the molecular bonds that held an Infinity Stone in its housing. He was Thanos's most trusted hand, and he moved through battle like a quiet storm, never loud, always final. At his side was his partner Proxima Midnight, who fought with a spear made from a collapsing star. When she threw it, the spear split into three shafts of light mid-flight, each one seeking a different target. Captain America himself would need help to stop her.

Ebony Maw was different — he did not rely on brute force at all. Where Corvus Glaive cleared a room with steel, Ebony Maw moved through it with words. He was a tactician and a persuader, capable of talking his way through doors that armies could not breach. He could also, with his telekinetic gifts, reshape a room into a trap without touching anything. He called Thanos "my father" with what sounded like genuine reverence.

The largest of the Black Order was Cull Obsidian — sometimes called Black Dwarf — enormous even by the standards of beings who fought alongside Thanos. He carried a chain-axe that could level small buildings and wore armor so thick it deflected energy blasts with a shrug. He was not subtle. He was not meant to be. He was the signal that a battle had begun in earnest.

And then there were Gamora and Nebula, the daughters Thanos had raised from conquered worlds, already described in the story of Titan. They had not chosen this family. They had been taken into it, shaped by it, scarred by it in ways Thanos never fully understood, and they would each find their own path away from it in time.

Together, the Black Order had carried out Thanos's work on a hundred worlds. They were not mercenaries — they believed, at least most of them, in the mission. They were the instrument of Thanos's grand plan before the Infinity Stones changed everything, and they would fight for him until the very end, when the Avengers' final battle left them, one by one, turning to ash in the Wakandan fields.
