When the Teen Titans had been a team only a few months, a spacecraft descended through the clouds above Jump City and landed in the courtyard of Titans Tower. Out stepped Komand'r — Blackfire, princess of Tamaran — looking sleek in dark armor trimmed with silver. She said she was Starfire's beloved sister and had searched the galaxy to find her. She hugged Koriand'r warmly in front of everyone, and Koriand'r — because her heart was open and hope was always stronger than her memory of pain — let herself believe it.

Blackfire was charming. She mastered every Earth game in an afternoon, flew cartwheels above the bay, and won races with Cyborg and Beast Boy. She called Koriand'r by her childhood nickname. And slowly she began to suggest that Starfire's teammates didn't truly understand her, that Earth was not really home, that Tamaran needed her back.

Robin noticed something wrong. He had trained with Batman, the world's greatest detective, and he knew how to watch for the things people did not say. He saw Blackfire glancing at the Tower's security systems, asking questions about Starfire's powers that were too precise to be idle curiosity. He brought his concern to Koriand'r, and she felt the old wound open — the memory of being handed over, of the cold ship, of years without sunlight.

The confrontation came when Blackfire revealed a new deal: she had promised to deliver Starfire to a Gordanian warlord in exchange for a Centauri Moon Diamond that would buy her power over a small star system. She had never changed. The warmth had been a mask over the same cold ambition that had betrayed her sister years before.

Starfire did not cry. She let the sadness pass through her the way Tamaran's twin suns pass through clouds and keep shining. Then she raised her hands and starbolts blazed brilliant green. Blackfire was fierce and fast, and their battle lit the sky above Jump City in emerald and violet arcs. But Starfire fought with something Blackfire had never learned: the strength that comes from protecting the people you love. She drove her sister back, broke the Gordanian deal, and sent Blackfire away — not in anger, but with quiet, steady resolve. The door was always open if Blackfire chose to change. Starfire would not be deceived again.

Robin found her on the Tower roof afterward, sitting with her legs dangling and her eyes on the stars. He sat beside her and neither said much. Sometimes the best thing a friend can do is simply stay, and that night, that was everything.
