What would you teach, if you were a superhero and a group of younger heroes needed to learn from you?

Robin would teach strategy. Cyborg would teach technology. Raven would teach focus. Beast Boy would teach — well, Beast Boy would teach a lot of things enthusiastically and with extensive animal demonstrations.

And Starfire? Starfire would teach navigation.

Not with maps. With stars.

She took the new trainees up to the Tower roof on clear evenings — a group of young heroes, twelve and thirteen years old, still figuring out their powers, still figuring out themselves. She had them lie on their backs and look at the sky. She named Earth stars first — visible from Jump City on a clear night. Then stars she navigated by in space. Then, in late autumn, if you knew where to look — a faint point far southwest.

That one is Tamaran.

She told them her story: falling toward Earth, not knowing what planet it was, landing hard in a parking lot in Jump City. A young man named Dick Grayson reaching out his hand. English flooding into her mind in one moment, like a door opening.

"I'm teaching you to feel oriented," she said. "Not geographically. Inside yourself."

One night, a trainee named Ari sat quietly while the others pointed at stars. Ari could make force fields, which was excellent, but she was afraid of open spaces, which was difficult. She said, very quietly: "I don't think I'm brave enough to be a Titan."

Starfire sat down beside her. She did not say "yes you are!" right away. She thought carefully — she always thinks carefully about the important things.

She said: "I did not feel brave when I arrived here. I felt lost. I felt far from home. What made me brave was not feeling ready. It was doing the thing in front of me because it mattered. And then the next thing. The bravery comes from the doing."

Ari was quiet. Then: "Which one is Tamaran?"

Starfire pointed.

"You left your whole planet," Ari said. "Your people."

"I left some of my family," Starfire said. "And I found the rest."

She looked at Jump City's lights spreading out across the bay below the Tower. She thought of Robin and Cyborg and Raven and Beast Boy. She thought of every rooftop, every battle, every evening of just being together. She thought of a parking lot and a hand and an English word she loved: home.

She had chosen this. Every single day, she chose it. And choosing something — really choosing, with your whole heart — makes it yours in the deepest possible way.

Ari looked at the star. Then she looked at Starfire.

"I think I'll stay," she said.

"Good," said Starfire. And she smiled.
