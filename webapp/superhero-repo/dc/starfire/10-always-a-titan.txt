Years after the Teen Titans had grown into a network spanning the world, a younger group came to Titans Tower to train. They were twelve and thirteen years old, newer to their powers, uncertain in ways that were immediately familiar to anyone who had once been uncertain themselves. Cyborg ran training sessions. Raven ran academics. Beast Boy supervised morale with great enthusiasm. And Starfire taught what she called navigation.

It was not a course about maps.

She took the new Titans to the Tower roof on clear evenings and had them lie on their backs and look at the sky. She named the stars visible from Earth, then the ones she navigated by in space, then the faint star southwest that was Tamaran's system — visible in late autumn, if you knew where to look. She told them what it had been like to fall toward Earth not knowing what it was. She told them what Robin had looked like, extending a hand in a parking lot. She told them what it felt like to hear a language pour into your mind and understand, for the first time, where you were.

She was teaching them to feel oriented. That was the most useful thing she knew how to pass on.

One evening a young trainee named Ari — who could generate force fields and was terrified of large open spaces — said quietly that she did not think she was brave enough to be a Titan. Starfire sat down beside her and did not immediately disagree. She thought carefully, the way she always did with important things.

She said: "I did not feel brave when I arrived here. I felt lost and very far from home. What made me brave was the same thing that will make you brave: you stop waiting to feel ready, and you do the thing in front of you because it matters. Then you do the next thing. The bravery comes from doing."

Ari was quiet. Then: "Which one is Tamaran?"

Starfire pointed at the faint star southwest.

"You left your whole planet," Ari said softly. "Your people. Your family."

"I left some of my family," Starfire said. "And I found the rest."

She looked at Jump City's lights spreading across the bay, warm and familiar and hers. She thought about Robin and Cyborg and Raven and Beast Boy, about every rooftop and battle and quiet evening in between. She thought about what it meant to belong somewhere you had chosen rather than somewhere you were born.

It meant everything. And it had meant everything from the very first day she had stood in a smoke-scorched parking lot and decided, with her whole heart, to stay.
