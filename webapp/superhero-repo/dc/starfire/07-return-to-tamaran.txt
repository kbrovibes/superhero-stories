The message arrived through a Tamaranean courier probe, small and cylindrical and still warm from hyperspace. It said, in the formal language of the royal court, that Tamaran had been struck by ion storms and the ancient energy conduits that channeled sunlight through the palace district had gone dark. Without them, children and elderly — those who could not yet or could no longer generate starbolts efficiently — were weakened. The message was signed by the High Councillor. Koriand'r's name was at the top.

She told the Titans she needed to go. Robin said immediately: he was coming with her.

They made the journey through the Vega system in a borrowed Tamaranean spacecraft. Robin studied navigational charts. Starfire prepared herself. Tamaran was still the world where Blackfire lived, where the court that had once bargained her away still governed, where she was technically still a princess with obligations she had set aside.

The first sight of Tamaran through the port made her breath catch: the twin suns low on the horizon, the planet's surface a warm amber-gold in their combined light. Beautiful. It had always been beautiful.

The Tamaranean people received her with the respect due a princess and a genuine warmth that moved her more than the ceremony did. Children born after her departure had grown up hearing her name. A small girl with eyes like polished copper pressed a silver-leafed branch into her hands at the palace gates and looked up at her without speaking.

The conduit problem involved crystalline energy nodes fractured by the ion storms. Starfire flew into the shafts herself — her body was effectively a controlled solar source — and channeled precise starbolts into each node to reactivate it. It took a full day of careful work. When the last node lit and warm golden light bloomed across the palace district, a sound rose from the people outside: the Tamaranean harmonic of celebration, forty voices and then four hundred, rising and rising.

She left three days later. The High Councillor had offered her a permanent place. He was careful and respectful about it. She thanked him and said no.

Robin was at the ship. She climbed aboard and watched Tamaran shrink behind them, the twin suns bright, the silver-leafed branch in her pack. Tamaran was the world that made her. Earth was the world she had chosen. Those were not the same thing, and she held both of them carefully, like something real and worth keeping.
