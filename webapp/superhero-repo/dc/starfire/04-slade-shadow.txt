Of all the enemies the Teen Titans had ever faced, none was as calculated or as patient as the man they called Slade. He wore an orange-and-black mask that covered half his face, spoke in a measured voice that never raised or broke, and seemed to know more about each Titan than they knew about themselves. His plans unfolded over weeks and months. He did not fight out of rage. He fought to win, completely and permanently.

Slade had studied Starfire with particular care. He knew that her powers were tied to her emotions — that her love and joy amplified her starbolts, that her warmth made her formidable. His plan was to use those very qualities against her. He sent mechanical probes into Jump City disguised as everyday objects, observing the Titans' patterns. Then he engineered a crisis: a group of civilians trapped in a collapsing warehouse near the waterfront, a trap designed to pull the Titans apart. He separated Starfire from the others with collapsing corridors and EMP bursts that momentarily scrambled Cyborg's systems, leaving her to face one of Slade's combat robots alone.

The robot was programmed specifically to neutralize her. It was quick, armored, and equipped with light-dampening shields meant to contain her starbolts. When she fired, the energy dispersed uselessly. When she flew, it tracked her with perfect precision. She landed hard twice against the warehouse walls, and for a moment she was uncertain — which was, of course, exactly what Slade intended.

What he had not calculated was the way Starfire's heart worked under pressure. Doubt did not freeze her. It clarified things. She stopped trying to blast through the robot's shields and switched to pure physical force — catching its arm mid-swing and using its own momentum to drive it into a support pillar. Slade had studied her starbolts. He had not fully studied her Tamaranean strength. The robot crumpled. She was back with her team in four minutes.

The Titans regrouped, rescued the civilians, and followed the signal trail Cyborg had picked up during the EMP burst. They did not catch Slade that night — he was always three steps ahead of a direct chase. But Starfire brought back a fragment of the robot's processing unit, and Cyborg spent three days reverse-engineering Slade's observational software from it. They learned his methods. They learned his patterns.

Slade would return. He always did. But the Titans would be ready, and Starfire had learned something valuable: her greatest strength was not her starbolts or her flight. It was the quality that Slade had tried to exploit — her refusal to stop thinking, to stop caring, even when someone was trying very hard to make her afraid.
