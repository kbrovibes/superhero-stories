The most dazzling thing about Koriand'r was not her emerald starbolts, though they were spectacular enough to make stargazers mistake her for a meteor. It was not her flight, though she could climb to the edge of Earth's atmosphere and drift there without a suit. It was the fact that all of it — every bit of her extraordinary power — came from the same source as her extraordinary kindness. They were the same thing, wearing different shapes.

On Tamaran, all people absorbed solar energy through their skin. The Tamaranean gift was to convert that absorbed light into concentrated bursts of force — starbolts — vivid green and fierce. But Koriand'r's abilities had been transformed by the Citadel's experiments into something far beyond anything her homeworld had seen. She could absorb the light of any star, not only Tamaran's twin suns. Beneath Earth's yellow sun, she was effectively refueled constantly, her cells drinking in solar energy the way plants absorb water. It gave her strength that would stagger any opponent, and speed in the air that could take her from Jump City to New York in under a minute.

But the most remarkable aspect of her powers was the link to her emotional state. Robin had noticed it early on: in her happiest moments, her starbolts burned like small green suns. In her most determined moments, they had a focused precision that could score the same crater-sized mark on a target three times in a row. Cyborg had run tests with her agreement — measuring starbolt output against her self-reported emotional state — and the results were undeniable. Joy and love amplified her to nearly twice her baseline power.

This meant that every time a villain tried to hurt the people she cared about, they were accidentally making her stronger.

She flew with no need for wings or jets, generating what physicists in Jump City University's astrophysics department privately called "incomprehensible directed solar force." She could carry several teammates while flying at high speed without any loss of altitude. In space — her natural element, in many ways — she needed no oxygen. She could survive vacuum the way a fish can survive deep water.

What the Titans had learned to count on most, though, was her eye. Starfire never wasted a starbolt. She hit what she aimed at, and she chose what she aimed at carefully — engines rather than pilots, foundations rather than walls, weapons rather than hands. She fought with precision because she cared what happened after the fight. That was always the thing that set her apart: she thought past the conflict to the world that would exist on the other side of it. And she wanted that world to be good.
