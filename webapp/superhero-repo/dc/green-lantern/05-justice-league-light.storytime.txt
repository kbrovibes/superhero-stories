There is a moment in every large operation — when the plans are made, when the heroes are assembled, when the threat is named and the path forward is agreed — when someone asks: but how do we hold it all together?

In the Justice League, that answer often involved a very large green structure and a test pilot from Coast City.

Hal Jordan had found, in his years working alongside Batman and Superman and Wonder Woman and the others, that his role was rarely the most visible one. Superman hit things, which was impressive and useful. Wonder Woman cut through obstacles that needed cutting, which was also impressive and extremely useful. Batman planned, which nobody else in the universe did quite as well.

Hal built the scaffolding.

He was the one who, when the team needed to reach an underwater base two miles down with no submarine available, constructed a green pressure dome and walked everyone through the deep water, the walls glowing steadily against the crushing dark. He was the one who, when a battle was fought in the absolute black of a cavern deep underground with no light source for half a mile in any direction, generated a slow, steady green glow — not a weapon, not a shield, just light — filling the space so that everyone could see and no one had to guess.

The Justice League called this the Justice League Light. It was not an official designation. The Flash had coined it one evening after a mission in the caves beneath an ancient Atlantean ruin, when Hal had been quietly illuminating the space for four hours while everyone else did their work. "You're like a green sun today," the Flash had said. "Justice League Light."

Hal had shrugged. It sounded about right.

Batman worked with him the most closely, in the quiet before operations — the two of them at the planning table while the others waited, Batman laying out tactical maps and Hal translating the maps into constructs. "Here," Batman would say, pointing to a building. "We need a bridge across from this roof to this one. Can you hold it for ten minutes?" And Hal would know, from the ring's steady pulse, whether ten minutes was possible and how much willpower it would cost and what he would have left afterward for the fight itself.

These were the calculations no one saw. The mental arithmetic of a Green Lantern: how much power for the platform, how much for the shield, how much held in reserve for the thing you didn't plan for — because something always came up that you didn't plan for.

He built a series of green bridges across the cracked terrain of a volcanic island while the team moved behind him, the ground splitting and the air hot with sulfur, and he held each bridge long enough for the last person to cross before it dissolved and he built the next one. He counted seven bridges that day. He did not mention the count to anyone.

He built a pocket of compressed air inside a flooding structure once — a room-sized green bubble, holding twenty-three civilians, sealed against the water, while Superman cleared the flooding mechanism and Wonder Woman guided the people to safety one by one through a green tube Hal extended from the air pocket to the surface. He held the bubble for eleven minutes. When he released it, he sat down immediately and ate three sandwiches.

The Flash said, "I didn't know constructs tired you out."

"Everything tires you out if you do enough of it," Hal said.

"That seems inefficient."

"That's what bodies are," Hal agreed.

What mattered was that the team could count on him. Not just for one thing, not just for the specific, not just for the role that was written in the mission plan — but for whatever the moment needed. A platform. A wall. A light. A net. A hand.

He was the builder of the Justice League, the one who shaped the space they all moved through and held the structures they all needed. Not the loudest part of any operation. Not the part that made the morning news.

The part without which the morning news would have been considerably grimmer.

Hal Jordan recharged his ring one evening on the Watchtower, standing at the observation window with the whole turning planet below him — the blues and greens of it, the thin shimmer of atmosphere at the edges — and said the oath quietly, to himself, the way you say things that matter when no one is listening.

His ring blazed steady and green.

There was work to do. He was ready.
