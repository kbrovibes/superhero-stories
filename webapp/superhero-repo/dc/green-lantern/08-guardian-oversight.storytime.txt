High on a great floating platform above the city of Oa, in a chamber carved from emerald stone, sat the oldest beings in the entire universe.

They were called the Guardians.

If you were lucky enough to be allowed into their chamber, you would see a long curving row of small blue-skinned figures with bald heads and ancient eyes. They sat in carved stone seats. They wore long red robes. They did not move very much. They did not smile very often. They had been alive for billions and billions of years, and they had seen more than anyone could imagine.

It was the Guardians who had created the Green Lantern Corps, long ago. It was the Guardians who made the laws. And it was the Guardians who watched, with calm dispassionate eyes, over every single thing the Lanterns did.

Hal Jordan and the Guardians did not always get along.

You see, the Guardians believed in LOGIC. In calm. In rules carved in stone billions of years ago. They thought that feelings — anger, fear, love, hope — were dangerous things that should be kept locked away. They preferred their Lanterns quiet. Obedient. Predictable.

Hal Jordan was none of those things.

Hal was a test pilot from Earth. He believed in his GUT. He believed in helping the person right in front of him even when the math said somebody else needed help more. He had a temper. He had a sense of humor. He liked to argue.

The Guardians sighed when they heard his name. Hal sighed when he heard theirs.

But this was not a story of enemies. It was a story of two very different ideas, trying to share a galaxy.

One of their biggest arguments happened over a small green planet. A strange cosmic anomaly was about to destroy its star system. The Guardians, with their calm cool voices, ordered Hal to evacuate the people — and then to LEAVE. The planet's animals, plants, and wildlife were not "sentient enough," they said. Their loss was, statistically, insignificant.

Hal stared up at them in the great chamber. "Insignificant?" he said. He could feel his face going red. "Those are LIVES."

"You will follow your orders, Hal Jordan," the chief Guardian said calmly.

Hal did not follow his orders.

Instead, he flew to that planet himself, and with the largest construct he had ever made — a great emerald ark, big enough to hold thousands of creatures — he scooped up every living thing he could find. Every odd-feathered bird. Every six-legged fox. Every shy underwater singing creature. He flew them, ark and all, to a safe new home on a green world he had picked out himself.

The Guardians were FURIOUS when he returned. They lectured him for hours. They threatened to revoke his ring.

But they could not deny the results. The species lived. And Hal stood there, tired and dusty and still glowing, and he did not apologize.

That was Hal Jordan. Cold logic on one side, warm-blooded morality on the other.

And yet — and this is important — Hal was not stupid. He knew the Guardians were not villains. They had brought peace to thousands of worlds. They had built incredible technologies. They had stopped wars that would have destroyed entire galaxies. Their oversight kept the universe from sliding into chaos. Hal respected that. He just thought they had forgotten something important: that PEOPLE are not statistics.

The Guardians, in turn, came to respect Hal. They saw, slowly, what his fiery humanity could do. They saw that in the very worst crises, when their cold logic failed, Hal's stubborn heart could turn the tide. He was a wild card. A spark. A surprise.

As the years passed, Hal grew into a senior Lantern, and something unexpected happened. He became a BRIDGE. He could speak to the Guardians in their language of order, and he could speak to the younger Lanterns in their language of feeling. He explained each side to the other. He smoothed arguments. He kept the Corps strong.

He was the diplomat of the emotional spectrum, with one foot in each world.

Sometimes, too, Hal uncovered uncomfortable truths the Guardians had kept hidden. The dark history of the Manhunters they had once created. The existence of the other colored corps — the red of rage, the yellow of fear, the orange of greed. Each truth shook the Corps. Each truth made it stronger in the end. Hal was their conscience, the voice that said, even to beings billions of years old, "You can still do better."

The Guardians, who never apologized, did not apologize. But they listened. And slowly, slowly, they changed.

In the end, Hal Jordan and the ancient blue Guardians of the Universe came to a quiet kind of mutual respect. He would always be a rebel. They would always be a mystery. But together — somehow — they were the foundation of galactic peace.

Late one evening, as the green sun set over the emerald spires of Oa, Hal stood in the Great Chamber looking up at the long row of ancient blue figures.

He nodded once.

They nodded back.

Different as starlight and stone. Bound, forever, by the same green oath.
