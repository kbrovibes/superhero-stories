Close your eyes for just a moment. Can you picture a great, shadowy city? Tall buildings reaching up toward the clouds, gargoyles perched on every corner, a moon hiding behind silver fog. This city is called Gotham, and at night — oh, at night — something wonderful watches over it.

But to understand the guardian of Gotham, we have to go back. Back to a boy named Bruce.

Bruce Wayne grew up in the grandest house you've ever imagined. Wayne Manor sat on a hill above Gotham City, with more rooms than you could count and a butler named Alfred Pennyworth who knew exactly how young Bruce liked his breakfast. Alfred was kind and steady and wise, and he took care of Bruce with every bit of his heart.

Bruce had everything a boy could want. But one evening, his mother Martha and his father Thomas took him to see a movie downtown — a swashbuckling adventure full of heroes and swords — and afterward, they walked home through a quiet shortcut called Park Row. The night was still. The stars were out.

And then something happened. Something sad and quiet, like a door closing forever. A stranger stepped out of the shadows on that alley — a place that would later be called Crime Alley — and Bruce's parents were taken away from him. Just like that. The pearls from his mother's necklace scattered across the cobblestones with a sound like tiny bells. Bruce stood alone in the dark.

He never, ever forgot that night.

Alfred wrapped young Bruce in his arms and brought him home, and he stayed beside him through all the long, empty days that followed. But something had changed inside Bruce. A quiet fire had been lit. He made himself a promise — a solemn, iron promise — that he would do whatever it took to make sure no child in Gotham would ever feel that frightened and alone again.

So Bruce got to work. Can you picture it? Day after day, year after year, Bruce studied. He read every book in the library at Wayne Manor. He learned chemistry and detective work and languages from countries all around the world. He trained his body until he could run across rooftops and leap between buildings without a stumble. He traveled to the far corners of the Earth to learn from the best teachers he could find.

And when he came home — when he finally came home to Alfred and to Gotham — he needed a symbol. Something that would tell the criminals of Gotham to be afraid. Something that told the good people of Gotham: you are not alone.

One evening, a bat swooped through the broken window of the manor and fluttered around the room. Most people would have shooed it away. Bruce smiled.

Bats, he thought, are misunderstood. They fly in the dark, when no one else will. They find their way without eyes, using only patience and cleverness. They're not scary at all — they're wonderful. And if something wonderful can be misunderstood, maybe it is exactly the right symbol for him.

That night, Batman was born. WHOOSH — a shadow across rooftops. CLICK — a golden utility belt snapped around his waist. The Batmobile growled to life in the cavern beneath Wayne Manor. Alfred stood at the top of the hidden staircase, holding a cup of tea, watching his boy become something extraordinary.

By day, Bruce Wayne signs checks that build hospitals and schools and parks for the people of Gotham. By night, Batman climbs the tallest towers and keeps watch over every crooked alley and every quiet street.

And every night, a great beam of light cuts through the Gotham fog — the Bat-Signal, reaching all the way up to the clouds. It is Gotham's way of saying: we need you. And it is Batman's way of saying: I'm here.

No superpowers. No magic. Just one boy who made a promise, and kept it.

Can you picture that? A dark cape spreading like wings against the silver moon, and a city that knows — no matter how scary the night — it is never alone.
