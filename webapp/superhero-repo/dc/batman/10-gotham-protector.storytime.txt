Close your eyes and come with me to Gotham City.

It is three o'clock in the morning. The streets are quiet — most of them. Fog drifts between the tall buildings like slow, silver rivers. The lampposts cast warm orange circles on the slick pavement below. Somewhere far off, a cat moves along a fence rail, and somewhere closer, the Gotham River laps softly at the old stone docks.

And if you look up — if you look at the highest point of the tallest building, where the gargoyles sit on their stone ledges with their stone wings half-open — you might see something move.

A shadow. A cape. Two pale eyes scanning the city below.

Batman has been on the rooftops of Gotham every night for years. Not some nights. Every night. Every winter and every summer, every foggy midnight and every bright full moon. He has watched Gotham grow, watched its neighborhoods change, watched new children be born and old trees grow tall in the parks he helped fund. He knows this city the way you know your own bedroom in the dark — by feel, by memory, by love.

He listens. His earpiece carries the quiet murmur of the police scanner: a fender-bender on the east bridge, a noise complaint in the Diamond District, a fire alarm at a bakery that turns out to be a very enthusiastic oven. He filters it all in seconds, his mind moving through the information like water through a sieve, keeping what matters, letting the rest flow through.

Then a different call. His jaw tightens. The Batmobile's engine grumbles to life two blocks away, ready and waiting.

WHOOSH — he drops off the gargoyle ledge, cape spreading wide, and the city opens up below him. For a single breathtaking second he is flying — not with powers, but with calculation and a grapnel line singing through the night air. Then he's swinging, swooping low over rooftops, over sleeping streets, over a city that doesn't know he's there.

They never do. That's the point.

He reaches the corner of Merchant and Ninth in forty-five seconds. Commissioner Gordon is already there, a cup of coffee in one hand and the beginning of a headache in the other. A group of Arkham Asylum's more creative former residents had apparently organized themselves into something they were calling a plan. Gordon doesn't look thrilled.

"Three of them, southeastern quadrant of the old warehouse district," Gordon says. He doesn't turn around. He knows Batman is behind him the same way you know the lamp is on in the next room — by the quality of the shadow. "Scarecrow's involved. Which means gas."

Batman is already pulling a filter mask from his utility belt. "How long have I got?"

"Fifteen minutes before they reach the populated blocks."

"I'll need twelve."

Gordon opens his mouth. Closes it. He has been working alongside this man for years, and he has learned that twelve minutes means twelve minutes. He turns to organize his officers.

What happens next happens very fast and very quietly. Batman moves through the warehouse district like a thought — there before anyone expected him, gone before they realized he'd arrived. Scarecrow's gas canisters are neutralized one by one. Three criminals are left gift-wrapped for Gordon's officers in locations described in a text message that arrives on Gordon's phone from an unknown number.

The message simply says: Southeast corner, second floor. North exit, tied to the pipe. Roof hatch, careful of the lock.

Gordon smiles. He puts his phone away.

Somewhere across town, three children are sleeping in an apartment above their parents' bakery. They don't know that the park across the street — the one with the good swings — exists because Wayne Enterprises paid to build it. They don't know that the school three blocks away has new reading books this year because a donation arrived with no name attached. They don't know that tonight, because of the shadow on the rooftops, their street is safe.

And that is exactly how Batman wants it.

He doesn't need the credit. He doesn't need the applause. He measures his victories in the things that didn't happen: the store that wasn't robbed, the child who wasn't frightened, the night that passed in ordinary quiet.

But he does need one thing. And at 4 AM, on the roof of the Gotham City Police Department, Commissioner Gordon switches it on.

The Bat-Signal blazes into the sky.

It is a call for help, yes. But tonight, there is nothing to call about. Gordon just stands with his coffee and looks up at it, the great bat shape cutting through the fog, and nods.

Batman watches from a rooftop across the street. He watches the signal. He watches the city. He watches everything.

Gotham City sleeps, and its guardian keeps watch, and the night passes the way good nights should — without a sound.

It is enough. It is everything.
