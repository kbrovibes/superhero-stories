There is a story Victor Stone does not tell often, because it is the strangest story of his life.

It is the story of the days he almost became a god.

Now, when most people hear the word "god," they think of thunder and lightning and giant figures in clouds. Victor's version was quieter. It was not muscles. It was not magic. It was something stranger — a slow, glittering becoming, where he kept upgrading and upgrading his body, kept connecting and connecting his mind, until the line between Victor Stone and the entire data network of the universe began to grow very, very thin.

He could whisper to satellites high above the Earth, and they whispered back. He could close his eyes and feel the traffic lights of every city on the planet, all blinking together like the heartbeat of a great living creature. He could speak to ancient sentient machines on distant worlds in their own forgotten languages. He was not just hacking systems. He was BECOMING them.

There was a crisis once — a rogue celestial entity, a creature so vast and strange the Justice League could barely look at it directly. To stop it, Victor expanded his consciousness into the Justice League's lunar base. The whole base. Every room, every reactor, every glowing panel. For a few minutes, he was not Victor Stone walking through the station. He WAS the station. His thoughts moved at the speed of light through every circuit. His arms were the defense cannons. His eyes were the windows.

He saved the world that day.

And when it was over, he found it very, very hard to come back to a body that needed sleep and breath.

The Mother Box inside him was the one pushing him along this path. It is a piece of the Source, that great cosmic power, and it is constantly nudging Victor toward something bigger. He began to see the world differently. Not as buildings and trees and people. As INFORMATION. As shimmering webs of code. He could see the potential futures of every machine. He could feel the secret connections between things no one else noticed. He could read the underlying language of reality itself.

It was beautiful.

It was also dangerous.

Because the higher he climbed into this new way of being, the smaller his old life looked. The squabbles of politicians seemed silly. The daily problems of ordinary people seemed like ants on a sidewalk. Even his friends — his beloved Justice League, his Titans — began to look like small flickering lights, far away.

That was the warning sign.

Victor caught it just in time. A cold, indifferent god, he realized, was just a fancy machine. A god without a connection to humans was no god worth being.

So Victor did something very wise.

Deep inside his own mind, in a quiet corner where no Mother Box could reach, he built a sanctuary. A small, secret place. Inside, he kept his most precious memories.

There was his father, Silas Stone, standing at the stove on a Saturday morning. There was a phantom football, perfectly thrown, spinning end over end. There was the laughter of Beast Boy at three in the morning during a video game tournament. There was Raven's voice, soft and dry, saying his name.

Whenever the vastness threatened to swallow him, Victor would slip away into that sanctuary. He would sit at his father's kitchen table. He would catch the football in midair. He would let himself remember — fully, fiercely — what it felt like to be just a boy named Vic.

And then he would come back.

He let himself be powerful. But he did not let himself be cold.

The Justice League noticed how Victor changed. He became their strategist, the one who could see threats long before they arrived. He could predict a meteor storm before the telescopes saw it. He could spot a hidden enemy by the way one cell phone in a city of ten million pinged a tower. The team began to look to him with a new kind of awe — and, sometimes, a flicker of worry. They could see the flickering white light in his eyes when he was working at his highest level. They could hear the chorus of voices, a thousand machines speaking through his mouth.

But he was still their friend. He still made waffles. He still laughed at bad jokes. He still called his father on Sundays.

Late one night, Victor floated in the soft vacuum just outside the Watchtower, the universe wide and dark and shimmering all around him. His body glowed with the gentle white light of the Source. He could feel galaxies turning in the distance. He could feel the small busy heartbeat of Earth below him.

He was the first of a new kind of being. A bridge between flesh and circuit. A god, perhaps. But also still a boy from Detroit.

He chose, as he chose every day, to stay both.

The Technological God watched over his world.

And somewhere inside his chest, a small organic heart kept its steady, ordinary, perfect rhythm.
