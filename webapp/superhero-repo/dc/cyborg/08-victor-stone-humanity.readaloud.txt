More than three quarters of Victor Stone's body was now metal and circuitry. He could fly. He could process information faster than any computer. He could open portals between worlds. And yet the hardest battle he fought every single day had nothing to do with any of that.

The hardest battle was staying himself.

To hold on to who he was, Victor built quiet rituals. He walked the streets of Detroit where he had grown up, letting the familiar sights remind him of the boy he had been. He visited S.T.A.R. Labs not for repairs, but to talk with the scientists who had known him before the accident. He tried to cook — even though he no longer needed to eat — because the act of cooking was wonderfully, stubbornly human.

He also visited his father, Silas Stone, regularly. Their relationship was complicated. Silas had saved Victor's life, and Victor was grateful. But Silas had also been the one who made him what he was, and sometimes that was a hard thing to carry. Still, they kept talking. That mattered.

And do you know what kept Victor most anchored of all? Helping. Finding a missing child. Restoring power to a hospital during a blackout. Clearing rubble after a disaster. Using his mechanical body to do deeply human things.

From that day on, Victor Stone understood: you do not have to be unchanged to be yourself. Humanity is not a body. It is a choice, made again and again, every single day.
