There is a heartbeat inside Victor Stone that is not entirely his own.

If you listened very carefully — not with your ears, but with something deeper, some sense that only works in the presence of truly extraordinary things — you might hear it: a warm, low pulse, like a song hummed under the breath, repeating in a language older than any human tongue.

That is the Mother Box.

It came from a world called New Genesis — a world so different from Earth that no human words really fit it. A world of golden light and impossible architecture, where technology and life had merged so completely that machines thought and felt and sang. The Mother Box was created by a scientist named Himon, using something called Element X, and it was not merely a computer. It was alive. Not alive the way a dog is alive, or a tree, or a person — but alive in a subtler, stranger way, like how a song is alive as long as someone is still listening.

When the accident at S.T.A.R. Labs scattered shards of the Mother Box across Victor's broken body, the Box did something remarkable: it chose him. It didn't just merge with his cybernetics the way a battery connects to a circuit. It threaded itself through his nervous system, learned the rhythm of his thoughts, and settled into the space between his heartbeats like it had been waiting for exactly this person for a very long time.

Victor spent months learning what it could do. Not what it could do in theory — what it could do with him specifically, working together as a team.

The first time he opened a Boom Tube, he was standing in S.T.A.R. Labs' loading bay, surrounded by his father's team of scientists who all stepped back at once when the air in front of Victor tore open.

That is the only way to describe it. The air tore, pulled apart like fabric, and behind it was a corridor of amber and orange light that stretched somewhere else entirely — somewhere impossible, somewhere that smelled of static and starlight. Victor felt the Mother Box singing in his chest. He reached into the light, pulled at it, shaped the destination with his mind, and stepped through.

He came out in S.T.A.R. Labs' roof garden, four floors up. The Boom Tube sealed shut behind him with a sound like a perfectly placed drumbeat. DOOM.

He did it again. This time to the other side of Detroit. Then to a location fifteen miles away. Each time the Mother Box guided him — not by giving him directions, but by sharing a feeling, the way you feel when you're going in the right direction even if you can't see the destination yet.

The Sonic Cannon was different. That one he built himself, or at least shaped himself — the cybernetic chassis was his father's design, but the tuning was Victor's, calibrated over a hundred test sessions in an empty warehouse on the edge of the city. He could set it to a frequency that scattered robot parts without touching organic tissue. He could set it to a frequency that disrupted electronic locks. He could set it, at maximum power, to something that hit like a freight train.

He almost never used maximum power.

"You could do so much more with it," Gizmo had once told him, during a conversation they had that Victor had not technically agreed to having, through the bars of a containment cell.

"I know," Victor had said. "That's exactly the point."

Power, the Mother Box seemed to murmur. Not a word — a feeling. Like the warmth from a fireplace, suggesting that too much fire burns down the house.

Together, Victor and the Mother Box were something the universe had not quite seen before — a bridge between the human and the alien, between the digital and the living, between the technology of Earth and the ancient intelligence of New Genesis. When Victor interfaced with alien machines, they recognized the Mother Box's signal and opened to him like a door recognizing a familiar key.

When he was tired — not his body, which barely got tired anymore, but his mind, his human self, the part that still missed football games and autumn in Detroit — the Mother Box was there. That quiet hum in his chest. That reminder that he was not alone, that something remarkable had chosen to share his life.

He carried the Mother Box's pulse the way other people carried memories of kind words or good meals — as a steady, small warmth at the center of everything.

He was Cyborg. Half human, half machine, fully himself. And in his chest, an ancient alien intelligence kept a gentle rhythm, like a second heart that had always known he was worth saving.
