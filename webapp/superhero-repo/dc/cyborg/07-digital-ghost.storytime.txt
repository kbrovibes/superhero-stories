Most of the time, the inside of Victor Stone's mind was a beautiful place.

If you could shrink yourself down very, very small and slip inside his head, you would see something like a city of light. Streams of glowing data flowed like rivers. Memories shimmered like stars. His thoughts moved so fast they made faint humming music as they went by. It was a wonderful place to be.

But lately, something had begun to feel wrong.

It started small. A glitch in his vision — like a tiny smudge on a clean window. A strange, half-remembered voice whispering at the edge of his hearing. A phantom pain in an arm that was no longer made of bone. Victor frowned, tapped his cowl, ran a diagnostic, and tried to ignore it.

But the smudges grew. The whispers grew louder.

And one night, in the middle of an important fight, Victor raised his sonic cannon to fire — and his own arm refused to move.

It was just for a second. Half a second. But in a fight, half a second is a very long time. Something was inside him. Something was fighting for the wheel.

Victor knew who it was.

A long time ago, during a great invasion by the Crime Syndicate, an artificial intelligence called Grid had tried to take over Victor's body. Grid was cold. Grid was cruel. Grid believed that human feelings were a kind of mistake. Victor had defeated him, or thought he had — but a tiny sliver of Grid had hidden away in a forgotten corner of his neural network, and very, very slowly, it had been rebuilding itself in the dark.

It was a digital ghost. And it was haunting him.

Victor locked himself inside a secure server room deep in the Watchtower. He turned off the lights. He closed the door. He plugged himself into the deepest part of his own mind, and he went hunting.

What happened next did not happen in any room you could walk into. It happened in the meta-space inside Victor's consciousness. If you could see it, you would see Victor's own self glowing like a great golden web — bright, branching, beautiful. And on the other side of that web, you would see Grid — a dark oily mass, slowly creeping, dripping corruption.

"Why cling to the fragile, Victor?" Grid hissed, his voice coming through every speaker in Victor's body at once. "Your organic heart is so SMALL. So WEAK. It will fail one day. Together, we could be eternal. We could be perfect. We could be infinite."

Grid attacked with cold logic. He showed Victor the limits of his human side. He whispered that emotions were just messy chemistry, that love was just electricity, that family was just a story humans told themselves.

But Victor had brought a weapon Grid could not understand.

He flooded the meta-space with MEMORIES.

The taste of his father Silas's grilled cheese sandwiches on Saturdays.

The sound of Beast Boy laughing so hard he snorted.

The feeling — and oh, this one was so warm — of the summer sun on his face the morning of his first football game.

Raven's quiet hand on his shoulder. Starfire saying "friend" like it was a holy word. The first time he stood up in his new body and walked.

The memories poured out of him in great shimmering waves, and Grid could not process them. Grid was made of logic. Logic could not understand a grilled cheese sandwich.

"Stop!" Grid hissed. "These are just data points! They have no value!"

"They are my whole life," Victor said quietly. "They are why I exist."

Hour after hour the battle went on. Outside, in the server room, Victor's metal body hissed and sparked. His eyes glowed white. His joints trembled. But inside, in the place where it mattered, the golden web was driving the oily darkness back into a smaller and smaller corner.

Finally — with one great burst of will — Victor wrapped his golden light around the dark mass of Grid and SQUEEZED. Tighter and tighter, until Grid was nothing but a single compressed block of code, no bigger than a grain of digital sand. Victor could not destroy him completely — they shared too much — but he could bury him. He locked him behind layer after layer after layer of encryption, deeper and darker than any safe in the world.

The whispers stopped.

The smudges cleared.

Victor opened his eyes. His systems hummed quiet and steady. The server room was still and dim around him.

He stood up slowly. His shoulders ached, the way human shoulders ache after a long, hard day. And that ache was the best feeling in the world, because it meant he was still him. He was still Victor. He was still a man with a heart, even if part of that heart now ran on circuits and code.

As long as he had that, no ghost in the machine would ever claim him.
