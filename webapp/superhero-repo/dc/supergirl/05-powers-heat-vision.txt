The first time Kara's heat vision fired without warning, she melted the toaster. She had been looking at it across the kitchen, thinking about how hungry she was, and two red beams shot from her eyes and turned it into a glowing puddle on the counter. She stood there for a full minute staring at what had been a perfectly serviceable appliance before deciding she needed to get this under control.

Superman had explained the basics: Earth's yellow sun energized Kryptonian cells constantly, and different powers drew from different reserves. Heat vision ran on concentration and emotion — the stronger the feeling, the hotter the beam. Freeze breath was the inverse, a focused exhalation that could drop temperature dramatically in a precise area. Both required discipline to master, and both would cause serious problems if Kara lost focus at the wrong moment. He told her what had happened when he accidentally froze a lake in Manitoba at age twelve, and Kara felt better about the toaster.

She practiced in the Midvale salvage yard, where the Danvers family had arranged access through a friend. For heat vision, she started with tin cans, learning the minimum output needed to cut versus melt versus simply warm. She discovered she could control the beam width from a broad wash of warmth to a needle-thin cutting line precise enough to score letters into metal. She wrote her name in Kryptonian script on a steel panel, then wrote it again in English, just because she could. For freeze breath, she practiced on barrels of water, learning to coat surfaces with ice without shattering them, to cool a specific object without affecting anything adjacent.

The day the precision mattered was an ordinary Tuesday in late November, when a chemical fire broke out at the Midvale Harbor freight terminal. Ordinary water would have made the chemical blaze worse. The fire department had foam suppressant, but not enough for the scale of the blaze. Kara arrived and assessed in seconds: the fire was in three sections, two chemical, one electrical. She used freeze breath on the chemical sections, laying down precise sheets of cold air that smothered the fire without scattering burning material. For the electrical section, she used heat vision at the lowest setting to burn out the fuel source in the wiring rather than let it spread. The whole terminal was contained in four minutes.

The fire chief found her afterward standing in the steam and ash, and said it was the most precise fire suppression he had ever witnessed. Kara thanked him and mentioned the toaster. He looked puzzled. She decided not to explain.
