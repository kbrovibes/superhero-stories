The first thing Kara noticed was the light — a sickly green glow coming from a lead-lined case that a man named Marcus Drayne had opened inside Midvale's old textile warehouse. Drayne was a collector who had paid an exorbitant amount to acquire what he called a "Kryptonian meteorite fragment" and intended to use it as leverage in a hostage situation involving three employees he had trapped inside. He had heard that Kryptonians could be weakened by their own planet's irradiated remnants, and he had gambled that Supergirl would come when she heard the emergency signal.

She came. And the moment she crossed the threshold of the warehouse, her legs went wrong.

Kryptonite — genuine green kryptonite, a fragment of Krypton's core rendered radioactive by the planet's explosion — affected her in a way that was difficult to describe to anyone who had never felt it. It was not simply pain, though it was that too. It was a hollowing out, a removal of the thing inside her cells that Earth's sun put there. She felt small, and slow, and entirely breakable in a way she had forgotten was possible. She went down on one knee in the warehouse doorway.

Drayne was not a fighter. He was a frightened man who had put himself in a situation he no longer fully controlled, and when Kara did not immediately collapse to the floor the way he had expected, he looked uncertain. Kara, on one knee and unable to fly, cataloged her options. The lead-lined case was on a table twelve feet away. Lead blocked kryptonite radiation completely — she knew that from Superman's briefings. The three employees were against the far wall, unharmed.

She did not try to stand. She crawled.

Not heroically. Not easily. She crossed the warehouse floor on hands and knees, fighting the radiation every inch, and when she reached the table, she flipped the lid of the lead case shut with one shaking hand. The green glow vanished. She felt the sun's energy begin to return to her cells within seconds, a warm tide coming back in.

She was standing by the time the DEO arrived. Drayne had already sat down on the floor and was waiting to be collected — his plan had not included the possibility that she would simply keep moving. The employees were uninjured. The kryptonite was secured by the Department of Extranormal Operations and cataloged as case evidence.

Kara sat on the warehouse steps afterward and thought about the crawling, which she was not going to romanticize. She had been on the floor of a warehouse and she had done what needed doing. Being vulnerable was not the worst thing. Stopping because you were vulnerable — that was the only version that actually defeated you.
