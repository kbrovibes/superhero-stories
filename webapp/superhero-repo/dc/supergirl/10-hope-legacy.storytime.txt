Once upon a time, a hero received a note in her locker.

It was tucked between a history textbook and a library book about Earth geography that Kara kept forgetting to return, and it was written in careful block letters on a single sheet of notebook paper, folded three times to make a small precise rectangle.

She unfolded it.

"Can you teach me to be brave? My name is Maya. I'm in seventh grade."

No last name. No classroom number. Just that.

Kara stood at her locker for a long moment, holding the paper, feeling something complicated and warm settling in her chest.

She asked around carefully over the next two days, and she found Maya Vasquez on a Tuesday afternoon in the school library.

Maya was eleven years old and small for her age, with dark braided hair and very attentive eyes. She was sitting alone at a table near the back of the library, reading a book about astronomy, her finger tracing the diagrams of stellar nebulae with a focused attention that reminded Kara of her father doing the same thing at his desk in Argo City.

Kara sat down across from her without asking.

Maya looked up. She did not look surprised. She had been expecting this.

She had, Kara discovered, figured out that Kara and Supergirl were the same person approximately two months ago. The evidence she cited was methodical: the similar face structure, the same height, the fact that Supergirl had once landed in the school parking lot on a day when the sixth-floor windows were being cleaned, visible to anyone who looked, and the fact that Kara had been absent from class that same afternoon under the explanation of a dental appointment, which Maya found improbable.

She had told no one.

"Why not?" Kara asked.

Maya considered this seriously. "Because it wasn't mine to tell," she said finally.

Kara looked at her for a moment. Then she decided that Maya Vasquez was one of the finest people she had met since arriving on Earth.

They sat in the library and talked for two hours.

About fear — what it felt like, where it lived in your body, the specific cold sensation of walking into a room where you did not know anyone. About being new somewhere and having to learn the rules of a place that everyone else already knew without thinking. About the difference between being brave and being unafraid, which Kara said were not the same thing at all, not even close.

She told Maya about her first month in Midvale.

She told her about the homesickness — the particular kind that has no remedy because the place you are homesick for no longer exists. She told her about the morning she sat on the roof of the Danvers house before school and cried for a while, quietly, because the sunrise was a different color than Krypton's and that particular difference was too much that morning.

Maya listened without saying anything.

Then Kara told her about the lunch tray.

It had been her first week at Midvale High, and the school lunch trays had a spring-loaded lid system that everyone else operated without thinking, and Kara — who could stop a speeding train with her bare hands — had stood at the lunch line for four minutes unable to figure out the latch and too embarrassed to ask anyone for help.

Maya burst out laughing. A real laugh, surprised out of her, the kind that comes before you have time to decide whether to let it out.

It was, Kara realized, the first real laugh she had heard from Maya since finding her.

"Being brave," Kara said, when Maya's laughter had settled, "isn't about having no fear. It is about deciding that the thing you are afraid of is worth moving toward anyway."

Maya repeated this quietly to herself, as if filing it.

They started meeting at the school track on Saturday mornings.

Not as Supergirl and a student. As two people who ran in the same direction. Kara ran alongside Maya — no flying, no super-speed, just her feet on the track and her breath in the cold morning air — and they talked about whatever had been hard that week.

The girl in Maya's class who had a talent for making dismissive comments in exactly the right tone to be plausibly accidental. The science teacher, Mr. Okafor, who had pulled Maya aside after class and said her work was the most original thinking he had seen in the district science fair in years, and that he wanted her to enter.

That one had stopped Maya cold.

"What if I enter and I'm not good enough?" she said.

Kara thought about the resonance device above Tokyo. About the dam at Harker's Mill. About the warehouse floor.

"You will find out," she said. "That is the only way to find out. And finding out is always better than not knowing."

Maya entered the district science fair with a project on stellar formation — the process by which clouds of gas and dust collapse under gravity to form stars. She had chosen the topic, she told Kara, because she had always thought stars were the most persistent thing in the universe. They spent millions of years forming. They burned for billions of years after. They did not quit.

The project came in second place.

Maya was quiet in the moments immediately after the announcement, and Kara watched from the bleachers in plain clothes — jeans, a green jacket, her hair down, no cape, no S-shield — as Maya stood in the science fair hall holding her second-place ribbon and thinking.

Twelve minutes later, Mr. Okafor brought someone over to introduce.

Her name was Dr. Priya Mehta, and she was a researcher from S.T.A.R. Labs who had served as one of the fair's judges. She had flagged Maya's project specifically. They talked for forty minutes. Kara watched from the bleachers without being seen.

By the end, Maya had been offered a summer volunteer position in S.T.A.R. Labs' astrophysics division.

She found Kara afterward, in the corridor outside the main hall, holding the second-place ribbon in one hand and a S.T.A.R. Labs information packet in the other. Her eyes were bright.

"I wasn't afraid," she said.

She said it the way you say something you did not entirely expect to be able to say.

Kara smiled.

"I know," she said.

She thought about Zor-El. She thought about the glyph on her chest, the ancient symbol of the House of El, and what her father had said it meant.

"Bravery compounds," she said to Maya. "Each time you do it, the next time comes a little easier. And then you start doing it in front of other people. And they see how it looks. And then something grows that is larger than just you."

Maya looked at the ribbon in her hand. At the packet. At the window and the afternoon sky beyond it.

"Like stars?" she said.

Kara thought about clouds of gas and dust, patient and slow, collapsing over millions of years toward the tremendous light of a new sun.

"Exactly like stars," she said.

She walked home through the afternoon in plain clothes, without flying, because sometimes walking was the right speed. Above her, the S-shield was invisible under her jacket, but she knew it was there.

The work of the House of El.

It had begun on Krypton, in the silver towers of Argo City.

It was here now, in Midvale, in a seventh-grader with a second-place ribbon and a summer at S.T.A.R. Labs waiting for her.

It would go on.

That was what hope meant.

That was always what it had meant.
