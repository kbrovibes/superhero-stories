Okay. This is the last story in Kara's adventures, and it might be the most important one yet. It does not start with a villain. It starts with a note.

A NOTE! In Kara's school locker!

In careful block letters on notebook paper: "Can you teach me to be brave? My name is Maya. I'm in seventh grade."

Kara asked around and found Maya Vasquez in the school library — eleven years old, very small, very quiet, sitting alone with a book about astronomy. Maya had moved to Midvale four months ago and was having a hard time finding her place. She had also, through very careful observation AND a poorly-timed Supergirl landing in the school parking lot, figured out that Kara and Supergirl were the same person.

She had not told anyone.

Kara sat down across from her. And they talked for TWO HOURS.

About fear. About being new somewhere. About the difference between being brave and being unafraid. Kara told Maya that she had cried twice in her first month in Midvale. Once from homesickness. And once — are you ready for this? — because she could NOT get the lunch tray to open and was too embarrassed to ask for help.

Maya burst out laughing. A real, surprised laugh.

And Kara said: "Being brave isn't about having no fear. It's about deciding that the thing you're afraid of is worth moving toward anyway."

They started meeting at the school track on Saturday mornings. Kara ran alongside Maya — NOT flying, NOT super-speed, just running — and they talked about whatever was hard that week.

The girl in Maya's class who said dismissive things. The science teacher who noticed Maya's work and wanted her in the district science fair. The terrifying idea of doing something in public when you weren't sure if you were good enough yet.

Maya entered the science fair with a project about stellar formation. She won SECOND PLACE. She was disappointed for about twelve minutes. Then a S.T.A.R. Labs researcher who had judged the fair came over to introduce herself. Then they had a long conversation. Then Maya was offered a summer volunteer position.

Kara watched from the bleachers in regular street clothes. No cape. No S-shield.

Nobody needed to know Supergirl was there.

Maya found her afterward, holding her ribbon, and said: "I wasn't afraid."

And Kara told her the truth. "Bravery compounds," she said. "Every time you do it, the next time gets a little easier. And then you start showing other people how it looks. And then you've built something that lasts."

The S-shield means hope. Zor-El taught that to Kara. Kara taught it to Maya.

The work of the House of El goes on.

And that is how a hero's legacy really grows.
