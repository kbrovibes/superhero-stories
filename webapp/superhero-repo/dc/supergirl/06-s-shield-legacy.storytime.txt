Once upon a time, there was a symbol older than any hero.

It looked a little like the letter S, but it was not. On the planet Krypton, it was an ancient glyph — a carved shape belonging to the House of El — and it meant one word.

Hope.

Kara had grown up with it. Her father Zor-El wore it on the front of his coat, just above his heart. His father had worn it. His father's father had worn it, going back farther than any of them could trace. When Kara was very small she had asked her father what the symbol meant, and he had crouched down to her level and said it carefully, as if each word mattered: "It means we keep trying. Even when it is hard. Especially then."

Kara had carried that with her in the rocket, tucked inside her chest alongside her mother's voice.

When she arrived on Earth, her cousin Kal-El — Superman — was already wearing the symbol on his cape. He had made it his own, and the people of Earth had come to recognize it. When they saw that shape in the sky, they knew: something good is coming. Something brave and strong, coming toward you as fast as it can.

After a few months in Midvale, Kara decided it was time to make her own version.

She sat at the kitchen table of the Danvers house on a quiet Saturday afternoon with a needle and thread and a length of deep blue fabric. She had the pattern in her memory exactly — she had been looking at it her whole life. She cut and sewed with careful, patient hands.

She pricked her finger on the needle twice.

The first time, she looked at her fingertip with genuine surprise. She was a Kryptonian. A speeding car could not scratch her. A steel beam could not leave a mark. But a sewing needle, apparently, could find the one small spot where she was not paying attention.

The second time it happened, she laughed.

The finished suit was everything she had imagined: deep blue, with a flowing red cape, and the S-shield — clean and strong — centered on the chest.

She wore it publicly for the first time on an ordinary afternoon outside a grocery store. She was helping carry someone's bags to their car when a small girl in a yellow raincoat looked up at her, eyes wide, and pointed at the symbol.

"That's the Superman lady," the girl said with great confidence.

Kara smiled. She was not offended. She was, after all, still figuring out exactly what her own name meant. She was the Superman lady's cousin. She was Kara Zor-El from Argo City. She was a student at Midvale High. She was Supergirl.

She was all of those things, and the symbol on her chest held all of them.

The night the symbol really spoke to her was a cold night in winter, a few weeks before the year ended.

A call came over the emergency frequency: the Harker's Mill dam was failing. Harker's Mill was a town of four thousand people, fourteen miles from Midvale, sitting in a narrow valley. The dam was old — built sixty years ago — and cold rain and early snowmelt had pushed the reservoir far beyond what it was built to hold. The first crack had appeared in the early evening. By the time Kara arrived, the crack was three feet wide and water was pouring through in a white roar.

She worked for one hour and twenty-three minutes.

She hauled boulders from the valley walls with her bare hands, carrying loads that would have taken a construction crane, dropping them precisely into the breach. She welded them in place with heat vision — ZZZT ZZZT ZZZT — careful, precise passes that sealed the gaps between the stones. When the water flow shifted and threatened to go around her barrier, she used a long, sustained breath to redirect the current, holding it aside while she placed the next layer of stone.

The rain kept falling. The temperature dropped. The floodwater rose to her waist, and then higher, cold enough to make most people's hands stop working. Her hands kept working.

The dam held.

She heard the moment it held — heard the terrible roar of escaping water drop to a rush, then to a trickle, then to silence. She stood in the dark flood water up to her chest, breathing hard, arms aching in a way Kryptonian arms were not usually supposed to ache.

The town of Harker's Mill was completely silent. Four thousand people, asleep in their beds, with no idea how close the water had come.

Kara looked down at herself. She was covered in mud and silt from crown to boot — her suit, her cape, her hair, all of it the color of river clay.

Except the S-shield on her chest. Somehow, impossibly, it had stayed clean. The white and gold symbol of the House of El glowed in the dark, bright and clear, as if the night itself wanted to keep it visible.

She looked at it for a long time.

"It means we keep trying," she said quietly to no one at all. "Even when it is hard. Especially then."

Her father's voice. Her family's crest. A promise older than this planet.

She flew back to Midvale through the rain, trailing silver drops.

The House of El had built the meaning of that symbol over generations, on a world that no longer existed. On Krypton, in the silver towers of Argo City, in the notebooks of Zor-El.

Kara Zor-El was going to carry it forward.

One dam at a time.
