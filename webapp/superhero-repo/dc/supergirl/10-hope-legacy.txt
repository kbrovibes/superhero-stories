The note was tucked into Kara's locker between her history textbook and a library book about Earth geography she kept forgetting to return. It was handwritten in careful block letters on notebook paper: "Can you teach me to be brave? My name is Maya. I'm in seventh grade." There was no last name and no explanation beyond that.

Kara asked around carefully and found Maya Vasquez in the school library — eleven years old, small for her age, sitting alone and reading a book about astronomy with the focused expression of someone who had learned to be very quiet. Maya had moved to Midvale four months ago from another city and was struggling to find her footing. She had figured out that Kara and Supergirl were the same person through a combination of attentiveness and the fact that Kara had once landed in the school parking lot visible to the sixth floor windows. She had not told anyone.

Kara sat down across from her and they talked for two hours — about fear, about being new somewhere, about the difference between being brave and being unafraid. Kara told her that she had cried twice in her first month in Midvale, once from homesickness and once because she could not get the lunch tray to open and was too embarrassed to ask for help. Maya had laughed at that, a real laugh, surprised out of her. Kara said that being brave wasn't about having no fear. It was about deciding that the thing you were afraid of was worth moving toward anyway.

They met at the school track on Saturday mornings for a month. Kara ran alongside Maya — not flying, not at super-speed, just running — and they talked about whatever was hard that week. About the girl in Maya's class who made dismissive comments. About the science teacher who had noticed Maya's work and wanted her to enter the district science fair. About how it felt to do something in public when you weren't sure if you were good enough yet.

On the day of the science fair, Maya's project on stellar formation came in second place. She was disappointed for approximately twelve minutes, and then she was not. The science teacher introduced her to a researcher from S.T.A.R. Labs who had served as a judge, and that conversation led to a summer volunteer position.

Kara was in the bleachers in street clothes, not as Supergirl. Nobody needed to know she was there. Maya found her afterward, holding her second-place ribbon, and said: "I wasn't afraid." Kara said she knew. She said the thing about bravery was that it compounded — each time you did it, the next time came a little easier, and then you started showing other people how it looked, and then you had built something that outlasted you. The S-shield meant hope. Kara had been taught that by Zor-El, and she was teaching it now. The work of the House of El continued, on a planet that had become home.
