Before Kara Zor-El ever set foot on Earth, she had already lost everything. She grew up in Argo City, a shining place of silver towers and wide blue skies on the planet Krypton. Her father, Zor-El, was a scientist, and her mother, Alura, filled their home with laughter and music. Kara was thirteen years old, clever and quick, with a gift for languages and a habit of staying up late to watch the twin moons rise over the rooftops. Krypton was beautiful. It was also dying.

When the planet's core began to fail, Zor-El made a desperate choice. He built a small rocket and placed Kara inside it, programming the navigation for a blue planet called Earth — the same world where her cousin Kal-El had already landed. The last thing Kara heard before the hatch sealed shut was her mother's voice saying, "Be brave. Be kind. Be who you are." Then the rocket fired, Krypton shattered behind her, and Kara was alone in the dark between stars.

The journey took longer than expected. Kara arrived on Earth years after Kal-El, who had grown up to become Superman, the greatest hero the planet had ever known. He met her ship when it landed in a field outside Midvale and looked at her with eyes she recognized immediately — they were her cousin's eyes, the same shade of blue as the Kryptonian sky. He handed her a warm coat and said, "Welcome. You're safe now." It was, after all, not so different from what Alura had said.

Living in Midvale meant learning everything at once: English, school hallways, cafeteria food, the way Earthlings laughed. Kara discovered quickly that Earth's yellow sun charged her cells like a battery. She could fly. She had heat vision that could cut steel and freeze breath that could ice over a river. She was, in every measurable way, as powerful as Superman. But power is not the same as wisdom, and Kara was still learning both.

She chose the name Supergirl and sewed the S-shield — the Kryptonian symbol her whole family had worn — onto a blue suit with a red cape. The first time she flew at full speed over Midvale, the sonic boom rattled every window on the block. She laughed, surprised by her own strength. Somewhere below, the lights of a small Earth town glowed warm and ordinary. She was far from Argo City, far from her mother's voice. But she was not lost. She was beginning.
