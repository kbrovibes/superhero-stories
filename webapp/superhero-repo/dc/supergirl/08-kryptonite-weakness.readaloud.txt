Okay. This one is important. This is the story about kryptonite — and about something even more powerful than being invincible.

A man named Marcus Drayne had done something very foolish. He had acquired a fragment of genuine green kryptonite — a piece of Krypton's core, irradiated by the planet's explosion, still glowing that terrible cold green. He had it in a lead-lined case in Midvale's old textile warehouse. And he had three employees trapped inside.

His plan: use the kryptonite to stop Supergirl.

Kara came in fast. She hit the warehouse door. She crossed the threshold.

And her legs stopped working.

Let me explain what kryptonite does. It is not simply painful — though it IS painful. It removes the sun-energy from Kryptonian cells. That energy is what makes Kara strong and fast and able to fly. Without it, she is still Kara Zor-El, but she feels small and slow and breakable in a way she had almost forgotten was possible. She went down on one knee in the doorway.

Drayne stared at her. She was supposed to fall over completely. She did not fall over completely.

From one knee, Kara took inventory. Green case: twelve feet away on a table. Lead case, closed, blocks all the radiation — she knew that from Superman. Employees: unharmed, at the far wall. Drayne: scared, not dangerous.

She did not try to fly. She could not fly.

She crawled.

Not dramatically. Not easily. She moved across that warehouse floor on her hands and knees, one foot at a time, fighting the terrible green light every inch of the way.

And she reached the case. And she flipped the lid shut.

CLICK.

The green glow disappeared. Warm sun-energy flooded back into her cells within seconds. By the time the DEO showed up, Kara was standing. Drayne was sitting on the floor, waiting to be collected. He had not planned for "she just keeps moving."

The employees were safe. The kryptonite was secured.

Kara sat on the warehouse steps afterward, thinking about the crawling. It was not a heroic image — a hero on the floor of an old warehouse, moving slowly toward a box.

But then she thought: that is exactly what being a hero means. Not that you are never knocked down. That you keep moving even when you are.

Being vulnerable isn't the worst thing.

Stopping because you are vulnerable — that's the only thing that truly defeats you.
