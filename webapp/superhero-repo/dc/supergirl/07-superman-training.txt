Superman had been on Earth long enough to have learned most of his lessons by accident. He had broken school lockers and bent doorknobs and once, memorably, cracked the floor of the Kent farm kitchen by stepping down too hard while distracted. He did not want Kara to learn the same way, so on the first Saturday after she arrived in Midvale, he flew out to Kansas and they spent the day at the Kent farm going through the fundamentals together. Martha Kent made lunch and did not say a word about the crater Kara accidentally made in the east pasture.

Superman was not a teacher by nature — he was a journalist, a reporter who happened to also save the world, and his approach to instruction was thorough but patient. He taught Kara the altitude where commercial aircraft flew and the lanes she should avoid, the sonic boom suppression technique that let her travel at supersonic speed without rattling every window in a fifty-mile radius, and the particular concentration required to use X-ray vision selectively without seeing through everything simultaneously, which was, he admitted, deeply unpleasant before you learned the filter.

They sparred seriously twice, in a remote area of Wyoming far from any population. The first time, Kara came at him too fast and too straight, and he redirected her into a sandstone butte with a single practiced shoulder move. She came out of the rock formation still arguing that she almost had him. The second time, she lasted considerably longer, had clearly been thinking about his technique, and actually caught him off-balance once before he recovered. He told her she had good instincts. He meant it.

What he found harder to teach was the weight of it. Being invulnerable meant you were always the one who could stay — always the one in the burning building, always the one between the car and the crowd. Most people could walk away from a danger they couldn't handle. A Kryptonian didn't have that exit. Superman had spent years finding the equilibrium between being needed everywhere and being one person. He told Kara he still hadn't fully solved it. She told him that was the most honest thing he'd said all day.

Kara flew back to Midvale that evening carrying three of Martha Kent's cookies in her cape pocket, which was not structurally designed for that purpose but worked fine. Superman had been her cousin her whole life but she had known him as a stranger until today. Now he was something closer to family in the truest sense — the person who had the same view of the sky that she did and understood why it felt like home.
