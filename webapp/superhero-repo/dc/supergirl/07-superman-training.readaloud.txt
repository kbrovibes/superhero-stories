Hey, do you want to know what it looks like when the two most powerful heroes on Earth train together? Yes? Then buckle up!

Superman — Clark Kent, reporter for the Daily Planet — is not exactly a natural teacher. But he is very thorough. And the first Saturday after Kara arrived in Midvale, he flew out and took her to the Kent farm in Kansas, where he grew up, for a full day of practice.

Martha Kent made sandwiches. She said nothing about the crater Kara accidentally punched into the east pasture.

Superman taught Kara the altitude rules first: commercial aircraft fly between 30,000 and 40,000 feet — stay OUT of those lanes! Then the sonic boom trick: a specific tensing of every muscle in your body that lets you hit supersonic speed without KABOOM shattering every window for miles. Very useful. Very hard. Kara got it on the sixth try.

Then X-ray vision. Oh boy. Before you learn the filter, Superman said, X-ray vision shows you EVERYTHING — walls, floors, pipes, the stuff inside the walls and floors and pipes. It is, he admitted, deeply unpleasant. He taught her the mental switch that lets her choose what to look through. She practiced until she could scan a building and only see what she intended.

Now the fun part: SPARRING!

They flew to a remote part of Wyoming, far from anyone. First round: Kara came in fast and straight — WHOOOOSH — and Superman redirected her into a sandstone butte with one practiced shoulder move. KRAANG. She came out of the rock still insisting she almost had him.

Second round: she lasted MUCH longer. She had been thinking. She varied her angles, doubled back, used misdirection. She even caught Superman off-balance once — really off-balance! — before he recovered.

"You have good instincts," he told her.

He meant it, and Kara knew it.

Then he said the harder thing. Being invulnerable means you are always the one who can stay. You are always the one who goes into the burning building when everyone else runs out. That is a big responsibility. He said he still hadn't completely figured out the balance.

"That's the most honest thing you've said all day," Kara told him.

She flew home that evening with three of Martha Kent's cookies tucked into her cape. Her cousin had become real family today. He was the one other person in the universe who understood exactly what it felt like to look at the Earth sky and feel at home.

That was worth a lot more than a few sparring lessons.

WHOOOOSH — she was already back over Midvale by sunset!
