Slade Wilson was the most patient enemy the Teen Titans ever faced. He did not fight them directly. He watched, sent agents and elaborate traps, and taught himself where each Titan was strong and where they could be broken. He wore a half-orange, half-black mask and spoke in the measured voice of someone who was always the most dangerous person in the room.

When Slade moved, he moved through the edges of the Titans' world — taking their records, turning information into leverage. Robin, as leader, felt the pressure most, and Slade knew it. He always knew where pressure would land hardest. That was what separated him from every villain before: he planned around the team rather than through them.

Beast Boy's role was not the front-line charge he preferred. It was surveillance and waiting — neither natural for him, but both necessary. Slade's network stretched across Jump City's coastal ports, and the Titans needed eyes in places a human team couldn't reach. Garfield became a green pigeon and sat on Slade's warehouse roof for three hours. He became a green spider and mapped a coastal facility's corridors through the ventilation for Cyborg and Robin outside. He became a sea lion and tracked a supply boat across Jump City Bay until Raven and Starfire intercepted it from the air.

The big confrontation came at the industrial port, and Slade was there himself — which he rarely was, which meant he'd decided this move mattered. The Titans fought as a unit, and the difference from a year earlier was measurable: they covered each other's angles without having to be told. Robin went after Slade directly. Starfire and Raven ran aerial suppression on the agents. Cyborg held the main entrance. Beast Boy went low and fast, driving agents toward Cyborg's line as a green wolf, cutting off the retreat as a green hawk, and finally — when Slade made for the water — diving into the bay as a green orca and surfacing between Slade's boat and open sea.

Slade escaped. He always did, that first time. But the Titans had disrupted the operation enough to shut down the network he had built, and they had done it together, covering every angle, no gaps.

Robin called it a draw. Garfield said it felt like a win. Cyborg pointed out they were both right. Raven said nothing but sat down on the dock and for a long moment did not move, and Starfire put a careful hand on her shoulder, and Beast Boy sat beside her as a small green cat — compact and warm — because sometimes that was the right shape to be.
