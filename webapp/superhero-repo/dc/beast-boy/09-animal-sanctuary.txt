Between missions, Garfield Logan spent his free afternoons at a wildlife rehabilitation center on the outskirts of Jump City called the Redwood Animal Sanctuary. He had started going there not as a hero but as himself — a green teenage boy with an encyclopedic knowledge of animal biology and a genuine ease around creatures that the sanctuary's human volunteers couldn't quite replicate.

The sanctuary's director, Dr. Priya Nair, had been skeptical at first. Teenage volunteers were common; reliable ones were not. But Garfield showed up every Thursday and Saturday and knew more about the animals in the recovery bay than most staff. He knew the correct temperature for a juvenile great horned owl. He knew the injured harbor seal in pen three had developed a preference for being approached from the left. Beyond book knowledge, the green-monkey serum had given him something harder to name: an attunement to creatures that went beyond reading behavior.

The animals trusted him differently than they trusted others. The owl ate from his hand. A terrified river otter let Garfield lift it within forty minutes of arrival — other staff had needed three days. He didn't transform at the sanctuary. That felt like a violation of the trust the animals gave him simply as Garfield. He came as a person and they accepted him that way.

There was a juvenile California condor at the sanctuary named Rosalind, brought in with a wing injury from a coastal wind farm. Condors are not affectionate birds; they are large, quiet, observant creatures with very long memories. Rosalind watched Garfield for three weeks before she allowed him near enough to handle her. After that she was indifferent to him with the particular indifference of an animal that has decided you are not interesting because you are already known. Garfield found this deeply satisfying.

Dr. Nair asked him once, after a particularly long afternoon with a pair of injured river otters and a hawk that kept trying to escape its enclosure, why he came. He was, she noted, a superhero. He presumably had other options for his time.

Garfield said: "I spent the first five years of my life surrounded by animals. My parents were scientists. The animals in the Congo were more comfortable around me than most people were. And now I can be anything, but I still choose to come here and be nothing. Just a person, being trusted." He paused. "I like who I am here."

Dr. Nair said that was a very good reason.

Garfield agreed, and went back to cleaning the hawk's enclosure, which was a job that needed doing regardless of what shape you were in.
