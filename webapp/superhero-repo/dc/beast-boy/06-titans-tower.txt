Titans Tower stands at the edge of Jump City Bay, a gleaming T-shaped structure on a small rocky island, visible from the waterfront like a lighthouse shaped like the first letter of a team name. It is the home base, training ground, living room, and kitchen of the Teen Titans — and for Garfield Logan, it is the closest thing to a permanent home he has had since the research station in the Congo.

His room in the Tower is on the fourth floor, east-facing, and it is chaos in the most personal way: walls covered with posters of bands and animals, a desk buried under half-finished comics and snack wrappers, and an entire shelf dedicated to the field guides his mother gave him. He never moved those guides to storage. They are organized by continent, and he has read each one so many times the spines are soft.

The common room on the upper floor is where the team does everything that isn't a mission: eating Robin's precisely scheduled meals, watching films that Beast Boy selects and Raven occasionally tolerates, arguing about what constitutes a real breakfast (Cyborg says meat; Garfield says cereal is technically a grain). The big wraparound couch has permanent indentations now, the kind that come from years of five people sitting in the same spots.

The training room below the common floor is where Garfield has learned the most. Not just new forms — Robin runs drills that have nothing to do with transformation. Reaction time. Coordination with teammates. Reading a chaotic situation and finding the leverage point, not just the biggest target. He and Cyborg have a long-running competition on the obstacle course. The score is roughly tied and both of them refuse to admit it.

The Tower's computer system — maintained by Cyborg — tracks all active threats in and around Jump City. Garfield checks it every morning, a habit borrowed from Robin, who checks it approximately every four minutes. The kitchen is stocked with enough cereal to survive a siege: not an official design requirement, but an unofficial one.

What the Tower gives Garfield, more than any single feature, is this: five people who come home to it. Robin, always three steps ahead of the next problem. Starfire, who greets every morning like it is a miracle. Raven, who reads in every quiet corner and whose presence is its own kind of steadiness. Cyborg, whose laugh fills the upper floor when something genuinely funny happens. And Garfield, green and loud and ready, who takes the same seat on the couch every single time and calls the Tower, without any irony at all, his favorite place in the world.
