Once upon a time, on a Thursday afternoon in early spring, Garfield Logan walked through the gates of the Redwood Animal Sanctuary and was not Beast Boy at all.

He was just a boy. Green, carrying a backpack with a change of clothes and a granola bar for later. He stopped at the entrance to sign the volunteer log, as he always did, and walked down the path to the recovery bay where the animals who were healing lived in their careful enclosures.

The Redwood Animal Sanctuary sat on the outskirts of Jump City in a stretch of low coastal hills. Dr. Priya Nair had built it over many years — a precise, methodical woman who ran the sanctuary with the care of someone who had decided animals deserved the same rigor as any medical practice.

She had been uncertain about Garfield when he first appeared. Teenage volunteers were common. The reliable ones were rare. But he kept showing up, which was the first test, and he knew things, which was the second.

He knew the juvenile great horned owl in recovery bay two needed dim light from eleven to three. He knew the harbor seal in pen three preferred approach from the left. He knew the difference between an otter who needed space and one who was withdrawing, and those were not the same thing.

The sanctuary's animals responded to him differently than they responded to others — with the ease of creatures who sensed, on some frequency below hearing, that here was someone who understood them. The owl ate from his hand. A terrified river otter arrived in a winter storm and let Garfield lift it within forty minutes. Other staff had needed three days.

He did not transform at the sanctuary. He came as a person. The animals knew him as a green boy who moved quietly and was reliably present on Thursdays and Saturdays, and they accepted him on those terms.

Then there was Rosalind.

Rosalind was a California condor, juvenile, brought in with a wing injury after being found grounded near a coastal wind farm. She was large and dark and quiet in the way of condors — serious creatures with long memories and no interest in being charmed. She watched Garfield from her enclosure for three weeks without moving toward him.

Then one Thursday she walked to the center of the enclosure, looked at him with her enormous dark eye, decided he was acceptable, and went to the other corner.

From that day on she treated him with the warm indifference of an animal who has thoroughly assessed something and found it unremarkable. She was not affectionate. But she allowed his approach for her treatment with the placid cooperation of someone who has decided this is routine.

Garfield found this profoundly satisfying.

One evening Dr. Nair found him on a bench outside the recovery bay, eating his granola bar, watching the hills go gold.

She sat beside him.

"You're a superhero," she said. "You could be doing many things on a Thursday afternoon."

Garfield thought about it honestly.

"When I was little," he said, "in Africa — I was surrounded by animals before I could remember. My mom studied how they talked to each other. And the animals would come and sit near me because I was small and quiet. The birds would land near me while I read." He turned the wrapper in his hands. "I can be any of them now. Any creature. And that's useful. But here I come as myself. Just Garfield. And they trust me anyway — not because I'm something powerful. Because I show up and I'm careful and I'm consistent."

He looked at the recovery bay.

"I like who I am here," he said simply.

Dr. Nair was quiet for a moment. "That," she said, "is a very good reason."

She went back inside.

Garfield sat on the bench another moment, watching the coastal hills go from gold to the soft grey of early evening. A heron came in low over the wetland at the sanctuary's edge and landed without sound in the tall grass. He watched it settle — the angle of the neck, the stillness, the body simply doing what it was built for.

His mother had taught him that watching was its own kind of understanding.

He tucked the wrapper in his pocket, picked up his broom, and went back to the hawk enclosure in the last of the evening light. The hawk watched him from its perch, the wing healed, the flight feathers nearly grown back. In another three weeks it would be ready to go.

He swept carefully and methodically, not rushing, not thinking about what came next.

Just this. Just here. Just a green boy with a broom, being exactly who he wanted to be.

That was more than enough.
