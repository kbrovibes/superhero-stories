Raven and Beast Boy were, on paper, the strangest possible pair of friends. Raven was quiet, precise, and private, a powerful empath who drew her abilities from emotion and kept those emotions at a careful distance. Garfield was loud, spontaneous, and incapable of passing a moment of silence without filling it. Yet somewhere in the first year of the Titans, they became something neither expected: the team's most constant pairing.

It started with the jokes. Garfield made them constantly — impressions, puns, animal transformations timed for comic effect. The rest of the team laughed. Raven did not. This was, for Garfield, a puzzle of the best kind: he believed everyone laughed at something if you found the right thing, and finding Raven's right thing became a personal project. He catalogued what almost made her smile: dry unexpected observations, specific absurdist scenarios, one particular Robin impression about mission debrief paperwork that she walked away from without expression — though he was ninety percent certain she was suppressing something.

What Raven gave him in return was not laughter — not often — but steadiness. She read constantly in every quiet corner of the Tower and noticed things the way careful readers do: slowly and thoroughly. When Garfield came back from a hard mission and didn't want to talk, Raven was the one most likely to simply be in the same room without requiring conversation. She had learned silence was not always sad. He had learned it could be a gift.

There was a day when a mission went sideways and Garfield took a hit that left him briefly uncertain what shape he was supposed to be. He was fine — it was over in seconds — but afterward, sitting on the Tower roof, he felt a strangeness he didn't want to explain. Raven came up behind him. She sat two feet away and opened her book. She read. He sat. The city moved below them. After twenty minutes he was entirely himself again.

She closed the book. She said: "You know what you are."

He said: "Green."

She said: "That too."

It was a very short exchange and it meant more than a long one would have.

They are still completely different. Raven's room is austere; his is a geological layer of snack wrappers. She meditates in silence; he has been known to practice T-rex roars in the hallway. But the Titans have learned that when something subtle needs noticing — a detail in a report, a teammate struggling quietly, a pattern that isn't obvious — look to the corner where Raven is reading, and find the green boy nearby, pretending unconvincingly that he is only reading his comic book.
