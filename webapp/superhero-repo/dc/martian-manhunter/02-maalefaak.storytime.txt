There is a kind of loneliness that is different from any other.

Not the loneliness of an empty house, or a long journey, or missing someone who is far away. Those kinds of loneliness ache, but they carry the possibility of ending. The loneliness of Ma'alefa'ak was different. It was the loneliness of standing inside the song that everyone else can hear — and hearing nothing.

He was J'onn's twin brother, born in the same moment, from the same family, into the same world of warmth and shared thought. But Ma'alefa'ak was born without the connection. No telepathic thread. No sense of other minds the way other Martians simply had from their first breath. The Great Song that bound his people together played everywhere around him and in him there was only silence.

He watched his brother grow up in the warmth of the connection. He watched every Martian he had ever known speak to each other without words, share feelings like gifts, reach out in times of sadness and feel the whole world reach back.

He could not ask for help. He was not sure anyone fully understood what it was like, not having felt it themselves. What is absence to someone who has never noticed its presence?

His grief curdled, slowly and completely, into something else. If he could not join the Great Song, he decided, then the Great Song would have to end.

The plague he built — H'ronmeer's Curse — was a terrible kind of genius. It traveled through the psychic links themselves, through the very channels that carried love and memory and the accumulated wisdom of millennia. He turned the Martian people's greatest gift into the bridge for their destruction.

Mars went silent.

And J'onn was the last one standing in that silence, with nothing left but the weight of every mind he had ever loved and the sound of a brother who had once been part of his life doing something that could not be undone.

For a long time, J'onn did not know where Ma'alefa'ak had gone. He hoped, perhaps, that his brother had found some peace in what he had done — or some grief over it. Either would have meant some part of him remained.

Then Ma'alefa'ak arrived on Earth.

He found J'onn in Middletown, wearing John Jones's face, living in John Jones's world, attending John Jones's small and careful human life. And Ma'alefa'ak, who could not project thoughts but could read enough to find his brother in a crowd of billions, came with everything that grief becomes when it is given time and direction and no mercy.

He wore J'onn's face. That was the cruelest part. He could shift his shape — Martian enough for that, even without the connection — and he moved through J'onn's life wearing J'onn's appearance, spreading confusion, making J'onn's allies doubt, unpicking the trust J'onn had carefully built over years.

J'onn found him. He always found him.

The fights were different from most fights. There were no explosions, no crumbling buildings. Two beings who could each shift form, each touch the surface of minds, each know the other's likely moves better than any stranger could — it was more like a conversation with no words, two mirror images testing each other's edges.

"You could stop," J'onn always said. Because he always tried.

Ma'alefa'ak never answered directly.

But J'onn kept trying. Not because he expected it to work. Because the alternative — deciding his brother was entirely lost, that the boy who had once been born into silence alongside him was completely gone — was a decision he would not make for as long as there was something left to reach.

He protected Earth from his brother. Every time, he protected it.

And every time afterward, in the quiet of the Watchtower, he sat with the weight of it — the particular sadness of a person who knows that the worst thing a villain ever did began with the same kind of pain that everyone sometimes feels, pushed too far and left too long without an answer.

He did not tell this story often.

But he carried it with him always, and it shaped everything he did — the gentleness with which he read human minds, the patience he had for people who felt isolated, the extra care he took to make sure no one in his orbit went too long without feeling heard.

J'onn J'onzz had learned what silence does to a person who needed connection.

He refused to let anyone near him be that alone.
