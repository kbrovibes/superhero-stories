Close your eyes, and try to imagine a world where no one is ever alone.

Not because they live close together. Not because they talk all day. But because every mind on the whole world is gently, beautifully connected — like a million instruments all playing the same song at the same time, each one different, each one heard. On the planet Ma'aleca'andra — the world that humans call Mars — the Martian people called this the Great Song.

J'onn J'onzz grew up inside the Great Song. He could feel the warmth of his wife M'yri'ah's thoughts the way you feel sunshine on your face. He could sense his daughter K'hym's dreams from across the city, small and bright and wondering. He walked the streets of his people and the streets hummed with shared feeling — curiosity, love, the steady contentment of a civilization that had built something beautiful.

J'onn was a Manhunter. A peacekeeper. He kept the peace not with force, but with understanding — because when you can feel what others feel, you rarely need to raise your voice.

The cities of Mars were not built from stone or steel. They were sung. Martian architects would focus their psionic energy — the mental power that every Martian carried like a birthright — and resonate with the living rock, and the rock would rise into towers and bridges and domes, shaped by thought and feeling. The Great Song and the built world were not separate things. They were the same thing.

J'onn could not have imagined losing it.

And then it was gone.

The plague came without warning — H'ronmeer's Curse, traveling through the very psychic links that bound his people together. What had been their greatest gift became the path of their undoing. The Great Song, which had carried love and learning for millennia, now carried something terrible. J'onn watched the cities go quiet, world by world, district by district. He watched the light leave his civilization.

He held M'yri'ah's hand. He held K'hym close. The plague took them too.

He was the last one.

He does not speak often about what those final hours on Mars were like. But anyone who looks at him — truly looks, past the green skin and the glowing eyes and the alien frame — can see something quiet and permanent in his expression. The weight of being the only one left who remembers.

He was pulled from Mars without warning.

A scientist on Earth — a small, kind, lonely man named Dr. Saul Erdel — had built a machine in his study in Middletown that he called a transmatter beam. He had aimed it at the stars as an experiment, not quite believing it would work. It worked. It pulled a signal from across the void and materialized it in his study — and the signal was a being, green-skinned and suddenly present, who was J'onn J'onzz, the last Martian.

The shock of transit, the shock of arrival, the sudden roar of Earth — so loud, so dense, so full of separate minds all talking without hearing each other — was too much for Dr. Erdel. He passed away, quietly, in his chair.

J'onn was alone on a strange world with no way home.

He did not collapse under the weight of this. He had already survived the impossible once. He had the discipline of a Manhunter and the endurance of a Martian, and he chose, slowly and carefully, to understand this world that had called him.

He took a human shape — John Jones, detective, with a broad face and kind eyes and a long gray coat. He rented a small apartment in Middletown and began solving cases. He had a gift for it; Martian telepathy reads human motives the way most detectives can only guess at them.

He patrolled. He helped. He listened to a world full of billions of separate minds who could not hear each other, and he loved them anyway — loved their loud, imprecise, fumbling, tremendous effort to connect.

He found chocolate-sandwich cookies in a corner shop one afternoon, and something about the small, perfectly portioned sweetness of them — not too much of anything, just right — made him buy the whole shelf.

He had a new home now. It was very different from the last one. But J'onn J'onzz had learned the hardest possible lesson a person can learn: that love does not require permanence to be real.

He would protect this world. He would protect it with everything he had.

For his people. For Dr. Erdel. For Earth.

For the Great Song, which existed now only in one mind — but existed still.
