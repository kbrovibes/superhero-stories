There is a question that people ask about the Justice League, sometimes: how does a team of the world's most powerful beings — people who can fly, who can run faster than sound, who can stop tanks with their hands — how do they stay together? How do they make decisions when everyone is the strongest person in the room?

The answer is J'onn.

Not because he is the most powerful, although he is formidable. Not because he is the loudest, because he rarely is. He is the one who listens. Who genuinely, deeply, without agenda, listens — with a mind that can hear what people mean underneath what they say.

In a battle, the team moved through a telepathic web he maintained. Not eavesdropping — he was very careful about what he shared and what he kept private — but a light connection, like a held hand, so that when Superman needed to know where Wonder Woman was positioning without taking a second to look, he simply knew. When the Flash was about to run into a situation that two other team members were already approaching from opposite sides, J'onn felt the overlap and smoothed it out in a fraction of a second. No collision. No wasted effort. The team moved like something that had been together long enough to breathe in sync.

After battles, he did the work that never made the reports.

He noticed that Hal Jordan, after very large constructs that required sustained willpower for long periods, had a particular kind of exhaustion — not physical, more like the feeling after a very long test. J'onn would find him in the observation room and not say anything, just be present in a way that did not require Hal to perform any kind of recovery he wasn't feeling.

He noticed that Batman, who was the most careful and controlled person on the team and always seemed entirely certain, had occasional moments — usually after missions involving children in danger — where the certainty ran out. J'onn never brought this up with Batman directly. He simply made sure the debrief meeting afterward was the kind where problems were named and solved and closed, where there were no open questions left hanging.

He noticed that Superman sometimes longed, not in a way he would ever complain about, for a version of the team that included someone from Krypton. Someone who would just simply know, without explaining, what it felt like to be from somewhere else entirely. J'onn did not offer to fill this role — that was not what Superman needed. He offered to listen, on the evenings when Superman stood at the Watchtower window looking in the direction of the sky where Krypton had been.

He brought the cookies to team meetings. This was not accidental.

There was something about the ritual of it — the plain, sweet, uncomplicated pleasure of a chocolate-sandwich cookie, which had no agenda and no complexity — that served as a reset. An interruption of the serious. A small, repeated signal: we are not only heroes here. We are also people who like cookies.

Wonder Woman had asked him once, early in their friendship, whether he found human emotion difficult to navigate — so much louder and less ordered than Martian psychic communication.

"It is very loud," he agreed. "And very imprecise. And very earnest." He paused. "I find it extraordinary."

She smiled. "Because it's so different from your world?"

"Because it is so genuinely trying," he said. "Every person I have ever met is doing their very best with what they understand about the world. Even the people causing harm, which does not excuse it — they are working from some model of reality that makes sense to them. To read a human mind is to understand how hard everyone is working."

He held out the package of cookies.

She took one.

This was the thing J'onn brought to the Justice League that no mission report could fully capture: the knowledge that everyone on the team was carrying something. The certainty that every person was more than their most visible moment. The patience to be present in the space where someone was struggling and to not require them to be fine before they were ready.

He was the heart of the team. He knew this and did not emphasize it. The heart of a body does not announce itself. It simply keeps working, keeps circulating, keeps the whole living thing going.

J'onn J'onzz had come from a world built on connection.

He had lost that world.

And he had decided, with the full weight of a Martian Manhunter's will and the quiet, enormous love of a man who had survived the impossible, to build another one — in this loud, imprecise, earnestly trying, extraordinarily human team.

He opened the package of cookies.

Everyone reached.

The Watchtower hummed.

And the heart of the Justice League kept on beating.
