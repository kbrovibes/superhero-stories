There are villains who are dangerous because they are strong. And there are villains who are dangerous because they can reach inside your mind and make you afraid of the very thing you trust most.

Despero was both.

He came from a world called Kalanor — a planet of red skies and ancient, tired stone, where power was the only currency that mattered and mercy was considered a weakness you grew out of. He was enormous — taller than most doorways, built like a monument, his skin a deep burnished red. But it was not his size that made grown heroes step back when he arrived.

It was the third eye.

Set in the center of his broad forehead, it glowed with a psionic light that had no natural color — somewhere between amber and flame, shifting. And when Despero focused that eye on you, the world changed. Not the actual world — the world you lived in inside your head. Your fears rose up wearing the faces of the things you loved. Your confidence hollowed out. Your certainty that you were doing the right thing became murky and doubtful.

He called this "Mayavic" energy — a reshaping of perceived reality. He had conquered the population of Kalanor with it before they could organize a defense. He had broken the will of civilizations the way other tyrants broke their armies.

He came for Earth because Earth had people who mattered to J'onn J'onzz. And Despero had decided that J'onn was worth the journey.

The Justice League assembled when Despero arrived — Superman, Wonder Woman, Green Lantern, the Flash, Batman — and the first thing J'onn did was not a physical thing at all. He extended his telepathy outward, formed it into a web, and wrapped it around every one of his teammates.

He had explained this to Batman beforehand. "When Despero's psionic field touches you, it looks for the edges of your fear. It finds the things you trust — your own mind, your instincts — and makes them uncertain. I will not let it in."

Batman had looked at him for a moment and then said, simply: "I know."

The battle was fought on two levels simultaneously.

In the physical world, Superman traded blows with Despero that cracked the ground beneath them. Wonder Woman's lasso carved arcs of golden light through the air. Green Lantern built construct after construct, each one buying seconds for the team to regroup.

And in the mental world — the layer beneath, the space between thoughts — J'onn held the shield. He could feel Despero's Mayavic energy pressing against him like pressure against glass: persistent, searching, looking for the crack. J'onn breathed and held. He had practiced stillness on a dying planet. He had held himself together alone for longer than most people could imagine being alone. Despero's psychic strength was formidable.

J'onn's endurance was deeper.

When the Flash moved at full speed, Despero's third eye tried to overwhelm his spatial perception — make him uncertain of where he was in the world. J'onn felt it happen and corrected it instantly, sending a clear, warm signal: you know exactly where you are. You can trust yourself.

The Flash's speed did not waver.

The fight lasted eleven minutes, which is a very long time when something is trying to convince you the world is made of shadows.

In the end, it was J'onn who broke through. Not with a physical strike — with a telepathic push, direct and precise, aimed not at Despero's control systems but at the part of him that had once wanted to be something other than a tyrant. It was a small part. It was buried very deep. But J'onn found it, because J'onn always found the part of a mind that was still capable of feeling something.

The Mayavic field faltered. Just for a moment. But a moment was enough.

Wonder Woman's lasso wrapped around Despero's third eye. Green Lantern's construct sealed the containment. Superman steadied it.

Done.

J'onn released the mental web gradually, like letting go of a rope hand over hand, and felt each of his teammates' minds settle back into themselves — clear and whole.

"Was that..." the Flash started.

"Yes," said J'onn.

"The whole time?"

"Yes."

"You were doing all of that and also helping us think straight and also—"

"It is what I do," J'onn said.

He did not say it proudly. He said it the way you state a fact about yourself that is simply true, that you have accepted completely.

He sat down on the ground.

Someone put a package of chocolate-sandwich cookies in his hand.

He was fairly sure it was Batman, but Batman was already looking the other direction.

J'onn ate three cookies and felt the world settle back into its proper weight.

The Great Song was gone. But its last singer was still here, still standing, still holding the minds of the people he loved steady and whole.

That was enough. It was more than enough.
