In the basement of STAR Labs in Central City, there is a piece of equipment that looks a little bit like an ordinary treadmill and is, in fact, one of the most extraordinary devices ever built.

It is called the Cosmic Treadmill.

Barry Allen designed it himself, working from principles that he had worked out over years of running — of feeling, at certain speeds, a change in the quality of the world around him. At the edges of the very fastest he could run, something shifted. Time became permeable. The space between moments became visible, the way the threads of a cloth become visible when you hold it up to the light. He understood that the Speed Force — the cosmic energy that connected him to motion at every scale in the universe — was not simply a fuel source. It was a place.

The Cosmic Treadmill was the key to visiting it.

At precisely calibrated vibrations — frequencies that Barry had mapped over dozens of experimental runs, marked in notebooks that Cisco Ramon once tried to read and described as "physics written by someone running downhill" — the Treadmill opened a gateway. Not a door exactly, not a window. More like a membrane between one kind of existence and another, thinning until it was possible to step through.

The Speed Force, on the other side, is not a place with an address. It does not have walls or ceilings or a floor in any ordinary sense. What it has is light — a rushing, golden, omnidirectional light that moves in the direction of forward even when you can't tell which direction forward is. It has sound, though the sound is not made of waves — it is made of something that precedes waves, something that vibration is only one expression of. It has a feeling that Barry could never quite describe to Cisco, though he tried many times:

"Like being inside every river and every wind at the same time," he said once. "But also completely still."

"That's impossible," Cisco said.

"Yes," Barry agreed.

Past, present, and future coexist in the Speed Force. This is not philosophical — it is literal. At sufficient depth, Barry could perceive events that had not yet happened alongside events that had been over for centuries, all running together like colors in water. He learned, through careful and sometimes catastrophic experiment, that the Cosmic Treadmill could aim this perception — could point him toward a specific moment in time rather than sending him spinning randomly through history.

He had used this for good purposes. He had also, occasionally, used it and created problems that took considerable effort and the assistance of Jay Garrick — the original Flash, the first person to run at speeds that touched the Speed Force, a good man with a winged helmet and forty years of hard experience — to sort out.

Jay Garrick had visited Barry in the Speed Force once, when Barry had gone deeper than he'd intended and the way back had become unclear. He had appeared as a shape in the golden light, walking without apparent effort in a direction that seemed to be outward, and said: "Don't chase it. Move parallel to it and let the current bring you around."

Barry had done this, and it had worked.

He thought about Jay often, in the Speed Force. He thought about what it meant to be the inheritor of something that Jay had helped build — the idea of the Flash, the tradition of the speedster, the understanding that the Speed Force was not owned by any one person but was shared across every person who had ever touched it, a community of connection stretching across time.

The Cosmic Treadmill sat in its corner of STAR Labs, humming faintly, always slightly warm to the touch. Cisco had run every diagnostic on it that was possible to run and had concluded that it was in a constant state of receiving — like a radio that was always tuned to a very specific and very distant signal.

Barry visited it on evenings when he needed to think. He didn't always run it. Sometimes he just sat beside it and felt the gentle vibration and let his thoughts settle.

The Speed Force was always there. It had always been there. It would be there long after the Cosmic Treadmill was gone, long after Central City was a memory, humming at the base of all motion in the universe, patient and inexhaustible, connecting every moving thing to every other.

Barry Allen had simply found a way to say hello to it.

And it, in its way, had always said hello back.
