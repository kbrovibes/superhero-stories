In the heart of Central City, there stands a very special building. It has gleaming walls and wide windows, and above the entrance hang three bold words: The Flash Museum. It is the city's love letter to its greatest hero.

Inside, visitors walk through halls filled with wonder. There are the Flash's original suits — each one a little different, each one telling the story of a new challenge met and overcome. There is a replica of the Cosmic Treadmill, glowing softly under a spotlight. There is Barry Allen's forensic laboratory equipment, the very tools he used to solve crimes from the inside out. And there is a special wing called the Hall of Rogues, where the captured weapons of Captain Cold and Mirror Master and the rest are kept safely behind glass — a reminder of the battles that shaped Central City's history.

But the museum is more than a collection. It holds lectures about science and time. It has programs where children come to learn about the ocean of energy called the Speed Force. It throws events where the whole city gathers to celebrate the hero who runs for all of them.

And do you know what Barry Allen does sometimes, on a quiet afternoon? He walks through those halls in his ordinary clothes — just a man among the crowd — and he listens. He listens to parents telling their children about the Flash's adventures. He sees young eyes go wide. And he feels, not pride exactly, but something warmer: gratitude that the running means something to someone.

And that is how the Flash Museum became not just a building, but the heartbeat of Central City itself.
