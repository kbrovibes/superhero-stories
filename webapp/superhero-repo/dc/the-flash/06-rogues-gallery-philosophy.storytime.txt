If you sat down with the Central City Rogues and asked them about themselves — which almost nobody does, because the Rogues are criminals and sitting down with criminals for a conversation is an unusual approach — you would discover something surprising.

They have a philosophy.

Not the elaborate kind that requires a seminar to understand. A simple kind, which is often the most durable kind: Crime is a profession. A profession has standards. Standards protect everyone, including the people on the wrong side of the law.

Captain Cold had developed this philosophy over years of careful observation. He had watched what happened to criminal operations that ran on chaos and cruelty, and what he observed was that they didn't last. They attracted the kind of attention that ended badly. They hurt people in ways that made law enforcement treat them as urgent priorities rather than tolerable nuisances. And they turned their members against each other, because cruelty has no loyalty and chaos eats its own practitioners first.

The Rogues, by contrast, were still here. They had been operating in Central City for years. They had records, yes — arrest records, trial records, the whole paper trail of a professional criminal organization — but they were also still intact, still coordinated, still present, which was more than could be said for several other operations that had tried their luck in Central City and found the Flash a more complete obstacle than anticipated.

"Cold calls it a code," Mirror Master once explained to a new recruit, gesturing with a mirror-portal that opened and closed like an eye behind him. "What it is, is good business."

The code had specific provisions. No killing — ever, under any circumstances, and any member who crossed this line was no longer a member. Never, ever target the Flash's family, his friends, or anyone known to be connected to him personally. Don't push the Flash to the point where he stops being measured, because a measured Flash was a manageable problem and an unmeasured Flash was not a manageable anything. And maintain — this one was Cold's personal addition — a certain respect for the city itself.

The city was their city, after all. They lived here. Their families were here. When a catastrophe threatened Central City — a real one, the kind that came from outside and didn't care about the local crime ecosystem — the Rogues had, on several documented occasions, stood alongside the Flash to address it. Not because they liked each other. Because Central City was home, and home was the one thing even professional criminals defended.

The Flash appreciated this more than he admitted publicly.

"You know," he said to Captain Cold after one such incident, in the brief, odd window between the emergency and the arrival of the CCPD, "there's a version of you that uses all of this for something different."

"Is there," said Cold, flat and not quite a question.

"The organization, the discipline, the code. You could run something legitimate."

"Boring," said Cold.

"I figured you'd say that." Barry looked at him. "Still. You have more principle than most people I know. That means something."

Cold looked at him for a moment with eyes the color of winter. "Don't go soft on me, Allen."

"Too late," Barry said cheerfully, and then the CCPD arrived and the conversation ended in the usual way.

The Rogues provided Central City with something that nobody mentioned in any official report: stability. A criminal ecosystem with rules was, paradoxically, safer than one without. The Rogues kept other, less principled operations from establishing themselves in Central City. They kept the chaos out. The Flash stopped their jobs. They planned better jobs. The Flash was faster. They were smarter.

It was, as Captain Cold had once said, a professional relationship.

And somewhere in the Flash Museum, in the Hall of Rogues exhibit, there is a display case with Captain Cold's cold gun in it — decommissioned, behind glass, with a placard that reads: "The Rogues. Central City's most organized criminal outfit. Notable for their strict code of conduct and their unexpected role in the city's civic stability."

Captain Cold, if he ever visited, would probably nod at this placard and think it was about right.

He would not say so out loud.

But he would probably nod.
