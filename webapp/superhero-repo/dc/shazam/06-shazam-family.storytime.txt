When Billy Batson first became Shazam, he thought the great gift the Wizard had given him was the lightning.

The strength of Hercules. The wisdom of Solomon. The speed of Mercury. The thunder, the bolt, the booming SHAZAM that turned a small lonely boy into a grown-up champion in a red suit.

He thought THAT was the gift.

He was wrong.

The real gift came later, in a small two-story house in Philadelphia, where a kind couple named Victor and Rosa Vasquez had taken Billy in as their foster child. The house was full to bursting. There were five other foster kids living there, all of them, like Billy, looking for a family that would not give them up.

There was Freddy Freeman, who walked with a crutch and could rattle off the powers of every superhero who had ever existed. There was Mary Bromfield, the oldest, with her steady eyes and her habit of putting band-aids on everyone's scrapes. There was Darla Dudley, the very smallest, who was made entirely out of giggles and glitter. There was Eugene Choi, who could fix any computer in three minutes flat. And there was Pedro Pena, the quiet strong one, who hugged you without saying a word when you needed it.

For a long time, Billy held himself apart from them. He was used to running. He was used to being alone. Family, he had decided long ago, was something other people had.

But the Vasquezes were patient. The other kids were patient. They saved him a seat at dinner every night, even when he did not come down. They left the porch light on for him. They waited.

Slowly, Billy began to thaw.

And then one terrible day, a battle came to Philadelphia that was too big for Billy to fight alone. Surrounded by enemies, with his foster siblings in danger, Billy did something that the Wizard had whispered to him might one day be possible.

He grabbed his siblings' hands. He looked them in the eyes. And he shared the lightning.

KRA-KOW.

The bolt came down again, but this time it did not strike only Billy. It struck all six of them.

When the light cleared, six champions stood in the street. Six grown-up heroes in bright crackling suits. Each one of them wore a slightly different color, but the same golden lightning bolt blazed on every chest.

The Shazam Family was born.

And they were beautiful.

Each one of them brought something different. Freddy, in his blue suit, was the fastest thinker — quick with a quip, quicker with a tactical idea. Mary, in her gold, was the moral anchor — calm, certain, the second-in-command Billy could always lean on. Darla, in her violet, was the family's pure joy — fast as Mercury, hugging EVERYONE she met. Eugene, in his green, kept the technology side of every mission humming. Pedro, in his red, was the strongest of them all — quiet, immovable, the wall behind which his siblings could always find shelter.

Together they were more than the sum of their parts.

But it was not just the powers that made them special.

When they transformed, something invisible happened too. A psychic bond opened between them. They could feel each other's hearts. They could sense each other's fears. They could coordinate without speaking, finishing each other's sentences and each other's punches. They were not just a team. They were a FAMILY, fighting for one another, drawing strength from one another, never letting each other fall.

Outside of battle, they were still just kids in a Philadelphia row house. They sat around the dinner table eating Rosa's homemade enchiladas. They fought over the bathroom in the morning. Freddy stole the last of the cereal. Darla left her glitter pens uncapped. Pedro fell asleep on the couch with the TV on. And in between, they did homework, and they laughed, and they made each other feel safe.

Billy, who had spent his whole young life walking through the city alone, finally had a place where someone always saved him a seat.

In times of crisis, when the people of Philadelphia looked up at the sky and saw six bright figures streaking across the clouds — red and blue and gold and violet and green and yellow — they felt a kind of hope they had not felt before. This was not one lonely hero. This was a FAMILY of heroes. A family that fought because they loved each other.

They had faced ancient magical threats. They had faced corporate villains. They had faced the Seven Deadly Sins themselves. And every time, they had won, because together they were unbreakable.

Late one evening, after a long day of school and a longer night of saving the city, Billy sat on the front porch of the Vasquez house in his pajamas. The street was quiet. The fireflies blinked in the yard.

The screen door creaked open. Mary sat down beside him with two mugs of hot chocolate. Then came Freddy, scooting to the other side. Then Darla, climbing into Billy's lap. Then Eugene, then Pedro. Soon all six of them were squeezed onto the porch step like a family of slightly tired puppies.

Billy looked at them. His eyes shone.

He was Earth's Mightiest Mortal. He could call down lightning. He could fly. He could lift a truck.

But the greatest power he had ever been given was sitting all around him in pajamas, holding cocoa, calling him brother.

The Shazam Family was home.

And Billy Batson, finally, had one.
