Long before Billy Batson, before the Rock of Eternity, before the lightning that turns a boy into a champion, there was a council of seven Wizards.

They were the very first guardians of magic. They sat together on stone thrones in the deepest places of the world, and they kept the balance between light and shadow. They were not gods. They were something else — older, quieter, wiser than any king.

One by one, over the long centuries, the other six Wizards passed away. Until at last there was only one left.

His name was Shazam.

He sat alone on his stone throne for three thousand years.

Three thousand years.

Imagine that. Imagine sitting in one chamber, lit only by torches that never burn out, listening to the slow tick of the cosmos, while empires rose and fell on the surface of the world above. While oceans changed shape. While stars wandered slightly in the sky. The Wizard Shazam saw all of it from his stone seat, his white beard growing longer and longer, his eyes growing bluer and quieter.

He was waiting. He was waiting for the right child.

When Billy Batson walked into his chamber for the first time, the Wizard knew it had been worth the wait.

The Wizard did not choose Billy because Billy was strong. He chose Billy because Billy was GOOD. Good in the small ways. Good when no one was watching. Good when life had given him every reason to be hard.

"Power without goodness destroys itself," the Wizard told him on that first day. "Goodness without power cannot protect what matters. You have goodness. I am offering you the power."

And so the lightning came. SHAZAM. And the Wizard, his long vigil complete, faded into spirit.

But here is the wonderful part. The Wizard did not go away.

His body was gone. But his spirit, his voice, his ancient wisdom — those stayed at the Rock of Eternity. And whenever Billy needed advice, he could return to the great chamber, sit down on the old stones, and speak with him.

Billy goes often.

Sometimes it is for serious things. A magical threat Billy does not understand. An ancient artifact someone is trying to use for evil. A dispute between deities who are about to come to blows. In those moments, Billy sits cross-legged before the empty stone throne and speaks aloud, and the Wizard's voice answers, softly, from everywhere at once.

The Wizard rarely gives Billy direct answers. He speaks in riddles. In parables. In long slow questions that send Billy thinking for days. "What does the river do when it meets a stone, Billy?" he might ask. Or, "What is heavier — a sword, or the choice not to use it?"

Billy used to be frustrated by this. Now he understands. The Wizard does not want Billy to memorize answers. The Wizard wants Billy to BECOME the kind of person who finds his own answers.

Sometimes Billy comes for smaller things, too.

When he and Freddy have argued about something silly and he cannot tell who was right, Billy might come to the Wizard. When he is worried about a school assignment, or about Mary going off to college, or about the way the new kid in his class always sits alone at lunch — Billy comes. He sits down. He talks. And the old voice listens, never judging, never hurrying him.

The Wizard tells him stories from the past, too. About the other six Wizards. About the early champions who held the lightning before him. About long-ago battles against shadows older than memory. Each story has a lesson tucked inside it like a candy in a wrapper.

He also speaks honestly about his own life. About the long lonely vigil. About the mistakes he made — and yes, even ancient Wizards make mistakes. About the times he doubted whether he could keep going. About the joy he found, in the end, when at last he saw Billy step into his chamber.

This honesty has built a deep trust between the Wizard and Billy. They are mentor and student. But they are also, in a quieter way, friends. And in some ways, the Wizard is a kind of grandfather Billy never had.

Billy is not the only one who benefits from the Wizard's wisdom. Through Billy, that wisdom flows out into the world. Into Mary's careful judgments. Into Freddy's strategies. Into Darla's bright kindness. Into every decision the Shazam Family makes. The Wizard's voice ripples outward, like a stone dropped into a wide quiet pond, touching shores he himself will never see.

Late at night, when the Rock of Eternity is silent and the torches burn low, Billy sometimes sits alone in the great chamber. The Seven Sins moan softly in their stone prisons. The cosmos hums beyond the walls.

Billy looks up at the empty throne. He whispers, "Goodnight, old friend."

And from somewhere not quite anywhere, a soft voice answers, "Goodnight, Champion."

Then Billy stands. He pulls his red cape around his shoulders. And he flies home to Philadelphia, carrying three thousand years of wisdom inside the heart of a single brave boy.

The architect of the Champion sleeps now.

His legacy never will.
