Long before Billy Batson ever shouted "SHAZAM!" for the first time, there was another champion. His name was Teth-Adam, and he came from the ancient land of Kahndaq thousands of years ago. The Wizard chose him first — gave him divine lightning, the strength of gods, the speed of the heavens. And for a while, Adam used it to protect his people.

But then Adam lost his family. And the grief crept in, and the power grew heavy, and kindness slowly hardened into something cold and unyielding. By the time the world knew him as Black Adam, his idea of justice had become: the strong rule, the weak obey, and no one questions the champion of Kahndaq.

When Billy Batson received the Wizard's gift, Black Adam was furious. He considered Billy an unworthy child pretending to hold a power that was rightfully his. Their battles sent lightning crashing across the sky, shaking the very world beneath their feet.

And yet. There were moments when Adam remembered who he once was. Moments when a greater threat arose and he stood — briefly, reluctantly — on the side of the right. Those moments told Billy that somewhere under all that anger, the good man who had once fought for Kahndaq was still there.

And do you know what Shazam did? He never stopped hoping Adam could find his way back.

From that day on, Billy Batson understood the heaviest lesson a hero can learn: that power without compassion does not protect people. It only isolates them.
