Long before Billy Batson ever shouted "SHAZAM!" for the first time, there was another champion. His name was Teth-Adam, and he came from the ancient land of Kahndaq thousands upon thousands of years ago!

The Wizard chose Teth-Adam first. He gave him divine power drawn from Egyptian gods — Shu, Heru, Amon, Zehuti, Aton, and Mehen — and Adam used it to protect his people. He was magnificent! A true champion of his age!

But then something terrible happened. Adam lost his family. The grief sank in, heavy as stone. The power he carried grew darker with it. Kindness hardened slowly into something cold and fierce. By the time the world knew him as Black Adam, his idea of justice had become: the strong rule, the weak obey, and no one questions me!

When Billy Batson received the Wizard's gift, Black Adam was absolutely furious. This boy? THIS child? An unworthy pretender holding what was rightfully his! Their battles were ENORMOUS — CRACK! BOOM! LIGHTNING CRASHING LIGHTNING! — the very ground shaking beneath them, reality crackling under the force of two champion-level powers colliding!

But here is the remarkable thing: sometimes — in the middle of all that anger and conflict — a greater threat would arise. And Black Adam would stand, briefly, reluctantly, on the side of right. He was fiercely protective of Kahndaq and its people. That fierce love was still real.

Those moments told Billy something important: somewhere under all that ancient anger, the good man who had once fought for his people was still there.

And Billy never stopped hoping he might find his way back.

Power without compassion protects no one. It only isolates you — further and further — from everything you ever loved.
