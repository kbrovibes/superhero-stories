Deep, deep beneath the streets of Gotham City — past the cave walls where bats sleep in upside-down rows, past the underground pools that shimmer blue-black in the darkness, past the display cases of suits and gadgets that Batman has worn over the years — there is a room filled with light.

Not sunlight. Not moonlight. The clean, cool glow of a thousand screens.

This is the Bat-Computer, and this is where Barbara Gordon does some of her most important work.

Can you picture it? An enormous curved screen that stretches from floor to ceiling, split into dozens of panels — some showing maps of Gotham City in real time, some running lines of code like waterfalls of glowing green text, some displaying weather satellites and police radio frequencies all at once. The keyboard beneath it is the length of a dining table. The hum of the processors fills the air like a low, steady song.

Barbara settles into the chair and the screens light up for her. She could be anywhere in the world right now — any database, any satellite feed, any locked filing system in any government archive. The Bat-Computer is connected to everything.

Tonight she has a case.

Three banks in the Gotham Financial District reported unusual chemical signatures in their ventilation systems. Not dangerous — not yet — but unusual. The kind of chemical signature that suggests someone has been testing something. Planning something.

A regular detective would take days to work through the evidence. Barbara takes forty-seven minutes.

She cross-references the chemical compound against every research paper published in the last twenty years. She maps the delivery dates of the chemical precursors against the shipping logs of three chemical suppliers. She overlays the bank locations on a map of Gotham and draws lines between them. The lines form a triangle. The center of the triangle falls on a warehouse in the East End.

She pulls up the warehouse records. Leased six months ago. Payment in cash. The lessee's name leads to a dummy company. She digs through four more layers of shell corporations and finds, at the very bottom, a name she recognizes.

She sends the file to Batman and Robin with a three-line summary at the top.

By the time Batman reaches the warehouse, Batgirl has already mapped its interior layout from the city's drainage schematics, identified two exits he did not know about, and flagged a secondary security system that would have triggered an alarm.

The Bat-Computer also trains her.

On nights when no cases are pressing, Barbara runs simulations — virtual Gotham City scenarios that test her reflexes and her decisions. A building on fire; which civilians to evacuate first? A getaway car with three possible routes; which does she intercept? A hostage scenario with four variables changing simultaneously. She runs each scenario until she solves it, then runs it again with a new variable.

She has completed every simulation the computer offers. So she writes new ones.

She writes a simulation involving an earthquake and a flooding river and a power outage, all at the same time, just to test herself. She fails it six times. On the seventh attempt, she solves it with twelve minutes to spare. She saves the scenario for Batman and Robin to try.

Batman runs it once. He calls her afterward and says, very quietly, "That was hard."

Barbara smiles and says, "I know."

The Bat-Computer holds every case file from every mission Batman has ever run — hundreds of them, going back years. Barbara has read all of them. She studies the patterns: which villains are escalating, which ones are quieter than usual (often a bad sign), which criminal networks have connections that cross borough lines. She builds a prediction model — a map of what might happen next, based on what has happened before.

This is the kind of work you cannot see from the outside. Nobody cheers for it. But it is the work that keeps people safe.

On quiet nights, when the city is still and no alarms are ringing, Barbara pulls up the ancient history files she keeps in a private folder — Roman aqueducts, medieval cryptography, the logic systems of early computing pioneers — and learns something entirely new, just because knowing things is one of her greatest pleasures.

She once told Robin that knowledge is like a utility belt. Every new skill you learn is another tool in the compartment. The more tools you carry, the more problems you can solve.

Robin said, "Can I borrow your simulation?"

She said, "I already sent it to you."

He failed it eight times. He has not told her yet. But she already knows.

That is the thing about being very, very good at reading patterns. You always know more than people think you do.

The Bat-Computer glows. The maps shift and scroll. And somewhere in the Batcave, deep beneath Gotham City, Barbara Gordon is thinking three steps ahead of every villain in town.
