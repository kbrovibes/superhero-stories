The night that changed everything did not happen on a rooftop. It happened in the quiet of a living room, and when it was over, Barbara Gordon found herself staring at the ceiling of a hospital room with a heart that felt very still.

The doctors were kind, but the news was hard. She would not walk again. The flips, the chases, the adrenaline of the hunt — all of it seemed like a distant dream. For weeks, Barbara sat in her wheelchair and felt the weight of the silence pressing down.

But then, something shifted. If she could not be the hands and feet of the mission, she could be its mind. She could be the eye in the sky — the invisible force that turned the tide before the first punch was thrown.

And do you know what she built? She called herself Oracle. She tapped into Gotham's police databases, then into global satellite networks, then into encrypted frequencies that criminals thought were hidden forever. She became the voice in every hero's ear during the most dangerous moments. Nightwing. Batman. The entire Justice League. They all relied on her steady, calm guidance.

From her clocktower sanctuary, Barbara watched over Gotham day and night. Her father, Commissioner Gordon, began finding detailed reports on his desk — faster, better, more precise than anything his officers could produce. He never knew his own daughter was sending them.

From that day on, Barbara Gordon proved that a hero's strength is not measured by the ability to stand, but by the resolve to keep fighting.
