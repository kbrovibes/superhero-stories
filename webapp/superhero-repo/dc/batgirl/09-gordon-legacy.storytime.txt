Before Barbara Gordon was Batgirl, before she was Oracle, before she could even reach the top shelf of the bookshelf in her bedroom, she was a Gordon.

And in Gotham City, being a Gordon meant something.

Her father, James Gordon — Jim, to his friends — was the Police Commissioner. He wore a long brown coat and round glasses, and he kept his pipe in his pocket because his wife had asked him to stop smoking it inside. He was steady. He was patient. He was the kind of man who never, ever, ever gave up, even when Gotham seemed to be falling apart all around him.

Little Barbara grew up watching her father from the front porch. She watched him leave for work before the sun came up. She watched him come home long after she should have been asleep. Sometimes she would sit on the top of the stairs in her pajamas and listen to him on the telephone, his voice low and tired, talking about the city he loved.

He taught her many things, but the most important lesson was this. The law, he said, is a tool. But justice — real justice — is a choice you make every single day. You choose it again in the morning. You choose it again at night. You never stop choosing it.

Barbara remembered those words when she became Batgirl. She remembered them again when she became Oracle. They were the steady ground beneath everything she did.

The Gordon legacy, you see, was not about the badge on her father's chest. It was not about the cape on Barbara's back. It was about a stubborn, glowing thing inside the human heart that says: I will not give up. I will not give up. I will not give up.

There was one terrible time in Gotham, called No Man's Land, when an earthquake cracked the city in half and the rest of the country closed its bridges and said, "We cannot help you." Gotham was on its own. The lights went out. The streets fell silent. Bad people came crawling out of the dark.

And in those long, hungry months, Barbara — as Oracle, in her clocktower — became her father's secret partner. He walked the broken streets in his long coat, holding the city together with sheer will. She whispered in his ear, slipped him maps, warned him of danger before it arrived. She never told him it was her. He never asked who his guardian angel was. But sometimes, walking home through the rubble, he would look up at the rooftops and smile, just a little.

They were two sides of the same coin, the public hand and the hidden hand, both reaching for the same thing.

But the legacy was heavy, too. Barbara worried about her father every single day. He had been hurt before. He had been kidnapped. He had been targeted by the worst monsters in Gotham simply because he refused to give up. And Barbara, in her secret life, knew that her own choices could put him in even more danger. That fear sat in her chest like a small cold stone.

She kept choosing the work anyway. Because she was a Gordon. And Gordons did not stop.

Sometimes Barbara found herself standing in between two worlds — the world of vigilantes in capes, and the world of police officers in uniforms. Batman did things Jim Gordon could never officially approve of. The GCPD did things Batman did not always trust. Barbara became the bridge between them, the diplomat of the shadows, gently making sure the two sides did not break each other.

One soft summer evening, Barbara sat with her father on the porch of his old house. Gotham hummed in the distance — the far-off sound of traffic, of sirens, of the great living city. They did not talk about secret identities. They did not talk about Oracle or Batman or any of the heavy things. They talked about the small good things. A neighbor's new puppy. A kid Jim had helped find his way home. A new book Barbara was reading.

Jim looked over at his daughter. He did not say anything. But his eyes were proud, the way they had been when she was tiny and had taken her first wobbly step.

Later, as Barbara wheeled herself down the ramp and pulled on her cowl for the night, she felt the full warmth of her heritage wrap around her like her father's old brown coat.

She was Barbara Gordon. Daughter. Hero. Keeper of the flame.

And she would carry it as far as her hands could reach.
