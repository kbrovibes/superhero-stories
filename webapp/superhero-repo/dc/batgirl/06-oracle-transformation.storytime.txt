The night that changed everything didn't happen on a rooftop. It didn't happen in the rain, with capes flying. It happened in a quiet living room, with a soft yellow lamp on, and a knock at the door.

Barbara Gordon was at home. She was reading. And then a stranger she should never have opened the door to was on the other side, and something sad and sudden happened, and the next thing Barbara knew, she was looking up at the white ceiling of a hospital room, listening to the slow steady beep of a heart monitor.

Beep. Beep. Beep.

The doctor sat beside her bed. He was very kind, and he held her hand, and he told her the truth as gently as a person can tell a hard truth. Barbara would not walk again. Her legs, which had carried her across so many rooftops as Batgirl — which had flipped over banisters and kicked open warehouse doors and chased criminals through the alleys of Gotham — her legs were going to be still now.

She closed her eyes for a long time.

Can you imagine, just for a moment, what it would feel like to wake up and discover that the body you trusted to do anything you asked it to could not do that thing anymore? Barbara felt it like a great quiet ocean filling up her chest.

For weeks after she came home, Barbara sat in her wheelchair by the window, watching Gotham glitter below her. She wanted to be sad — and she was sad — but Barbara Gordon had always been something else, too. She was clever. Brilliantly, fiercely clever. The kind of clever that solved a puzzle in her head before she even reached for a pencil.

And in those long quiet weeks, a new idea began to glow inside her, small at first and then brighter and brighter.

If her body could not run across the rooftops, her mind still could. Her mind could go anywhere. Her mind could fly through wires and satellites and computer screens faster than any cape ever could.

So Barbara got to work.

She built a console of glowing monitors in the top of an old clocktower, where the great gears turned slowly above her like a heartbeat. CLICK, CLACK went the clock. TAP, TAP, TAP went her fingers on the keys. She tapped into Gotham PD's databases. She listened to encrypted criminal radios. She wove herself into the spider's web of the entire city until she could feel every thread of it humming.

She chose a new name. Not Batgirl anymore. Something older. Something wiser. Someone who sees things others cannot see.

Oracle.

Oracle became the voice in every hero's ear. When Nightwing crept along the rooftops, it was Oracle who whispered, "Three guards on the left, two on the right, go now." When Batman needed a building's blueprints in the middle of a chase, it was Oracle who slipped them onto his screen before he asked. She was the eye in the sky, the brain behind the fists, the quiet certain voice that turned chaos into a plan.

One night, while she was guiding Nightwing through a tricky heist down at the Gotham docks, Barbara felt something she had not felt in a very long time. She felt joy. Pure, bright, sparkling joy. She had picked the exit route, slipped past every security camera, and alerted the police all in the space of one breath.

She realized something then, sitting alone in her glowing clocktower with the gears turning and the city humming. She was doing MORE good now than she ever had as Batgirl. Her injury had not been the end of her story. It had been the beginning of a bigger one.

Down in the streets below, her father, Commissioner Jim Gordon, walked into his precinct and found a perfect dossier sitting on his desk. He did not know who had left it. He looked up at the rooftops and smiled. Whoever this Oracle was, he thought, the city was lucky to have her. He did not know it was his own daughter. Barbara watched him from her monitors and felt a soft, secret pride bloom in her heart.

The sun began to rise over Gotham. Long pink shadows stretched across her monitors. Barbara took a deep breath and looked out at the waking city.

She was Oracle. The light in the darkness. The truth in the lies. The heartbeat of a city that never slept.

And she was just getting started.
