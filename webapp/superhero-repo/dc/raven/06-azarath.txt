Azarath existed in a dimension parallel to Earth, separated by barriers that ordinary physics could not explain. The dimension was ancient — shaped over centuries by the meditation of its people into a place of extraordinary quiet. The towers were white and smooth, built of stone that seemed to absorb light. The water between them was deep silver. The sky was a gentle blue-grey that never quite committed to sunrise or sunset.

Raven remembered Azarath with the particular sharpness of someone who left home before they were quite ready. The smell of the incense in the meditation halls. The sound of the bells the monks rang at the beginning and end of each day. The library, which was three stories of scrolls and hand-copied texts and maps of dimensions that had no names in any language Raven had found on Earth. Her mother Arella had a room that smelled of lavender and old paper, and she kept the window open so that the silver water below made a soft sound against the tower walls at night.

The central meditation hall of Azarath was a circular room with no walls — only columns, and between the columns, open air above the water. The monks of Azarath sat there for hours each morning, not speaking, simply practicing the stillness that was their most important teaching. Raven had learned to sit with them from the time she was very small. At first it was difficult — her mind wandered, she felt impatient, she was distracted by the emotions of all the monks sitting around her, each one a small warm signal of focused peace. But over time the practice settled into her body. She carried it with her everywhere. It lived in her breathing.

When Raven left Azarath to join the Teen Titans in Jump City, she brought two physical things with her: her dark blue cloak, which had been woven in Azarath from a fabric that helped her focus her power, and a single small book of Azarathian meditations that she kept on her nightstand in Titans Tower. She read from it most evenings. Starfire once picked it up curiously and found that the text shifted language as she looked at it, appearing in Tamaranian for a moment before settling back into Azarathian. She set it down very carefully.

Azarath was not gone. It was simply far. Raven kept it with her in the ways that mattered: in her breathing, in her steadiness, in the calm she brought to every hard moment. The monks had taught her that a place lived in you long after you left it. She had found that to be exactly true.
