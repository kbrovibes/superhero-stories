There was a new Titan for a few weeks: a girl named Maya who had just discovered she could move small objects with her mind. She was twelve years old, easily startled, and deeply embarrassed — her power activated whenever she felt nervous, and she felt nervous constantly. Books fell off shelves. Juice boxes exploded. A full glass of water once levitated to the ceiling during dinner. Robin was tactful. Starfire was enthusiastically kind. Beast Boy's fascination made things worse. Cyborg's instruments also made things worse.

Raven asked to speak with Maya in the library.

She made two cups of tea — chamomile, from the tin she kept on the second shelf — and set one in front of Maya and one in front of herself and didn't say anything immediately. Maya sat very still, trying not to be nervous, which made her more nervous, which made the teaspoon next to her cup lift slightly off the table and then clatter back down.

"That happens to me too," Raven said.

Maya looked at her.

"Not with teaspoons anymore," Raven said. "But when I was your age, in Azarath, I once moved every book on an entire shelf simultaneously because I heard something startling outside the window." She paused. "It was a bird."

Something in Maya's face shifted — the particular release that happens when you realize someone else has actually been where you are.

Raven explained, carefully and without making a production of it, what she had learned: that the power would keep getting stronger whether she controlled it or not, so the question was not how to make it stop but how to learn its language. That the feeling underneath an uncontrolled power surge was always something specific — not "nervous" in general but one particular thing, and if she could find that particular thing she could start to work with it rather than against it. That this took time and practice and was not a problem to be fixed but a skill to be built.

Maya listened. She asked two questions. Raven answered both.

They worked together, that afternoon and on several afternoons that followed, on exactly the practice the monks of Azarath had taught Raven: witnessing. Noticing. Breathing through. Before Maya left to go back to her own city, three weeks later, she could make a small object rise and hold steady in the air for nearly a minute. She was not finished learning. She was started.

At the door, Maya said thank you. Raven said, without any ceremony, "The people who helped me were patient. I'm returning that." She went back inside. In the library, her teacup was where she'd left it, and her book was waiting.
