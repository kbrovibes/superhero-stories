The alarm in Titans Tower sounded at 2:47 in the morning. An entity called the Dissonance — a being of pure hostile emotion, built from centuries of accumulated bitterness — had materialized in Jump City's power grid and was growing. It sent waves of raw, directionless overwhelm through anyone nearby: not any one emotion, just a churning storm that made people freeze, argue, and forget what they cared about.

The Titans arrived at the substation within minutes. Cyborg's instruments went haywire — the entity scrambled every electronic system it touched. Beast Boy flew overhead as an eagle, caught a wave of the Dissonance's output, and landed hard on the roof shaking his head. Starfire's starbolts dissolved before reaching the center. Robin adapted and adapted, and nothing worked.

Raven stood very still and studied it. Vast, loud, hostile — but no center. She had read about entities like this in one of Azarath's oldest scrolls. The ancient monks had written their advice in a short, careful passage: they were not defeated by force but by contrast. You did not match the noise. You did not fight it on its own terms. You provided something it had no framework for.

She sent the empathic signal her team had learned to recognize over months of fighting together: gather. Trust me. The Titans came to her. She asked one thing: feel something real. Specific. Genuinely yours.

Robin thought about Gotham City, and the night he decided what kind of hero he would be. Starfire thought about Tamaran, her home star, the way it looked from space. Cyborg thought about his father's workbench and the smell of machine oil. Beast Boy thought about a sunset from the roof of the tower on a very ordinary, very good evening.

Raven took each of those clear and genuine feelings and wove them together with her own: the quiet of Azarath, the silver water, the bells. She sent the whole thing outward into the Dissonance — not a blast, but a presence. Real emotion meeting formless noise.

The Dissonance faltered. Then it collapsed inward. It was over in four minutes. The power grid hummed back to steady.

The five of them sat on the substation roof in the dark and nobody said much of anything, and it was the most comfortable silence Raven could remember in a long time.
