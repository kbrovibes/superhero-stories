Once upon a time, there was a girl who could feel everything.

Not her own everything — though she felt that too. Everyone's everything. The feelings of every person nearby, arriving as clearly as sound or light, as constant and real and uninvited as weather.

This was Raven's empathy.

For most people, empathy is something practiced: you try to imagine how another person might feel, and you improve with time. For Raven, it was more like an extra sense — something she was born with, impossible to turn off, arriving as constantly as sound or light. When she was small in Azarath and the monks sat in the morning circle, she felt each of them: steady, warm, focused. Twelve calm flames ringing her on every side.

The monks had a word for what she needed to learn: witnessing. It meant being fully present to another's experience — truly feeling it — while staying yourself. Keeping your own footing. Like learning to swim without drowning: you had to be in the water, but you had to know you were also you.

She practiced witnessing for years, held the thread of herself gently like a kite string, and learned from every mistake.

By the time she arrived in Jump City and began living with the Teen Titans, she had become very good at it.

She felt them all, always. Robin's disciplined focus was the sharpest, the most precise — a single clean note, like a tuning fork struck once. Cyborg's emotions were wide and warm, the kind of warmth that filled a room without effort, practical and generous. Beast Boy was — Raven had thought about how to describe this many times and had arrived at: a pinball machine that was very enthusiastic about everything. Bright, bouncing, genuinely delighted by most things, occasionally crashing into the walls of whatever mood he was in with great energy. Starfire was like standing near a sun that had decided to be gentle: open, radiating, honestly glad.

She held all of them, every day, at once.

It made her perceptive in ways that surprised her teammates until they grew accustomed to it. She often knew when a mission was going wrong before the comms announced it — she could feel the small cracks in a person's focus before the evidence appeared. She knew when Robin was overstretched, when Cyborg was carrying something he hadn't said yet, when Beast Boy's brightness had a hollow edge that meant he needed company more than he needed space.

She rarely said any of this aloud. That was not how she worked.

What she did instead was appear, quietly, at the right moment.

A word, offered softly from a page away. A presence, sitting nearby without announcement. Once — during a planning session that had run far too long and in which Robin was making increasingly precise gestures that Raven had learned meant he was tired and about to be too stubborn to admit it — a mug of chamomile tea, set on the table beside him without comment. He had looked at it. Then at her. She had already returned to her book.

He had drunk the tea. He had reconsidered the decision that had been circling in his head. He had made a better one.

He had never thanked her for the tea specifically. He had once said, on a different day entirely, looking out at the city from the tower roof: "I'm glad you're on this team, Raven." She had stood beside him and said nothing, which was, for both of them, perfectly sufficient.

This was the thing that surprised people about Raven, when they got to know her well enough to be surprised: she was extraordinarily kind.

Not in the ways that announce themselves. She would not rush to embrace you when you were sad. She would not say "oh, poor you" or offer you cheerful advice or try to make the feeling go away. She had learned, from years of feeling feelings, that feelings did not want to be made to go away. They wanted to be known.

So she would sit nearby. Not right beside you — just nearby. Settled. Not looking at you directly but not pretending you weren't there. Present. Steady. A quiet signal in the air that said: I know. I am here. You are not alone in this.

Her teammates had learned, over time, to recognize this. And to treasure it.

Beast Boy once said, flopped across the couch one evening after a hard day, "Raven, you're doing the thing."

"What thing," she said, without looking up from her book.

"The thing where you're making the room feel better without doing anything."

She turned a page.

"Go to sleep, Beast Boy," she said.

He was asleep in four minutes — the most comfortable sleep he'd had in a week.

That was Raven's empathy: not a power to display, but a warmth to give. Quietly, steadily, without needing anyone to notice, from the steady place she'd been building since she was small.

It was, perhaps, the most extraordinary thing about her.
