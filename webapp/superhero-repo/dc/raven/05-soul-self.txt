Of all Raven's abilities, the one that surprised people most was not telekinesis — moving objects without touching them was something they could at least describe. It was her soul-self. When Raven summoned it, a shape of deep black-violet energy would rise from her and take wing, enormous and silent, built like a raven with a wingspan wider than a bus. It was not a projection. It was not a trick. It was genuinely part of her, the visible form of her inner self, sent out into the world as a separate entity that could act while she remained standing still.

The soul-self could fly ahead to scout a threat, moving through locked doors and barriers as though they were smoke. It could wrap itself around something and carry it, gently or forcefully depending on what the moment required. It could shield teammates from harm by forming a barrier of living shadow around them. It could reach into enclosed spaces — a collapsed building, a sealed underground room — and bring someone out to safety. In Azarath, the practice of sending forth a soul-self was considered an ancient and serious discipline, something that took years of meditation to develop. Raven had been working at it since she was small.

The soul-self knew things Raven knew. It felt what she felt. When Raven was troubled, the soul-self moved differently — more cautiously, with a kind of restless sweep. When she was determined, it flew straight and fast. Once, during a long and difficult mission, Cyborg noticed that the soul-self had settled quietly over the entire team, extending its shadow like a wide, protective canopy, and he realized that Raven had been worrying about all of them and had done this without making a sound about it.

She kept the soul-self inside most of the time, folded within herself, felt but not seen. Letting it out fully required trust — trust that the situation called for it and trust in the steadiness of her own feelings. There was a limit: the more emotionally turbulent she became, the harder the soul-self was to control precisely. She had learned that balance by practice rather than rules, the way a musician learns to hold a bow.

Starfire once asked Raven what it felt like to fly inside the soul-self, to be the bird-shape rather than the girl. Raven thought about this for longer than most people would expect. "It feels like being entirely yourself," she said, "with nowhere to hide and no reason to." Starfire said that sounded beautiful. Raven agreed that it was.
