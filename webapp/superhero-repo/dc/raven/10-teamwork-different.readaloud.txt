This story is about someone new. Her name is Maya, she is twelve years old, and she is having the WORST superpower week of her entire life.

She had just discovered she could move objects with her mind. Which will be wonderful — but right now it activates every single time she gets nervous. And Maya is nervous A LOT.

CLATTER — a book fell off the shelf at breakfast for no reason. PSSSSHHH — a juice box at lunch. And during dinner? A FULL GLASS OF WATER slowly levitated all the way up to the ceiling while everyone at the table watched in silence.

The team did their best. Robin said several encouraging things. Starfire said it was "magnificently extraordinary" and gave three hugs. Cyborg brought instruments, which made Maya more nervous, which made the saltshaker levitate, and then he put the instruments away. Beast Boy turned into a puppy and ran in a circle, which helped precisely nobody.

Maya looked like she very much wished she could become invisible — which, thankfully, was not one of her powers.

Then Raven appeared in the doorway.

"Maya. Come have tea."

In the library: two cups of chamomile. Raven sat down and said nothing for a moment. Maya tried SO hard not to be nervous — which made her MORE nervous — and the teaspoon lifted and clattered.

"That happens to me too," Raven said.

Maya stared. "Really?"

"Not teaspoons anymore," Raven said. "But when I was your age, in Azarath, I moved every book on an entire shelf simultaneously. All forty of them." She paused. "Because I heard something outside the window."

Maya waited.

"It was a bird," said Raven.

And Maya laughed — surprised right out of her — and something in her face went from tight to loose, like a knot finally giving way.

Raven explained: the power would keep growing either way. So the question wasn't how to stop it, but how to learn its language. Find the specific thought underneath a surge — not "nervous" in general, but the one particular thought — name it, breathe through it. Witness it. This was what the monks of Azarath had taught Raven. Now she taught Maya.

They worked that afternoon. And the next. And the next.

Three weeks later, Maya held a small stone perfectly still in the air for fifty-four seconds. Then set it gently down. Her smile could have lit the whole tower.

At the door: "Thank you."

Raven: "The people who helped me were patient. I'm returning that."

That is what kindness looks like, passed from person to person — across years and miles and dimensions — landing exactly where it needs to.
