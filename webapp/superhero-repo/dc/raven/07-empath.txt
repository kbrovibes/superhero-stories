Empathy, for most people, is a skill: the ability to imagine how someone else might be feeling. For Raven, it was something closer to a sense organ — as involuntary and constant as hearing. When she walked through a crowded street in Jump City, she did not choose to feel the emotions around her any more than she chose to hear the traffic or smell the food carts. They arrived. The businesswoman hurrying to a meeting carried a hum of anxious focus; the child on the corner waiting for a light carried a bubble of impatient happiness; the elderly man outside the bookshop carried a long, slow contentment that was one of the pleasanter things to absorb.

Raven had learned early that this ability required boundaries. Not emotional walls — she was not cold or unfeeling — but the careful kind of attention that lets you notice something without drowning in it. The monks of Azarath called this witnessing: being fully present to another's experience without losing your own footing. She practiced it every day. When she sat in Titans Tower with her team, she felt them all: Robin's disciplined focus, Cyborg's warm practicality, Beast Boy's bright and frequently chaotic energy, Starfire's open-hearted gladness. She had stopped trying to tune any of it out. Instead she had learned to hold it all at once, the way a musician in an ensemble hears their own instrument clearly while also hearing the whole.

The empathy made her a remarkably perceptive teammate. She often knew when a mission was going wrong before the data made it obvious, because she could feel the shift in focus when something unexpected happened to one of her friends. She had a quiet habit of appearing with exactly the right thing at the right moment — a word, a presence, once a mug of chamomile tea for Robin during a very long strategy session that left her certain he was going to make a decision he'd regret — that she never announced or explained.

Her empathy was also the source of something that surprised people: Raven was deeply kind. Not in a loud way, not in the way that announces itself. She would not be the one to sweep you into a hug when you were sad, though she understood exactly how sad you were. She would be the one to sit nearby, at an angle, not quite looking at you, letting you know without saying it that the feeling was known, that you were not alone in it, that someone steady was right there. Her teammates had learned, over time, that this was its own kind of warmth. Sometimes it was the best kind.
