Once upon a time, in the middle of the night, the alarm in Titans Tower went off.

It was 2:47 in the morning. Outside, Jump City was dark and quiet. Inside, five heroes pulled on boots and cloaks — or in Raven's case, who had not quite been asleep anyway, simply rose and went.

The alarm was for something called the Dissonance.

Raven knew the term from Azarath's oldest scrolls: an entity built not from flesh or machine but from accumulated feeling. Centuries of bitterness pooled and coiled until it achieved a kind of sentience — directionless hostility that wanted nothing except to persist and grow. This one had found Jump City's power grid. It was feeding.

The Titans arrived in minutes. The entity was a distortion in the air above the transformer banks, crackling and reaching like something alive. Cyborg's instruments went dark immediately — the Dissonance scrambled every electronic system it touched. He switched to manual, jaw set. Beast Boy became an eagle and rose above it, caught a wave of the entity's output — raw, directionless overwhelm, like standing inside a feeling with no name — and landed back on the roof shaking his head. "That's a lot," he said. Starfire fired three starbolts, bright and fierce. They dissolved before arrival. Robin moved along the perimeter, reading the pattern. He threw three sequential strikes and each vanished without effect. The entity had no skeleton. No point of leverage. You could not grapple weather.

Raven had been standing still since they arrived. This was not inaction.

She was reading it with her empathy, moving through it slowly the way you move through a complicated piece of music — listening not for the noise but for what was underneath. What she found: enormous and loud and hostile — but no center. Noise without a source. It fed by absorbing frightened feelings nearby, amplifying them, and sending them back out again. A loop that could sustain itself indefinitely.

She had read about this in one of Azarath's oldest scrolls. The passage was short. Do not match the noise. Provide something it has no framework for. Something real. Something genuinely felt.

She sent the empathic signal — the one her teammates had learned to recognize over months of working together, wordless and warm: come here, trust me. They came. Even Cyborg, navigating with instruments dark. Even Robin, mid-strategy, who stopped and turned and walked back with the expression of someone placing complete trust in someone else.

They stood together on the rooftop.

"I need each of you to feel something real," Raven said quietly. "Something specific and true that actually belongs to you."

Robin closed his eyes. He thought about Gotham City and a decision he had made there years ago — the exact moment when he had decided what kind of person he would be. The feeling was sharp and clear and entirely his own.

Starfire thought about Tamaran, her home world, orbiting its warm golden star. The view of it from space, the last time she had seen it from that angle. Not sad. Just real, and hers.

Cyborg thought about his father's workbench — the smell of machine oil and metal shavings, the orderly rows of tools, the easy rhythm of Saturday mornings when he was small and nothing was complicated.

Beast Boy thought about the tower roof on an evening when the sky went pink and gold and then purple, and nothing particular was happening, and it was exactly enough.

Raven felt all four of them. She gathered those feelings alongside her own — the towers of Azarath, the silver water, the bells, the smell of incense, her mother's voice at the end of a long day — and sent all five outward into the Dissonance. Not a blast. Not a weapon. A presence. Real feeling, specific and sourced, moving like clean air into something that had never known what real felt like.

The Dissonance paused.

Then it came apart — the distortion cracking along invisible seams, the tendrils retracting, the crackling dying down until, all at once, the way bad weather sometimes lifts entirely, it was gone. Done. The power grid hummed back to steady. The city's lights held.

Four minutes.

The five Titans sat on the substation roof in the dark and quiet. No one said anything for a while. The city was below them, lights in every window, the bay just visible at the edge of the night. Raven sat slightly apart, as she usually did, but she let the thread of her teammates' feelings stay close: Robin's steady calm, Cyborg's warmth, Starfire's contented glow, Beast Boy — who had closed his eyes and was leaning against a ventilation pipe and appeared to be, improbably, already back asleep.

She let herself feel all of it. Fully. Without distance.

After a while, Cyborg said quietly, "Nice work, Rae."

Raven looked at the city.

"Nice work, all of us," she said.
