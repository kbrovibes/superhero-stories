Once upon a time, there was a place between the places.

It was not on any map in any library, not in any atlas or globe. It existed in a dimension parallel to Earth — close, in the way two pages of a book press together without quite touching. It had a name: Azarath. And if you knew how to listen, you could almost hear it.

Azarath was old. For centuries, the people who lived there had been practicing one thing together: stillness. They breathed slowly. They held their feelings gently. They found the quiet place inside themselves and lived from there. And over all those centuries, the practice had soaked into the place itself. Azarath had become what its people had tried to become.

It was peaceful.

White towers rose from water the color of old silver, deep and absolutely still. The sky above was a gentle blue-grey — not dark, not bright — the particular shade of a world that had decided not to rush. In the center stood a meditation hall open to the air: just columns, and between them, sky and water and morning light.

This is where Raven grew up.

Her mother Arella had a room with a window that caught the sound of the water at night. Arella was gentle and thoughtful — always with something warm to drink nearby, always listening carefully when Raven spoke, which made Raven want to speak carefully and truthfully. She had come to Azarath seeking safety and had found it among these quiet towers and patient monks. She raised Raven in the same spirit.

From the time she was very small, Raven sat in the morning circle with the monks. At first it was hard. Her mind wandered. She could feel all the monks' emotions around her like small warm candles — focused, peaceful, steady — and the contrast between their calm and her own busy thoughts made her fidget. She wanted to read. She wanted to explore the three-floor library with its ancient scrolls and its maps of dimensions that had no names in any language spoken on Earth. She wanted to ask questions.

The monks would simply look at her, softly, and breathe.

And slowly — not quickly, never quickly, but steadily and surely — Raven learned.

She learned that a feeling was not a command. She learned that her empathy became manageable when she held it with open hands rather than clutching at it. She learned that the most powerful thing she had was not any one ability but the steadiness underneath all of them, built breath by breath in the morning circle, year after year.

She carried all of that when she left.

She brought only two physical things: her dark blue cloak — woven in Azarath from a thread that helped focus her power, soft and familiar, smelling faintly of incense and silver water — and one thin book of Azarathian meditations, the same book the youngest monks received when they first began their practice. She kept it on her nightstand in Titans Tower and read from it most evenings before sleep.

Starfire found it once after a long mission. She picked it up curiously and opened it — and the text shifted, just for a moment, into Tamaranian, the language of Starfire's own home world. Her eyes went very wide. Then the text shifted back to the curling script of Azarath.

Starfire set it down very carefully with both hands, as though it were something alive.

She lasted about thirty seconds before finding Raven in the kitchen making tea. "Your book spoke to me in Tamaranian!"

"It does that," Raven said. "It appears in whatever language the reader needs."

"Does it know which language I need?"

"Apparently," said Raven, and took a sip.

Starfire sat at the kitchen table, her expression quite moved. "Tell me about Azarath," she said. "What was it like to grow up there?"

Raven held her tea in both hands. She thought about the white towers and the silver water and the sound at the base of the towers at night. She thought about her mother's warm room and the morning circle and the library that glowed faintly without any lights.

She told Starfire about all of it.

And far away — in that dimension between the dimensions, in the still water and the gentle blue-grey sky — Azarath kept its peace, exactly as it had always done, exactly as it had taught Raven to keep hers.
