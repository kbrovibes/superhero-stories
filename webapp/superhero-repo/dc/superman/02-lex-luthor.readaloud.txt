In the shadows of Metropolis's gleaming towers sat a very different kind of powerful man. His name was Lex Luthor, and he was possibly the cleverest human being alive. From the top floors of LexCorp he could see the whole city — and he did not like what he saw in the sky. He did not like Superman one bit.

Lex believed that humanity should reach its own greatness, on its own, without help from an alien from another planet. It felt to him like cheating. So he poured his enormous wealth and his brilliant mind into plans — always plans — to prove that Superman was not the hero everyone believed him to be. He built advanced weapons. He whispered stories to newspapers. He used his political influence like a chess piece, always moving, always calculating.

But here was the thing that always stopped Lex, the flaw buried inside his brilliant mind: he simply could not understand why a being with god-like powers would choose to help others and ask for nothing in return. He kept searching for the trick, for the hidden ambition. And it blinded him, every time.

And do you know what happened next? Superman looked at Lex not with anger, but with sadness. He could see what an incredible force for good that remarkable brain could be — if only it were pointed in the right direction. Superman refused to sink to Lex's level, no matter what schemes the man devised.

And that is how the great rivalry of Metropolis continued — not a battle of fists, but a battle between two visions of what the world could be. One man who had everything except humility, and one alien who had everything and gave it away freely.
