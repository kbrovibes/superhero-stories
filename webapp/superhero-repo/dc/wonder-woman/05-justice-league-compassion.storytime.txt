There is a crisis meeting happening in the Justice League Watchtower, and the air in the room is very tense.

Two heroes are disagreeing. Not about tactics — they disagree about tactics regularly and have worked out a functional system for it. They are disagreeing about something harder: about what to do with a person who has done something wrong, but not because they wanted to.

Batman thinks the answer is straightforward. Superman thinks the answer is more complicated. The Flash is watching them both and quietly wishing someone had brought snacks for these situations.

And then Diana speaks.

She speaks quietly, which is one of the things that makes people pay attention to her. She does not raise her voice to be heard — she lowers it. And the room settles.

"What are we actually arguing about?" she asks.

Batman and Superman both turn to look at her.

"We are arguing about what justice means," she says. "And before we decide what to do, we should agree on that."

This is what Diana brings to the Justice League that nobody else does quite as well: she asks the question before the question. She makes the team slow down — not in a way that wastes time, but in a way that saves it, because the decisions made in a hurry often need to be undone in a hurry, and Diana has three thousand years of Amazon wisdom telling her that the harder you push without thinking, the further you tend to go wrong.

Superman is the League's moral compass, the voice that reminds everyone what they are doing this for. Batman is the strategist, the one who never stops thinking three steps ahead. Diana is something else: the one who holds the ground between them. She is the reason the League doesn't split in two on the days when Superman's idealism and Batman's pragmatism feel like they are pulling in opposite directions.

She mediates. She heals. She holds things together the way a good knot holds rope together — not by pulling tighter, but by distributing the tension evenly.

She is also, when needed, the one who walks into the situation before anyone else has decided it's safe.

The crisis that day involved a coastal city in the grip of flooding that had been accelerated by forces not entirely natural. There were people on rooftops. There were people in attic windows. There were people in boats that were not rated for what the water was doing. The situation was bad and getting worse.

Diana was already in the air before the briefing ended — WHOOSH — heading straight for the city, her golden lasso trailing behind her in the wind. Below on the comm, she heard Batman say, "The cause of the flooding is still—"

"I know," she said. "I'll handle the people first. Tell me when you have the cause."

The Flash arrived alongside her a heartbeat later, looking cheerful in the way the Flash always looked cheerful, even in disasters. "Race you to the north district?"

"Go," Diana said.

They moved through the flooded city like two bright lights — the Flash a red-gold blur lifting children from rooftops faster than children expected to be lifted, Diana moving deliberately, her strength steady as bedrock, lifting a school bus that had been caught in the current and carrying it to higher ground in one smooth motion, SPLASH, water cascading off the sides.

She noticed things, as she worked. She noticed the woman on the third-floor ledge who was holding two cats and would not leave without them — Diana held all three to her chest and flew them to the community center without comment. She noticed the elderly man watching from a second-floor window who did not call for help but who was clearly not able to manage the water alone — she landed on his sill and simply said, "May I?" and held out her hand.

When it was over — when the flooding was addressed and the cause neutralized and the city beginning the long work of cleanup — the Justice League gathered in a dry corner of the civic center to debrief. Fifty-three people evacuated in forty minutes. Zero casualties.

Superman said, "That was remarkable."

Diana looked at him. "It was all of us."

"You went in first," he said.

"Someone has to." She coiled her lasso back at her hip. "When they see someone going toward them, not away — it changes everything. It's not just rescue. It's the assurance that the world hasn't forgotten them."

Batman was quiet, as he often was when Diana spoke about things like this. He thought about it for a long time after, on the way home in the Batwing, watching the city pass below.

He thought she was right. He didn't say so out loud.

But the next crisis, he arrived twelve seconds faster than usual.

Even Batman, apparently, could be inspired.
