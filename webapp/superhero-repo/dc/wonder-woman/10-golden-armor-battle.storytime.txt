There is armor in the treasury of Themyscira that has not been worn in a very long time.

It is kept in the oldest part of the temple, behind three locked doors whose keys Hippolyta carries on a chain around her wrist. The room itself is carved from the living rock of the island, lit by torches that never quite go out, and in the center of it, on a stand of carved stone, the armor waits.

It is gold. Or it was gold once — it is something richer now, something that has absorbed the light of centuries and holds it, so that even in the torch-lit room it glows like its own small sun. Massive wing shapes fold from the back — the wings of a great bird, beaten metal and feather-thin but strong enough to carry a person through skies that most wings could not survive. The helmet sits above it with a face opening shaped like a visor, and the chest plate bears markings in old Amazonian that mean, roughly: she who stands between the world and its undoing.

Her name was Asteria.

If Diana is the hero of this age, Asteria was the hero of a different age — a time so long ago that it lives only in the oldest Amazonian records, in the chapters that are difficult to read because the scribes who wrote them were recording something too large for ordinary language. When the Amazons fled to Themyscira, pursued by forces that would have destroyed them, Asteria chose to stay behind. She put on this armor — this golden, wing-spread, world-weight armor — and she stood in the path of what was coming.

The records do not describe what happened precisely. The records say: she held.

For how long, they do not say. Long enough. The Amazons reached safety. Asteria gave them time that was not available any other way, and she paid for it in the way that the greatest heroes sometimes must.

Diana wears the armor in her honor.

She does not wear it lightly. She does not wear it to battles that could be managed otherwise. The armor comes out when the threat is the kind that requires someone to stand between the world and its undoing — exactly as Asteria once did — and to hold, regardless of what holding costs.

The night Diana first wore it in battle, the sky was the color of bruised plums and the enemy on the horizon was large in a way that went beyond size — large in the way that certain threats are large, not physically but existentially, the kind of large that makes ordinary language feel inadequate.

She felt the armor settle around her. The wings unfolded with a sound like the opening of something very old — not a creak, but a deep, slow exhalation, as if the armor were taking its first breath in a very long time. And then they caught the air and Diana rose.

Not like flying usually felt — quick and precise and responsive. This was different. This was the feeling of something huge being channeled through something very small, the power of all those generations of Amazonian courage flowing through metal and into Diana's hands and arms and feet and the absolute center of her chest where conviction lives.

She moved through the battle the way light moves through a storm — not deflected but not deterred, cutting through the confusion and the noise and the overwhelming scale of it and staying focused on what mattered. The armor's blows landed with the force of thunderbolts — BOOM — KRAAAAANG — each strike sending shockwaves outward through the chaos. The wings became a shield when she needed them, folding around her against the largest impacts, and she felt them absorb the force and redistribute it, the way a sail redistributes the wind.

She thought of Asteria, who had done this once without knowing if the Amazons had reached safety. Who had stood in that gap in the dark without knowing the outcome. Who had simply stood.

Diana stood.

When it was over — when the threat had passed and the sky began to clear and the first stars appeared in the gaps of cloud overhead — Diana removed the helmet.

The armor cooled around her. The wings settled. She stood in the field in the quiet aftermath, and she let herself feel all of it: the exhaustion, the relief, the weight of what had been required, and underneath all of that, steady as the island stone, the gratitude.

She removed the armor piece by piece in the field, carefully, and held the chest plate for a moment in her hands.

"Asteria," she said quietly.

The night was still. The first crickets had started.

She folded the wings, piece by piece, the way you fold something precious. She would carry it back to Themyscira. She would return it to the room with the three locked doors.

And it would wait there, as it always had, for the next time the world needed someone to stand.
