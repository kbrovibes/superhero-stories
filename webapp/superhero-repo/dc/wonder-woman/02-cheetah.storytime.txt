There was once a scholar named Barbara Ann Minerva who knew more about ancient ruins than almost anyone alive.

She could read languages that had been dead for two thousand years. She had spent decades in jungles and deserts and dusty archives, following threads of history that no one else had the patience to follow. Her office at the museum was covered floor to ceiling in maps and photographs and careful notes. She was brilliant. She was dedicated. She was, by any measure, someone whose gifts were extraordinary.

Diana called her a friend.

They had met at a conference on ancient artifacts, and despite being very different people — Diana warm and open, Barbara careful and bookish and not entirely comfortable at parties — they had recognized in each other someone who genuinely loved the past. They spent long evenings talking about old civilizations and forgotten weapons and the myths that people use to explain things too large to explain any other way.

So when Barbara told Diana about the ritual she had found — a ritual in an ancient African text that she believed could grant the power of a cheetah goddess — Diana listened carefully. She listened to the whole description. And then she said, gently, "Barbara. I don't think you should."

Barbara did not listen.

The ritual had a catch — it required someone who truly put the goddess's purpose first, above everything, even themselves. But Barbara was thinking about what the power would feel like, and about the years she had spent in second place behind people with advantages she'd had to earn, and that thought — that hungry, bitter thought — was in her heart when she completed the ritual.

The power came. And it came twisted.

She became the Cheetah — and if you have never imagined a Cheetah villain, picture this: a woman and a great cat in one form, moving with a speed and grace that is terrifying and beautiful all at once. Tawny fur. Claws that could scratch diamonds. Eyes that saw in the dark and tracked every movement. And a rage, somewhere underneath all that speed, that had been building for years.

Diana faced her in the rain forest, in a clearing where the ancient trees rose sixty feet on all sides, their roots making walls and tunnels along the ground. The fight was fast — so fast that the birds above scattered before the sound reached them, erupting into the canopy in a chattering cloud.

WHOOSH — the Cheetah lunged. KRANG — Diana caught the claws on her silver bracelets and the impact rang like a bell and sent both of them skidding through the wet undergrowth. The Cheetah was stronger and faster than anything Diana had faced in a long time. Her claws could find the edges of Diana's invulnerability and press — HISS — and Diana felt it. She felt it in the way that pain is felt by someone who very rarely experiences pain — sharply, with surprise.

But Diana did not fight back with anger. She fought back with sadness.

Because every time she looked at the Cheetah — at those yellow eyes full of fury and hunger — she saw Barbara. The scholar with the quiet laugh and the encyclopedic knowledge of dead civilizations. The person who had been her friend.

"I know you're in there," Diana said, catching the Cheetah's wrists and holding them still, meeting those blazing eyes without flinching. "I know you, Barbara Ann Minerva."

The Cheetah snarled. The name seemed to hurt her. Or the recognition. Perhaps they were the same thing.

Diana did not try to win. She tried to hold on. She stayed in the fight — patient, steady, absorbing blow after blow — until the Cheetah's fury burned through its own fuel and the creature slowed, breathing hard in the rain, crouched on a root as wide as a table.

Then Diana did something that made no tactical sense and all human sense: she sat down on the root across from her and said, "Talk to me."

In the quiet after the storm, there was a long silence in the rain forest. The birds came back one by one.

Diana doesn't always win these encounters — not the way winning usually counts. Sometimes the Cheetah escapes. Sometimes she regroups and finds new ancient power to wield. But something accumulates in those moments of eye contact in the rain — some slow, patient work of recognition that Diana keeps doing, time after time.

Because Diana's greatest act of strength is not a throw or a block or a Lasso. It is the refusal to give up on a person. Even when that person has forgotten who they are.

Especially then.
