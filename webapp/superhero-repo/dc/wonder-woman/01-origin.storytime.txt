There is an island hidden from the rest of the world.

The gods of ancient Olympus placed a veil of mist and magic around it long ago — thick and soft, drifting over the water like silver smoke — and behind that veil, out of sight of ships and satellites and every map ever drawn, lies the island of Themyscira.

Oh, it is beautiful. Can you picture it? White marble temples rising from green hills, their columns catching the morning light like torches. Gardens full of flowers that bloom all year without stopping. A harbor where the sea is the deepest blue you have ever seen, and fish the color of jewels move in great slow wheels through the clear water. And all around the island, the sound of the Amazons — their laughter and their songs and their weapons ringing on shields as they train in the warm afternoon light.

The Amazons have lived on Themyscira for three thousand years. They are fierce and brilliant and kind — warriors and scholars and healers all at once, a people who built their civilization on the twin pillars of strength and compassion. Their queen is Hippolyta, a woman of such grace and such power that even the Olympian gods listen when she speaks.

And Hippolyta wanted a daughter.

She went to the water's edge one morning and shaped a child from the pale clay of Themyscira's shore — shaping the curve of a cheek, the small fingers, the sleeping face — and breathed into it every wish she had ever made. And the Olympian gods, who loved Hippolyta, answered. Aphrodite breathed grace and beauty into the clay. Demeter gave it the strength of the earth itself. Athena whispered wisdom into the small, still ears. Hermes touched the tiny feet with speed. Artemis gifted the eyes of a hunter. And from Hestia, the last of the five, came something quietly extraordinary: an unbreakable connection to the truth.

And then the child opened her eyes.

Her name was Diana.

She grew up on Themyscira like a sunrise grows up over the horizon — gradually, gloriously, filling every space she entered with light. She trained with the Amazon warriors from the time she could hold a sword, learning to move in battle with a precision and grace that made even her teachers shake their heads in admiration. She studied in the temples, reading the records of three thousand years. She swam in the harbor with the jewel-colored fish and ran races on the hills with her laughing companions and sat beside her mother Hippolyta in the evenings and asked questions that had no easy answers.

Her warrior mentor, Antiope — the greatest fighter Themyscira had ever produced — taught her something that most people learn much later, if ever at all: that strength without discipline is just noise, and discipline without love is just cold. "Fight because you love," Antiope told her. "Never fight because you hate."

Diana did not forget this.

When Steve Trevor's plane crashed out of the sky and came down in the waters off Themyscira — SPLASH, the first man ever to enter their protected world — Diana was the one who pulled him from the sea. She had never seen a man before. She looked at him with open curiosity, this bedraggled pilot from somewhere beyond the veil, and listened to his stories of the world outside.

The world outside was at war, Steve told her. A great war, the kind that swallowed whole countries. People were suffering. He needed to get back.

Diana listened. And she made a decision.

She entered the tournament that no Amazon had ever wanted to win — the contest that chose who would carry Themyscira's honor into the wider world. She competed in secret, in a mask, against warriors she had trained alongside her whole life. She was faster, stronger, steadier than anyone else on the field.

When the mask came off and Hippolyta saw her daughter's face, there was a long, aching silence between them. A mother's love and a daughter's calling, meeting each other across the tournament ground.

Hippolyta clasped a golden lasso around Diana's hip. She placed silver bracelets on her wrists. She looked at her daughter — this child of clay and divine breath and three thousand years of Amazon courage — and she did not say be safe, because she knew Diana could not be held back by those words.

She said, "Make us proud."

WHOOSH — Diana soared into the world beyond the veil, her dark hair streaming behind her, her golden lasso catching the light, the sky enormous and open around her.

The world had never met an Amazon princess before.

It was about to.
