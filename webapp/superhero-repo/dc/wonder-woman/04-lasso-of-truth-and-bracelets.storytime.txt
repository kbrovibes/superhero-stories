Before Diana left Themyscira, her mother gave her two gifts.

The first was a rope of living gold, supple as silk and unbreakable as anything in the known world. The Lasso of Truth — forged from the Girdle of Gaea, the great earth mother herself — coiled at Diana's hip like a sleeping serpent, warm to the touch, glowing faintly with the light of an honest fire. Anyone bound by the Lasso must speak the truth. Not because it hurts them not to — but because in the Lasso's presence, truth is simply the only thing that will come out. Deception dissolves. Illusions clear. Minds clouded by ancient spells find the fog lifting. The Lasso does not demand the truth harshly. It invites it, the way a warm room invites someone in from the cold.

The second gift was a pair of bracelets. Silver and impossible — made from the remnants of Zeus's own Aegis, the divine shield of the sky father — they were so deeply a part of Diana that she had worn them since childhood, working with them in training until they were as natural as her own hands. She could move them faster than sight. She had been striking arrows and spears out of the air since she was ten years old.

Now she used them to stop bullets.

Can you picture it? Someone fires something at Diana — CRACK — and her wrists cross in front of her, the bracelets snapping together — KRANG — and the impact rings out like a bell and the thing that was coming at her deflects away harmlessly. It looks simple. It looks easy. It is neither. Behind it are years of training, thousands of hours in the practice yards of Themyscira, a discipline so ingrained that her body responds before her mind has finished thinking.

And when she crosses the bracelets — really crosses them, with force and intention, in moments of dire need — something older wakes up. KRAAAAANG — a shockwave of divine energy erupts outward from the crossed silver, a pulse of pure power that can scatter a crowd of attackers or collapse a structure or simply clear a space that needs clearing, like a thunderclap made visible. Diana uses this rarely. It costs something, in the way that things of genuine power always cost something.

Together — the Lasso and the Bracelets — they tell a story.

The Bracelets were called the Bracelets of Submission, once. They were symbols of a time when the Amazons had been enslaved, before Hippolyta led them to freedom. The Amazons kept the name — not to remember their pain, but to remember what they had overcome. They wore those bracelets and trained with them every day as a reminder that strength does not mean never being in a difficult situation. It means getting up from one.

The Lasso was something else. The Amazons believed that truth was not just a virtue but a force — real and tangible and able to change things when it was wielded with courage. Most people know the truth inside them. They just need help saying it out loud. The Lasso gives them that help.

Diana carried both of these things into the modern world. And she found that the modern world had a great deal of use for them.

A corporate executive entangled in the Lasso stopped mid-lie and — to his own visible surprise — told the truth. A general whose armies were on the brink of unnecessary conflict found, with the Lasso coiled around his wrist, that the war he had been building toward was not about the principle he'd been claiming. A frightened person, in the middle of a crisis, found that a warm loop of golden rope around their shoulders felt oddly like being held — and they stopped panicking and started thinking.

The Bracelets turned away missiles and stopped warlords and kept Diana upright in fights that would have destroyed anyone less prepared. They rang in the halls of Arkham Asylum and on the plains of ancient battlefields and in the glass corridors of the United Nations.

One day, a little girl outside the UN building pointed at Diana's wrists and asked, "What are those?"

Diana crouched down — she always crouched down when children asked her things, so they were at the same height — and held one up to the light.

"These," she said, "are a reminder. Of where my people came from, and how far they have come."

"Do they hurt?"

"Never," Diana said. "They feel like home."

The child thought about this. Then she nodded, satisfied, and went back to her mother.

Diana straightened. The Lasso rested at her hip, warm as ever. The Bracelets caught the afternoon light.

She had crossed an ocean with these. She would cross many more.
