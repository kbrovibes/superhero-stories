Long before there was a Robin, there was a boy named Dick Grayson, and Dick Grayson belonged to the most famous trapeze act in the world. They were called the Flying Graysons, and they traveled with Haly's Circus from city to city in a long line of painted train cars. Dick was eight years old. His mother, Mary Grayson, used to say that he didn't walk on the high wire — he danced on it. His father, John Grayson, taught him every twist and tuck and somersault. There was a quiet wooden chest in their train compartment full of red, yellow, and green sequined costumes, and Dick was the smallest, brightest one of them all.

One night the circus came to Gotham City. The big top was packed. Bruce Wayne was sitting in the front row in a quiet dark suit, because Bruce always liked to support the city he had lost his own parents to. The Flying Graysons climbed up the long ladder. The drumroll began. And then something went terribly wrong with the trapeze ropes. The ropes had been tampered with by a mobster named Tony Zucco, who had been trying to scare the circus owner into paying him money. Dick's parents fell. The big top went very, very quiet.

Dick sat in the sawdust afterward in his little Flying Graysons costume, holding his knees. He did not cry loudly. He just stared at the empty trapeze. He felt the same kind of empty that Bruce Wayne had once felt in a place called Crime Alley, many years before. Bruce knew that empty very well. He walked over, knelt down, and said quietly, "I know."

Bruce became Dick's guardian. He took him home to Wayne Manor — the big house on the hill outside Gotham, with the long driveway and the kind butler named Alfred Pennyworth who made hot cocoa with extra marshmallows. At first Dick was very quiet. He missed his mother's laugh. He missed the smell of sawdust. He missed the swing of the trapeze under his hands.

But one night Bruce showed Dick something. He led him through a tall grandfather clock, down a long stone staircase, and into the Batcave. The bats fluttered up in a soft cloud. Dick's eyes went wide. Bruce said, "I lost my parents too. I built this so the same thing wouldn't happen to anybody else." Dick looked at the cave, and at Bruce, and at the great glowing computer screens, and he understood. He had found his second home. And he had found his purpose.
