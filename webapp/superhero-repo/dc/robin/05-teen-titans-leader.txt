By the time Dick Grayson turned fifteen, he had been Robin for years. He was still bright and still acrobatic, but he was getting older now, and Gotham City could no longer be the whole world. Across the country, in a sunny coastal city called Jump City, four other young heroes were looking for a fifth — someone smart enough to lead them. They were Cyborg, the half-man half-machine genius with the blue circuit-lights and the booming sonic cannon. Starfire, the orange-skinned princess from the planet Tamaran, who could fly and throw bolts of green starbolt energy from her hands. Raven, the quiet daughter of a powerful sorcerer, who could lift cars and fold shadows with a whisper. And Beast Boy, the green-skinned trickster who could transform into any animal he had ever seen.

They had heard about Robin all the way from Gotham. The bright, fearless, trapeze-trained sidekick of the Batman. Cyborg flew the team's T-shaped jet to Gotham one weekend to ask Dick if he'd come west and help them found a team. Bruce surprised everyone by saying yes. He said, "Dick. You've been my partner long enough. It's time to be somebody's leader."

The five of them set up base in the T-Tower, a tall building shaped exactly like the letter T, standing on a small island in Jump City Bay. Robin's first job as leader was to teach the others how to work together. They were all enormously powerful in their own way, but they had never trained as a team. Starfire wanted to fly straight at every problem. Raven wanted to think. Cyborg wanted to blast. Beast Boy wanted to make jokes. Robin learned to use each of them at exactly the right moment — Starfire's flight to scout, Raven's shadows to cover an escape, Cyborg's cannon to break through walls, Beast Boy turning into a mouse to slip under a door.

Their first real mission was against a villain named Slade — a calm, deadly man in a half-orange half-black mask who wanted to recruit Robin as his apprentice. Slade thought Robin would be tempted by power. He was very wrong. Robin used everything Bruce had taught him about patience and stillness, and everything Starfire and Cyborg and Raven and Beast Boy had taught him about teamwork, and together they outsmarted Slade and locked him in his own underground hideout.

The Teen Titans became famous. Their T-communicators — little round devices shaped like a T — were given out to other young heroes around the world. And Dick Grayson, the boy who once flew on a trapeze under a striped big top in Gotham, now led the most cheerful, mismatched, powerful team of young heroes the world had ever seen. The robin had grown into a leader of birds.
