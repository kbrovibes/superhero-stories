Robin's first real mission as Batman's partner began with a phone call to Commissioner Gordon down at the Gotham City Police Department. A gang called the Red Mask Crew was robbing the First National Bank on Sprang Street. Gordon flipped on the Bat-Signal. Up on the roof of Wayne Manor, the great yellow light bounced off the clouds, and Bruce turned to Dick and said simply, "Suit up." Two minutes later the Batmobile rumbled out of the cave, low and dark, with Batman behind the wheel and a small bright Robin riding shotgun.

The bank was a mess. Smoke alarms whined. A glass door was shattered. Six robbers in red ski masks were stuffing canvas bags full of money while a clerk hid trembling behind the counter. Batman swept in through a high skylight, his cape spread wide, and three of the robbers turned and ran. The other three lifted their guns and aimed at the dark figure on the rafters. Robin, who had slipped in through a side window, was already on the move. He flipped down off a chandelier. He bounced off the marble counter. He landed in a perfect somersault behind the gunmen, and as he came up, he tapped the first one neatly on the shoulder so the man would spin around in surprise.

"HOLY BANK HEIST, BATMAN!" Robin shouted, his bright cape swinging behind him. The robber blinked at him — at this small, bright, grinning bird where a moment ago there had been nothing — and Robin laid him out gently with a single trapeze-perfect flip-kick. Batman swept across the lobby, dark and silent. Robin spun and chirped and bounced and danced. Together they cleared the bank in less than ninety seconds.

Robin's bright "Holy something, Batman!" became a tradition that night. Whenever Robin saw something surprising — a trap door, a giant rubber ducky filled with stolen jewels, a getaway boat shaped like a swan — he would shout it out. "Holy mackerel, Batman!" "Holy umbrella, Batman!" Bruce never told him to stop. The truth was, Bruce liked it. The cheerful bright noise of Robin's voice broke up the long heavy silence of the cowl. It reminded Bruce that he was not, anymore, alone in the dark.

When the police arrived, Commissioner Gordon walked into the bank lobby and found six red-masked robbers neatly tied with Bat-rope, and on top of the pile sat a small grinning boy in a red, yellow, and green costume, waving at him. Gordon, who had been a Gotham cop for twenty-five years, took off his hat, scratched his head, and laughed out loud for the first time in months. Gotham, he realized, had just gotten a little brighter.
