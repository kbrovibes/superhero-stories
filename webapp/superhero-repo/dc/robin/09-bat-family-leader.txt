There came a day, years into Dick Grayson's life as Nightwing, when Gotham City lost its Batman. It was during a long, hard crisis — Bruce had vanished, lost in time, and for many months nobody knew if he was coming back. Without Batman watching over it from the rooftops, Gotham started to fray. The Joker grew bolder. The Penguin came out of hiding. Smaller gangs began carving up the streets. Commissioner Gordon kept the Bat-Signal lit every single night, but for the first time in twenty years, nothing answered.

The Bat-Family met in the Batcave to talk about what to do. Alfred Pennyworth was there, calmer than anyone but his hands shaking just a little around the teapot. Tim Drake — the brilliant young Robin of that era — was there, certain he could find Bruce wherever he was. Damian Wayne, Bruce's small fierce son, was there, sword on his back, scowling. Barbara Gordon — Oracle — was there in her wheelchair, monitoring the city through every camera. They all turned to Dick. They were waiting.

Dick understood what was being asked of him. Somebody had to be Batman. Not forever, but until Bruce came home. Gotham could not stand without one. He sat down in the great stone chair in front of the giant computer for a long moment, his face quiet. He thought about his mother, and the trapeze, and the catch. He thought about Bruce kneeling in the sawdust ring. He thought about how Bruce had told him, that first night in the cave, "I lost my parents too." Dick took a slow breath. Then he stood up and walked over to one of the long glass cases.

He picked a Batsuit. Not Bruce's classic one — a new one. He had it tailored to fit. He kept his own bright blue bird logo across the chest, alongside the dark bat ears and the long sweeping cape. It was a Batman that belonged to a man who had once been a Robin. He took the cowl in his hands. He looked at it for a long time. Then he put it on.

Damian became his Robin. The fierce little ten-year-old boy stood beside Dick that night on the roof of the GCPD, in the bright Robin tunic that Alfred had altered and tailored for him, scowling out at Gotham — and Dick, in the long black cape and the cowl, put a hand gently on his small shoulder. "Holy first night, partner," Dick said. Damian rolled his eyes and tried not to smile. The Bat-Signal went up. The new Batman and the new Robin swung off the roof together. Gotham, for the first time in a very long time, lifted its head.
