After Bruce Wayne showed Dick Grayson the Batcave, things at Wayne Manor began to change. Alfred Pennyworth started cooking bigger breakfasts — pancakes with banana faces, double helpings of toast. Bruce started coming home earlier. And every evening, after dinner, Dick would follow Bruce down the hidden staircase behind the grandfather clock to begin his training.

The training was hard. Bruce taught Dick how to fight the way he had learned in Tibet and Japan — quick, precise, with no wasted motion. He taught him how to throw a Batarang so it curved through the air and came back. He taught him how to pick a lock with two thin tools and how to disappear into shadow. Dick was already an incredible acrobat — the trapeze had given him that — so he could leap higher and tumble faster than almost anyone Bruce had ever met. But Bruce taught him patience. He taught him the long stillness of waiting in the dark on top of a gargoyle, listening for footsteps on a wet street.

One night, Bruce said it was time to choose a name. He laid a stack of newspaper clippings on the table in the Batcave. He said, "If we're going to do this together, you're going to need your own. Not a copy of mine. Yours." Dick thought about it for a long time. He thought about the trapeze. He thought about the wind on his face when he flew through the air. And he thought about his mother, Mary Grayson, who had called him mio piccolo robin — my little robin — because robins were the bright birds that came back first in spring, no matter how cold the winter had been.

"Robin," Dick said. "I want to be Robin."

Alfred made the first costume. It had a red tunic with the bright yellow letter R stitched on a black circle over the heart. It had green sleeves like wings and green leggings like the trapeze tights he used to wear. There was a yellow cape lined with black, and a small black domino mask. When Dick put it on, he looked in the mirror and saw — not a sad boy in a quiet manor — but a brave bright bird, ready to fly again.

He climbed up to the highest rafter in the Batcave. He looked down at Bruce in the suit, the cowl, the long dark cape. He shouted, "Catch!" — and he leaped off, twisting through the air in a perfect triple somersault, the way his father had taught him. Bruce caught him, wrist to wrist, like the Flying Graysons always had. And just like that, the Dynamic Duo was born.
