After Dick Grayson grew out of the Robin costume, somebody else had to wear it. That's how it works in Gotham. The little red and yellow tunic with the great big R never stays on a shelf for long. Over the years, Dick became something none of them had expected — the big brother of an entire family of Robins. He trained Jason Todd. He trained Tim Drake. He trained Damian Wayne. He passed the cape along, one Robin at a time.

Jason Todd was first. Bruce found Jason trying to steal the tires off the Batmobile in Crime Alley — the same alley where Bruce had lost his parents. Bruce brought him home. Jason was angry, and he was sharp-tongued, and he had a temper as quick as lightning. Dick, who was Nightwing by then, came back to Gotham to help. He taught Jason how to land a flip without breaking a wrist. He taught him that anger could be turned into focus. Jason never softened all the way, but he loved Dick, and Dick loved him, and that mattered.

Tim Drake was second. Tim was the smartest of all of them. He had figured out Batman's secret identity all by himself by age twelve, from photographs in old newspapers, just by paying very close attention to Dick Grayson's old triple somersault. He showed up at Wayne Manor one rainy night and told Bruce, in a steady, polite voice, that the Bat-Family needed a Robin again. Dick visited Tim almost every weekend during his training. He took Tim onto rooftops in Blüdhaven and taught him the small quiet things — how to read a city, how to listen for footsteps in fog, how to throw a Bird-arang with two fingers.

Damian Wayne was last. Damian was the hardest. Damian was Bruce's son, raised by the League of Assassins, with a sword on his back and a permanent scowl. He didn't WANT a big brother. He thought Dick was silly. He thought the Robin colors were "humiliating." But Dick was patient with him in a way that nobody else in the family quite could be. Dick would meet Damian on the Wayne Manor lawn at sunrise, with two practice escrima sticks, and they would spar. Dick let Damian win sometimes. Damian, very slowly, started to come up the long stone staircase from the cave at breakfast time instead of staying down there alone.

Years later, somebody asked Dick what the most important thing he had ever done was. Dick thought about it for a long time. He didn't say "leading the Titans." He didn't say "becoming Nightwing." He didn't say "wearing the cowl when Bruce was lost." He said, very quietly, "I taught my little brothers how to fly." Because the trapeze had always known this. The catch is the most important part. And Dick Grayson, the boy who used to be caught wrist-to-wrist by his father, had grown up to be the catcher for an entire family of Robins.
