Dick Grayson could not be Robin forever. There comes a time when every Boy Wonder has to stop being a boy. Dick was nineteen now. Taller than Bruce. Stronger. He had led the Teen Titans for four years. He had fought villains across the entire western seaboard. And somewhere inside him, the bright red-yellow-green costume that Alfred had stitched when he was nine was starting to feel small in a different way — not the wrong size, but the wrong shape. Dick was still cheerful, still acrobatic, still the heart of any team he was in. But he was not a sidekick anymore.

He sat with Bruce on the back porch at Wayne Manor one autumn night. He had come home for a visit. They watched the stars come out over the long fields. Dick said, "I think I'm done being Robin." Bruce did not answer for a long time. Then he said, very simply, "I know." Bruce had been waiting for this. He had always known that Robin was the name of a boy, and Dick was not a boy any longer.

Dick told Bruce a story that night that he had never told him before. Superman had once told him about a hero from Kryptonian legend — Nightwing, a great warrior who soared through the city of Kandor in a long dark cape, protecting people. Superman had said, "If you ever needed a new name, Dick, I think Nightwing is yours. A bird that flies after the sun goes down." Dick had remembered that for years.

He designed the new suit himself, with Alfred's help. It was nothing like Robin. The bright red and yellow and green were gone. The new suit was sleek black, slim-fitting, made of armored mesh, with a single bold bird-shape spread in royal blue across the chest — wings reaching from shoulder to shoulder. The mask was black, longer than the old domino. The boots were black. The gauntlets held no Bird-arangs. Instead Dick carried two short batons — escrima sticks, the kind he had been training with in secret for years — clipped to his back.

He put the suit on for the first time in the Batcave. He stood in front of the mirror. Behind him, on a mannequin, was the old red-and-yellow Robin uniform, lit softly under glass. Dick looked at the boy version of himself, all bright sequins and circus colors, and then at the tall blue-winged figure in the mirror, and he understood. He wasn't leaving Robin behind. He was carrying Robin inside him. The little robin that came back every spring had grown into a Nightwing — a bird with darker feathers, but still bright, still brave, and ready to fly even after the sun had gone down.
