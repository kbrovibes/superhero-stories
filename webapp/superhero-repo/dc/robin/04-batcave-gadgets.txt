Robin had his own corner of the Batcave. It was a workbench just to the left of the giant computer where the Bat-Signal feed glowed all night. On the workbench were three things he had made with his own hands. A wooden tray of Bird-arangs — small bright throwing tools shaped like little birds in flight, painted yellow and red. A coil of jumping line that he had spliced himself from spare grappling rope. And a brand-new utility belt, slimmer than Bruce's, in bright yellow with a black R-buckle in the middle. Alfred had stitched the belt for him, but every pouch on it Robin had designed himself.

The Bird-arangs were Robin's specialty. They were smaller and lighter than Batman's Batarangs, so they could be thrown faster and in pairs. Robin could throw one with each hand at the same time — left-right, left-right — like a circus juggler. He had taught himself to bank them off walls, ceilings, even moving objects. Once, in a warehouse on the docks, he banked a Bird-arang off a swinging hook so it came around behind a goon and clonked him gently on the back of the helmet. The goon went down with a confused "oof."

The R-belt was even more clever. Robin had filled the pouches with the kinds of things a small acrobatic crime-fighter actually needed. A coil of thin climbing rope. A grappling hook that fired from a wrist-launcher. Small smoke pellets the size of marbles, in different colors. A magnifying glass for clues. Tiny lockpicks. A handful of yellow flares. A roll of strong tape that Alfred called "the universal tool, sir." And a little folded photo of Mary and John Grayson, tucked into the inner lining where it could not get wet.

But Robin's greatest gadget was not in any of his pouches. It was simply himself. His acrobat-trained body. Years of trapeze work had given Dick a sense of his own weight in midair that almost no one else on Earth had. He could leap, twist, flip, catch a ledge with two fingers, and somersault around a moving fan blade without slowing down. Bruce once said, watching Dick practice from the catwalk, "He doesn't fight gravity. He has a conversation with it." Alfred wrote that line down on an index card and pinned it to the fridge in the manor kitchen.

The first time Dick used all of it together was on the Sprang Bridge during a hostage situation. He fired his grappling hook upward, swung across a hundred-foot gap, flipped twice in midair, threw a pair of Bird-arangs sideways to disarm two henchmen, dropped a smoke pellet for cover, and landed in front of a frightened bus driver with a smile and a "Holy cliffhanger, Batman — we got him." The bus driver applauded. Even Batman, watching from the suspension cable above, allowed himself a small nod. The Boy Wonder was a one-bird circus, and Gotham City was his big top.
