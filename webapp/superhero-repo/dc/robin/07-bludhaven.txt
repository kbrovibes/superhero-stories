When Dick Grayson became Nightwing, he needed a city. Not Gotham — that was Bruce's. Not Jump City — that was the Titans'. He needed somewhere of his own. He looked at a long list of American cities and chose the one almost everybody else had given up on. Blüdhaven, just down the coast from Gotham, an old port city full of rusting factories, foggy alleys, and corrupt police officers. The mayor was crooked. The mob ran the docks. The buses ran late. Most heroes wouldn't have touched Blüdhaven with a ten-foot grappling hook. Dick saw all of that and said, "Perfect."

He rented a small apartment in a brick walk-up on Parkthorne Avenue. He kept his Nightwing suit folded in a hidden compartment under the bed. By day, he worked as a rookie officer with the Blüdhaven Police Department, badge 168, partnered with a sergeant named Amy Rohrbach who quickly figured out that Dick was a much better detective than a rookie should ever be. By night, he was Nightwing, swinging between the city's rusted warehouses and broken neon signs, two escrima sticks spinning in the fog.

Blüdhaven was tougher than Gotham in some ways. In Gotham, the bad guys had bright purple suits and laugh-out-loud names like the Joker and the Riddler. In Blüdhaven, the bad guys wore ordinary suits. They ran trucking companies and shipping crews. They paid off the wrong cops. The danger was quieter and harder to see. Dick had to learn a different kind of detective work — listening to longshoremen on the docks, sitting in diners with retired cops, mapping the small flow of bribes from warehouse to warehouse. He used everything Bruce had taught him about patience.

The biggest enemy Nightwing faced in Blüdhaven was an enormous, calm crime boss named Roland Desmond, who called himself Blockbuster — a man the size of a refrigerator, with shoulders like a doorway, and a fortune in stolen city money. Blockbuster owned half of Blüdhaven on paper. Nightwing took him apart one piece at a time, court case by court case, raid by raid, until even Blockbuster's own people stopped showing up to work for him. It took two years. It worked.

The people of Blüdhaven slowly started to notice. The buses began running on time. The mayor was arrested for accepting bribes. The docks reopened with honest crews. None of it was loud or famous the way Batman's victories in Gotham were loud and famous. But up on the rooftops of Parkthorne Avenue, in the fog, in the dark, a young man with a blue bird across his chest stood with his escrima sticks crossed at his back and looked down at his city. And for the first time, it felt like his city.
