Every now and then, the King of Atlantis goes home.

Not to the palace in the deep — though that is home now, genuinely and fully home, in the way that a place becomes home when you have chosen it and filled it with the people you love. He goes to the other home. The first one. The rocky point on the Maine coast where the lighthouse stands.

Amnesty Bay.

He drives up from the coastal highway in a rental truck — he is not Arthur of Atlantis on these days, just Arthur, wearing a flannel shirt that Tom Curry would have recognized and boots that are slightly too damp from the beach below. He parks on the gravel lot by the keeper's house and sits for a moment listening to the sound of the Atlantic, which is the sound of his whole life, underneath everything else.

The lighthouse is maintained by the Amnesty Bay Historical Society now. They keep it painted and its mechanism working. The beam sweeps on its schedule. Tom Curry's name is on a small plaque near the door: THOMAS CURRY, LIGHTHOUSE KEEPER, with the years of his service and the simple inscription below: He kept the light.

Arthur traces the letters with one finger.

Inside the keeper's house, nothing has changed from what he remembers. The same kitchen table where he ate breakfast as a boy, the same view of the water from the east window, the same creak in the third step of the staircase that used to give away any attempt to sneak downstairs before dawn. He knows this house in his bones. He knows which drawer sticks in humidity and which floorboard rings hollow and where Tom used to keep the spare keys to the light room.

He finds the compass on a shelf in the study.

It is old — older than Arthur — and weathered in the way of tools that have been genuinely used, the brass worn smooth along the handle, the glass slightly clouded. But the needle still turns. He holds it level and watches it settle.

North. Steady and clear and entirely certain of itself, the way his father was entirely certain of himself, without rigidity or defensiveness — just the calm of someone who had found his direction a long time ago and seen no reason to change it.

Tom Curry had never cared about royal bloodlines. Arthur had understood this when he was very young and it had taken him a long time to understand why it was so important — why it mattered that his father had looked at this strange, serious boy who talked to fish and breathed underwater and held him with exactly the same steady regard that he held everything else. No extra weight attached to the Atlantean heritage. No diminishment of the lighthouse keeper's son. Just: you are my boy. That's the whole of it.

"Dad," Arthur says quietly in the empty study, "they gave me the throne. For real this time. All seven kingdoms."

The lighthouse beam sweeps past the window in its slow arc, casting a stripe of light across the floor.

"I thought you should know," Arthur says.

Outside, a storm is building on the horizon — not a local one, the kind the Atlantic builds in autumn, pulling itself together over open water with a slow, deliberate energy. Arthur can feel it through his feet, through some deep sense that is not exactly touch but that is calibrated to the sea the way a tuning fork is calibrated to a note. He knows this storm. He knows its character. He grew up watching these come in.

He goes outside and sits on the rocks at the base of the point, where Tom used to sit with him when Arthur was small, in the last light of the evening. The spray comes in cold. The tide is at a particular point in its cycle. The smell is exactly what it has always been: salt and kelp and something else that has no name, the smell of where the land decides to be done and the sea begins.

He was born here. Between two worlds. At the edge.

He has lived his whole life at the edge, and he has learned something that most people never have to learn: the edge is not a compromise. It is not a failure to fully belong to either side. The edge is the most interesting place. The edge is where the two things meet, where the conversation happens, where each world discovers what it needs from the other.

He is the bridge. He has always been the bridge.

The lighthouse beam sweeps. The storm is getting closer. Arthur turns his face into the spray, eyes half-closed, feeling the Atlantic in all its cold and permanent enormity press against the rocks below him.

He knows who he is.

He knows it in the way the compass needle knows north — steadily, simply, without having to think about it.

He goes back inside. He stays one more night in the keeper's house. In the morning, he drives back to the coast road, parks the truck, and walks to the water's edge.

And he dives. He goes home to the other home, the deep one, the palace in the blue-green glow, where Mera is waiting with news and the council has a report and the whales have been asking about the northern route again.

He carries the compass in his pocket.

He always carries it.
