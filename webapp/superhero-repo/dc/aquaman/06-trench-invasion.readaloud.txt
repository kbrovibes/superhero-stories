The ocean has a midnight zone — a place so deep that sunlight never reaches it. The water there is freezing cold and pressed down with a weight that would crush most things flat. For Arthur Curry, it felt like home. But not everything that lived in that darkness was peaceful.

From the deepest trenches of the Atlantic came the Trench — creatures out of a nightmare, driven by pure, wild hunger. They had bioluminescent eyes that blinked in the black water like cold stars. Their teeth were as sharp as needles. Their claws could shred reinforced steel. And there were hundreds of them, surging upward like a living tide.

Arthur gripped the golden Trident of Neptune and called to his friends in the sea. Great white sharks and massive orcas answered, racing in from the surrounding water to meet the swarm. The battle was a swirling storm of silt and silver and shadow. Arthur moved through it like a current — fast and fluid, his trident carving bright arcs through the dark. One enormous creature, the matriarch of the swarm, lunged straight for him. He spun, drove the trident deep, and a shockwave of golden energy burst outward, sending the horde tumbling back.

But there were too many. Arthur needed something bigger. He slammed the butt of his trident into the seabed with all his strength — and the geothermal vents below erupted in great bursts of heat and light. The Trench creatures screeched and scrambled away from the blinding brightness, retreating into the deepest crevices they could find.

And that is how Arthur Curry learned that to be King of the Seven Seas means standing watch over even the darkest corners, so nothing that hungers for the light ever reaches it.
