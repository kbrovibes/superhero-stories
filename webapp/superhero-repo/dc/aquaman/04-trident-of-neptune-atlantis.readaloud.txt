Every great king has a symbol of their rule. For the King of England it might be a golden crown. For the King of Atlantis, it is something far more magnificent: the Trident of Neptune.

The Trident is no ordinary weapon. It is made from a special gold-colored metal that never rusts, no matter how many centuries it spends in the deep salt sea. When Arthur holds it and focuses his will, the ocean listens. Waves rise on command. Storms go quiet. Paths open through currents that would crush a submarine. The Trident is the voice of the sea, and Arthur is the one it chooses to speak through.

And the city it protects — Atlantis — is something you have to close your eyes to truly picture. It sits far below the Atlantic Ocean, where the water is cold and blue and impossibly deep. The towers are made of coral and shimmering glass, and they glow with a soft light the color of a moonlit tide pool. The streets are not walked — they are swum. Children dart between sea turtles on their way to school. Merchants travel in vehicles that look like bright, colorful fish. At the center of it all stands the great palace where Arthur and Queen Mera look after their people.

Do you know what Arthur loves most about Atlantis? It is not the grandeur or the history. It is the ordinary life of it — the laughter of children, the hum of the current-powered lights, the healthy reefs blooming just beyond the city walls.

And that is how the Trident of Neptune and the City of Atlantis became not just tools of a hero, but the home of a king who truly cared.
