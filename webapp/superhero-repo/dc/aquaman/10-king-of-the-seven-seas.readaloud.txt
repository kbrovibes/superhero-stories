Every ocean in the world has its own name, its own depth, and its own secrets. Together, the Seven Seas cover more of the Earth than all the land put together. And on the day of his coronation, Arthur Curry accepted the most important title of his life: High King of all of them.

It had not been easy to get here. The seven underwater kingdoms each had their own proud traditions, their own ancient grievances, and their own reasons not to trust a man who was also half-human. There was warlike Xebel. There were the mysterious scholars of the Trench. There were the hidden dwellers of the Brine. For years, Arthur had swum from kingdom to kingdom, listening, mediating, proving through both his actions and his words that a united ocean was stronger than a divided one.

At the Great Altar of Atlantis, before the bioluminescent towers shining above and an assembly of representatives from every corner of the world, Arthur raised the golden Trident of Neptune high. He did not speak of conquest. He spoke of stewardship. And the words he said echoed through the water and into history: "The sea does not belong to the King. The King belongs to the sea."

He made new laws to protect the coral reefs. He signed treaties with surface nations to stop the pollution of the water. He built a task force to defend the deep against the ancient terrors that still lurked in the unexplored crevices of the world. Do you know what he felt most proud of, though? Watching a child play among the kelp forests, laughing without a care.

And that is how Arthur Curry — lighthouse boy, half-Atlantean prince, champion of the depths — became the King of the Seven Seas, and made the ocean a little safer, a little fairer, for every creature in it.
