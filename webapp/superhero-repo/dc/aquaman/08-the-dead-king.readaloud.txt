Long before Arthur Curry was born, long before Atlantis had coral towers and glowing streets, there was a first king. His name was Atlan. And his story did not end well. In a rage so terrible it changed the shape of the world, Atlan had pulled a great continent beneath the waves. The kingdom sank. The age ended. And Atlan was buried in the ice of the Arctic, sleeping through the centuries — until the day his tomb began to wake.

Arthur traveled alone to find it. The Arctic water was biting cold even for him, full of grinding ice and old, whispering silence. He found the entrance: a towering archway carved from black obsidian, frosted over with ice that never melted, even in summer. Inside, the walls were covered with murals of a golden age that ended in sorrow. And at the center of the chamber, on a throne of frozen kelp, sat Atlan.

The Dead King's eyes opened. Cold blue fire filled them. He rose, gripping a scepter of dark, unyielding metal, and he pressed his ancient grief directly into Arthur's mind — visions of Atlantis sinking again, of a king who failed his people because he belonged to two worlds at once.

Arthur fought back. He drove the golden Trident of Neptune into the ice, filling it with warmth and light. "You are a ghost of a failed era!" he cried. And instead of destroying Atlan, he did something harder: he channeled the Dead King's grief back into the earth, letting the ancient anger dissolve rather than explode.

And that is how Arthur Curry, holding the dark scepter of the Dead King in one hand and his own golden trident in the other, showed that the greatest act of a new king is to learn from the oldest mistakes — and choose a kinder way.
