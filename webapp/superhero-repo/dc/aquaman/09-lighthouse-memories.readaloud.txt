Being king of seventy percent of the world's surface is an enormous, wonderful, heavy responsibility. And sometimes — when the weight of it gets really heavy — Arthur Curry does exactly what any person does when they need to feel better. He goes home!

Home, for Arthur, is a lighthouse on the coast of Amnesty Bay on the Atlantic shore. The stone walls are worn smooth by salt air and decades of wind. The light at the top turns around and around, steady as a heartbeat, guiding ships safely past the rocks. Most people walking past see just a lighthouse. Arthur sees his whole childhood.

This is where Thomas Curry raised him. Tom was a lighthouse keeper — quiet, steady, full of gentle humor — a man who loved the sea and who loved his son even more. When Arthur was small and first realized he could speak telepathically with fish, he came running to his dad, uncertain and a little scared. Tom's face went from surprised — to lit up from inside with fierce, protective love. "That's extraordinary, Arthur," he said. "Come show me." He didn't care that Arthur was the son of an Atlantean queen. He cared that Arthur was a good person. That was the only thing in the world that mattered to him.

When the crown of Atlantis feels too heavy, when the disputes between the seven kingdoms feel impossible, when being the bridge between two worlds feels very lonely — Arthur comes back to Amnesty Bay. He sits on the old wooden pier, listening to the waves. He lets the rain wash over him during storms — not bothered at all by water, obviously! Inside the lighthouse he finds an old weathered compass, its needle still pointing perfectly true north. He holds it in his hand and remembers his father's calm voice.

You don't need a throne to be a king. Tom Curry had no throne, no trident, no royal blood. Just honesty, and love, and the steady light that guided people safely home. He built those things into Arthur long before Atlantis ever came calling.
