On the coast of a place called Amnesty Bay, there stands an old lighthouse. The stone walls are worn by salt and wind, and the light at the top has been turning for more years than anyone can count. To most people, it is just a beacon in the dark. To Arthur Curry, it is home.

This is where his father, Tom Curry, raised him. Tom was a lighthouse keeper — a quiet, steady man who loved the sea and loved his son. When Arthur was small and first realized he could speak to the fish, he came running to his father, uncertain and a little afraid. Tom's face went from surprised to something even better: fierce, protective love. He didn't care that Arthur was the son of a queen. He cared that Arthur was a good person. That was the only thing that mattered.

On evenings when the crown of Atlantis felt heavier than the whole ocean, Arthur came back to Amnesty Bay. He would sit on the old wooden pier in the salt air, listening to the waves, and feel the weight lift a little. He thought about Mera. He thought about the Justice League. He thought about the enormous job of being a bridge between two worlds that so often misunderstood each other. It could feel very lonely.

Then a storm would roll in — rain lashing the lighthouse, waves crashing below — and Arthur would simply sit in it, letting the water wash over him. Inside, he'd find an old compass with a needle still pointing true north, and he'd hold it in his hand, remembering his father's voice.

From that day on, Arthur Curry knew that the strongest foundation a king can have is not a golden trident — it is the memory of someone who believed in him before he was anyone at all.
