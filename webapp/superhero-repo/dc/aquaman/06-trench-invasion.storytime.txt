There are places in the ocean that even Aquaman finds difficult.

Close your eyes and go there with me for a moment. Imagine going down not just to the deep — not just past the light and the color and the noise — but past all of that, past the midnight zone, past everything that swims or grows or moves in any way you would recognize. Past the places where the seafloor has been mapped and named and studied. All the way down into the fractures of the Earth itself.

This is the Trench.

The Trench is the kind of dark that has weight to it. The kind of cold that is not merely cold but the specific cold of something that has never once experienced warmth. The creatures that live there are like something from the oldest dreams — bioluminescent eyes in the dark, great white needles of teeth, bodies that the pressure has formed into shapes that logic in the upper world wouldn't predict. They are not evil. They are simply from somewhere so far from the world most people know that the concepts of good and evil have not reached them.

But they were coming up.

Arthur felt the disturbance through the Trident before he saw anything — a pressure change that moved upward through the water column like a whisper, multiplied: many creatures, moving together, from very deep, with the directed energy of something that has a destination.

He went down to meet them.

Deeper. Deeper. Past the zones. Into the dark. The water temperature dropped until it registered on the Trident as a vibration he could feel in his teeth. The pressure around him increased to levels that would have ended anyone not born of both worlds.

The first eyes appeared in the dark below: dozens of glowing points, yellow-green and cold. Then hundreds. Then too many to count separately. The Trench was rising.

WHOOOOSH — the matriarch of the swarm came first, larger than the rest, her teeth longer than Arthur's arm, her bioluminescent markings pulsing in patterns he read as aggression. He put the Trident in his hand and held his ground — not advancing, not retreating, just present, in the water between them and the upper world, steady.

He called. Not a shout — a signal through the water, the Trident amplifying his communication into the language of the deep. Not words: pressure and vibration and pattern, the basic vocabulary of every creature in the ocean. He asked what they were moving toward and why.

The response was not language. It was feeling: heat, disturbance, above — something in the upper water was wrong, and the Trench creatures were responding the way deep-sea creatures occasionally did when the pressures above them changed in ways their ancient instincts coded as threat.

Arthur understood this. A geological disturbance in the trench above — volcanic activity, probably, triggered by shifting plates — had sent a pressure wave downward that the Trench read as danger from above. They were evacuating. Into the zones above them.

He couldn't calm the geological disturbance from here. But he could give them a different direction.

He called the apex predators — the great whites patrolling the continental shelf, the orca pods moving through the cold Atlantic. They came at his signal: shadows in the dark, enormous and controlled, moving into position around the flanks of the Trench swarm. Not to fight — to redirect.

The matriarch lunged. Arthur drove the Trident down — not into her, but into the seabed below her, channeling the Trident's power into the geothermal layer, drawing up a surge of warmth and light from the earth's interior. KRAKOOM — the vents opened in a rush of heat and luminescence.

The Trench creatures — adapted to the absolute dark, to the cold that never varies — stopped.

The light hit their light-sensitive eyes. The warmth hit their cold-calibrated sensory systems. They did not like any of it. They retreated. Not in panic — gradually, backing down toward the crevices and the deep where the conditions made sense to them, the matriarch going last, her bioluminescent markings pulsing slowly as she descended.

The ocean sealed back over them. The dark returned. The Trench was quiet again.

Arthur hung in the water column for a long moment, the Trident in his hand, breathing the sea.

He thought about what it meant to rule the ocean. Not just the beautiful parts — the glowing coral and the dolphin pods and the whales singing to each other across hundreds of miles. Also this part: the deep and the dark and the creatures whose existence was frightening and whose needs were as real as any other's.

A king defended all of his kingdom.

Even the darkest and most frightening parts.

Even the parts that lunged.

He turned and swam up, slowly, through the layers of the dark toward the faint memory of light above.
