On the coast of Maine, where the Atlantic comes in big and cold and uncompromising against the rocky shore, there is a lighthouse.

It stands on a point of land that juts into the sea like a finger, and its beam sweeps the water in slow, steady circles every night — counting off the seconds the way a heartbeat counts off a life, reliable and calm, a promise to every sailor that says: you are not lost; I can see you; come home.

The lighthouse keeper's name was Tom Curry. He was a quiet man with large hands and a steady face, the kind of person who found meaning in doing something necessary and doing it well. He had lived his whole life on that point of land, with the sound of the sea always present — in the walls of the lighthouse, in the kitchen, in the small garden where things grew sideways in the constant wind — and he loved it the way you love something that is both beautiful and demanding, which is the most complete kind of love.

He had not expected to fall in love with the sea in any other sense.

Her name was Atlanna. She came out of the water on a storm-dark night — not washed up, not rescued, but walking from the surf with the easy dignity of someone for whom the ocean is home, which is exactly what it was. She was from Atlantis, a kingdom in the deep that Tom had no more reason to know about than you have reason to know about the capital cities of other continents on other planets. But she was real, and she was here, and they recognized something in each other in the way that certain people recognize each other — immediately and without explanation.

She stayed. For a time, she stayed.

Their son was born in the lighthouse, with the sound of the sea in every wall.

Arthur was a sturdy, serious child who learned to walk on a rocky shore and to talk with the fish in the tidal pools before he could read. Tom watched this with the calm of a man who has decided that life is too extraordinary to be surprised by — who had already fallen in love with a woman from an undersea kingdom, and had readjusted his expectations accordingly.

"The fish talk back?" Tom asked once.

"Yeah," Arthur said, watching a crab. "They're not very interesting, though. This one keeps thinking about the rock."

Tom nodded. "What about the rock?"

"It wants to go back under the water." A pause. "I think."

Tom thought about this. "Well," he said, "I suppose that's the same thing the crab is thinking."

Arthur looked up at him. He had his father's steadiness and something else — something from the deep, something patient and enormous and not entirely of this world. "Do you think I'm weird?" he asked.

Tom Curry put a hand on his son's shoulder. "I think you're exactly right," he said.

Atlanna was eventually called back to Atlantis — pulled by duties that Tom understood but that left a silence in the lighthouse that the sea never fully filled. She left knowing that Arthur was safe and loved and in exactly the right hands. She left knowing that the lighthouse beam would keep turning, patient as ever, keeping watch.

Arthur grew up between worlds. He swam in the cold Atlantic and dived deeper than any ordinary person should be able to and came back up with news from the fish and the whales and once, memorably, from an elderly octopus who had strong opinions about currents. He learned to navigate by starlight and by the movement of water in ways that felt like memory, as if the sea itself was reminding him of something he had always known.

When he was old enough to understand, Tom told him about Atlantis. About Atlanna. About the kingdom waiting beneath the waves that was as much Arthur's birthright as the lighthouse on the Maine coast.

Arthur dove into the deep and found it: the coral towers glowing soft blue in the dark water, the silent streets where people moved like the sea moved, the great throne at the center of Atlantis that had been waiting.

He came back up. He stood on the rocks in the spray and looked at the lighthouse.

Both worlds. Not one or the other. Both.

He became Aquaman: king of the ocean and guardian of the shore, the person who stood at the edge between the deep blue world and the wide land world, making sure neither one forgot the other. In shimmering orange scales and sea-green leggings, with the golden Trident of Neptune in his hand, he was the bridge that the sea and the land had always needed.

Seventy percent of the Earth is ocean. And all of it answers to him.
