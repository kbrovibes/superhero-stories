Some days inside the Xavier School for Gifted Youngsters were perfectly ordinary. People had breakfast. People trained. People sat in the library or worked in the greenhouse or fixed things in the lab. Hank made tea. Ororo sometimes sang under her breath while she watered the plants. Remy dealt himself patience games at the kitchen table.

And some days the mansion crackled with a different kind of electricity.

It started on a Thursday at quarter to eight, over scrambled eggs.

Scott Summers had been up since dawn working on the mission plan for a Brotherhood convoy intercept in New Jersey. He had calculated approach angles and timing windows and each team member's assignment. He had done it carefully. He laid the printed plan on the kitchen table at breakfast because that was when the whole team was together.

Logan picked it up. He read it. He set it down.

"This is wrong," he said.

The kitchen went very still.

Scott looked up from his coffee. "No it isn't."

"The approach here leaves the team exposed."

"The timing accounts for that."

"Only if the Brotherhood doesn't move early."

"The probability model—"

"I've done fieldwork since before your probability model was invented," Logan said flatly.

Scott's jaw tightened. "I know what I'm doing."

"So do I," said Logan.

That was essentially the end of the argument out loud. But it was not the end of its effects. The conversation had a weight that spread through the rest of the day. Jean sat in the library with a book open to page fourteen for an hour and never turned the page. Storm went to the greenhouse and repotted six plants with more care than any of them needed. Gambit found reasons to be outdoors. Rogue sat on the back wall, watching the sky.

Hank worked in his laboratory all afternoon and turned his music up.

Professor Xavier sat in his study with his hands folded on the desk and waited. He had learned, over many years, that people needed to move through things at their own speed. Jumping in too early usually just added another thing to be upset about.

By evening, though, the wait was long enough.

He sent a gentle thought to Jean — not words, just a suggestion, like a door opening. She came to his study a few minutes later.

"Ask them to meet me in the Danger Room," he said. "Tonight."

Jean looked at him. "Both of them?"

"Both of them," he said.

At eleven o'clock, Scott and Logan stood in the Danger Room in the semi-dark. The floor was empty. The walls were quiet. The room hadn't been activated yet.

Xavier's voice came through the ceiling speakers.

"Simple scenario," he said. "Two operatives. One extraction objective. The way forward requires both of you."

The room activated. Two corridors opened — north and south.

Logan went north. Scott went south. Neither of them spoke.

North corridor: Logan moved fast and quiet, the way he always did, and found what he was looking for not sixty seconds in. A sensor array, angled precisely to cover the south approach. Anyone coming from the south — anyone — would have tripped it instantly. He dismantled it in fifteen seconds and kept moving.

South corridor: Scott moved methodically, checking angles. He found the extraction route tucked low on the east wall — lower profile, better concealment, significantly easier approach from the south. The kind of exit that looked like nothing special unless you were scanning for it. Someone coming from the north would have taken a harder, more exposed route and never known there was a better option.

They reached the center relay point at roughly the same time.

Logan spoke first. "Your south approach had a sensor on it."

Scott looked at him steadily. "Your north approach missed the low exit."

Neither of them said anything for a moment.

"Both routes make the other one safer," Scott said.

"That's the point," said Logan.

Xavier's voice came through the room, quiet and warm. "Your instincts are different from each other because they are supposed to be different. You have different experience, different perspectives, different ways of reading a situation. None of that is a problem. It only becomes a problem if you stop listening."

They walked out of the Danger Room at midnight.

Neither mentioned the morning again.

At breakfast on Friday, Logan picked up the mission plan and circled a correction with a pencil — a small adjustment to the north approach timing. He slid it across the table without comment.

Scott looked at it. He picked up his own pencil and made two brief notations, adding the low-exit route to the southern approach.

He slid it back.

Logan looked at the additions. He nodded once.

The mission went cleanly.

Goodnight. Sometimes the best things come from putting two different ideas together.
