Here is a quiet story. Not every story is loud. Some of the best ones happen on rooftops, over tea, with a deck of cards.

Rogue — from a small town in Mississippi — wore gloves every day, summer and winter. Her power: bare skin contact absorbed another person's memories and abilities straight into her. Useful in battle. In regular life, it meant keeping distance from everyone. Not by choice. By necessity.

The Xavier School roof garden had planters and a view of the Westchester hills. In the evenings, students drifted up for cards or conversation. Rogue sat near the north planter with a book. The others, who liked her and meant well, gave her space.

For two weeks, Remy LeBeau watched this from across the roof.

Remy was from New Orleans. He was easy-going and quick and not in a hurry about anything. He decided what he wanted to do and waited until the right moment to do it.

On Wednesday evening he climbed to the roof with a deck of cards and sat down on the planter box across from her. He didn't introduce himself or explain. He just started doing a card trick — a simple one, where a card vanished from one hand and reappeared in the other.

Rogue lowered her book. "What's the trick?"

"No trick," he said. "The card wants to be somewhere else. I help it get there." A little pink light pulsed between his fingers as he charged it slightly, just enough to move it.

She looked at him to see if he was being funny. He wasn't, really.

He showed her three more tricks. She watched with complete attention. She didn't touch anything — she never touched anything she didn't mean to. But she stayed.

The next evening he came back with tea. He set her cup down well within reach, without making anything out of it. She picked it up.

They talked about the card game the others were playing. They talked about whether the old oak trees in the drive had names. They talked about nothing important, which meant they talked about everything comfortable. The visits became a regular thing.

Remy was always careful about distance. He never made a show of being careful — just was. There is a difference.

One evening Rogue said, "You know that even with gloves, if I—"

"I know," Remy said.

"Most people—"

"I know," he said again, simply. "I'm not most people."

The sun went down over Westchester. The oak trees went dark against the gold. Below, the others laughed at their card game.

Rogue held her tea in her gloved hands and looked at the sky.

The next morning she saved him a seat at breakfast.

That was how she answered.
