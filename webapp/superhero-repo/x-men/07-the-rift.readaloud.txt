Have you ever had an argument in the morning that made the whole rest of the day feel like walking on thin ice?

Cyclops and Wolverine had that argument on a Thursday. Over breakfast. At the kitchen table. About a mission plan.

Scott had written out a careful, detailed strategy for intercepting a Brotherhood convoy in New Jersey. Approach angles, timing, assigned positions. He was proud of it. He had worked on it since six in the morning.

Logan looked at it for about thirty seconds.

"This is wrong," Logan said.

Scott looked up. "No it isn't."

"The approach here puts the whole team in the open."

"The timing accounts for that."

"The timing assumes the Brotherhood doesn't move early."

"The probability—"

"I've done this for thirty years. The probability isn't the point."

The kitchen went silent. Storm looked at her orange juice. Hank became very interested in his toast.

The argument stopped there — but the tension didn't. It spread through the mansion like weather. Jean found a book and didn't read it. Gambit and Rogue stayed outside most of the afternoon. Hank put on loud music in the lab.

That evening, Professor Xavier knocked on two separate doors.

At eleven o'clock, Scott and Logan walked into the Danger Room.

The scenario Xavier had set was this: two operatives, one objective, and no possible way to complete it alone. Scott took the south corridor. Logan took the north.

North corridor: Logan moved through it quickly, found a sensor array pointing directly into the south approach, and dismantled it without thinking twice. Someone coming from the south would have walked right into it.

South corridor: Scott spotted an exit tucked low on the east wall — lower exposure, better cover. Someone coming from the north would have missed it entirely and taken the harder approach.

They met at the center relay point.

Logan said: "Your south approach had a sensor aimed straight at it."

Scott said: "Your north approach missed the low exit."

They looked at each other in the half-dark of the Danger Room.

"Both routes make the other one safer," Scott said.

"That's the point," Logan said.

Xavier's voice came through the room speakers, quiet and a little satisfied. "Your instincts are different because they're supposed to be. You are not the same person. That is why you are a team."

They walked out at midnight. Neither said anything about the morning.

At breakfast on Friday, Logan circled a correction on the mission plan and pushed it across the table. Scott read it. He nodded. He made two notations of his own.

The mission went cleanly.

Disagreements don't always end with someone being wrong. Sometimes they end with something better.
