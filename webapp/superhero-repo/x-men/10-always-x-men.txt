Professor Xavier had a tradition on the last day of each year: he gathered the X-Men in the great hall after dinner and asked each of them the same question. Not about missions completed or threats neutralized. He asked: "What has surprised you most this year?"

Logan said, "How quiet it can be here." It was the most words he had offered at one of these gatherings.

Rogue said, "That I can still be surprised." She looked at Gambit when she said it. He looked at the ceiling.

Nightcrawler said he had been surprised that no one had ever asked him to hide his blue skin or his tail or the faint smell of sulfur when he teleported. Kurt Wagner had grown up in places that asked him to be less visible. The Xavier School had simply handed him a uniform and told him to train.

Jean Grey said she had been surprised by how loud joy was compared to worry. She had spent years bracing for other people's fear and sadness. The noise of happiness, she discovered, was warmer in every way.

Hank McCoy said he had been surprised that the boiler kept breaking, given the number of times he had fixed it. Everyone laughed, and he meant them to.

Ororo Munroe said she had been surprised to find she missed the mansion when away — that the Westchester hills and the oak trees had become a kind of weather system inside her, something she felt returning when she came home.

Scott Summers was last, as always, because he was always the one who took longest to say things. He said he had been surprised that he could look at a table of people who were all completely different from him — in age, in power, in temperament — and feel something that had no tactical name at all. Not readiness. Not confidence. Something older and less useful than either.

"What's it called?" Nightcrawler asked, genuinely curious.

Scott thought about it for a moment.

"Family," he said.

Xavier sat at the head of the table and looked at them — the eight faces turned toward him, each one marked by everything the year had asked of them. He thought of Cerebro and the signals it had found, the frightened minds scattered across a world that didn't always understand them.

He thought of the question he had asked each of them, the first day they arrived: your gift is not a curse. He thought of how long it had taken each of them to believe it, and how much of that work had been done right here, by each other.

"Again next year?" he asked.

"Obviously," said Logan.

Xavier smiled.
