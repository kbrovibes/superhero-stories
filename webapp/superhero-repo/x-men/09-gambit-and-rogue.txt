The students of the Xavier School used the roof garden in the evenings when the weather was kind, and on those nights Rogue sat alone near the north planter with a book while everyone else played cards or talked. It wasn't that she didn't like the others. It was that the gloves on her hands were a constant reminder of the distance she had to keep.

Remy LeBeau had been watching this for about two weeks before he did anything about it. He was not a person who rushed things.

He climbed to the roof on a Wednesday evening with a deck of cards and sat down on the planter box across from her. He didn't explain himself or make a speech. He just started doing a card trick — a simple one, where a card vanishes from one place and appears in another. Rogue looked up from her book.

"What's the trick?" she asked.

"No trick," he said. "The card wants to be somewhere else. I help it get there."

She looked at him for a moment to see if he was making fun of her. He wasn't.

They didn't talk about powers that night. Remy showed her three more card tricks. She watched without touching anything, because she never touched anything she didn't mean to, but she watched with full attention, and that was something. When the others drifted up to the roof, she didn't leave. She stayed.

The next evening he was back with two cups of tea. He set hers down within arm's reach without making it into anything. She picked it up. They talked about small things — the card game the others were playing, whether the oak trees were old enough to have names. Safe, ordinary things.

The evenings became a regular thing. Remy was careful never to reach for her hands or touch her accidentally. He didn't make a performance of being careful. He was just careful, the way careful people are when they understand that something matters.

One evening Rogue said, "You know that even with gloves, if I squeeze too hard, it still—"

"I know," he said simply.

"Most people find that—"

"I know," he said again. "I'm not most people."

She looked at him. He dealt himself a card and it charged faintly pink in his fingers before settling. They sat on the roof with the evening sky going gold over Westchester and the sound of the others laughing below, and it was, for Rogue, one of the most ordinary and necessary evenings she had ever had.

She said nothing else about it. Neither did he. But she started saving him a seat at dinner.
