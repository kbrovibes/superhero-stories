Imagine getting a letter in the mail. Not a birthday card. Not a bill. A real letter, handwritten in neat careful ink, from a man named Charles Xavier who says — and I want you to listen to this part — "I know what you can do. Come to my school. You belong here."

That letter changed everything for four very unusual young people.

WHOOOOSH — first to arrive at the Xavier School for Gifted Youngsters was Scott Summers. Scott wore special ruby-quartz glasses every single day, because without them his eyes fired optic blasts so powerful they could punch through solid stone. ZZZAP! He was terrified of taking them off. Xavier looked right at him and said, "We'll fix that. Together."

Next came Ororo Munroe, all the way from Kenya, and when she walked up the front steps, a little thunder rumbled in the clear blue sky — RUMBLE RUMBLE — as if the clouds were announcing her arrival. She could control the weather. All of it. Wind, rain, lightning, blizzards. She had been hiding that her whole life.

Then Hank McCoy loped in on feet as big as dinner plates with hands to match. Hank had a brain the size of a library and a body that made people stare. His first day at the mansion he took apart the broken boiler in the basement and put it back together — CLANG CLANG WRENCH CLANG — humming the whole time. Fixed!

Last came Jean Grey. She didn't knock anything over when she arrived. She didn't need to. She just floated her suitcase up the stairs with her mind — WHOOOMP — and every book on the hallway shelf fluttered open like a hundred startled birds.

Professor Xavier watched all of this with a warm, steady smile. He gathered his four new students in the great hall, sat in his wheelchair at the center of the room, and looked at each of them in turn.

"The world out there is afraid of mutants," he said honestly. "I won't pretend otherwise. But fear comes from not understanding something. Your job — our job — is to show the world there is nothing to fear."

"What do we call ourselves?" Scott asked.

Xavier smiled. "The X-Men."

Scott looked at Ororo. Ororo looked at Hank. Hank looked at Jean. Jean looked at all of them and for the first time in years, nobody was staring at her the wrong way.

Outside the mansion windows, autumn leaves spun in a small spiral — and nobody could tell if it was the wind or Ororo being happy.

It was probably Ororo being happy.

The X-Men had begun.
