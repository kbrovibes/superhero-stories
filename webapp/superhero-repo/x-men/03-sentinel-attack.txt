The call came on a Tuesday morning while Hank McCoy was halfway through a stack of blueberry pancakes. Cerebro had detected a signal: three Sentinel robots — massive purple-and-grey machines built by Bolivar Trask to hunt mutants — had appeared on the streets of Midtown Manhattan, scanning crowds and closing in on a frightened twelve-year-old girl named Maya whose mutant powers had surfaced the night before. The X-Men scrambled to their jet, the Blackbird, in under four minutes.

The Blackbird set down on the Hudson River's west bank, its engines muffled by Storm's cloud cover. The Sentinels were visible from blocks away: each one thirty feet tall, their domed heads sweeping red targeting beams across the streets. Maya had taken shelter behind an overturned taxi and was shaking. Citizens had scattered. The Sentinels moved slowly but they never stopped.

Cyclops split the team without hesitation. "Beast — taxi, get the girl clear. Storm — air cover and keep the heads busy. Jean — hold the first Sentinel's arms so it can't reach. I'll take the joints." He didn't wait for debate. The plan was already in motion.

Jean raised her hands and the nearest Sentinel lurched, its massive arms frozen at its sides. Storm climbed into the sky on a column of rising wind — CRACK — lightning speared the second Sentinel's sensors, blinding it. Beast covered twenty meters in two bounds and scooped Maya out from under the taxi in one arm, carrying her to safety behind a concrete barrier. Cyclops fired twice: one optic blast caught the first Sentinel at the knee joint with a sound like a car collision, and the machine buckled and sat down hard on the pavement.

The third Sentinel had already recalibrated. It raised a giant hand toward Storm — but Jean had anticipated it, sweeping her telekinesis across its targeting array. The machine's arm swung wide, missing by twenty feet. Storm called in a downward gust that pressed it back two city blocks, giving Cyclops the angle he needed. One more blast, perfectly placed, and the third Sentinel folded.

The street fell quiet. Maya stared at the crumpled machines around her, then at the five people landing and jogging toward her. Beast set her down carefully.

"You're safe," Jean said quietly. "We know today was frightening. But you're not alone." She glanced at Xavier's face in her earpiece, knowing he was already arranging everything.

That evening, a letter arrived at Maya's home in Westchester. It was written in careful, hopeful handwriting.
