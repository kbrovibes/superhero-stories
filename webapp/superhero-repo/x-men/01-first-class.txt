In a grand mansion on the outskirts of Westchester, New York, a man named Charles Xavier sat before a machine unlike anything else on Earth. Cerebro was a silver helmet connected to an enormous computer, and when Xavier placed it on his head, his mind stretched outward like ripples on a pond. He searched for young people whose DNA carried something extraordinary — people the world called mutants. One by one he found them, frightened and alone, and he sent letters written in careful, hopeful handwriting to each of their homes.

Scott Summers arrived first, wearing ruby-quartz glasses that kept his optic blasts from cutting through walls. He was quiet and serious, carrying enormous guilt about the power behind his eyes. Ororo Munroe came next, all the way from Kenya, the air around her crackling with static whenever she felt strong emotion. She had been calling lightning since she was a child and had never once been able to talk about it. Hank McCoy followed — a young genius with oversized hands and feet who had spent his life being stared at in school hallways. Jean Grey arrived last, a girl from Annandale-on-Hudson who could move objects without touching them and sometimes heard other people's thoughts so loudly she could not sleep.

Xavier met each of them in his study and said the same simple thing: "Your gift is not a curse. You simply need to learn how to use it." He called the mansion the Xavier School for Gifted Youngsters, and he meant every word of that name.

The first week was strange and wonderful. Scott nearly sliced the kitchen table when his glasses slipped. Ororo accidentally frosted all the east-wing windows during a bad dream. Hank rebuilt the school's boiler in two hours just to do something with his hands. Jean rearranged every book on the third-floor shelf without leaving her room. They were a collection of accidental disasters, slowly learning to become something purposeful together.

On Friday evening Xavier gathered them in the great hall. "You are the X-Men," he told them, "named for the extraordinary in each of you. You will train here, grow here, and when the world needs you, you will protect it — all of it, even the parts that are afraid of us." Scott looked at Ororo. Ororo looked at Hank. Hank looked at Jean. Outside, the first autumn leaves drifted past the mansion's tall windows, and for the first time in a long time, none of them felt alone.
