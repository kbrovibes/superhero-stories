The mission was supposed to be straightforward. Wolverine — Logan — and Storm were sent ahead of the team to scout a Brotherhood safehouse buried in a network of tunnels beneath an old industrial facility in upstate New York. What they found was a collapsed passage, a sprung alarm, and six locked steel doors standing between them and the surface.

Logan wasn't worried. His adamantium claws — three blades bonded to his skeleton by a process he didn't like to remember — could cut through most things, and his healing factor meant injuries healed in minutes. But the tunnels were narrow, low, and full of metal sensor cables that Magneto's allies had installed. The moment Logan's claws touched the first door, every alarm in the facility lit up.

"Cagey," Storm said, which meant: you might have led with that information.

"Claws were faster," he said.

Storm, for her part, could not use the weather underground. Her power needed open sky — clouds, pressure, the great breathing circulation of the atmosphere. Down here there was only close air, rock walls, and the distant drip of groundwater. She was the most powerful mutant on the team in an open field and, right now, not much help at the lock.

So they figured it out together. Logan listened — really listened, ears sharp beyond what any human could manage — and identified the echo pattern of the tunnels, mapping which passages were empty and which were not. Storm used her meteorological senses to detect the faint air movement from distant ventilation, triangulating which direction led upward. Together they moved through the facility silently, Logan going first at intersections, Storm reading the air at every junction.

At the fifth door, Logan found a junction box instead of a deadbolt. He looked at it, looked at Storm, and stepped aside. Storm placed two fingertips on the wires inside and sent a precise, focused pulse of static electricity — one quarter of what she would use to guide a lightning bolt — through the lock's magnetic circuit. The door clicked open.

"Huh," Logan said. High praise.

They surfaced through a drainage grate forty minutes after the alarm had sounded, stepping into the grey November air. The rest of the team was waiting with the Blackbird thirty meters away. Cyclops raised an eyebrow.

"We were about to come in," Jean said.

"Didn't need it," Logan said.

"We figured it out," Storm said, and there was a quiet satisfaction in her voice that was different from smugness. It was the particular warmth of discovering that you and a difficult person fit together better than either of you expected.

Logan almost smiled.
