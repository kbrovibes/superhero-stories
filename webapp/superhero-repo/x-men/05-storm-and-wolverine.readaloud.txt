Logan is not always the easiest person to be on a mission with.

His skeleton is laced with adamantium, his healing factor fixes injuries in minutes, and he has three claws on each hand that cut through almost anything. He is extremely useful.

He just doesn't always explain his plan before he does it.

Storm and Logan were sent to scout a Brotherhood safehouse beneath a factory in upstate New York. The tunnels were narrow. The air smelled like rock dust and old machines.

Logan approached the first steel door.

"Hold on," Storm started — but SNIKT — three adamantium claws came out, cut through the lock in one smooth stroke, and every alarm in the entire underground facility lit up at once. BEEEEEP BEEEEEP BEEEEEP BEEEEEP.

Storm looked at him.

He looked at the door. "Claws were faster," he said.

Now here is the problem. Storm's power — calling wind, summoning lightning, shaping the weather — needs the SKY. She is extraordinary in an open field or up on a rooftop. Underground, in a tunnel full of rock and sensor cables, with no sky anywhere above her?

She needed a different plan.

So did he.

They stood in the flashing alarm light and thought.

Logan's ears are sharper than any human's. He can hear a footstep two rooms away or the difference between a full tunnel and an empty one by the way sound bounces. He listened carefully, head tilted. "Clear left. Clear right. Something moving two levels up."

Storm closed her eyes. She could feel air movement — the faintest breath from far away — the kind that comes from a ventilation shaft pointed upward. She pointed. "That direction is up."

They moved together. Logan went first at every intersection — pause, listen, go. Storm checked every junction for the direction of the airflow. Forty minutes, five doors, zero wrong turns.

At the fifth door there was no lock — just a junction box of wires inside a small panel.

Logan stared at it. Then he stepped aside.

Storm placed two fingertips on the wires. She found the magnetic circuit running through the lock. She sent through it the smallest, most careful spark of static electricity she had ever produced — a tiny fraction of a real lightning bolt — just enough.

CLICK.

The door swung open.

Logan looked at it. Then at her. Then he said: "Huh."

Coming from Logan, that was practically a standing ovation.

They surfaced through a drainage grate into cold November air. The Blackbird was thirty meters away. Cyclops was already walking toward them.

"We were about to come in," Jean said.

"Didn't need it," Logan said.

Storm smiled. "We figured it out."

She meant more than just the tunnels.
